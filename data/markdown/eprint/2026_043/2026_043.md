# Classical Obfuscation of Quantum Circuits via Publicly-Verifiable QFHE

#### Abstract

A classical obfuscator for quantum circuits is a classical program that, given the classical description of a quantum circuit Q, outputs the classical description of a functionally equivalent quantum circuit <sup>Q</sup><sup>e</sup> that hides as much as possible about <sup>Q</sup>. Previously, the only known feasibility result for classical obfuscation of quantum circuits (Bartusek and Malavolta, ITCS 2022) was limited to "null" security, which is only meaningful for circuits that always reject. On the other hand, if the obfuscator is allowed to compile the quantum circuit <sup>Q</sup> into a quantum state <sup>|</sup>Qe⟩, there exist feasibility results for obfuscating much more expressive classes of circuits: All pseudo-deterministic quantum circuits (Bartusek, Kitagawa, Nishimaki and Yamakawa, STOC 2023, Bartusek, Brakerski and Vaikuntanathan, STOC 2024), and even all unitaries (Huang and Tang, FOCS 2025).

We show that (relative to a classical oracle) there exists a classical obfuscator for all pseudo-deterministic quantum circuits. As our main technical step, we give the first construction of a compact quantum fullyhomomorphic encryption (QFHE) scheme that supports public verification of (pseudo-deterministic) quantum evaluation, relative to a classical oracle.

To construct our QFHE scheme, we improve on an approach introduced by Bartusek, Kitagawa, Nishimaki and Yamakawa (STOC 2023), which previously required ciphertexts that are both quantum and non-compact due to a heavy use of quantum coset states and their publicly-verifiable properties. As part of our core technical contribution, we introduce new techniques for analyzing coset states that can be generated "on the fly", by proving new cryptographic properties of the one-shot signature scheme of Shmueli and Zhandry (CRYPTO 2025). Our techniques allow us to produce QFHE ciphertexts that are purely classical, compact, and publicly-verifiable. This additionally yields the first classical verification of quantum computation protocol for BQP that simultaneously satisfies blindness and public-verifiability.

### <span id="page-2-0"></span>1 Introduction

Program obfuscation disguises the inner workings of an algorithm while leaving its behavior unchanged. This concept traces back to the roots of modern cryptography [DH22], and ever since the formalization of indistinguishability obfuscation (iO) [BGI+01], constructing iO has been one of cryptography's overarching goals. It took no less than a decade to come up with the first candidates for secure iO [GGH+16], and almost an additional decade to base classically-secure iO on well-studied assumptions [JLS21]. Current efforts continue along this trajectory, with an active line of work devoted to developing quantum-secure iO, eventually grounded in similarly well-understood assumptions (e.g. [GGH15, BGMZ18, CVW18, APM20, BDGM23, WW21],...).

The original notion of program obfuscation implicitly considers computation as classical, and accordingly, seeks to obfuscate classical algorithms. Now, as quantum computation increasingly emerges as a standard model of computation, it is natural to revisit the problem in this broader context: Can quantum algorithms be obfuscated as well?

Ideal Program Obfuscation: From Classical to Quantum. Our understanding of obfuscation in the quantum setting is still at a very early stage: In particular, we still don't know how to base indistinguishability obfuscation for expressive classes of quantum circuits on (post-quantum) indistinguishability obfuscation for classical circuits. Instead, recent work has turned to exploring the relationship between *idealized* notions of obfuscation in the classical and quantum worlds [BM22, BKNY23, BBV24, HT25]. In such an idealized model, an obfuscation of a circuit C is considered secure if everything that can be learned from the obfuscation  $\widetilde{C}$  can be simulated given only *oracle access* to C.

Of course, ideal obfuscation cannot be achieved in full generality, as there are well-known counterexamples demonstrating its impossibility [BGI+01]. Nevertheless, studying the connection between classical and quantum ideal obfuscation remains valuable for at least two reasons. First, it isolates a fundamental question: Does the existence of an ideal obfuscator for classical computation imply one for quantum? Second, indistinguishability obfuscation is widely regarded as a strong heuristic for ideal obfuscation (e.g., it is the best-possible obfuscation [GR07]). Thus, establishing that ideal classical obfuscation gives rise to ideal quantum obfuscation results in candidates for quantum obfuscation by instantiating the black-box with (post-quantum) indistinguishability obfuscation.

Prior work [BKNY23, BBV24, HT25] has established that, assuming a classical ideal obfuscator for classical circuits, one can construct a quantum ideal obfuscator for quantum circuits. In this setting, the obfuscator itself is a quantum algorithm, and on input a quantum circuit Q it outputs a quantum state  $|\widetilde{Q}\rangle$  serving as the obfuscated program.<sup>2</sup> A distinctive feature of these constructions is that the obfuscated object consists of unclonable states: Once  $|\widetilde{Q}\rangle$  is produced, it cannot be duplicated. As a consequence, it is impossible to create a single obfuscation of a quantum circuit and distribute it broadly. Moreover, these obfuscations cannot be transmitted over the classical channels that comprise the internet today.

However, the goal of constructing a classical obfuscator for quantum circuits (with security for non-null circuits<sup>3</sup>), even in the ideal setting, has so far remained out of reach. This raises our central motivating question:

Is a quantum obfuscator necessary to obfuscate expressive quantum circuits?

Or can a classically-described quantum program be obfuscated by a classical obfuscator?

We address this question by constructing a classical obfuscator for all pseudo-deterministic circuits.

### <span id="page-3-0"></span>1.1 Classical Obfuscation via Publicly-Verifiable QFHE

Previous work [\[BKNY23\]](#page-52-5) identified pseudo-deterministic quantum circuits as a particularly useful class for obfuscation. A pseudo-deterministic quantum circuit Q from n to m qubits is one that, up to negligible error, computes a classical function: there exists a function F : {0, 1} <sup>n</sup> → {0, 1} <sup>m</sup> such that for every input x ∈ {0, 1} <sup>n</sup>, the output of Q(x) equals F(x) with probability 1 − negl(n), where the probability is taken over the measurement of its output. This class is both expressive—capturing, for example, all quantum algorithms that solve problems in NP, including Shor's factoring algorithm [\[Sho99\]](#page-54-3)—and structurally well-suited for cryptographic applications like obfuscation. Indeed, pseudo-deterministic circuits have already served as the foundation for obfuscating increasingly general classes of quantum computation [\[BBV24,](#page-51-2) [HT25\]](#page-53-4). Motivated by these properties, our work focuses on the obfuscation of pseudo-deterministic circuits. Crucially, we consider obfuscation through only classical means.

As also observed in previous work [\[BKNY23\]](#page-52-5), a natural route toward obfuscating pseudo-deterministic circuits relies on constructing quantum fully-homomorphic encryption (QFHE) with public verification of homomorphic evaluation for such circuits.

- A QFHE scheme is defined by algorithms (Gen, Enc, Eval, Dec), where all but the evaluation algorithm Eval can be regarded as classical. On input a ciphertext ct = Enc(x) and a quantum circuit Q, the evaluation algorithm Eval produces an evaluated ciphertext ct<sup>e</sup> which encrypts the classical value <sup>Q</sup>(x).
- In the publicly-verifiable variant of QFHE, Eval additionally outputs a succinct classical proof π, and correctness can be certified by an efficient classical verification algorithm Ver, which checks that the homomorphic evaluation has been honestly computed, and outputs Ver(ct, Q, cte, π) ∈ {⊤, ⊥}.

It is straightforward to see that, given such a powerful QFHE, obfuscation of pseudo-deterministic quantum circuits follows naturally. This can be accomplished by obfuscating the verifier and applying the standard "verify-then-decrypt" paradigm. Concretely, the obfuscation of a circuit Q consists of encrypting its classical description, i.e., ct<sup>Q</sup> ← Enc(Q). On input x ∈ {0, 1} <sup>n</sup> and the encrypted circuit ctQ, the evaluator computes (cte, π) <sup>←</sup> Eval(ctQ, Ux), where <sup>U</sup><sup>x</sup> denotes the universal quantum circuit with input <sup>x</sup> hard-wired. To recover the (classical) output Q(x), the obfuscation makes use of a classical oracle implementing the circuit

$$C(x,\widetilde{\mathsf{ct}},\pi) := \begin{cases} \mathsf{Dec}(\widetilde{\mathsf{ct}}) & \text{if } \mathsf{Ver}(\mathsf{ct}_Q,U_x,\widetilde{\mathsf{ct}},\pi) = \top \\ \bot & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

Thus, only honestly evaluated ciphertexts are ever decrypted, and the obfuscation securely reproduces the functionality of Q.

The central role of Pauli Functional Commitments. However, it turns out that verification of QFHE evaluations is a particularly delicate problem in quantum cryptography.

- On the one hand, by definition, evaluating QFHE ciphertexts is a quantum sampling task. The quantum QFHE evaluation gets the classical ct and outputs a quantumly computed classical sample cte, which is going to have high entropy even if the underlying evaluated circuit <sup>Q</sup> is deterministic. In fact, this entropy was shown to be somewhat inherent in [\[Shm22a,](#page-53-6) [Shm22b\]](#page-53-7). This seemingly presents a barrier, since it remains open how to classically verify quantum sampling problems with negligible soundness error ([\[CLLW22\]](#page-52-7) has shown how to achieve inverse-polynomial error, which, if used in the template above, would allow a polynomial-time attack on the obfuscation scheme).
- On the other hand, the special structure of certain QFHE schemes, most notably Mahadev's construction [\[Mah20\]](#page-53-8), enables a partial solution. As shown in [\[Bar21\]](#page-51-3), the use of Mahadev's measurement protocol [\[Mah18\]](#page-53-9) on Mahadev's QFHE scheme allows for verification of QFHE evaluations with negligible soundness error. However, since the measurement protocol of Mahadev is privately verifiable, then so is the resulting QFHE scheme.

{4}------------------------------------------------

The technical centerpiece behind Mahadev's measurement protocol was later abstracted by [\[BKNY23\]](#page-52-5) as a Pauli functional commitment (PFC). Roughly speaking, a PFC allows a classical verifier to issue a public classical commitment key CK. A quantum prover can then commit to a quantum state in such a way that, later, measurements of Pauli observables (in particular, standard or Hadamard basis measurements) on the state can be verified through classical communication. Viewed through this lens, the state of the art can be summarized as follows: Mahadev [\[Mah18\]](#page-53-9) constructs a privately verifiable PFC; Bartusek [\[Bar21\]](#page-51-3) demonstrates that applying Mahadev's PFCs to QFHE homomorphic evaluation yields privately verifiable evaluations; and finally, [\[BKNY23\]](#page-52-5) show how to construct, relative to a classical oracle, PFCs with public verification, albeit with a quantum commitment key |CK⟩.

Measurement protocols, or in their formal abstraction as PFCs, were originally devised to allow a classical client to force a quantum server to perform some prescribed computation. Existing results on public verification, however, require the commitment keys to remain quantum, which prevents them from being generated by a classical verifier. This leads us to what we view as a fascinating technical question in its own right: can one design classical measurement protocols that are publicly verifiable? As we have explained, resolving this question would not only advance the theory of quantum cryptography but would also translate directly to our applications, enabling public verification of QFHE evaluations and, ultimately, classical obfuscation of quantum circuits.

### <span id="page-4-0"></span>1.2 Results

We summarize our main results in this section.

Theorem 1 (Informal). Assuming the quantum hardness of the Learning With Errors problem, there exists classical obfuscation for (classically-described) pseudo-deterministic quantum circuits in the classical oracle model.

Along the way, as a tool for our obfuscation scheme, we build the first publicly-verifiable QFHE scheme for pseudo-deterministic circuits.

Theorem 2 (Informal). Assuming the quantum hardness of the Learning With Errors problem (resp. plus an appropriate circular-security assumption), there exists a leveled (resp. unleveled) quantum fully-homomorphic encryption in the classical oracle model, with the following properties.

- Classical encryption of classical messages.
- Succinct, classical, and public verification of evaluation for pseudo-deterministic circuits.

Main Technical Contribution. Put plainly, our main technical contribution is the first publicly-verifiable quantum measurement protocol in the classical oracle model. Before our work, a quantum prover could prove the integrity of a measurement (standard-basis or Hadamard) on a quantum state only to a single verifier holding a trapdoor. More formally, we construct the first publicly-verifiable Pauli functional commitment scheme with classical keys. As a central part of our construction and security proof, we construct one-shot signatures (and hash functions) with what we call delayed collapsing. Delayed collapsing was implicitly introduced in [\[BKNY23\]](#page-52-5), in the context of random coset states (and we intuitively explain how it relates to Pauli functional commitments, in Section [2.1\)](#page-6-1). We identify delayed collapsing as a fundamental property for hash functions; a delayed collapsing hash implies both collapsing hash functions and non-collapsing collisionresistant hash functions. We prove that the one-shot signatures construction of [\[SZ25\]](#page-54-4) induces not only a non-collapsing collision-resistant hash function (which was previously known), but a delayed collapsing hash function. To prove our results, we show a new stronger analysis of one-shot signatures, and prove new quantum information theoretical properties of random coset states, which may be of independent interest. See more details in our Technical Overview (Section [2\)](#page-6-0).

As an additional contribution that may be of independent interest, we show that post-quantum succinct ideal obfuscation for classical Turing machines exists in the classical oracle model (Section [5\)](#page-33-0). We do this via

a natural "FHE + SNARK" approach, and combine techniques from [\[CMS19,](#page-52-8) [Zha19a\]](#page-54-5) in order to establish post-quantum security.

Finally, we note that our quantum obfuscation scheme is in fact succinct, meaning that the size of the obfuscated program grows only with the description size of the plaintext quantum program, as opposed to the number of physical gates in the circuit. We emphasize that, even though our construction includes a classical oracle, the classical circuit implemented by the oracle is itself succinct. This means that succinctness is preserved when the classical oracle is heuristically instantiated with post-quantum indistinguishability obfuscation.

As an immediate corollary, we obtain the first succinct randomized encoding scheme for pseudo-deterministic quantum computation. A succinct randomized encoding allows for compiling a circuit Q and an input x into a succinct representation <sup>Q</sup>[(x) in time much less than evaluating <sup>Q</sup>, such that <sup>Q</sup>[(x) reveals only <sup>Q</sup>(x) and nothing else about x. Succinct randomized encodings have had several important applications in classical cryptography [\[BGL](#page-52-9)<sup>+</sup>15], and we expect them to be widely useful in the quantum setting as well.

### <span id="page-5-0"></span>1.3 Discussion

Comparison with prior work on CVQC. Our publicly-verifiable QFHE protocol naturally yields a new type of classical verification of quantum computation (CVQC) protocol for BQP. Indeed, given some quantum computation Q, the verifier simply encrypts their input x as Enc(x), and the prover responds with Enc(Q(x)) along with a proof π of correct evaluation.

This protocol has several desirable properties: The prover remains blind to the verifier's input x, the proof π is publicly-verifiable (implying for example that the prover cannot break security by repeatedly querying the verifier and learning whether they accept or reject), and the client's work does not grow with the size of Q. In particular, we obtain the first CVQC protocol that is both blind and publicly-verifiable, and on top of that it is both non-interactive and succinct. We summarize a comparison with prior work in Table [1.](#page-6-2)

Open problems. Our work highlights the utility of classical-client publicly-verifiable QFHE, both for its applications in CVQC and in obfuscation of quantum computation. While we construct this primitive in a manner that supports pseudo-deterministic evaluation, we leave open some interesting questions for future work.

- Can we construct classical-client publicly-verifiable QFHE for all possible functionalities (i.e. quantum circuits that yield an arbitrary distribution over classical outputs)? This would be interesting to achieve even with non-negligible soundness (achieving negligible soundness for sampling problems is a well-known and difficult open problem), and even without compactness.
- Can we prove security of classical-client publicly-verifiable QFHE from post-quantum indistinguishability obfuscation? Unfortunately, it still remains open to establish the security of publicly-verifiable CVQC under iO even forgetting about blindness and compactness.

Another natural direction is to expand the class of quantum programs that can be classically obfuscated. While our approach builds on [\[BKNY23\]](#page-52-5), an alternative approach of [\[BBV24,](#page-51-2) [HT25\]](#page-53-4) has yielded obfuscation for all unitaries, but with a quantum obfuscator. The reason that we build on [\[BKNY23\]](#page-52-5) as opposed to [\[BBV24,](#page-51-2) [HT25\]](#page-53-4) is that the quantum component of the [\[BKNY23\]](#page-52-5) obfuscated program contains "only" coset states, while the quantum component of the [\[BBV24,](#page-51-2) [HT25\]](#page-53-4) obfuscated programs contain more complicated states, in particular magic states encoded in the so-called coset state authentication code [\[BBV24\]](#page-51-2). It is a fascinating open question whether this approach can also be de-quantized, which may yield classical obfuscation of unitaries and beyond.

### <span id="page-6-0"></span>2 Technical Overview

As mentioned above, the main technical workhorse behind our results can be seen as a *publicly-verifiable* measurement protocol with a classical verifier. To this end, we construct, relative to a classical oracle, *publicly-verifiable* Pauli functional commitments with classical commitment keys. We next recall what PFCs are, discuss previous work, and then present our new techniques.

### <span id="page-6-1"></span>2.1 Publicly-Verifiable PFCs and Previous Work

A publicly-verifiable PFC with classical keys (Definition 5) is given by algorithms (Gen, Com, OpenZ, OpenZ, DecZ, DecX), where the classical algorithms here are Gen, DecZ, DecX. Our standard scenario is a classical verifier Ver that wants to make a Pauli measurement on some unknown n-qubit state  $|\psi\rangle$  held by a quantum prover P. In this instance, the flow of a PFC goes as follows.

- 1. Ver samples classical keys  $(CK, dk) \leftarrow Gen(1^{\lambda})$ . We think of CK as a public commitment key in the form of an ideally-obfuscated classical circuit, and dk is a secret decoding key. Ver sends CK to the prover.
- <span id="page-6-3"></span>2. P commits to its state by computing  $(c, \mathcal{B}, \mathcal{U}) \leftarrow \mathsf{Com}^{\mathsf{CK}}(|\psi\rangle_{\mathcal{B}})$ , where c is a classical commitment string,  $\mathcal{B} := (\mathcal{B}_i)_{i \in [n]}$  is the original n-qubit register that held  $|\psi\rangle$  and  $\mathcal{U} := (\mathcal{U}_i)_{i \in [n]}$  is an additional quantum register with some poly(n)-qubits, now entangled with  $\mathcal{B}$ . P sends c to the verifier.
- 3. Ver chooses some measurement basis  $h \in \{0,1\}^n$ , where  $h_i = 0$  denotes measuring qubit i of  $|\psi\rangle$  in the standard basis and  $h_i = 1$  denotes Hadamard basis. Ver sends h to the prover.
- <span id="page-6-4"></span>4. P decommits to the Pauli observables of its state by applying  $u_i \leftarrow \mathsf{OpenZ}(\mathcal{B}_i, \mathcal{U}_i)$  for every  $h_i = 0$  and  $u_i \leftarrow \mathsf{OpenX}(\mathcal{B}_i, \mathcal{U}_i)$  otherwise. P sends  $u := (u_i)_{i \in [n]}$  to the verifier.
- 5. Ver can now privately decode the measurement outcomes by applying the corresponding decoding procedures  $DecZ(dk,\cdot,\cdot)$  and  $DecX(dk,\cdot,\cdot)$ . For each  $i \in [n]$ , the corresponding algorithm takes as input the commitment c and the value  $u_i$ , and outputs a measurement result  $b_i \in \{0,1\}$ .

In words, what happens above is that at Step 2, the prover generates an *encoding* of the state  $|\psi\rangle_{\mathcal{B}}$ , by embedding it into a larger space  $(\mathcal{B}, \mathcal{U})$ . Then, at Step 4, the prover generates encodings of standard basis measurements  $u_i \leftarrow \mathsf{OpenZ}(\mathcal{B}_i, \mathcal{U}_i)$  or Hadamard basis measurements  $u_i \leftarrow \mathsf{OpenZ}(\mathcal{B}_i, \mathcal{U}_i)$ , for each  $i \in [n]$ . The logical values of these encoded measurements are later extracted by Ver using dk.

{7}------------------------------------------------

**PFC Security: Private vs Public Verification.** Intuitively, security of a PFC ensures that the logical standard basis measurements cannot be swapped. A bit more formally, for each part  $i \in [n]$  of the committed state on register  $(\mathcal{B}_i, \mathcal{U}_i)$  at the end of Step 2, if we project the state onto encodings of standard basis measurements for some fixed value  $b \in \{0, 1\}$  (i.e., strings  $u_i$  such that  $\mathsf{DecZ}(\mathsf{dk}, c, u_i) = b$ ), then no adversary can map this state to encodings of standard basis measurements for value 1 - b (i.e., strings  $u_i$  such that  $\mathsf{DecZ}(\mathsf{dk}, c, u_i) = 1 - b$ ). In fact, this is just one way to describe the traditional notion of post-quantum binding for classical messages (equivalent to collapse-binding and sum-binding).

In a privately-verifiable PFC, the verifier's decoding key dk must remain completely inaccessible to the prover throughout the protocol, and such a PFC was (implicitly) constructed in [Mah18]. In a publicly-verifiable scheme, as considered by [BKNY23], we aim to give the prover access to the verifier's decoding functionalities without compromising the binding security stated above. A natural attempt at such a definition would require the scheme to remain secure against a prover given quantum oracle access to the classical functions  $DecZ(dk,\cdot,\cdot)$ ,  $DecX(dk,\cdot,\cdot)$ .

It turns out that, for our application (as well as for [BKNY23]), the security of publicly-verifiable PFC need not hold<sup>4</sup> if the prover is given uninterrupted access to both  $DecZ(dk,\cdot,\cdot)$ ,  $DecX(dk,\cdot,\cdot)$ . Instead, we consider the relaxation where the prover P is given quantum access to  $DecZ(dk,\cdot,\cdot)$ ,  $DecX(dk,\cdot,\cdot)$  only up to the collapse of the encoding register  $(\mathcal{B},\mathcal{U})$ . That is, we consider a game where a malicious prover P\* prepares the commitment  $(c,\mathcal{B},\mathcal{U}) \leftarrow Com^{CK}(|\psi\rangle_{\mathcal{B}})$  as in Step 2, with additional access to both  $DecZ(dk,\cdot,\cdot)$ ,  $DecX(dk,\cdot,\cdot)$ . Then, the PFC challenger "removes" access to the Hadamard decoding functionality  $DecX(dk,\cdot,\cdot)$ , and collapses registers  $(\mathcal{B}_i,\mathcal{U}_i)_{i\in[n]}$  to encodings of classical measurement results  $\{b_i\}_{i\in[n]}$ . Then, given access to only  $DecZ(dk,\cdot,\cdot)$ , the prover P\* is required to map these encodings to encodings of classical measurement results for the opposing values  $\{1-b_i\}_{i\in[n]}$ . To re-iterate, the prover has full access to the verification functionalities while preparing its commtiment, but loses access to the Hadamard decoding functionality while attempting to map between standard basis openings. To satisfy the above notion of binding it is sufficient for our scheme to formally satisfy a property called single-bit binding with public decodability, which is made formal in Definition 7.

**Public Verification via Delayed Collapsing.** The only previous result on publicly-verifiable PFCs is due to the work of Bartusek, Kitagawa, Nishimaki and Yamakawa [BKNY23], which achieves the above (in a classical oracle model), though where the commitment key  $|CK\rangle$  is quantum, and has size proportional to the number of qubits n in the state  $|\psi\rangle$  to be committed.

Without going too deep into their construction, we remark that it makes use of a common tool in quantum cryptography: quantum coset states [CLLZ21]. A quantum coset state is defined by a random subspace  $S \subset \mathbb{Z}_2^{\lambda}$  and shift  $v \in \mathbb{Z}_2^{\lambda}$ , and is defined as

$$|S+v\rangle := \frac{1}{\sqrt{|S|}} \sum_{x \in S} |x+v\rangle$$
.

As a major part of their security proof, [BKNY23] prove a delicate information-theoretic statement about coset states, which we refer to as the **delayed collapsing** property in this work. In a nutshell, they consider a classical oracle  $\mathcal{O}$  such that quantum access to  $\mathcal{O}$  enables implementing the projector onto the coset state, but when access to  $\mathcal{O}$  is revoked, the coset state becomes indistinguishable from the coset state measured in the standard basis.

To elaborate, the ability to verify cosets is given by quantum oracle access to the classical membership check for the sets S+v and  $S^{\perp}$ , where  $S^{\perp}\subseteq\mathbb{Z}_2^{\lambda}$  is the dual subspace of S (i.e., vectors that have an inner product of 0 with all vectors inside S). It is a seminal result [AC12] and common knowledge by now that quantum access to S+v and  $S^{\perp}$  enable implementation of the projector  $|S+v\rangle\langle S+v|$ , while still preserving unclonability of the state  $|S+v\rangle$ . However, the delayed collapsing property required by [BKNY23] is stronger than unclonability of  $|S+v\rangle$ .

<span id="page-7-0"></span><sup>&</sup>lt;sup>4</sup>In fact, there is good reason to believe that this definition is not satisfiable at all, since a Hadamard basis decoding functionality intuitively gives the committer the ability to perform a projection in the Hadamard basis and thus change their value in the standard basis.

This delayed collapsing property is established in [BKNY23, Claim 4.10],<sup>5</sup> by building on the innerproduct adversary method of [AC12]. To be precise, the statement is as follows. A quantum adversary, given  $|S+v\rangle$  and quantum oracle access to both S+v,  $S^{\perp}$ , outputs some state on registers  $\mathcal{R}_1$ ,  $\mathcal{R}_2$ . The first part  $\mathcal{R}_1$  holds a superposition of elements in the coset S+v. The second part  $\mathcal{R}_2$  is possibly entangled with the first part  $\mathcal{R}_1$ , and holds arbitrary quantum information that was learned from the oracle access to S+v and  $S^{\perp}$ . Then, the challenger "takes away" oracle access to the membership check  $S^{\perp}$ , flips a coin  $b \leftarrow \{0,1\}$ , and either measures  $\mathcal{R}_1$  or not depending on the bit b. The task is for the adversary to now guess b. Note that this would have been easy given access to  $S^{\perp}$ . That is, during the first stage, the coset state is publicly-verifiable and non-collapsing, and during the second stage, access to  $S^{\perp}$  is revoked and the state becomes indistinguishable from collapsed.

Circling back to our publicly-verifiable PFCs, our desired security notion (again, formalized in Definition 7) is reminiscent of the delayed collapsing property of coset states. Observe that the binding of PFCs can be interpreted as "revocable equivocality" as follows: The adversary has quantum access to DecZ (dk,  $\cdot$ ,  $\cdot$ ), DecX (dk,  $\cdot$ ,  $\cdot$ ) and makes a commitment. Having access to both oracles (and in particular the Hadamard decoding oracle DecX (dk,  $\cdot$ ,  $\cdot$ )), it may be possible to equivocate from a state that has a superposition of encodings of measurement result  $b \in \{0,1\}$ , to encodings of the opposite measurement result 1-b. However, the security definition ensures that once access to DecX (dk,  $\cdot$ ,  $\cdot$ ) is removed, equivocation becomes computationally intractable.

Indeed, the above connection between delayed collapsing and public verification of PFCs was observed and formalized in [BKNY23]. However, as mentioned, the previous work deals only with quantum (and long) keys. In our work we seek to construct a scheme with classical (and potentially short) keys, and to this end we develop new techniques.

### <span id="page-8-0"></span>2.2 Our Work: One-Shot Signatures with Delayed Collapsing

Our work begins with the natural idea to employ one-shot signatures (OSS) in order to generate the coset states required by  $|CK\rangle$ . OSS is a powerful primitive in quantum cryptography, first defined in [AGKZ20] and recently constructed for the first time in [SZ25]. The OSS of [SZ25] is defined with respect to public parameters that enable anyone to sample their own unclonable coset states, and to publicly verify these states. That is, the sampled coset states cannot be cloned even by the sampler that produced them. Moreover, the public parameters can be used to sample an unbounded polynomial number of such unclonable states.

From Quantum to Classical Keys (and, from long to short keys). In principle, the use of OSS may eliminate two unwanted properties of the [BKNY23] PFC in a single swipe: OSS can enable commitment keys that are both classical and short. However, it turns out that the previous results on OSS are insufficient for our needs.

The OSS of [SZ25] is given by three classical oracles  $(P, P^{-1}, D)$ . In our context here, P,  $P^{-1}$  can be thought of as the oracles that allow for generation of coset states, and D is the oracle that allows for verification of the quantum tokens. In particular, D can be used to detect collapsing and distinguish between a uniform superposition over the coset and a collapsed classical state. [SZ25] prove that the oracles  $(P, P^{-1}, D)$  allow to generate coset states that are unclonable, and are verifiable (in particular, non-collapsing) given the oracle D. For our PFCs, we will want the stronger, delayed collapsing property: We will further ask that once we revoke access to D, the generated states become indistinguishable from collapsed. More formally, we consider a game in which an adversary, given full access to  $(P, P^{-1}, D)$ , first samples a quantum coset state (and potentially some side information). Then, a challenger either measures the state or not in the standard basis, and eliminates access to the D oracle. Then, the adversary is required to tell which of the options occurred (this is formalized in Definition 10).

Reducing OSS with delayed collapsing to a coset collapsing game. This reduction captures the heart of our technical contribution, as it proves the delayed collapsing property of  $\overline{OSS}$ . This is done in three main steps: (1) We propose a novel information-theoretic property of coset states, which we formalize as the coset collapsing game. (2) We perform a careful analysis of  $\overline{OSS}$ , which deviates from the previous security analysis of  $\overline{SZ25}$  and ultimately reduces the delayed collapsing property of  $\overline{OSS}$  to the hardness of the coset collapsing game. (3) We prove the information-theoretic hardness of the coset collapsing game.

The coset collapsing game (formalized in Definition 11) is a game between a challenger and a two-stage adversary  $(A_1, A_2)$ . There is a public subspace S known to all parties and the game executes as follows:

- The challenger samples a random subspace  $T \subset S$ , and grants  $\mathcal{A}_1$  access to  $O_{T^{\perp}}$ , the membership checking oracle for  $T^{\perp}$ , which is a random superspace of  $S^{\perp}$ .
- $\mathcal{A}_1^{O_{T^{\perp}}}$  generates a state on register  $\mathcal{R}$ , to which the challenger applies a projective measurement onto a coset of T in S. That is, the left-over state will be in *some* superposition over *some* coset parallel to T (i.e., a set of the form T + x for some x).
- With equal probability, the challenger either
  - Does nothing further to  $\mathcal{R}$ , or
  - Measures  $\mathcal{R}$  in the standard basis.
- Finally  $A_2$  is given  $\mathcal{R}$  but no access to  $O_{T^{\perp}}$ .

We say that "cosets of T are collapsing" if no  $(A_1, A_2)$  has non-negligible advantage in guessing whether the final measurement was performed or not. In the proof of Theorem 6, we show that any adversary for the delayed collapsing of  $\overline{\text{OSS}}$  can be turned into an adversary for the coset collapsing game. As mentioned above, this proof follows the security analysis of [SZ25] for some of the steps, but deviates in others. For example, while [SZ25] work towards a reduction to the collision-resistance of some "coset partition function", we reduce to the (non-delayed) collapsing property of the same affine partition function.

We will now give a proof sketch of the hybrids used in the reduction from the delayed collapsing property of  $\overline{\sf OSS}$  to the hardness of the coset collapsing game.

Step 1: Bloating the dual. Like [SZ25], we first bloat the dual oracle D and move from  $(P, P^{-1}, D)$  (see Definition 3) to  $(P, P^{-1}, D')$ , where D' checks membership for a random superspaces  $T_y^{\perp}$  of  $S_y^{\perp}$ . A query bounded adversary will not notice the difference, since points in  $T_y^{\perp} \setminus S_y^{\perp}$  are hard to find. Concretely, we bloat the dual oracle as follows

$$D'(y, \mathbf{v}) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } \mathbf{v}^{\top} \mathbf{A}_y^{(1)} = \mathbf{0}^{n-r-s} \\ 0 & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases}$$

Here  $\mathbf{A}_y^{(1)}$  refers to only the last n-r-s columns of the matrix  $\mathbf{A}_y$ , which defines  $S_y^{\perp}$ . Identically to [SZ25], the superspaces  $T_y^{\perp}$  are chosen such that they are sparse subsets of the entire space, and each  $S_y^{\perp}$  is a sparse subset of  $T_y^{\perp}$ .

By the previous step, the adversary cannot tell the difference. Once we have eliminated the dual oracles, the adversary effectively has access only to  $\tilde{P}, \tilde{P}^{-1}$  of these smaller instances, which we are now free to simulate using a collapsing coset-partition function Q.

Concretely, by combining Lemmas 31 and 34 of [SZ25], we simulate the oracles  $P, P^{-1}, D'^{-1}$  using a coset partition function  $Q: \{0,1\}^{r+s} \to \{0,1\}^s$  (see Definition 4) as follows:

Step 3: Utilizing the collapsing of coset partition function. In this part of the hybrid argument, we utilize the fact that our coset partition function Q is a collapsing hash function, which essentially means that, for any superposition of inputs (corresponding to a particular output) to Q that the adversary can produce, it is computationally infeasible to tell whether a challenger measured the superposition in the computational basis, or didn't measure the superposition at all. Now more concretely, we show that, for a fixed y,  $\mathcal{A}_1^Q$  produces the following state on registers  $(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{R})$ ,

$$\sum_{\overline{\Gamma(x)}:Q(\overline{\Gamma(x)})=y,\widetilde{\Gamma(x)}} \mathbf{C}_y \left[ \overline{\Gamma(x)} \right] + \mathbf{d}_y$$

Since Q is collapsing, this state is indistinguishable from the state

$$\sum_{\widetilde{\Gamma(x)}} \mathbf{C}_y \left[ \frac{\overline{\Gamma(x)}}{\widetilde{\Gamma(x)}} \right] + \mathbf{d}_y$$

where  $\overline{\Gamma(x)}$  is some fixed value such that  $Q(\overline{\Gamma(x)}) = y$ .

Step 4: Unsimulating the bloated dual. Since Q is a (r+s,r,s) coset partition function, the preimage set  $Q^{-1}(y)$  has size  $2^s$  and is a coset of a linear space of dimension s. Using this fact, crucially, we observe that collapsing the preimages of Q essentially projects the  $\mathcal{U}$  register to a superposition over a coset of  $\operatorname{colspan}(\mathbf{A}_y^{(1)})$  in  $\operatorname{colspan}(\mathbf{A}_y)$ . Therefore, using knowledge of  $\mathbf{A}_y$ , the reduction can discard Q, and actively collapse to cosets of  $\operatorname{colspan}(\mathbf{A}_y^{(1)})$ . Thus, through a series of hybrids, we show that the state output by  $\mathcal{A}_1$  is a superposition over a coset of a specific subspace of  $\operatorname{colspan}(\mathbf{A}_y)$ , namely  $\operatorname{colspan}(\mathbf{A}_y^{(1)})$ . This is the coset collapsing game with  $T = \operatorname{colspan}(\mathbf{A}_y^{(1)})$ , and  $S = \operatorname{colspan}(\mathbf{A}_y)$ . Now, the final few hybrids of our reduction serve to randomize T, at which point the game is exactly reduced to the coset collapsing game.

Information-theoretic hardness of the coset collapsing game. It remains to show hardness of the coset collapsing game. Intuitively, since T is a random subspace of S chosen by the challenger, it should be hard for any adversary to distinguish whether or not a measurement on a coset of T in S was performed, after access to  $T^{\perp}$  has been revoked. This is established in three steps.

- Un-bloat the dual from  $T^{\perp}$  to  $S^{\perp}$ . It is standard practice that access to  $O_{T^{\perp}}$  for a random superspace  $T^{\perp}$  can be shown to be indistinguishable from access to  $O_{S^{\perp}}$ . However, this only holds if  $T^{\perp}$  is random and independent of the rest of the experiment. In our case, note that the challenger also depends on T to perform its intermediate coset measurement. Our saving grace is that we only need to argue about indistinguishability from  $O_{S^{\perp}}$  before the challenger's measurement, since the adversary's access to this oracle is revoked afterwards. In fact, this is the **only** place in our proof where we make use of the crucial fact that the adversary's access to the dual oracle is revoked after the challenge phase.
- Since S is public, access to  $O_{S^{\perp}}$  gives no additional information to the adversary, so we can ignore it.
- Re-viewing our game in this modified setting, the adversary's success in this game is exactly the diamond distance between the standard basis measurement channel and the coset-measurement channel over a random choice of subspace. This is something that can be directly analyzed, and we formally show this diamond distance to be exponentially small in Lemma 5.

#### <span id="page-12-0"></span>2.3 From PFCs to QFHE and Obfuscation

Once we are equipped with publicly-verifiable PFCs with classical keys, our main results follow naturally from the outline proposed in [BKNY23], with one notable exception. In particular, we use PFCs to upgrade a privately-verifiable QFHE scheme satisfying certain properties to a publicly-verifiable scheme. This compiler is presented in Section 6. Then, given a publicly-verifiable QFHE (in the classical oracle model) we use a straightforward "verify-then-decrypt" construction to obtain classical obfuscation for pseudo-deterministic quantum circuits in the classical oracle model (Section 7).

The notable departure from [BKNY23] mentioned above is necessitated by our requirement of succinctness. While neither the privately-verifiable QFHE scheme we start with, nor the PFC-based compiler are themselves succinct, we show how to restore succinctness with a generic approach based on (post-quantum) succinct ideal obfuscation of classical circuits, in the classical oracle model. More concretely, given a Turing machine P (which may take in a long input, but has a short output), a post-quantum succinct ideal obfuscator outputs a circuit  $\tilde{P}$  of size bounded by |P|, which is the size of the P's code, as opposed it its run-time. We provide the first construction of this object by combining (classical) FHE and post-quantum non-interactive succinct arguments of knowledge in the quantum random oracle model [CMS19]. The proof establishing ideal obfuscation of our construction requires a careful application of techniques from [CMS19], which we formalize in Section 5.

We describe some high level ideas below:

**Succint Ideal Obfuscation** Classically, an input succinct obfuscator can be constructed through fully homomorphic encryption (FHE) and SNARKs (succinct non-interactive arguments of knowledge) as follows:

the obfuscated circuit P˜ consists of the FHE encryption of P, FHE.Enc(P), along with an oracle Osk, which has the FHE encryption secret key sk hardcoded. # <span id="page-13-0"></span>3 Preliminaries

### <span id="page-13-1"></span>3.1 Notation

Definition 1 (Pseudo-deterministic quantum circuit). A family of psuedo-deterministic quantum circuits {Qλ}λ∈<sup>N</sup> is defined as follows. The circuit Q<sup>λ</sup> takes as input a classical string x ∈ {0, 1} <sup>n</sup>(λ) and outputs a bit b ← Qλ(x). The circuit is pseudo-deterministic if for every sequence of classical inputs {xλ}λ∈<sup>N</sup>, there exists a sequence of outputs {bλ}λ∈<sup>N</sup> such that

$$\Pr[Q_{\lambda}(x_{\lambda}) \to b_{\lambda}] = 1 - \mathsf{negl}(\lambda).$$

We will often leave the dependence on λ implicit, and just refer to pseudo-deterministic circuits Q with input x. In a slight abuse of notation, we will denote by Q(x) the bit b such that Pr[Q(x) → b] = 1 − negl(λ).

Sometimes, we will consider (potentially succinct) descriptions of pseudo-deterministic circuits, which we write as PQ, and refer to as (pseudo-deterministic) programs. That is, while Q is understood to consist of the list of gates in the circuit, P<sup>Q</sup> is understood to be an arbitrary (potentially short) description from which the entire list of gates Q can be derived. Thus, in general it may be the case that |PQ| ≪ |Q|.

•  $Dec(sk, ct) \rightarrow x$ : The classical deterministic decryption algorithm takes as input the secret key sk and a ciphertext ct and outputs a plaintext x.

It should satisfy the following properties.

• Correctness. For any  $(pk, sk) \in Gen(1^{\lambda}, D)$ ,  $ct \in Enc(pk, x)$  and circuits C of depth at most D

$$\mathsf{P}\left[ \mathsf{Dec}(\mathsf{sk},\mathsf{ct}') = C(x) : \begin{array}{c} (\mathsf{pk},\mathsf{sk}) \leftarrow \mathsf{Gen}(1^\lambda) \\ \mathsf{ct} \leftarrow \mathsf{Enc}(\mathsf{pk},x) \\ \mathsf{ct}' \leftarrow \mathsf{Eval}(\mathsf{ct},C) \end{array} \right] = 1 - \mathsf{negl}(\lambda).$$

• **Privacy.** For any QPT adversary  $A = \{A_{\lambda}\}_{{\lambda} \in \mathbb{N}}$ , polynomial D and messages  $x_0, x_1$ ,

$$\begin{split} \left| \Pr \left[ \mathcal{A}(\mathsf{pk},\mathsf{ct}) = 1 : (\mathsf{pk},\mathsf{sk}) \leftarrow \begin{array}{c} (\mathsf{pk},\mathsf{sk}) \leftarrow \mathsf{Gen}(1^{\lambda},D) \\ \mathsf{ct} \leftarrow \mathsf{Enc}(\mathsf{pk},x_0) \end{array} \right] \right. \\ - \Pr \left[ \mathcal{A}(\mathsf{pk},\mathsf{ct}) = 1 : (\mathsf{pk},\mathsf{sk}) \leftarrow \begin{array}{c} (\mathsf{pk},\mathsf{sk}) \leftarrow \mathsf{Gen}(1^{\lambda},D) \\ \mathsf{ct} \leftarrow \mathsf{Enc}(\mathsf{pk},x_1) \end{array} \right] \Big| = \mathsf{negl}(\lambda). \end{split}$$

- Compactness. There exists a fixed polynomial p such that any classical circuit C of depth at most D and it holds that
  - $-|pk|, |ct|, |ct'| \le p(\lambda, D, |x|)$  and
  - the runtimes  $Gen(1^{\lambda}, D, |x|)$ , Enc(pk, x), Dec(sk, ct') are bounded by  $p(\lambda, D, |x|)$ .

### <span id="page-14-0"></span>3.3 One-shot signatures (OSS)

<span id="page-14-1"></span>**Definition 3** (One-Shot Signature Scheme). A one-shot signature (OSS) scheme is a tuple of algorithms OSS = (Setup, Gen, Sign, Ver) satisfying the following:

- Setup( $1^{\lambda}$ )  $\rightarrow$  O: A classical probabilistic polynomial-time algorithm that given the security parameter  $1^{\lambda}$ , outputs a classical deterministic circuit O.
- $\mathsf{Gen}^\mathsf{O}(\ell) \to (\mathsf{vk}, |\mathsf{sk}\rangle)$ : A quantum polynomial-time algorithm that takes as input a message length  $\ell$ , has oracle access to  $\mathsf{O}$ , and samples a classical public key  $\mathsf{vk}$  and quantum secret key  $|\mathsf{sk}\rangle$ .
- $\mathsf{Sign}^\mathsf{O}(|\mathsf{sk}\rangle, m) \to \sigma$ : A quantum polynomial-time algorithm that has oracle access to  $\mathsf{O}$ , and, given the quantum key  $|\mathsf{sk}\rangle$  and message  $m \in \{0,1\}^\ell$  produces a classical signature  $\sigma$ .
- $Ver^{O}(vk, m, \sigma) \to \{\bot, \top\}$ : A classical deterministic polynomial-time algorithm that has oracle access to O and verifies a message m and signature  $\sigma$  relative to a public key vk.

**Correctness:** For any  $\ell$  and  $m \in \{0,1\}^{\ell}$ ,

$$\Pr_{\begin{subarray}{c} \mathsf{O} \leftarrow \mathsf{Setup}(1^\lambda, 1^\ell) \\ (\mathsf{vk}, | \mathsf{sk} \rangle) \leftarrow \mathsf{Gen}^\mathsf{O} \\ \sigma \leftarrow \mathsf{Sign}^\mathsf{O}(|\mathsf{sk}\rangle, m) \end{subarray}} \left[ \mathsf{Ver}^\mathsf{O}(\mathsf{vk}, m, \sigma) = \top \right] \geq 1 - \mathsf{negl}(\lambda).$$

**Security (Strong unforgeability):** For any  $\ell$  and QPT algorithm  $\mathcal{A}$ ,

$$\Pr_{\substack{\mathsf{O} \leftarrow \mathsf{Setup}(1^\lambda, 1^\ell) \\ (\mathsf{vk}, m_0, m_1, \sigma_0, \sigma_1) \leftarrow \mathcal{A}^\mathsf{O}}} [\mathsf{Ver}^\mathsf{O}(\mathsf{vk}, m_0, \sigma_0) = \top \wedge \mathsf{Ver}^\mathsf{O}(\mathsf{vk}, m_1, \sigma_1) = \top \wedge \sigma_0 \neq \sigma_1] \leq \mathsf{negl}(\lambda).$$

Note that an adversary breaks the strong unforgeability security guarantee even if it produces two different signatures  $\sigma_0 \neq \sigma_1$  of the same message  $m_0 = m_1$ .

We now describe the construction of one-shot signatures in [SZ25]. We will later use this construction in a non-black-box way in Section 4.2.

<span id="page-16-3"></span>**Theorem 3** (Measure and re-program [DFMS19, DFM20]<sup>13</sup>). Let A, B be finite non-empty sets, and let  $q \in \mathbb{N}$ . Let A be an oracle-aided quantum circuit that makes q queries to a uniformly random function  $H:A\to B$  and then outputs classical strings (a,z) where  $a\in A$ . There exists a two-stage quantum circuit Sim[A] such that for any predicate V, it holds that

$$\Pr\left[ V(a,b,z) = 1: \begin{array}{c} (a,\mathsf{state}) \leftarrow \mathsf{Sim}[\mathcal{A}] \\ b \leftarrow B \\ z \leftarrow \mathsf{Sim}[\mathcal{A}](b,\mathsf{state}) \end{array} \right] \geq \frac{\Pr\left[ V(a,H(a),z) = 1: (a,z) \leftarrow \mathcal{A}^H \right]}{(2q+1)^2}.$$

Moreover, Sim[A] operates as follows.

- Sample  $H: A \to B$  as a 2q-wise independent function and  $(i, d) \leftarrow (\{0, \dots, q-1\} \times \{0, 1\}) \cup \{(q, 0)\}$ .
- Run A until it has made i oracle queries, answering each query using H.
- When A is about to make its (i + 1)'th oracle query, measure its query registers in the standard basis to obtain a. In the special case that (i,d) = (q,0), the simulator measures (part of) the final output register of A to obtain a.
- The simulator receives  $b \leftarrow B$ .
- If d=0, answer  $\mathcal{A}$ 's (i+1)'th query using H, and if d=1, answer  $\mathcal{A}$ 's (i+1)'th query using  $H[a \to b]$ , which is the function H except that H(a) is re-programmed to b.
- Run A until it has made all q oracle queries. For queries i+2 through q, answer using  $H[a \rightarrow b]$ .
- Measure A's output z.

Note that the running time of Sim[A] is at most poly(q, log |A|, log |B|) times the running time of A.

$$\begin{split} \|\Phi_{1} - \Phi_{2}\|_{\diamond} &= \|\Phi_{1}'' - \Phi_{2}''\|_{\diamond} \\ &= \max_{\rho_{\mathcal{A},\mathcal{B}}} \|(\Phi_{1}'' \otimes I)(\rho_{\mathcal{A},\mathcal{B}}) - (\Phi_{2}'' \otimes I)(\rho_{\mathcal{A},\mathcal{B}})\|_{1} \\ &\leq \|(I \otimes \Phi_{1}) |\mathsf{EPR}\rangle\!\langle \mathsf{EPR}| - (I \otimes \Phi_{2}) |\mathsf{EPR}\rangle\!\langle \mathsf{EPR}| \|_{1}. \end{split}$$

For channels  $\Phi$  of the form in the proof of Claim 14,

$$\begin{split} I \otimes \Phi(|\mathsf{EPR}\rangle\!\langle\mathsf{EPR}|) &= \sum_{i} \sum_{x,x'} p_i(I \otimes \Pi_i) \, |x,x\rangle\!\langle x',x'| \, (I \otimes \Pi_i) \\ &= \sum_{i} \sum_{x,x' \in S_i} p_i \, |x,x\rangle\!\langle x',x'| \\ &= \mathsf{CNOT}(\Phi(|+\rangle\!\langle +|^\otimes) \otimes |0^n\rangle\!\langle 0^n|). \end{split}$$

Since the map  $\mathsf{CNOT}(\cdot \otimes |0^n\rangle\langle 0^n|)$  preserves the trace distance, we have that

$$\|(I \otimes \Phi_1) |\mathsf{EPR}\rangle \langle \mathsf{EPR}| - (I \otimes \Phi_2) |\mathsf{EPR}\rangle \langle \mathsf{EPR}| \|_1 = \|\Phi_1(|+\rangle \langle +|^{\otimes n}) - \Phi_2(|+\rangle \langle +|^{\otimes n})\|_1,$$

and Lemma 6 completes the proof.

#### <span id="page-32-0"></span>Lemma 6.

$$\left\|\Phi_1\left(\left|+\right\rangle\!\!\left\langle+\right|^{\otimes n}\right) - \Phi_2\left(\left|+\right\rangle\!\!\left\langle+\right|^{\otimes n}\right)\right\|_1 \le \frac{2^k}{2^n}.$$

*Proof.* For a fixed subspace T of  $\dim(T) = k$ ,

$$\sum_{u \in \mathsf{co}(T)} \Pi_u |+\rangle \langle +|^{\otimes n} \Pi_u = \sum_{u \in \mathsf{co}(T)} \left( \sum_{t \in T} |t + u\rangle \langle t + u| \right) \left( \frac{1}{2^n} \sum_{x, x' \in \mathbb{F}_2^n} |x\rangle \langle x'| \right) \left( \sum_{t' \in T} |t' + u\rangle \langle t' + u| \right)$$

$$= \frac{1}{2^n} \sum_{u \in \mathsf{co}(T)} \sum_{t, t' \in T} \sum_{x, x'} |t + u\rangle \langle t + u| x\rangle \langle x'| t' + u\rangle \langle t' + u|$$

$$= \frac{1}{2^n} \sum_{u \in \mathsf{co}(T)} \sum_{t, t' \in T} |t + u\rangle \langle t' + u|.$$

Grouping the cosets of T together, this is a block diagonal matrix containing  $2^{n-k}$  blocks, where each block is  $\frac{1}{2^n}\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^{\top}2^k\times 2^k$ , where  $\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^{\top}$  is the all ones matrix.

Average over all possible subspaces T, we can argue that by symmetry, every diagonal entry must be the same value, say  $\alpha$ , and every off-diagonal entry must also be the same value  $\beta$ 

$$\Phi_1\left(|+\rangle\langle+|^{\otimes n}\right) = \sum_x \alpha |x\rangle\langle x| + \sum_{x \neq x'} \beta |x\rangle\langle x'|.$$

By an averaging argument, we get that  $\alpha = \frac{1}{2^n}$  and  $\beta = \frac{2^k - 1}{2^{2n} - 2^n}$ .

Channel  $\Phi_2$  is simply the full standard basis measurement, so

$$\Phi_2\left(|+\rangle\langle+|^{\otimes n}\right) = \frac{1}{2^n} \sum_{x \in \mathbb{F}_2^n} |x\rangle\langle x|.$$

Since  $\mathbf{1}_d \mathbf{1}_d^{\top} - I_d$  has eigenvalues  $(0, -1, -1, \dots, -1)$ , we know that  $\|\mathbf{1}_d \mathbf{1}_d^{\top} - I_d\|_1 = d - 1$ , and

$$\left\| \Phi_1 \left( |+\rangle \langle +|^{\otimes n} \right) - \Phi_2 \left( |+\rangle \langle +|^{\otimes n} \right) \right\|_1 = \beta \cdot (2^n - 1) = \frac{2^k - 1}{2^n}.$$

## <span id="page-33-0"></span>5 Post-quantum Succinct Ideal Obfuscation

<span id="page-33-2"></span>Definition 13 (Post-quantum succinct ideal obfuscation). A (post-quantum) succinct ideal obfuscator in the oracle model consists of the following algorithms.

- Obf(1<sup>λ</sup> , P, T) → (O[P], pp): The PPT obfuscator takes as input the security parameter 1 λ , the description of a classical Turing machine P with single-bit output, and a time bound T = poly(λ), and outputs the description of a circuit O[P] and public parameters pp.
- EvalO[<sup>P</sup> ] (pp, x) → y: The PPT evaluation algorithm has oracle access to O[P], takes an input x, along with public parameters pp, and produces an output y.

It should satisfy the following properties.

- Correctness. For any 1 λ , P, T, x, and (O[P], pp) ∈ Obf(1<sup>λ</sup> , P, T), it holds that EvalO[<sup>P</sup> ] (pp, x) = P<sup>T</sup> (x), where P<sup>T</sup> (x) is the bit on the output tape of P after T steps.
- Succinctness. There exists a fixed polynomial p such that for any program P, time bound T, and (O[P], pp) ← Obf(1<sup>λ</sup> , P, T), it holds that |(O[P], pp)| = p(λ, |P|).
- Ideal obfuscation. There exists a QPT simulator S such that for any QPT adversary A, program P, time bound T, and auxiliary information z,

$$\left\{\mathcal{A}^{O[P]}(\mathsf{pp},z):O[P],\mathsf{pp}\leftarrow\mathsf{Obf}(1^\lambda,P,T)\right\}\approx\left\{\mathcal{S}^{P_T}(z)\right\}.$$

Classically, the above follows from FHE and SNARKs in the oracle model in a straightforward manner. In this section, we prove that the same construction is also post-quantum secure. Before describing the construction, we will introduce some relevant prelimnaries.

### <span id="page-33-1"></span>5.1 SNARKs

Definition 14. A succinct non-interactive argument of knowledge (SNARK) for NP in the quantum random oracle model (QROM) consists of the following algorithms. Let H : {0, 1} <sup>2</sup><sup>λ</sup> → {0, 1} λ be a fixed-size random oracle.

- ProveH(1<sup>λ</sup> , C, x) → (z, π): The p.p.t. prove algorithm takes as input the security parameter 1 λ , the description of a classical deterministic computation C, and an input x, and produces an output z and a proof π.
- VerH(1<sup>λ</sup> , C, z, π) → {⊤, ⊥}: The deterministic classical verification algorithm takes as input the security parameter 1 λ , the description of a classical deterministic computation C, an output z, and a proof π, and either accepts or rejects.

It should satisfy the following properties.

• Completeness. For any λ, C, x, z such that C(x) = z,

$$\Pr_{H}\left[\mathsf{Ver}^{H}(1^{\lambda},C,z,\pi)=\top:(z,\pi)\leftarrow\mathsf{Prove}^{H}(1^{\lambda},C,x)\right]=1.$$

- Succinctness. There exists a fixed polynomial p such that for any time T computation C, the running time of Ver<sup>H</sup>(1<sup>λ</sup> , C, z, π) is p(λ, log T, |C|, |z|). Note that |x| and the time T might be much larger than p(λ, log T, |C|, |z|).
- Argument of Knowledge. The argument (Prove, Ver) is an argument of knowledge with extraction probability κ if there exists a polynomial time quantum extractor E such that, for every input x and t query quantum oracle algorithm P˜, if, over a random oracle H, for π := P˜<sup>H</sup>, it holds that Ver<sup>H</sup>(z, π) = 1 with probability µ, the probability that E P˜ (1<sup>t</sup> , 1 λ ) outputs a valid witness for π is at least κ(t, µ, λ). Here the notaion E P˜ denotes that E has black box access to P˜.

### <span id="page-34-0"></span>5.2 Probabilistically Checkable Proofs (PCPs)

Definition 15. A probabilistically checkable proof (PCP) for a relation R with soundness error ε, proof length ℓ and alphabet Σ is a pair of poly-time algorithms (P, V) for which the following holds:

- Completeness: For every instance-witness pair (x, w) ∈ R, P(x, w) outputs a proof string Π : [ℓ] → Σ such that Pr[VΠ(x) = 1] = 1.
- Soundness: For every instance x /∈ L(R) and proof string Π : [ℓ] → Σ, Pr[VΠ(x) = 1] ≤ ε.
- Proof of Knowledge: The PCP (P, V) has knowledge error k if there exists a polynomial time extractor E such that for every witness x and proof string Π : [ℓ] → Σ, if Pr[VΠ(x) = 1] > k, then E(x, Π) outputs a valid witness w.

### <span id="page-34-1"></span>5.3 The construction of Micali

Let (P,V) be the corresponding PCP with prover P and verifier V. Before describing the construction formally, we will define some notation relevant to Merkle trees. Given a domain Z and ℓ = 2<sup>d</sup> , a Merkle tree on a list v = (vi)i∈<sup>ℓ</sup> ∈ Z <sup>ℓ</sup> with respect to the function H : Z×Z → Z is a list of values (zk,i)k∈{0,...d},i∈[2k] ∈ Z 2ℓ , where

$$\forall i \in \ell, z_{d,i} = v_i$$

$$\forall k \in \{0, \dots d-1\}, \forall i \in [2^k], z_{k,i} = H(z_{k+1,2i-1}, z_{k+1,2i})$$

The root of the merkle tree, denoted rt is z0,1. Given i ∈ [ℓ], where [ℓ] = 2<sup>d</sup> , and d is the depth of the tree, the authentication path for the i th leaf is

$$ap = (a_k)_{k \in d},$$

where a<sup>k</sup> is the sibling of the k th node on the path from v<sup>i</sup> (the i th node) to rt. To check whether an authentication path is valid, we define

$$\operatorname{CheckPath}^{H}(\mathsf{rt}, i, v, ap) = 1 \text{ iff } (\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) \leftarrow \operatorname{Expand}^{\mathbf{H}}(\mathsf{rt}, \mathbf{i}, \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{ap}) \text{ has } \mathbf{y_0} = \mathsf{rt},$$

where ExpandH(rt, i, v, ap) outputs all the input-output pairs, (x, y), that arise when validating an authentication path.

Extracting Trees: We will recall the algorithm Extract that is used to obtain a Merkle tree rooted at a chosen entry in the database corresponding to the compresssed oracle for H. This algorithm is used in [\[CMS19\]](#page-52-8). In more detail, Extract receives a database D : Z × Z → Z, a root value rt ∈ Z, and a height bound d, and outputs a rooteed binary tree T = (V, E) of depth at most d where V ⊆ Z × {0, 1} ≤d .

Extract(D,rt, d) :

- 1. Initialize the vertex set with the root V := {(rt, ∅)} and the edge set to be empty E := ∅.
- 2. While there exists an unmarked vertex (u, s) ∈ V such that u ∈ Im(D) and s ∈ {0, 1} <d:
  - Mark the vertex (u, s).
  - If ∃x ̸= x ′ : D(x) = D(x ′ ) = u, return ⊥.
  - Let x0, x<sup>1</sup> be the unique values such that D(x0, x1) = u.
  - Update the vertex set V := V ∪ {(x0, s||0),(x1, s||1)}.
  - Update the edge set E := E ∪ {((u, s),(x0, s||0)),((u, s),(x1, s||1))}.

Given a tree T := Extract(D,rt, d)we can define a string leaves(T) where for each i ∈ {0, 1} d , if there exists a vertex (x, i) ∈ T then leaves(T)<sup>i</sup> := x, else leaves(T)<sup>i</sup> := ⊥.

- SNARK.Prove(x, w) → π :
  - Prover P uses H to commit to the proof string Π ← P(x, w) via a Merkle tree, obtaining a corresponding root rt.

$$\operatorname{CheckPath}^{H}(\mathsf{rt}, i, v, ap) = 1$$

- Let r = H(rt).
- Run V(x, r):
  - ∗ For every query j<sup>i</sup> ∈ [ℓ], where j<sup>i</sup> is the is the location of the i th query made by V, V runs CheckPathH(rt, ap<sup>i</sup> , v, j), and returns v<sup>i</sup> if CheckPathH(rt, ap<sup>i</sup> , v, j) = 1.

### <span id="page-40-0"></span>6.1 Definition

<span id="page-40-1"></span>**Definition 16** (Publicly-verifiable QFHE). A leveled publicly-verifiable quantum fully-homomorphic encryption scheme for pseudo-deterministic circuits consists of the following algorithms (Gen, Enc, Eval, Ver, Dec).

- $Gen(1^{\lambda}, D, N, S) \rightarrow (pk, sk, PP)$ : The key generation algorithm is a PPT algorithm that takes as input the security parameter  $1^{\lambda}$ , an upper bound on circuit depth D, an upper bound on input length N, and an upper bound on circuit description size S, and returns a public key pk, a secret key sk, and the description of a classical deterministic circuit PP.
- $Enc(pk, x) \rightarrow ct$ : The encryption algorithm is a PPT algorithm that takes as input the public key pk and a classical plaintext x, and outputs a ciphertext ct.
- Eval<sup>PP</sup>(ct, Q)  $\rightarrow$  (ct,  $\pi$ ): The evaluation algorithm is a QPT algorithm that has oracle access to PP, takes as input a ciphertext ct, a quantum circuit Q, and outputs an evaluated ciphertext ct and proof  $\pi$ .
- Ver<sup>PP</sup>(ct,  $P_Q$ ,  $\widetilde{\mathsf{ct}}$ ,  $\pi$ )  $\to$  { $\top$ ,  $\bot$ }: The verification algorithm is a PPT algorithm that has oracle access to PP, takes an input ciphertext ct, a quantum program  $P_Q$  (i.e. a potentially succinct description of the quantum circuit Q), an output ciphertext  $\widetilde{\mathsf{ct}}$  and a proof  $\pi$ , and accepts ( $\top$ ) or rejects ( $\bot$ ).
- $Dec(sk, ct) \rightarrow x$ : The decryption algorithm is a PPT algorithm that takes as input the secret key sk and a classical ciphertext ct, and outputs plaintext x.

These algorithms should satisfy the following properties. Recall that pseudo-deterministic circuits Q are defined to have a single bit of output, without loss of generality.

• Correctness. For any polynomials  $D = D(\lambda)$ ,  $N = N(\lambda)$ ,  $S = S(\lambda)$ , any pseudo-deterministic program  $P_Q$  where Q has circuit depth at most D and  $|P_Q| \leq S$ , and any input x with  $|x| \leq N$ ,

$$\Pr\left[\begin{array}{c} \mathsf{Ver}^\mathsf{PP}(\mathsf{ct}, P_Q, \widetilde{\mathsf{ct}}, \pi) = \top \ \land \\ \mathsf{Dec}(\mathsf{sk}, \widetilde{\mathsf{ct}}) = Q(x) \end{array} \right. : \begin{array}{c} (\mathsf{pk}, \mathsf{sk}, \mathsf{PP}) \leftarrow \mathsf{Gen}(1^\lambda, D, N, S) \\ \mathsf{ct} \leftarrow \mathsf{Enc}(\mathsf{pk}, x) \\ (\widetilde{\mathsf{ct}}, \pi) \leftarrow \mathsf{Eval}^\mathsf{PP}(\mathsf{ct}, Q) \end{array}\right] = 1 - \mathsf{negl}(\lambda).$$

• **Privacy.** For any QPT adversary  $A = \{A_{\lambda}\}_{{\lambda} \in \mathbb{N}}$ , polynomials  $D = D({\lambda}), N = N({\lambda}), S = S({\lambda})$ , and messages  $x_0, x_1$ ,

$$\begin{split} \left| \Pr \left[ \mathcal{A}^{\mathsf{PP}}(\mathsf{pk},\mathsf{ct}) = 1 : \begin{array}{c} (\mathsf{pk},\mathsf{sk},\mathsf{PP}) \leftarrow \mathsf{Gen}(1^{\lambda},D,N,S) \\ \mathsf{ct} \leftarrow \mathsf{Enc}(\mathsf{pk},x_0) \end{array} \right] \\ - \Pr \left[ \mathcal{A}^{\mathsf{PP}}(\mathsf{pk},\mathsf{ct}) = 1 : \begin{array}{c} (\mathsf{pk},\mathsf{sk},\mathsf{PP}) \leftarrow \mathsf{Gen}(1^{\lambda},D,S) \\ \mathsf{ct} \leftarrow \mathsf{Enc}(\mathsf{pk},x_1) \end{array} \right] \right| = \mathsf{negl}(\lambda). \end{split}$$

• Soundness. For any QPT adversary  $A = \{A_{\lambda}\}_{{\lambda} \in \mathbb{N}}$ , polynomials  $D = D({\lambda}), N = N({\lambda}), S = S({\lambda})$ , pseudo-deterministic program  $P_Q$  where Q has circuit depth at most D and  $|P_Q| \leq S$ , and input x with  $|x| \leq N$ ,

$$\Pr\left[\begin{array}{c} \mathsf{Ver}^\mathsf{PP}(\mathsf{ct}, P_Q, \widetilde{\mathsf{ct}}, \pi) = \top \ \land \\ \mathsf{Dec}(\mathsf{sk}, \widetilde{\mathsf{ct}}) \neq Q(x) \end{array} \right. : \begin{array}{c} (\mathsf{pk}, \mathsf{sk}, \mathsf{PP}) \leftarrow \mathsf{Gen}(1^\lambda, D, N, S) \\ \mathsf{ct} \leftarrow \mathsf{Enc}(\mathsf{pk}, x) \\ (\widetilde{\mathsf{ct}}, \pi) \leftarrow \mathcal{A}^\mathsf{PP}(\mathsf{ct}) \end{array} \right] = \mathsf{negl}(\lambda).$$

• Compactness. There exists a fixed polynomial p such that the following holds. Fix any polynomials  $D = D(\lambda), N = N(\lambda), S = S(\lambda),$  any pseudo-deterministic program  $P_Q$  where Q has circuit depth at most D and  $|P_Q| \leq S$ , and any x with  $|x| \leq N$ , and let  $(pk, sk, PP) \leftarrow Gen(1^{\lambda}, D, N, S)$ ,  $ct \leftarrow Enc(pk, x)$ , and  $(\widetilde{ct}, \pi) \leftarrow Eval^{PP}(ct, Q)$ . Then it holds that

{41}------------------------------------------------

- $-|pk|, |sk|, |PP|, |ct|, |\widetilde{ct}|, |\pi| \le p(\lambda, D, N, S),$  and
- the run-times of  $\operatorname{Gen}(1^{\lambda}, D, N, S)$ ,  $\operatorname{Enc}(\operatorname{pk}, x)$ ,  $\operatorname{Ver}^{\operatorname{PP}}(\operatorname{ct}, P_Q, \widetilde{\operatorname{ct}}, \pi)$ , and  $\operatorname{Dec}(\operatorname{sk}, \widetilde{\operatorname{ct}})$  are bounded by  $p(\lambda, D, N, S)$ .

An unleveled publicly-verifiable QFHE scheme is defined exactly as above, except that Gen no longer takes a depth parameter D and all properties are defined without the parameter D.

### <span id="page-41-0"></span>6.2 Upgradable privately-verifiable QFHE

We will construct publicly-verifiable QFHE by *upgrading* a privately-verifiable scheme that satisfies certain properties. These properties are encapsulated in the following definition.

<span id="page-41-2"></span>**Definition 17** (Upgradable privately-verifiable QFHE). A leveled privately-verifiable QFHE scheme that is amenable to being upgraded to public-verifiability via our compiler has the following syntax. Let  $H: \{0,1\}^* \to \{0,1\}$  be a random oracle. Let  $\ell(\lambda,|Q|)$  be the "state size" parameter and  $p(\lambda,N,D)$  be the "compactness" parameter.

- $Gen(1^{\lambda}, D) \to (pk, sk)$ : The PPT key generation algorithm takes as input the security parameter  $1^{\lambda}$  and an upper bound on circuit depth D and outputs a public key pk and secret key sk.
- $Enc(pk, x) \rightarrow ct$ : The PPT encryption algorithm takes as input a public key sk and an input x and outputs a ciphertext ct.
- VerGen( $1^{\lambda}$ , ct, Q)  $\rightarrow$  pp, sp: The PPT verification parameter generation algorithm takes as input the security parameter  $1^{\lambda}$ , a ciphertext ct, and quantum circuit Q, and outputs public parameters pp and secret parameters sp.
- Eval<sup>H</sup>(pp, ct, Q)  $\rightarrow \pi$ : The QPT evaluation algorithm operates as follows.
  - Let  $\ell = \ell(\lambda, |Q|)$ , initialize an  $\ell$ -qubit state  $|\psi_{\mathsf{ct},Q}\rangle$  on register  $\mathcal{M} = (\mathcal{M}_1, \dots, \mathcal{M}_\ell)$ , and initialize two fresh registers  $\mathcal{Y}, \mathcal{Z}$ .
  - Apply a measurement  $M_{pp}$  on  $(\mathcal{M}, \mathcal{Z}, \mathcal{Y})$  that is classically-controlled on  $\mathcal{M}$  to obtain a string y.
  - Compute T = H(y), where T is parsed as a subset  $T \subseteq [\ell]$ . 19
  - Apply a measurement  $M_{pp,y,T}$  on  $(\mathcal{M},\mathcal{Z})$  that is classically-controlled on  $\mathcal{M}$  to obtain a string z.
  - For all  $i \in [\ell]$ , measure  $\mathcal{M}_i$  in the standard basis if  $i \in T$  or in the Hadamard basis if  $i \notin T$  to obtain a string  $m \in \{0,1\}^{\ell}$ .
  - Return  $\pi = (m, y, z)$ .
- $\operatorname{Ver}^H(\operatorname{sp},\operatorname{ct},Q,\pi) \to \{\bot,\widetilde{\operatorname{ct}}\}$ : The PPT verification algorithm takes as input secret parameters  $\operatorname{sp}$ , a ciphertext  $\operatorname{ct}$ , a quantum circuit Q, and a proof  $\pi$ , and outputs either reject  $(\bot)$  or a ciphertext  $\widetilde{\operatorname{ct}}$ .
- $Dec(sk, ct) \rightarrow x$ : The PPT decryption algorithm takes as input the secret key sk and a ciphertext ct and outputs a plaintext x.

It should satisfy the following properties. We leave probabilities taken over the sampling of the random oracle H implicit.

• **Privacy.** For any QPT adversary  $\{A_{\lambda}\}_{\lambda}$ , depth  $D = D(\lambda)$ , and messages  $x_0, x_1$ ,

• Completeness. For any  $(pk, sk) \in Gen(1^{\lambda}, D)$ ,  $ct \in Enc(pk, x)$ , and Q,

$$\Pr\left[\mathsf{Dec}(\mathsf{sk},\mathsf{Ver}^H(\mathsf{sp},\mathsf{ct},Q,\pi)) = Q(x): \begin{array}{c} \mathsf{pp},\mathsf{sp} \leftarrow \mathsf{VerGen}(\mathsf{ct},Q) \\ \pi \leftarrow \mathsf{Eval}^H(\mathsf{pp},\mathsf{ct},Q) \end{array}\right] = 1 - \mathsf{negl}(\lambda).$$

• Soundness. For any  $(pk, sk) \in Gen(1^{\lambda}, D)$ ,  $ct \in Enc(pk, x)$ , Q, and QPT adversary  $\{A_{\lambda}\}_{\lambda}$ ,

$$\Pr \left[ \begin{matrix} \mathsf{c} \mathsf{t} \neq \bot \land \mathsf{Dec}(\mathsf{sk}, \widetilde{\mathsf{ct}}) \neq Q(x) : & \mathsf{pp}, \mathsf{sp} \leftarrow \mathsf{VerGen}(\mathsf{ct}, Q) \\ & \mathsf{c} \mathsf{t} \neq \bot \land \mathsf{Dec}(\mathsf{sk}, \widetilde{\mathsf{ct}}) \neq Q(x) : & \pi \leftarrow \mathcal{A}^H(\mathsf{pp}) \\ & \mathsf{c} \mathsf{t} \leftarrow \mathsf{Ver}^H(\mathsf{sp}, \mathsf{ct}, Q, \pi) \end{matrix} \right] = \mathsf{negl}(\lambda).$$

- Incompatible standard-basis measurements. First, we introduce some notation.
  - Given a subset  $I \subset [\ell]$ , define  $\overline{I} := [\ell] \setminus I$ .
  - Given any  $m \in \{0,1\}^{\ell}$  and a subset  $I \subseteq [\ell]$ , we define  $m_{I \to *} \in \{0,1,*\}^{\ell}$  to be equal to  $m_i$  at indices  $i \in \overline{I}$  and equal to \* at indices  $i \in I$ .
  - Each choice of  $(\cdot, sp) \in VerGen(ct, Q)$  defines<sup>20</sup> a set of "standard basis positions"  $S \subseteq [\ell]$ , which we denote by S = S[sp] (not to be confused with the positions  $T \subseteq [\ell]$  that are measured in the standard basis).
  - We say that two sets of strings  $M_0, M_1 \in \{0, 1, *\}^{\ell}$  are incompatible if for any  $m_0 \in M_0, m_1 \in M_1$ , there exists an  $i \in [\ell]$  such that  $m_{0,i}, m_{1,i} \neq *$  and  $m_{0,i} \neq m_{1,i}$ .
  - Each choice of  $(\cdot, sp) \in VerGen(ct, Q)$  defines<sup>21</sup> two incompatible sets of strings  $M_0, M_1 \in \{0, 1, *\}^{\ell}$ .

Now, we need the following properties.

 $-\textit{ For any } (\mathsf{pk},\mathsf{sk}) \in \mathsf{Gen}(1^{\lambda},D), \; \mathsf{ct} \in \mathsf{Enc}(\mathsf{pk},x), \; \textit{Q}, \; (\mathsf{pp},\mathsf{sp}) \in \mathsf{VerGen}(\mathsf{ct},\textit{Q}), \; \textit{and proof} \; \pi = (m,y,z), \; it \; holds \; that$ 

$$\mathsf{Ver}^H(\mathsf{sp},\mathsf{ct},Q,(m,y,z)) = \mathsf{Ver}^H(\mathsf{sp},\mathsf{ct},Q,(m_{S\cap\overline{T}\to *},y,z)).$$

That is, Ver ignores all bits of m that are measured in the Hadamard basis, but correspond to "standard basis positions".

- For any  $(pk, sk) \in Gen(1^{\lambda}, D)$ ,  $ct \in Enc(pk, x)$ , Q, and QPT adversary  $\{A_{\lambda}\}_{\lambda}$ ,

$$\Pr\left[ \widetilde{\mathsf{ct}} \neq \bot \land m_{\overline{S} \cup \overline{T} \to *} \notin M_0 : \begin{array}{c} \mathsf{pp}, \mathsf{sp} \leftarrow \mathsf{VerGen}(\mathsf{ct}, Q) \\ \pi = (m, y, z) \leftarrow \mathcal{A}^H(\mathsf{pp}) \\ T \coloneqq H(y) \\ \widetilde{\mathsf{ct}} \leftarrow \mathsf{Ver}^H(\mathsf{sp}, \mathsf{ct}, Q, \pi) \end{array} \right] = \mathsf{negl}(\lambda).$$

That is, it is hard to find an accepting proof  $\pi$  such that  $m_{\overline{S} \cap \overline{T} \to *}$  (where only the standard basis positions that are measured in the standard basis remain) is in  $M_0$ .

- For any  $(pk, sk) \in Gen(1^{\lambda}, D)$ ,  $ct \in Enc(pk, x)$ , Q, and QPT adversary  $\{A_{\lambda}\}_{\lambda}$ ,

$$\Pr\left[ \begin{aligned} \widetilde{\mathsf{ct}} \neq \bot \land \mathsf{Dec}(\mathsf{sk}, \widetilde{\mathsf{ct}}) \neq Q(x) \land m_{\overline{S} \cup \overline{T} \to *} \notin M_1 : & \begin{aligned} \mathsf{pp}, \mathsf{sp} \leftarrow \mathsf{VerGen}(\mathsf{ct}, Q) \\ \pi = (m, y, z) \leftarrow \mathcal{A}^H(\mathsf{pp}, \mathsf{sp}) \\ T := H(y) \\ \widetilde{\mathsf{ct}} \leftarrow \mathsf{Ver}^H(\mathsf{sp}, \mathsf{ct}, Q, \pi) \end{aligned} \right] = \mathsf{negl}(\lambda).$$

That is, it is hard to find an accepting but faulty proof  $\pi$  such that  $m_{\overline{S} \cap \overline{T} \to *}$  (where only the standard basis positions that are measured in the standard basis remain) is in  $M_1$ , even given the secret parameters sp.

**Theorem 13.** The QFHE protocol given in Figure 3 satisfies correctness (see Definition 16).

*Proof.* This is a straightforward adaptation of the completeness part of [BKNY23, Theorem 5.12], where we use the correctness of OSS (along with the other primitives) rather than the signature token.  $\Box$ 

**Theorem 14.** The QFHE protocol given in Figure 3 satisfies privacy (see Definition 16).

*Proof.* This follows directly from the privacy of the upgradable privately-verifiable QFHE.  $\Box$ 

**Theorem 15.** The QFHE protocol given in Figure 3 satisfies compactness (see Definition 16).

*Proof.* This follows directly from the compactness of the upgradable privately-verifiable QFHE and succinctness of the post-quantum succinct ideal obfuscator. In particular, note that the output size of the program obfuscated by **Gen** is bounded by a fixed polynomial, and so PP consists of a bounded polynomial number of obfuscated programs (each with bounded circuit size).

<span id="page-45-1"></span>**Theorem 16.** The QFHE protocol given in Figure 3 satisfies soundness (see Definition 16).

Proof. We follow the soundness part of the proof of [BKNY23, Theorem 5.12], but with a couple of tweaks. Suppose there exists a program  $P_{Q^*}$ , input x, and adversary  $\mathcal{A}_1^{\mathsf{PP}}$  that violates the soundness of Definition 16, where we have dropped the indexing by  $\lambda$  for convenience. Our first step will be to appeal to the security of the succinct obfuscation (Definition 13) to replace  $\mathcal{A}_1$  with an adversary  $\mathcal{A}_2^{\mathsf{O},\mathsf{CK},\mathsf{PrivGen},\mathsf{H},\mathsf{PrivVer}}$  that has direct access to the large-input functionalities. This only has a negligble affect on the output of  $\mathcal{A}_1$ .

Next, we replace the PRFs  $F_{k_{\text{PFC}}}$ ,  $F_{k_{\text{Priv}}}$ ,  $F_{k_H}$  with random oracles  $H_{\text{PFC}}$ ,  $H_{\text{Priv}}$ , H. Since  $\mathcal{A}_2$  only has polynomially-bounded oracle access to these functionalities, this has a negligible effect on the output of  $\mathcal{A}_2$  [Zha12].  $\mathcal{A}_2$  can then be used to define an oracle algorithm  $\mathcal{A}_3^{H_{\text{Priv}}}$  that operates as follows.

- Sample (pk, sk, O, CK, PrivGen, H, PrivVer) as in  $Gen(1^{\lambda}, N, D, S)$ , except that  $H_{PFC}$  and H are sampled as random oracles.
- Sample  $ct^* \leftarrow Priv.Enc(pk, x)$ .
- Run  $\mathcal{A}_2^{O,CK,PrivGen,H,PrivVer}(ct^*)$ , forwarding calls to  $H_{Priv}$  (which occur as part of calls to PrivGen and PrivVer) to an external random oracle  $H_{Priv}$ .
- Measure  $\mathcal{A}_2$ 's output proof  $\pi^*$ , parse  $\pi^*$  as  $(\mathsf{pk}_{\mathsf{OSS}}^*, c^*, \sigma^*, m^*, y^*, z^*)$  and output  $a \coloneqq (\mathsf{ct}^*, Q^*, \mathsf{pk}_{\mathsf{OSS}}^*, c^*, \sigma^*)$  and  $\mathsf{aux} \coloneqq (m^*, y^*, z^*)$ .

{46}------------------------------------------------

Note that  $A_3$  makes  $p = \text{poly}(\lambda)$  total queries to  $H_{Priv}$ . Now, define V as in Figure 4. Then since  $A_1$  breaks soundness,

$$\Pr\left[V(a, \mathsf{H}_{\mathsf{Priv}}(a), \mathsf{aux}) = 1 : (a, \mathsf{aux}) \leftarrow \mathcal{A}_3^{\mathsf{H}_{\mathsf{Priv}}}\right] = \mathsf{non-negl}(\lambda).$$

Next, since  $p = \text{poly}(\lambda)$ , by Theorem 3 there exists an algorithm  $\mathcal{A}_4 := \mathsf{Sim}[\mathcal{A}_3]$  such that

$$\Pr\left[V((\mathsf{ct}^*, Q^*, \mathsf{pk}_{\mathsf{OSS}}^*, c^*, \sigma^*), s, (m^*, y^*, z^*)) = 1: \begin{array}{c} ((\mathsf{ct}^*, Q^*, \mathsf{pk}_{\mathsf{OSS}}^*, c^*, \sigma^*), \mathsf{state}) \leftarrow \mathcal{A}_4 \\ s \leftarrow \{0, 1\}^{\lambda} \\ (m^*, y^*, z^*) \leftarrow \mathcal{A}_4(s, \mathsf{state}) \end{array}\right] = \mathsf{non-negl}(\lambda).$$

Moreover,  $A_4$  operates as follows.

- Sample  $\mathsf{H}_{\mathsf{Priv}}$  as a 2p-wise independent function and  $(i,d) \leftarrow (\{0,\ldots,p-1\} \times \{0,1\}) \cup \{(p,0)\}.$
- Run  $A_3$  for i oracle queries, answering each query using the function  $H_{Priv}$ .
- When  $\mathcal{A}_3$  is about to make its (i+1)'th oracle query, measure its query register in the standard basis to obtain  $a := (\mathsf{ct}^*, Q^*, \mathsf{pk}_{\mathsf{OSS}}^*, c^*, \sigma^*)$ . In the special case that (i, d) = (p, 0), just measure (part of) the final output register of  $\mathcal{A}_3$  to obtain a.
- $\bullet$  Receive s externally.
- If d = 0, answer  $\mathcal{A}_3$ 's (i + 1)'th query with  $\mathsf{H}_{\mathsf{Priv}}$ . If d = 1, answer  $\mathcal{A}_3$ 's (i + 1)'th query instead with the re-programmed oracle  $\mathsf{H}_{\mathsf{Priv}}[(\mathsf{ct}^*, Q^*, \mathsf{pk}_{\mathsf{OSS}}^*, c^*, \sigma^*) \to s]$ .
- Run  $\mathcal{A}_3$  until it has made all p queries to  $\mathsf{H}_{\mathsf{Priv}}$ . For queries i+2 through p, answer with the reprogrammed oracle  $\mathsf{H}_{\mathsf{Priv}}[(\mathsf{ct}^*, Q^*, \mathsf{pk}^*_{\mathsf{OSS}}, c^*, \sigma^*) \to s]$ .

# <span id="page-48-0"></span>7 (Succinct) Classical Obfuscation of Pseudo-deterministic Quantum Circuits

<span id="page-48-2"></span>**Definition 18** (Classical Ideal obfuscation for Pseudodeterministic Quantum Circuits). A classical ideal obfuscator for a family of pseudo-deterministic quantum circuits is a pair of QPT algorithms (Obf, Eval) with the following syntax.

{49}------------------------------------------------

- Obf(1<sup>λ</sup> , PQ) <sup>→</sup> <sup>P</sup>Qe: Obf is a classical p.p.t. algorithm that takes as input the security parameter <sup>1</sup> λ and the classical description P<sup>Q</sup> of a quantum circuit Q, and outputs the classical description of an obfuscated circuit <sup>P</sup>Qe.
- Eval(PQe, x) <sup>→</sup> <sup>b</sup>: Eval is a (quantum) polynomial-time algorithm that takes as input the description <sup>P</sup>Q<sup>e</sup> of an obfuscated circuit <sup>Q</sup><sup>e</sup> and an input <sup>x</sup>, and outputs a bit <sup>b</sup> ∈ {0, <sup>1</sup>}.

A VBB obfuscator should satisfy the following properties for any pseudo-deterministic family of circuits Q = {Qλ}λ∈<sup>N</sup> with input length n = n(λ).

- Correctness: It holds with probability <sup>1</sup> <sup>−</sup> negl(λ) over <sup>Q</sup><sup>e</sup> <sup>←</sup> Obf(1<sup>λ</sup> , PQ) that for all x ∈ {0, 1} n, Pr[Eval(PQe, x) <sup>→</sup> <sup>Q</sup>(x)] = 1 <sup>−</sup> negl(λ).
- Security: There exists a QPT simulator {Sλ}λ∈N, such that for any QPT adversary {Aλ}λ∈N, pseudodeterministic programs PQ,

$$\left\{ \mathcal{A}_{\lambda} \left( \mathsf{Obf}(1^{\lambda}, P_Q) \right) \right\} \approx_c \mathcal{S}_{\lambda}^{O[Q]}$$

where O[Q] is the oracle that computes the map x → Q(x).

In addition, the ideal obfuscator could optionally satisfy a succinctness criterion.

• Succinctness: The size of the obfuscation <sup>P</sup>Q<sup>e</sup> <sup>←</sup> Obf(1<sup>λ</sup> , PQ) is polynomially bounded by the size of the description P<sup>Q</sup> of the input circuit, rather than the size of the circuit (in terms of number of gates) itself. That is, <sup>|</sup>PQe| ≤ poly(λ, <sup>|</sup>PQ|), rather than <sup>|</sup>PQe| ≤ poly(λ, <sup>|</sup>Q|).

### <span id="page-49-0"></span>7.1 Construction

- A publicly-verifiable QFHE for pseudo-deterministic circuits in the oracle model pvQFHE = (Gen, Enc, Eval, Ver, Dec) (Definition [16\)](#page-40-1).
- A post-quantum input-succinct obfuscator (SObf, SEval), which we constructed in Section [5.](#page-33-0)

Given the above building blocks, we give the following construction of classical obfuscation for pseudodeterministic quantum circuits.

{50}------------------------------------------------

#### <span id="page-50-0"></span>Classical obfuscation of pseudo-deterministic quantum circuits

Let U be the universal quantum circuit that takes as input the description P<sup>Q</sup> of a circuit of input of size N, where N is the length of an input to Q. Let D be the depth of U, and let S be a bound on the size of U. Let U<sup>x</sup> be the quantum circuit that takes as input the description of a circuit P<sup>Q</sup> and runs the universal quantum circuit U on PQ∥x.

- Obf(1<sup>λ</sup> , PQ):
  - Sample (pk,sk, PP) ← pvQFHE.Gen(1<sup>λ</sup> , D, |PQ|, S), and ct ← pvQFHE.Enc(pk, PQ).
  - Let DK(x, ct′ , π) be the following functionality. First, run pvQFHE.VerPP(ct, Ux, ct′ , π). If the output was ⊥, then output ⊥, and otherwise output pvQFHE.Dec(sk, ct′ ).
  - Sample DK<sup>f</sup> <sup>←</sup> SObf(1<sup>λ</sup> , DK).
  - Output <sup>Q</sup><sup>e</sup> := ct, PP, DK<sup>f</sup> .
- Eval(PQe, x):
  - Parse <sup>P</sup>Q<sup>e</sup> as (ct, PP, DK<sup>f</sup> ).
  - Compute (ct′ , π) ← EvalPP(ct, Ux).
  - Output <sup>b</sup> := DK<sup>f</sup> (x, ct′ , π).

Figure 5: Succinct classical obfuscation of pseudo-deterministic quantum circuits

Theorem 17. The construction in Figure [5](#page-50-0) satisfies correctness, security and succinctness definitions in Definition [18.](#page-48-2)

The proofs of correctness and security are almost identical to the proof of Theorem 6.1 of [\[BKNY23\]](#page-52-5).

Proof. First, correctness follows immediately from the correctness of the VBB obfuscator and the correctness of the publicly-verifiable QFHE scheme (Definition [16\)](#page-40-1). Note that even though the evaluation procedure may include measurements, an evaluator could run coherently, measure just the output bit b, and reverse. By the Gentle Measurement lemma, this implies the ability to run the obfuscated program on any poly(λ) number of inputs.

Next, we show security. For any QPT adversary {Aλ}λ∈<sup>N</sup>, we define a simulator {Sλ}λ∈<sup>N</sup> as follows, where {Aeλ}λ∈<sup>N</sup> is the simulator for the classical obfuscation scheme (CObf, CEval), defined based on {Aλ}λ∈<sup>N</sup>.

- Sample (pk,sk, PP) ← pvQFHE.Gen(1<sup>λ</sup> , D, |Q|, S), ct ← pvQFHE.Enc(pk, 0 |Q| ).
- Run <sup>A</sup>ePP,DK λ (ct), answering PP calls honestly, and DK calls as follows.
  - Take (x, ct′ , π) as input.
  - Run VerPP(x, ct′ , π). If the output was ⊥ then output ⊥.
  - Otherwise, forward x to the external oracle O[Q], and return the result b = O[Q](x).
- Output <sup>A</sup>e<sup>λ</sup>'s output.

Now, for any circuit Q, we define a sequence of hybrids.

- Hybrid 0: Sample <sup>Q</sup><sup>e</sup> <sup>←</sup> Obf(1<sup>λ</sup> , Q) and run Aλ(1<sup>λ</sup> , <sup>Q</sup>e).
- Hybrid 1: Sample (ct, PP, DK) as in Obf(1<sup>λ</sup> , Q), and run <sup>A</sup>ePP,DK λ (ct).

{51}------------------------------------------------

- Hybrid 2: Same as hybrid 1, except that calls to DK are answered as in the description of  $\mathcal{S}_{\lambda}$ .
- Hybrid 3: Same as hybrid 2, except that we sample  $\mathsf{ct} \leftarrow \mathsf{Enc}(\mathsf{pk}, 0^{|Q|})$ . This is  $\mathcal{S}_{\lambda}$ .

We complete the proof by showing the following.

- $|\Pr[1 \leftarrow \text{Hybrid } 0] \Pr[1 \leftarrow \text{Hybrid } 1]| = \mathsf{negl}(\lambda)$ . This follows from the security of the classical obfuscation scheme (CObf, CEval).
- $|\Pr[1 \leftarrow \text{Hybrid 1}] \Pr[1 \leftarrow \text{Hybrid 2}]| = \mathsf{negl}(\lambda)$ . Suppose otherwise. Then there must exist some query made by  $\widetilde{\mathcal{A}}_{\lambda}$  to DK with noticeable amplitude on  $(x, \widetilde{\mathsf{ct}}, \pi)$  such that DK does not return  $\bot$  but  $\mathsf{Dec}(\mathsf{sk}, \widetilde{\mathsf{ct}}) \neq Q(x)$ . Thus, we can measure a random one of the  $\mathsf{poly}(\lambda)$  many queries made by  $\widetilde{\mathcal{A}}_{\lambda}$  to obtain such an  $(x, \widetilde{\mathsf{ct}}, \pi)$ , which violates the soundness of the  $\mathsf{publicly}$ -verifiable QFHE scheme (Definition 16).
- $|\Pr[1 \leftarrow \text{Hybrid 2}] \Pr[1 \leftarrow \text{Hybrid 3}]| = \mathsf{negl}(\lambda)$ . Since sk is no longer used in hybrid 2 to respond to DK queries, this follows directly from the security of the publicly-verifiable QFHE scheme (Definition 16).

Finally, the scheme is succinct. This follows from the compactness of the pvQFHE scheme and the input-succinct obfuscation (SObf, SEval) scheme.

### <span id="page-54-1"></span>A.1 Correctness

*Proof of Theorem 4.* This proof is almost identical to the proof of Theorem 4.6 of [BKNY23], but we repeat it here for completeness. The only difference is that we are using one-shot signatures instead of signature tokens; this is what allows us to make the commitment keys entirely classical.

We will assume that the one-shot signature schemes  $OSS, \overline{OSS}$  are perfectly correct. If they are statistically correct, then the Pauli functional commitment scheme we obtain is still correct, because we allow for a negligible statistical distance.

We will first show that the map applied by  $\mathsf{Com}^{\mathsf{CK}}(b)$  in the case that the measurement of the first qubit of  $\mathcal{S}'$  is 1-b successfully takes  $|S_{y,1-b}\rangle \to |S_{y,b}\rangle$ . Since we are assuming perfect correctness from  $\overline{\mathsf{OSS}}$ , it suffices to show that for any balanced affine subspace  $|S_y\rangle$ ,

$$H^{\otimes n}\mathsf{Phase}^{O[S_y^\perp]}H^{\otimes n}\ket{S_{y,1-b}} \to \ket{S_{y,b}},$$

where  $\mathsf{Phase}^{O[S_y^\perp]}$  is the map  $|s\rangle \to (-1)^{O[S_y^\perp](s)} |s\rangle$ , and  $O[S_y^\perp]$  is the oracle that outputs 0 if  $s \in S^\perp$  and 1 if  $s \notin S^\perp$ . This was also shown in [AGKZ20].

Let  $S_y^*$  be the subspace parallel to affine subspace  $S_y$ , and let  $S_{y,b}^*$  be the coset of  $S_y^*$  whose first coordinate is b. We will use the facts that  $S_{y,1}^* = S_{y,0}^* + w$  for some w, and that  $S_{y,0} = S_{y,0}^* + v_0$  and  $S_{y,1} = S_{y,1}^* + v_1$  for some  $v_0, v_1$  such that  $v_0 + v_1 = w$ . Also note that for any  $s \in S_y^{\perp}$ ,  $s \cdot w = 0$ , and for any  $s \in (S_{y,0}^*)^{\perp} \setminus (S_y^*)^{\perp}$ ,  $s \cdot w = 1$ .

$$\begin{split} &H^{\otimes n}\mathsf{Ph}^{O[S_y^\perp]}H^{\otimes n}\left|S_{y,1-b}\right\rangle \\ &=H^{\otimes n}\mathsf{Ph}^{O[S_y^\perp]}H^{\otimes n}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2^{n/2-1}}}\left(\sum_{s\in (S_{y,0}^*)^\perp}(-1)^{s\cdot v_{1-b}}\right) \\ &=H^{\otimes n}\mathsf{Ph}^{O[S_y^\perp]}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2^{n/2+1}}}\left(\sum_{s\in (S_y^*)^\perp}(-1)^{s\cdot w+s\cdot v_b}\left|s\right\rangle +\sum_{s\in (S_{y,0}^*)^\perp\setminus(S_y^*)^\perp}(-1)^{s\cdot w+s\cdot v_b}\left|s\right\rangle \right) \\ &=H^{\otimes n}\mathsf{Ph}^{O[S_y^\perp]}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2^{n/2+1}}}\left(\sum_{s\in (S_y^*)^\perp}(-1)^{s\cdot v_b}\left|s\right\rangle +\sum_{s\in (S_{y,0}^*)^\perp\setminus(S_y^*)^\perp}(-1)^{1+s\cdot v_b}\left|s\right\rangle \right) \\ &=H^{\otimes n}\mathsf{Ph}^{O[S_y^\perp]}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2^{n/2+1}}}\left(\sum_{s\in (S_y^*)^\perp}(-1)^{s\cdot v_b}\left|s\right\rangle +\sum_{s\in (S_{y,0}^*)^\perp\setminus(S_y^*)^\perp}(-1)^{1+s\cdot v_b}\left|s\right\rangle \right) \\ &=H^{\otimes n}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2^{n/2+1}}}\left(\sum_{s\in S^\perp}(-1)^{s\cdot v_b}\left|s\right\rangle +\sum_{s\in (S_{y,0}^*)^\perp\setminus(S_y^*)^\perp}(-1)^{s\cdot v_b}\left|s\right\rangle \right) \\ &=H^{\otimes n}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2^{n/2+1}}}\left(\sum_{s\in (S_{y,0}^*)^\perp}(-1)^{s\cdot v_b}\left|s\right\rangle \right) \\ &=|S_{y,b}\rangle\,. \end{split}$$

Now consider applying  $\mathsf{Com}^\mathsf{CK}$  to a pure state. By the correctness of OSS,  $\mathsf{Com}$  can produce a valid signature  $\sigma_0$  of 0, and is able to access the  $\overline{\mathsf{O}} = (P, P^{-1}, D)$  oracles of OSS through  $\mathsf{CK}_P(\mathsf{vk}, \cdot)$ ,  $\mathsf{CK}_{P^{-1}}(\mathsf{vk}, \cdot, \cdot)$  and  $\mathsf{CK}_D(\mathsf{vk}, \sigma_0)$ . $$|\psi_{\mathsf{Com}}\rangle = \alpha_0 |0\rangle |S_{y,0}\rangle + \alpha_1 |1\rangle |S_{y,1}\rangle,$$

and a signature c on the bit 1.

We claim that measuring and decoding  $|\psi_{\mathsf{Com}}\rangle$  in the standard (resp. Hadamard) basis produces the same distribution as directly measuring  $|\psi\rangle$  in the standard (resp. Hadamard) basis. As a mixed state is a probability distribution over pure states, this will complete the proof of correctness.

First, it is immediate that measuring  $|\psi_{\mathsf{Com}}\rangle$  in the standard basis produces a bit b with probability  $|\alpha_b|^2$  along with a vector s such that  $s \in (S+v)_b$ .

Next, note that applying Hadamard to each qubit of  $|\psi_{\mathsf{Com}}\rangle$  except the first results in the state

$$\alpha_0 |0\rangle \left( \sum_{s \in (S_{y,0}^*)^{\perp}} (-1)^{s \cdot v_0} |s\rangle \right) + \alpha_1 |1\rangle \left( \sum_{s \in (S_{y,0}^*)^{\perp}} (-1)^{s \cdot v_1} |s\rangle \right),$$

and thus, measuring each of these qubits (except the first) in the Hadamard basis produces a vector s and a single-qubit state

$$(-1)^{s \cdot v_0} \alpha_0 |0\rangle + (-1)^{s \cdot v_1} \alpha_1 |1\rangle = \alpha_0 |0\rangle + (-1)^{s \cdot w} \alpha_1 |1\rangle.$$

So, measuring this qubit in the Hadamard basis is equivalent to measuring  $|\psi\rangle$  in the Hadamard basis and masking the result with  $s \cdot w$ . Recalling that  $s \cdot w = 0$  if  $s \in (S_y^*)^{\perp}$  and  $s \cdot w = 1$  if  $s \in (S_{y,0}^*)^{\perp} \setminus (S_y^*)^{\perp}$  completes the proof of correctness, since  $S_y^{\perp} = (S_y^*)^{\perp}$  and  $S_{y,0}^{\perp} = (S_{y,0}^*)^{\perp}$

### <span id="page-56-0"></span>A.2 Binding

<span id="page-56-1"></span>**Lemma 8.** The collapse-binding definition of Definition 9 implies the single-bit binding definition of Definition 7.

*Proof.* We will first define the following notion of binding, and then establish that the collapse-binding definition of Definition 9 implies this collapse binding definition.

<span id="page-56-2"></span>**Definition 19** (Single bit collapse binding). For any adversary  $\mathcal{A} := \{(\mathcal{A}_1, \mathcal{A}_2)_{\lambda}\}_{{\lambda} \in \mathbb{N}}$ , where each of  $\mathcal{A}_1$  and  $\mathcal{A}_2$  are oracle-aided quantum operations that make at most  $\operatorname{poly}({\lambda})$  oracle queries, define the single-bit collapse binding experiment SingleBitCollapseBindingExpt<sup> $\mathcal{A}$ </sup>(1<sup> $\lambda$ </sup>) as follows.

- $Sample (CK, dk) \leftarrow Gen(1^{\lambda}).$
- Run  $\mathcal{A}_1^{\mathsf{CK},\mathsf{DecZ[dk]},\mathsf{DecX[dk]}}(1^{\lambda})$  until it outputs a commitment c and a state on registers  $(\mathcal{B},\mathcal{U},\mathcal{R})$ . Here  $\mathcal{B}$  is a single-qubit register,  $\mathcal{U}$  is the opening register and  $\mathcal{R}$  holds the internal state of  $\mathcal{A}$ .
- Sample  $b \leftarrow \{0,1\}$ . If b = 0, do nothing, and if b = 1 measure  $(\mathcal{B}, \mathcal{U})$  with  $\{\Pi_{\mathsf{dk},c,0}, \Pi_{\mathsf{dk},c,1}\}$
- $Run \ \mathcal{A}_2^{\mathsf{CK},\mathsf{DecZ[dk]}}(\mathcal{B},\mathcal{U},\mathcal{R}) \ until \ it \ outputs \ a \ bit \ b'.$
- The experiment outputs 1 if b = b'.

We say that adversary  $\mathcal{A}$  is valid if the state on  $(\mathcal{B},\mathcal{U})$  output by  $\mathcal{A}_1$  is in the image of  $|0\rangle\langle 0| \otimes \Pi_{\mathsf{dk},c,0} + |1\rangle\langle 1| \otimes \Pi_{\mathsf{dk},c,1}$ . Then, we say that a Pauli functional commitment (Gen, Com, OpenZ, OpenZ, DecZ) satisfies publicly-decodable single bit collapse-binding if it holds that for all valid adversaries  $\mathcal{A}$ ,

$$\left|\Pr\left[\mathsf{SingleBitCollapseBindingExpt}^{\mathcal{A}}(1^{\lambda}) = 1\right] - \frac{1}{2}\right| = \mathsf{negl}(\lambda).$$
