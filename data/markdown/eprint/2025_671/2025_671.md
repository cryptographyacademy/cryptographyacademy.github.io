# Multi-signature in Fully Split Ring and Quantum Random Oracle Model

## **Abstract**

Multi-signature schemes are crucial for secure operations in digital wallets and escrow services within smart contract platforms, particularly in the emerging post-quantum era. Existing post-quantum multi-signature constructions either do not address the stringent requirements of the Quantum Random Oracle Model (QROM) or fail to achieve practical efficiency due to suboptimal parameter choices.

In this paper, we present a novel multi-signature scheme designed to be secure in the QROM and optimized for practical use. Our scheme operates over the polynomial ring  $\mathbb{Z}_q[X]/(x^n+1)$  with  $q \equiv 1 \pmod{2n}$ , enabling full splitting of the ring and allowing for efficient polynomial arithmetic via the Number Theoretic Transform (NTT). This structure not only ensures post-quantum security but also bridges the gap between theoretical constructs and real-world implementation needs.

We further propose a new hardness assumption, which is termed  $\nu$ -SelfTargetMSIS, extending SelfTargetMSIS (Eurocrypt 2018) to accommodate multiple challenge targets. We prove its security in the QROM and leverage it to construct a secure and efficient multisignature scheme. Our approach avoids the limitations of previous techniques, reduces security loss in the reduction, and results in a more compact and practical scheme suitable for deployment in post-quantum cryptographic systems.

## **Keywords**

Multi-signature, NTT, Quantum Random Oracle Model

Quantum computers have not only the ability to attack traditional cryptosystems, but also the power to query a function with multiple inputs at *a single query*. Currently, most signature schemes rely on a hash function to turn message(s) into challenge(s), and thus sign them. However, many of them use the random oracle model (ROM) to simulate the output of a hash function in their security proofs (e.g., [15]). When we consider the case of a quantum adversary, it always makes a query in superposition, which makes it hard for the simulator to find out what preimage the adversary is interested in. More threateningly, the adversary could make an attack on superposition that comprises different messages in one move. This uncertainty of such powerful adversaries calls eagerly for the security reductions over the quantum random oracle model (QROM) [4].

*QROM-based Multisignatures.* In this paper, we consider the case of *multi-signature* - an aggregation of signatures to show that multiple users agree on the same message. It is widely used in cryptocurrency and blockchain technologies. For the case of a digital wallet for cryptocurrency, a wallet could require multi-signatures (from any two public keys out to three) to authorize spending. So even if one secret key is stolen, the attacker cannot access the funds. It is also useful for escrow services in a smart contract. With a 2-out-of-3 multi-signature with a buyer, seller, and a neutral arbitrator, funds are only released when two of the three agree, avoiding the need to trust a single party.

Currently, there already exist some multi-signature solutions based on lattice-based hard problems [5, 7, 9, 12, 16], but few have the support for the quantum random oracle model [12, 16]. Such a need brings us to QROM-based multi-signatures.

Towards practical Multisignatures. Dilithium [11] is the most famous post-quantum secure signature scheme constructed via the "Fiat-Shamir with aborts" framework [18]. It is computed in a polynomial ring  $\mathcal{R}_q := \mathbb{Z}_q[X]/(X^n+1)$ , where q is a prime modulus. A QROM-based Dilithium was proposed in [15]. However, this QROM-based Dilithium faces a critical threat as pointed out by [6].

For the current QROM-based multi-signature relying on Dilithium [12], the chosen parameters are good for invertibility and make the security proof easy. More specifically, it sets the prime number by  $q \equiv 5 \pmod{8}$ . The choice of this parameter started from [20], with the underlying polynomial ring being  $\mathbb{Z}_q[X]/(X^n+1)$  and n being a power of two. Moreover, the invertibility comes from the isomorphism

$$\mathbb{Z}_q[X]/(X^n+1) \cong \mathbb{Z}_q[X]/(X^{n/2}-r) \times \mathbb{Z}_q[X]/(X^{n/2}+r)$$

where the  $\mathbb{Z}_q[X]/(X^{n/2} \pm r)$  are fields because both of them are unsplittable. However, this structure is not good for multiplication implementation. A naive polynomial multiplication is  $O(n^2)$ . It

Practical Dilithium would always choose a faster ring with  $q \equiv 1 \pmod{2n}$  as in [3]. The ring is fully split as  $\mathbb{Z}_q[X]/(X^n+1) \cong \mathbb{Z}_q^{\times n}$ , and NTT could make good use of this isomorphism [3]. The hardness of this scheme is based on the hardness of three computational problems: Module Learning with Errors (MLWE), Module Short Integer Solution (MSIS), and SelfTargetMSIS. # 1.1 Related works

**Researches on QROM.** There have already been some researches on QROM in the past decade. Following up with [23], lots of researchers agree that it is almost impossible to rewind the quantum machine, compared to the classical random oracle model, and it is inefficient to prove 2-soundness and similar properties.

After Zhandry [24] remarked that QROM could be easily simulated by 2q-wise independent functions, there are a lot of trials presenting more reliable properties of QROM and building schemes based on those properties. For example, it is shown in [22] how to adaptively reprogram a hash function. But still, the simulation, extraction, and rewinding are considered to be hard because of the superposition of quantum queries [17].

In 2019, Zhandry [25] proposed the compressed oracle model. This model uses lazy sampling and supposes that the hash outcome H(x) of any input x is a uniform superposition like  $\sum |y\rangle$  before taking measurements. If anybody wants to know the result, it must measure this register and further impact the register of a simulator. It gives the simulator more power to control and guess the inputs and outputs of hash queries. In [8], the authors provided an efficient simulation of the compressed oracle.

*QROM-Dilithium and NTT..* The QROM-Dilithium was raised in [15], in which the security could rely on either lossy key generation or SelfTargetMSIS. The lossy key generation relies firmly on  $q \equiv 5 \pmod 8$ , while the SelfTargetMSIS reduction could cater to different needs. However, it only showed that SelfTargetMSIS is secure in the random oracle model in [15]. In 2024, [14] showed a hardness proof on SelfTargetMSIS in the QROM model. Using [10], the authors showed SelfTargetMSIS is at least as hard as MLWE in the given parameter. Furthermore, it demonstrated that using  $q \equiv 1 \pmod{2n}$  with NTT acceleration would accelerate the original QROM-Dilithium several times. This QROM-based Dilithium faces a critical threat as pointed out by [6].

Other QROM Multisignature schemes. To the best of the authors' knowledge, there are two researches on QROM multi-signatures. The first one [12] provides a simple extension of the Dilithium to multi-signature. It uses [22] as the main reprogramming theory. After reprogramming, the authors proved that the schemes' unforgeability under chosen message attack (MS-UF-CMA) is almost equivalent to the unforgeability under no message attack (MS-UF-NMA). And to prove the MS-UF-NMA of the signature scheme,

the authors used the MLWE assumption to make the keys lossy. Then the authors presented a general search problem with bounded model such that no quantum adversary could easily attack the lossy MS-UF-NMA of the scheme.

The second QROM multi-signatures [16] provides a two-round solution on multi-signature, which relies on [22] for online-extractability. But it still uses a ring that does not provide fast NTT acceleration.

## 1.2 Our Contributions

There are two main contributions in our paper. To illustrate our contribution, we define some notations first. Apart from the ring  $\mathcal{R}_q$  with  $q \equiv 1 \pmod{2n}$  and n is a power of two, we also define  $\mathcal{R} := \mathbb{Z}[X]/(X^n+1)$ . For any  $a \in \mathbb{Z}_q$ ,  $a' \mod^{\pm}q$  is defined by the unique  $-(q-1)/2 \leq a' \leq (q-1)/2$  such that  $a' \equiv a \pmod{q}$ . For any  $r = r_0 + r_1 X + \dots + r_{n-1} X^{n-1} \in \mathcal{R}_q$ , we use the notation  $||r||_i := |r_i \mod^{\pm}q|$  and  $||r||_{\infty} := \max_i ||r_i||_i$ . For  $\mathbf{r} = (r_1, \dots, r_m) \in \mathcal{R}_q^m$ , we have  $||\mathbf{r}||_{\infty} := \max_i ||r_i||_{\infty}$ . For  $\tau \in \mathbb{N}$ ,  $B_{\tau}$  denotes the set of all elements in  $r \in \mathcal{R}_q$  with  $||r||_{\infty} = 1$  and the number of  $\pm 1$  coefficients is indeed  $\tau$ .  $I_m$  is an identity matrix of size m.

Contribution 1. . We propose a variant to the SelfTargetMSIS, named  $\nu$ -SelfTargetMSIS, and present a hardness proof for this variant. In the original SelfTargetMSIS, the adversary is given a matrix  $\mathbf{A} \in \mathcal{R}_q^{m \times k}$  with a hash function  $\mathbf{H} : \{0,1\}^* \to B_\tau$ , it needs to find out a message M and corresponding  $\mathbf{y} \in \mathcal{R}^{m+k}$  such that  $\|\mathbf{y}\|_{\infty}$  is small,  $\mathbf{H}(\begin{bmatrix}\mathbf{I}_m & \mathbf{A}\end{bmatrix}\mathbf{y}, M) = \mathbf{y}_{m+k}$ , of which  $\mathbf{y}_{m+k}$  is the (m+k)-th element of  $\mathbf{y}$ .

However, single hash target  $y_{m+k}$  is not suitable for multi-signature. In our variant v-SelfTargetMSIS, the adversary needs to find out a solution for v different targets at the same time. Precisely, it is given  $\mathbf{A} \in \mathcal{R}_q^{m \times k}$  and hash function  $\mathbf{H} : \{0,1\}^* \to B_\tau$ . The adversary needs to find out a solution  $\mathbf{A}' \in \mathcal{R}_q^{m \times (v-1)}$ , M, and  $\mathbf{y} \in \mathcal{R}^{m+k+v-1}$  such that  $\|\mathbf{y}\|_{\infty}$  is small, and

$$\forall i \in [v], H(i, \mathbf{A}', [\mathbf{I}_m \quad \mathbf{A} \quad \mathbf{A}'] \mathbf{y}, M) = \mathbf{y}_{(m+k-1)+i}.$$

Contribution 2. . We propose a multi-signature scheme based on [12]. Their scheme relies on the security of  $q \equiv 5 \pmod 8$  at Lemma 4.6 of [15], and further on Lemma 2.2 of [19]. Although the lemma provides invertibility, it is insufficient for NTT acceleration. We prove that our multi-signature scheme is secure at  $q \equiv 1 \pmod {2n}$ , by using v-SelfTargetMSIS. And instead of using only high bits of  $\mathbf{A}\mathbf{y}$ , we use  $\mathbf{A}\mathbf{y}_1 + \mathbf{y}_2$  to work around the threat posed by [6].

Moreover, [12] uses a lemma for getting reprogrammable from [22]. The lemma in [22] is stateless and simulated by a polynomial. It further implies the underlying hash function H<sub>2</sub> is length-preserving. And it also needs an additional almost compressed oracle to help [17], which brings more losses. To get rid of these limitations, we replace the [22] reprogramming with [13]. This change also reduces MS-UF-NMA to MS-UF-CMA of the multisignature and gets rid of the almost compressed oracle, which gives a better loss.

#### Quantum random oracle model (QROM) 2.1

A state of a qubit  $|\phi\rangle$  could be expressed as a two-dimensional complex vector  $|\phi\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle$  where  $\{|0\rangle, |1\rangle\}$  is an orthogonal basis of  $\mathbb{C}^2$  and  $|\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1$ . The qubit is in superposition when  $0 < |\alpha| < 1$ . Moreover, the state of *n* qubits could be written as  $|\phi\rangle = \sum_{x \in \{0,1\}^n} \alpha_x |x\rangle$  in  $\mathbb{C}^{2^n}$  and  $\sum_x |\alpha_x|^2 = 1$ . When measuring the state in the computational basis, it outputs x with probability  $|\alpha_x|^2$ .

One of the most important observations is that it is able to replace any hash function by a fixed polynomial following the Lemma 2.1.

<span id="page-2-0"></span>LEMMA 2.1 (LEMMA IN [24]). Let  $H: X \to \mathcal{Y}$ . There is no quantum polynomial time (QPT) adversary  $\mathcal{A}^{\mathsf{H}}$  that could distinguish a real hash  $|H\rangle$  and 2Q-wise independent function  $f_{2Q}$  when making at most Q quantum queries to the random oracle. And the  $f_{2Q}$  could be treated as a 2Q degree polynomial in  $\mathbb{F}_{y}$ .

#### 2.2 Lattice

In the original QROM-Dilithium [15], the security level of the Dilithium protocol is given by the advantages of module lattice  $\mathrm{Adv}_{k,l,\eta}^{\mathsf{MLWE}}(\mathcal{B})$ ,  $\mathrm{Adv}_{\mathsf{H},\tau,k,\ell+1,\zeta}^{\mathsf{SelfTargetMSIS}}(C)$ , and  $\mathrm{Adv}_{k,l,\zeta'}^{\mathsf{MSIS}}(\mathcal{D})$ . Thus, we rephrase the definition of these hardness assumptions in the style of [14].

Definition 2.2 (Module Learning with Errors (MLWE)). For  $m, k, \eta \in$  $\mathbb{N}$ , the advantage that any adversary  $\mathcal{A}$  breaking the hard assumption is

$$\mathbf{Adv}_{m,k,\eta}^{\mathsf{MLWE}}(\mathcal{A}) := \|\mathsf{Pr}\left[b = 0\middle|\mathbf{A} \leftarrow \mathcal{R}_q^{m \times k}, t \leftarrow \mathcal{R}_q^m, b \leftarrow \mathcal{A}(\mathbf{A}, t)\right] - \\ \mathsf{Pr}\left[b = 0\middle|\mathbf{A} \leftarrow \mathcal{R}_q^{m \times k}, (\mathbf{s}_1, \mathbf{s}_2) \leftarrow S_\eta^k \times S_\eta^m, t \leftarrow \mathbf{A}\mathbf{s}_1 + \mathbf{s}_2, b \leftarrow \mathcal{A}(\mathbf{A}, t)\right]$$

Definition 2.3 (Module Short Integer Solutions (MSIS)). For  $m, k, \gamma \in$  $\mathbb{N}$ , the advantage that any adversary  $\mathcal{A}$  breaking the MSIS is defined as

$$\begin{split} & Adv_{m,k,\gamma}^{\mathsf{MSIS}}(\mathcal{A}) \\ := \mathsf{Pr}\left[0 < \|\mathbf{y}\|_{\infty} \leq \gamma \wedge [\mathbf{I}_m|\mathbf{A}]\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{0} \middle| \mathbf{A} \leftarrow \mathcal{R}_q^{m \times k}; \mathbf{y} \leftarrow \mathcal{A}(\mathbf{A}) \right]. \end{split}$$

*Definition 2.4 (Self TargetMSIS).* For  $\tau$ , m, k,  $\gamma \in \mathbb{N}$  and  $H : \{0, 1\}^* \to$  $B_{\tau} \subset \mathcal{R}_q$ , the advantage that any adversary  $\mathcal{A}$  breaking the Self-TargetMSIS is defined as

$$\begin{split} & \mathbf{Adv}_{\mathsf{H},\tau,m,k,\gamma}^{\mathsf{SelfTargetMSIS}}(\mathcal{A}) := \\ & \mathsf{Pr} \left[ \begin{matrix} ||\mathbf{y}||_{\infty} \leq \gamma \\ \wedge \ \mathsf{H}([\mathbf{I}_m|\mathbf{A}]\mathbf{y}, M) = c \end{matrix} \middle| \mathbf{A} \leftarrow \mathcal{R}_q^{m \times k}; \left( \mathbf{y} := \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{r} \\ c \end{bmatrix}, M \right) \leftarrow \mathcal{A}^{|\mathsf{H}\rangle}(\mathbf{A}) \right]. \end{split}$$

The adversary has quantum access to the H hash function.

#### 2.3 **Multi-signature**

In multi-signature with the maximum signer size  $\tau$ , every individual participant could generate their own key pair, participate in interactively signing, and make verifications. We use the notation similar to [5], without using key aggregation and online/offline signing.

- pp  $\leftarrow$  Setup(1<sup> $\Lambda$ </sup>) takes a security parameter 1<sup> $\Lambda$ </sup> as input, and outputs public parameters. Throughout, we assume that pp is given as implicit input to all other algorithms.
- (sk, pk) ← KeyGen() generates a secret key sk and a public key pk.
- $\sigma \leftarrow \text{Sign}(\text{sk}, \text{pk} := \{\text{pk}\}, m)$  signs interactively on message m with signers who own the secret keys of **pk** where  $|\mathbf{pk}| \leq \tau$ , and outputs a signature  $\sigma$ .
- $\{0,1\} \leftarrow \text{Verify}(\mathbf{pk}, \sigma, m)$  verifies whether the signature  $\sigma$  is sign by the secret owners of all **pk** on the message *m* and  $|\mathbf{pk}| \leq \tau$ .

Definition 2.5. A multi-signature scheme Scheme is MS-UF-CMA safe if the advantage any adversary  $\mathcal{A}$  wins the given experiment with negligible probability.

$$\mathsf{Adv}_{\mathsf{Scheme}}^{\mathsf{MS-UF-CMA}} \coloneqq \mathsf{Pr}\left[1 \leftarrow \mathsf{Exp}_{\mathsf{Scheme}}^{\mathsf{MS-UF-CMA}}(\mathcal{A}, \lambda)\right] \leq \mathsf{negl}(\lambda),$$

where the experiment  $\mathsf{Exp}^{\mathsf{MS-UF-CMA}}_{\mathsf{Scheme}}(\mathcal{A},\lambda)$  is defined as

- (1)  $pp \leftarrow Setup(1^{\lambda}), (sk^*, pk^*) \leftarrow KeyGen().$
- (2)  $(\mathbf{pk}^*, \sigma^*, m^*) \leftarrow \mathcal{A}^{O_{\text{Sign}}}(\mathbf{pp}, \mathbf{pk}^*).$
- (3) Return Verify $(\mathbf{pk}^*, \sigma^*, m^*) = 1 \land \mathbf{pk}^* \in \mathbf{pk} \land (\mathbf{pk}^*, m^*) \notin Q$ . The oracle  $O_{Sign}(\mathbf{pk}, m)$  aborts if  $\mathbf{pk}^* \notin \mathbf{pk}$ . Otherwise, it sets the queried set  $Q \leftarrow Q \cup (\mathbf{pk}, m)$  and answers all interactive queries by  $Sign(sk^*, pk, m)$ .

Definition 2.6. A multi-signature scheme's MS-UF-NMA is the same as MS-UF-CMA except that no  $O_{Sign}$  query could be made.

### Self TargetMSIS variant on multiple hash 3 target

The reduction from a single target Self Target MSIS to MLWE was given in [14]. The first step is to define a "plain" version of Self TargetMSIS, known as Plain-SelfTargetMSIS, where the input matrix is not given in Hermite Normal Form. Next, it defines two experiments: the chosen coordinate binding experiment CCB and the collapsing experiment Collapse. The chain of reduction works as:

$$\begin{split} \text{SelfTargetMSIS}_{\mathsf{H},\tau,m,l,\gamma} \leftarrow \text{Plain-SelfTargetMSIS}_{\mathsf{H},\tau,m,m+l,\gamma} \\ \leftarrow \text{CCB}_{\tau,m,m+l,\gamma} \leftarrow \text{Collapse}_{m,m+l,\gamma} \leftarrow \text{MLWE}_{m+l,m,\eta}, \end{split}$$

of which the first transition requires  $q \equiv 1 \pmod{2n}$  and the final transition requires q > 16 and  $2\gamma \eta n(m+l) < \lfloor q/32 \rfloor$ .

The above result could not be directly used in a multi-signature scheme, because the original Self Target MSIS and Plain-Self Target MSIS only target one hash result, but multi-signature further requires each party to answer an individual challenge  $c_i$  independently. More importantly, the adversary is able to control what the public keys of the malicious parties are. So, we propose a variant of the original SelfTargetMSIS to a *v*-targets *v*-SelfTargetMSIS.

To prove the hardness of  $\nu$ -SelfTargetMSIS, we have to follow the route of [14], and consequently propose *v*-Plain-SelfTargetMSIS. We can reduce the CCB experiment to our *v*-Plain-SelfTargetMSIS. Hence, we can reuse the transition from CCB, Collapse to MLWE in [14].

Definition 3.1 (v-SelfTargetMSIS). For v as a positive integer,  $m, k, \gamma \in \mathbb{N}$  and  $H : \{0, 1\}^* \to B_{\tau}$ , the advantage that any adversary breaking the  $\nu$ -SelfTargetMSIS is defined as

$$\begin{aligned} & \mathbf{Adv}_{\mathsf{H},\tau,m,k,\gamma}^{\nu\text{-SelfTargetMSIS}}(\mathcal{A}) := \\ & \mathbf{Pr} \begin{bmatrix} \|\mathbf{y}\|_{\infty} \leq \gamma \wedge \|\mathbf{y}'\|_{\infty} \leq \gamma \\ \wedge & \mathsf{H}(i,\mathbf{A}',[\mathbf{I}_m|\mathbf{A}]\mathbf{y} + \mathbf{A}'\mathbf{y}',M) = c_i \\ \wedge & \mathbf{y}_{m+k} = c_1 \wedge \mathbf{y}_i' = c_{i+1} \end{bmatrix} \mathbf{A} \leftarrow \mathcal{R}_q^{m \times k}; \\ & (\mathbf{y},\mathbf{A}',\mathbf{y}',M) \leftarrow \mathcal{A}^{|\mathsf{H}\rangle}(\mathbf{A}) \end{bmatrix}, \end{aligned}$$

in which 
$$i \in [\nu - 1]$$
,  $\mathbf{y} \in \mathcal{R}_q^{m+k}$ ,  $\mathbf{A}' \in \mathcal{R}_q^{m \times (\tau - 1)}$  and  $\mathbf{y}' \in \mathcal{R}_q^{\nu - 1}$ .

Definition 3.2 (v-Plain-Self TargetMSIS). For v as a positive integer,  $m, k, \gamma \in \mathbb{N}$  and  $H : \{0, 1\}^* \to B_{\tau}$ , the advantage that any adversary breaking the *v*-Plain-SelfTargetMSIS is defined as

$$\begin{split} & Adv_{H,\tau,m,k,\gamma}^{\nu\text{-Plain-SelfTargetMSIS}}(\mathcal{A}) := \\ & Pr \begin{bmatrix} \|\mathbf{y}\|_{\infty} \leq \gamma \wedge \|\mathbf{y}'\|_{\infty} \leq \gamma \\ \wedge & H(i,\mathbf{A}',\mathbf{A}\mathbf{y}+\mathbf{A}'\mathbf{y}',M) = c_i \\ \wedge & \mathbf{y}_k = c_1 \wedge \mathbf{y}_i' = c_{i+1} \end{bmatrix} A \leftarrow \mathcal{R}_q^{m \times k}; \\ & (\mathbf{y},\mathbf{A}',\mathbf{y}',M) \leftarrow \mathcal{A}^{|H\rangle}(\mathbf{A}) \end{bmatrix}, \end{split}$$

in which 
$$i \in [\nu - 1]$$

### Reducing multi-target Plain-Self TargetMSIS 3.1 to multi-target Self TargetMSIS

In the security reduction, we assume that the forger has the quantum query to hash functions  $H_1$  and  $H_2$ , while it can only access the signing oracles classically just like the real-world classical communication channel. Without loss of generality, we assume the adversary is going to interactively query with the first party that acted by the simulator, which would involve the  $pk_1$  without knowing  $sk_1$ . And all other parties are controlled by the adversary.

LEMMA 4.1. If the multi-signature scheme QROM-Musig is secure in MS-UF-NMA model, the QROM-Musig is also secure in MS-UF-CMA with the advantage loss  $\frac{3Q_sE}{2}\sqrt{\frac{Q_H+Q_s\,vE}{2^\tau\binom{n}{\tau}}}$ , of which  $Q_s$  is the number of queries on signing,  $Q_H$  is the number of queries on hash, E is the expectation of the repeating time in signing.

Moreover,  $\mathbf{w}_i$  is identical to the original signing by

$$\mathbf{w}_{i} = \mathbf{A}\mathbf{y}_{i}^{(1)} + \mathbf{y}_{i}^{(2)} = \mathbf{A}(\mathbf{z}_{i}^{(1)} - c_{i}\mathbf{s}_{i}^{(1)}) + (\mathbf{z}_{i}^{(2)} - c_{i}\mathbf{s}_{i}^{(2)}) = \mathbf{A}\mathbf{z}_{i}^{(1)} + \mathbf{z}_{i}^{(2)} - c_{i}\mathbf{t}_{i}.$$

In each individual sub-game hopping, there is only one point under  $H_2$  to be reprogrammed, and the total number of hash queries is  $Q_H + Q_s vE$ . The random challenge space is  $B_\tau$  of size  $2^\tau \binom{n}{\tau}$ . As a result, we have

$$\left| \Pr[\mathcal{A}^{\mathsf{Game}_{3,i}} = 1] - \Pr[\mathcal{A}^{\mathsf{Game}_{3,i-1}} = 1] \right| \leq \frac{3}{2} \sqrt{\frac{Q_H + Q_S \nu E}{2^{\tau} \binom{n}{\tau}}}$$

Summing up all sub-games together, the difference turns to

$$\begin{aligned} & \left| \Pr[\mathcal{A}^{\mathsf{Game}_3} = 1] - \Pr[\mathcal{A}^{\mathsf{Game}_2} = 1] \right| \\ &= \sum_{i=1}^{Q_s E} \left| \Pr[\mathcal{A}^{\mathsf{Game}_{3,i}} = 1] - \Pr[\mathcal{A}^{\mathsf{Game}_{3,i-1}} = 1] \right| \\ &\leq \frac{3Q_s E}{2} \sqrt{\frac{Q_H + Q_s \nu E}{2^{\tau} \binom{n}{\tau}}}. \end{aligned}$$

Game<sub>4</sub>: At the final game, we notice that the signing oracle queries could be perfectly simulated without using any private information of the first party. Now consider an algorithm  $\mathcal{B}$  playing the MS-UF-NMA game and receiving (pp, pk\*). Then  $\mathcal{B}$  can run Game<sub>4</sub> with the MS-UF-CMA adversary  $\mathcal{A}$  using (pp, pk\*). If  $\mathcal{A}$  wins by outputting (PK\*,  $\sigma^*$ ,  $m^*$ ), then  $\mathcal{B}$  also wins by outputting

{7}------------------------------------------------

 $(\mathbf{PK}^*, \sigma^*, m^*)$ . Hence we have:

$$\mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{MS}\text{-}\mathsf{UF}\text{-}\mathsf{CMA}}_{\mathsf{QROM}\text{-}\mathsf{Musig}}(\mathcal{A})$$

$$\leq \mathbf{Adv}_{\mathrm{QROM\text{-}Musig}}^{\mathrm{MS\text{-}UF\text{-}NMA}}(\mathcal{B}) + \frac{3Q_{s}E}{2}\sqrt{\frac{Q_{H} + Q_{s}\nu E}{2^{\tau}\binom{n}{\tau}}} + \mathrm{negl}(\lambda).$$

Finally, we show that our multi-signature scheme is MS-UF-NMA secure.

LEMMA 4.2. Let  $\zeta = v(\gamma_1 - \beta - 1)$ . If there is any adversary who breaks MS-UF-NMA of QROM-Musig, there exists an adversary that breaks MLWE<sub>k,\ell,\eta</sub> or v-SelfTargetMSIS<sub>H,\tau,k,\ell+1,\zeta</sub>.

PROOF. From the point of verification, if there is any attack giving out  $(\mathbf{pk}, \mathbf{w}, \mathbf{z}^{(1)}, \mathbf{z}^{(2)})$ . The following equation must be correct for

• 
$$\|\mathbf{z}^{(1)}\|_{\infty} < \nu(\gamma - \beta)$$
 and  $\|\mathbf{z}^{(2)}\|_{\infty} < \nu(\gamma - \beta)$ ,

•  $\mathbf{A}\mathbf{z}^{(1)} + \mathbf{z}^{(2)} = \mathbf{w} + \sum_{v=1}^{v} c_i \mathbf{t}_i$ 

, in which the challenges are computed from the following formula

$$c_u = H_2(u, \mathbf{Az}^{(1)} + \mathbf{z}^{(2)} - \sum_{u=1}^{\nu} c_u \mathbf{t}_u, \mu, \mathbf{pk}).$$

It is equivalent to find out

$$\mathbf{y}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{z}^{(2)} & \mathbf{z}^{(1)} & \mathbf{c}_1 \end{bmatrix}^\intercal, \mathbf{y}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{c}_2 & \cdots & \mathbf{c}_U \end{bmatrix}^\intercal,$$

and there is a

$$A'_1 = \begin{bmatrix} I & A & t_1 \end{bmatrix}, A'_2 = \begin{bmatrix} t_2 & \cdots & t_U \end{bmatrix},$$

such that  $H_2(u, A_1'y_1 + A_2'y_2, \mu, pk) = c_u$ .

If the adversary could distinguish  $A'_1$  from the uniformly generated  $[I \ A]$ , it would break  $\mathsf{MLWE}_{k,\ell,\eta}$ . Otherwise, it turns out to be indeed the same form of  $\tau$ -SelfTargetMSIS<sub>H, $\tau,k,l+1,\zeta$ </sub>, except that  $\mathbf{pk}$  contains more information than  $\mathbf{A'}$  in  $\tau$ -SelfTargetMSIS. This change will not affect the security of such a problem, because  $\mathbf{A}$  is fixed before the  $\mathcal{A}$  could make an attack. Thus,

$$\begin{split} \mathbf{Adv}_{\mathsf{QROM-Musig}}^{\mathsf{MS-UF-NMA}}(\mathcal{A}) \\ & \leq \mathbf{Adv}_{k,\ell,\eta}^{\mathsf{MLWE}}(\mathcal{A}_1) + \mathbf{Adv}_{\mathsf{H},\tau,k,\ell+1,\zeta}^{\nu-\mathsf{SelfTargetMSIS}}(\mathcal{A}_2) + \mathsf{negl}(\lambda). \end{split}$$

4.2.1 Comparison. We remark that our proof strategy has a better loss compared to the existing scheme [12]. Their scheme uses [22] to reprogram and further answers the signing queries, while our scheme uses Lemma 2.7. Because the simulation of [22] requires a 2Q-wise independent function, and the loss is  $(4+\sqrt{2})\sqrt{Q}2^{-\theta/4}$ . Here  $\theta$  is the input collision-entropy. And to further control the collision-entropy, they require an almost-compressed random oracle to simulate the hash input which works like  $c_i := H_1(H_2(\cdot))$ . By adding up with the almost compressed oracle failure probability, their loss is  $\frac{2\sqrt{Q_1}}{2^{\theta/2}} + \frac{Q_s E(4+\sqrt{2})\sqrt{(Q_2+Q_s E-1)\,\nu}}{2^{\theta/4}}$  for the reprogramming part, while our scheme gets  $\frac{3Q_s E}{2}\sqrt{\frac{Q_H+Q_s\,\nu E}{2^\tau\binom{n}{\tau}}}$  instead. Here  $\theta$  could

be simply treated as the input length of  $H_2$ . Although it seems to be controllable that the loss decreases when the  $\theta$  increases, the length-preserving property of [22] prevents it from choosing the  $\theta$  freely. Moreover, we would expect the challenge space to be  $B_{\tau}$  when using Dilithium, which makes it hard to choose a proper  $\theta$  in reality. Instead, using Lemma 2.7 does not limit the relationship between the input space and the output space and could guide the parameter choosing in a better way.

## 5 Conclusion

In this paper, we introduce the v-SelfTargetMSIS problem and present a reduction from CCB to it. Consequently, we could further show that v-SelfTargetMSIS is at least as hard as the MLWE problem. Besides, we propose a multi-signature scheme based on the one proposed by [12]. Our multi-signature scheme can reach a much tighter loss by using v-SelfTargetMSIS and [13], and it is proven in  $q \equiv 1 \pmod{2n}$  that it benefits from NTT acceleration. Thus, our scheme is more practical and is expected to be widely used.
