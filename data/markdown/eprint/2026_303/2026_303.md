# TalonG: Bandwidth-Efficient Two-Round Threshold Signatures from Lattices

Liming Gao1,<sup>2</sup> [,](https://orcid.org/0009-0004-0211-4148) Guofeng Tang3<sup>⋆</sup> [,](https://orcid.org/0000-0002-0121-5471) Dingding Jia1,[2](https://orcid.org/0000-0002-3556-5605) , Yijian Liu1,<sup>2</sup> [,](https://orcid.org/0009-0001-5887-2392) Bingqian Liu1,<sup>2</sup> [,](https://orcid.org/0009-0007-3790-1522) Xianhui Lu1,<sup>2</sup> [,](https://orcid.org/0000-0001-7091-5810) Kunpeng Wang1,<sup>2</sup> [,](https://orcid.org/0000-0002-3848-6419) and Yongjian Yin1,[2](https://orcid.org/0009-0003-8982-315X)

<sup>1</sup> State Key Laboratory of Cyberspace Security Defense, Institute of Information Engineering, Chinese Academy of Sciences, Beijing, China {gaoliming,jiadingding,liuyijian}@iie.ac.cn {liubingqian,luxianhui,wangkunpeng,yinyongjian}@iie.ac.cn <sup>2</sup> School of Cyber Security, University of Chinese Academy of Sciences, Beijing, China <sup>3</sup> Singapore Management University, tang.guofeng789@gmail.com

Abstract. Threshold signatures split the secret key among n parties, where any subset of at least t parties can collaboratively produce a valid signature. They have been widely deployed in applications such as blockchain systems. Lattice-based threshold signatures have also attracted considerable attention due to their post-quantum security guarantees. However, existing lattice-based constructions still face significant efficiency challenges, particularly when the number of parties becomes large. Recent lattice-based threshold signatures such as TRaccoon (Eurocrypt'24) and Ringtail (S&P'25) support large thresholds, but they either require three interaction rounds or incur heavy communication costs in the two-round setting, limiting their practicality.

In this paper, we present TalonG, a novel two-round lattice-based threshold signature that overcomes these limitations via a new trapdoor semicommitment technique. This variant of commitment relaxes the standard binding requirement to a weaker form, allowing an efficient instantiation from the NTRU assumption and enabling a compact two-round signing protocol with low communication.

For t = 1024 and 128-bit security, TalonG achieves significant improvements among existing lattice-based threshold signatures: its total communication per party and public key size are both minimal, at 26.9 KB and 2.0 KB, respectively. While the resulting signature size is larger (17.7 KB), it remains practical and highly competitive. TalonG is thus wellsuited for real-world large-scale deployments where both round efficiency and communication load are critical.

Keywords: Threshold signatures · Lattice-based signatures · Trapdoor commitments · NTRU-type trapdoor.

# 1 Introduction

The concept of threshold signatures was first introduced in [\[Des90,](#page-29-0)[DF90\]](#page-29-1). It represents a cryptographic extension that combines digital signatures with threshold secret sharing. In a (t, n) threshold signature scheme, the signing key is distributed among n parties such that any group of at least t honest parties can jointly generate a valid signature without reconstructing the secret key, while any group of fewer than t parties cannot forge a new signature. Threshold signatures strengthen key security by removing single points of failure, making them particularly valuable in both blockchain systems and academic research. Their importance is further underscored by NIST's standardization of Multi-Party Threshold Cryptography [\[BP25\]](#page-28-0).

In the classical setting, various works have investigated the threshold version of the standard signatures, such as threshold Schnorr [\[KG20,](#page-30-0)[NRS21,](#page-31-0)[Lin24\]](#page-30-1), threshold ECDSA [\[CGG](#page-28-1)<sup>+</sup>20[,THL](#page-31-1)<sup>+</sup>24[,TX25\]](#page-32-0), threshold BLS [\[DR24\]](#page-30-2), and threshold BBS+ [\[DKL](#page-29-2)<sup>+</sup>23]. However, it is now widely recognized that these schemes will be insecure in a quantum world, where classical hard problems such as the discrete logarithm can be efficiently solved by Shor's algorithm [\[Sho97\]](#page-31-2) on largescale quantum computers.

Existing lattice-based threshold signatures. Lattices are considered as a reliable foundation for post-quantum cryptography. Consequently, lattice-based threshold signatures [\[dPKM](#page-29-3)<sup>+</sup>24[,EKT24](#page-30-3)[,BKL](#page-28-2)<sup>+</sup>25[,dPN25\]](#page-29-4) and multi-signatures [\[DOTT21](#page-29-5)[,BTT22](#page-28-3)[,Che23\]](#page-28-4) have attracted considerable attention from academic research, where a multi-signature is an n-out-of-n full threshold signature.

As a prelude to our work, we first give a brief overview of existing latticebased threshold signature schemes. For a more detailed discussion, refer to Section [1.2.](#page-3-0) When evaluating a threshold signature (or multi-signature) scheme, the following metrics are of particular interest.

- Large-scale support: It is an important metric since NIST's multi-party threshold cryptography call [\[BP25,](#page-28-0) Table 16] highlights use cases with a large number of parties (e.g., up to 1024). In real-world applications such as blockchain systems, the number of participants can reach hundreds or even thousands, making support for large-scale settings a practical and necessary consideration. Currently, many lattice-based threshold schemes can achieve this, such as [\[Che23,](#page-28-4)[EKT24,](#page-30-3)[dPKM](#page-29-3)<sup>+</sup>24[,BKL](#page-28-2)<sup>+</sup>25]. However, some schemes, such as [\[TPCZ23](#page-31-3)[,ADP24,](#page-27-0)[ENP24,](#page-30-4)[CTZ24,](#page-29-6)[GKS24,](#page-30-5)[dPN25\]](#page-29-4), are only suitable for the small-party case (e.g., < 20), as they rely on fully homomorphic encryption, replicated secret sharing, or other heavy techniques.
- Round complexity: In wide-area or large-scale deployments, network latency dominates cost. High round complexity often results in significant latency, limiting the practicality of such protocols. Lattice-based threshold signature schemes with the Fiat-Shamir framework can achieve a minimum of two communication rounds, as in [\[Che23](#page-28-4)[,CTZ24,](#page-29-6)[EKT24,](#page-30-3)[BKL](#page-28-2)<sup>+</sup>25].
- Communication and size efficiency: Lower communication overhead and smaller signature and public key sizes make lattice-based threshold signa-

#### 1.1 Our Contributions

We introduce TalonG, a practical two-round lattice-based threshold signature scheme, which supports a large threshold value t = 1024. Under the parameter setting corresponding to 128-bit security for t = 1024 (see Table [3](#page-26-0) for details), the signature size is 17.7 kilobytes (KB), and the public key size is 2.0 KB. The total communication cost per signer is 26.9 KB.

Technically, our construction builds on Raccoon [\[dPKPR24\]](#page-29-7)—a signature scheme that avoids rejection sampling and is derived from Lyubashevsky's latticebased Fiat-Shamir framework [\[Lyu09,](#page-30-6)[Lyu12\]](#page-31-4). Existing threshold signatures face a trade-off: they either require three rounds or incur high communication costs in the two-round setting. To overcome this limitation, we propose a new trapdoor semi-commitment scheme. This primitive only requires a weak form of binding—rather than the standard binding property used in prior threshold schemes such as [\[DOTT21](#page-29-5)[,GKS24\]](#page-30-5). We demonstrate that the loss of the standard binding property does not compromise security, since any adversarial advantage can be reduced to the hardness of the Ring Short Integer Solution (RSIS) problem. By replacing the standard commitment with our semi-commitment and instantiating the semi-commitment with an efficient NTRU-type trapdoor, we significantly reduce communication overhead and signature size while preserving two-round execution. This technique is generic and can also be applied to improve the efficiency of other trapdoor-commitment-based schemes like [\[DOTT21,](#page-29-5)[GKS24\]](#page-30-5). Furthermore, we employ modulus switching to achieve additional compression, resulting in even more compact signatures.

A trade-off is that both rounds of our protocol must be executed online, whereas one round in [\[EKT24\]](#page-30-3) and [\[BKL](#page-28-2)<sup>+</sup>25] can be pre-computed offline.

We compare the prior lattice-based threshold signatures with ours. As shown in Table [1,](#page-3-1) under large-scale settings, our scheme attains minimal communication and public key sizes, showing a significant improvement over prior works, while maintaining the signature size comparable to those of existing schemes. Concretely, we offer the following advantages:

This design significantly reduces communication overhead and lowers interaction latency.

#### <span id="page-3-0"></span>1.2 Related Works

Several lattice-based threshold signature schemes have advanced the state of the art in recent years. Boneh et al. [BGG<sup>+</sup>18] used fully homomorphic encryption (FHE) to create a universal thresholdizer. Applying it to lattice signatures yields a lattice-based threshold signature scheme. Agrawal et al. [ASY22] later refined this approach using Rényi divergence techniques to reduce FHE noise parameters. These two schemes, while round-optimal, remain theoretical and are difficult to instantiate due to their high circuit computational complexity. Taking advantage of the Fiat–Shamir paradigm, a variety of more efficient lattice-based threshold signature schemes have been developed.

Lattice-based Multi-Signatures. A notable step toward practicality was made by Damgård et al. [DOTT21], who constructed a two-round multi-signature

{4}------------------------------------------------

without relying on FHE. The scheme employed the Fiat-Shamir with Aborts (FSwA) paradigm and incorporated a lattice-based homomorphic trapdoor commitment (HTDC). Nevertheless, this scheme was limited to the n-out-of-n setting and did not support an arbitrary threshold t.

Chen [\[Che23\]](#page-28-4) proposed DualMS, an efficient lattice-based two-round multisignature scheme that significantly reduces both signature and public key sizes compared to prior works like MuSig-L [\[BTT22\]](#page-28-3). For 128-bit security, DualMS achieves approximately 27 KB (public key + signature) for up to 32 signers and 41 KB for up to 1024 signers. It builds on the idea from the Okamoto signature scheme [\[Oka92\]](#page-31-5), with the core innovation being a trapdoor-free "dual signing simulation" technique. DualMS avoids the lattice trapdoors used in [\[DOTT21\]](#page-29-5) and MuSig-L, thereby improving efficiency. On the other hand, its extension to threshold signatures remains unclear.

Threshold signatures with small-scale parties. Building upon [\[DOTT21\]](#page-29-5), Gur et al. [\[GKS24\]](#page-30-5) incorporated threshold FHE with HTDC to achieve a tworound t-out-of-n threshold signature scheme. By leveraging Fiat-Shamir-type signatures, they reduced circuit computational complexity and provided a concretely instantiable scheme. However, the reliance on HTDC came at the cost of larger signature sizes—for instance, signatures reach 46 KB and verification keys 13 KB in a 3-out-of-5 setting.

Alkadri et al. [\[ADP24\]](#page-27-0) presented a three-round n-out-of-n threshold signature scheme. Technically, it removes the need for homomorphic commitments and adopts a tree-of-commitments approach to reduce restart probability. For n = 7 and 128-bit security, the scheme achieves a public key size of 7 KB and a signature size of 12 KB. However, its computational complexity limits practicality to a small number of signers.

Espitau et al. [\[ENP24\]](#page-30-4) proposed Pelican, a robust threshold signature scheme based on standard lattice assumptions, which constitutes the first hash-and-sign lattice-based threshold signature. For a threshold of t = 16, Pelican produces signatures of about 12.3 KB and public keys of 12.8 KB at the 128-bit security level. The scheme guarantees robustness during both key generation and signing, but its communication overhead grows quadratically with the number of parties. In particular, when t = 1024, the communication cost reaches 116 MB, which hinders scalability.

Del Pino et al. [\[dPN25\]](#page-29-4) proposed a highly compact threshold signature scheme with signatures nearly as small as a single Dilithium signature (2.7 KB) with verification keys of 2.6 KB for small thresholds (t ≤ 8). Their construction relied on a novel secret sharing scheme with small shares. By improving the analysis of rejection sampling, they achieved tight security under standard MLWE and SelfTargetMSIS assumptions. This scheme significantly narrows the gap between threshold and non-threshold signature sizes, offering a practical solution for small-group settings. Recently, [\[BCdP](#page-28-7)<sup>+</sup>25] leverages a more efficient secret-sharing scheme to raise the threshold limit from t ≤ 8 to t ≤ 64.

As an alternative approach, Tang et al. [\[TPCZ23\]](#page-31-3) presented a lattice-based threshold signature using generic MPC techniques, achieving functional inter

changeability without altering the original parameters. Consequently, the resulting signatures retain the same size as the standard signatures. However, the communication overhead is substantial, making this approach suitable only for a small number of parties.

Threshold signatures with large-scale parties. Del Pino et al. [\[dPKM](#page-29-3)+24] presented a three-round scheme TRaccoon based on Hint-MLWE and SelfTarget-MSIS assumptions. This construction achieved signatures of about 12.7 KB and verification keys of 3.9 KB for thresholds t up to 1024 without heavy primitives such as FHE/HTDC. Note that for appropriately chosen parameters, the Hint-MLWE problem is equivalent to the standard MLWE problem, and similarly, the SelfTarget-MSIS problem is equivalent to the standard MSIS problem. Therefore, we did not make any special distinction in Table [1.](#page-3-1) Recently, Katsumata et al. [\[KRT24\]](#page-30-7) achieved adaptive security on TRaccoon with five rounds of communication, and del Pino et al. [\[dPKN](#page-29-8)<sup>+</sup>25] incorporated identifiable abort into it at the cost of higher communication overhead.

Espitau et al. [\[EKT24\]](#page-30-3) later introduced the first two-round scheme without FHE/HTDC components, relying on the Algebraic One-More MLWE (AOM-MLWE) assumption. Their offline-online design enabled non-interactive signing after preprocessing, yielding signatures of 10.8 KB and verification keys of 5.5 KB for threshold t up to 1024. Zhu and Tessaro [\[ZT25\]](#page-32-1) subsequently established the security proof of [\[EKT24\]](#page-30-3) under the Algebraic One-More Module-SIS (AOM-MSIS) assumption, which is equivalent to the standard MSIS and MLWE assumptions; however, this proof applies only in large-parameter regimes, resulting in substantially increased overhead.

Ringtail [\[BKL](#page-28-2)<sup>+</sup>25] synthesized these advances through a two-round design based on the standard MLWE assumption. By combining offline preprocessing with efficient online signing, it maintained low signature sizes (13.4 KB) and verification keys (4.5 KB) for 1024 threshold while avoiding non-standard assumptions. This construction represented a state-of-the-art balance between theoretical security, round efficiency, and practical overhead.

Overall, the trajectory of lattice-based threshold signatures has evolved from theoretical frameworks relying on heavy primitives toward efficient and compact signatures under standard assumptions.

# 2 Technical Overview

Our construction builds upon the lattice-based Fiat-Shamir paradigm. In this section, we review this paradigm, outline the difficulties involved in designing a two-round threshold signature, and summarize how we overcome them.

Lattice-based Fiat-Shamir Signatures. We start from the Raccoon signature scheme [\[dPKPR24\]](#page-29-7), whose main difference from the Fiat-Shamir-with-Abort signature scheme Dilithium [\[DKL](#page-29-9)<sup>+</sup>18] lies in avoiding rejection sampling according to the Hint-MLWE assumption, at the cost of a relatively larger signature size. That is, Raccoon also follows lattice-based "commit-challenge

{6}------------------------------------------------

response" framework [Lyu09,Lyu12] over a polynomial ring  $\mathcal{R}_q = \mathbb{Z}_q[X]/(X^N + 1)$ . Let  $\mathbf{A} \in \mathcal{R}_q^{k \times l}$  be a public matrix, and  $\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{A}\mathbf{s} + \mathbf{e} \in \mathcal{R}_q^k$  be a public vector, where the secret vectors  $(\mathbf{s}, \mathbf{e}) \in \mathcal{R}_q^l \times \mathcal{R}_q^k$  are sampled from a bounded distribution: typically a discrete Gaussian or a small-norm uniform distribution. To sign a message msg, the signing process proceeds as follows:

- 1. Sample ephemeral randomness  $(\mathbf{y}, \mathbf{e}')$  from a suitable distribution (e.g., discrete Gaussian or bounded uniform). Compute the "commitment" component  $\mathbf{w} = \mathbf{A}\mathbf{y} + \mathbf{e}'$ .
- 2. Compute the challenge  $c = \mathsf{H}(\mathbf{w} || \mathsf{msg} || \mathsf{vk})$ , where  $c \in \mathcal{R}_q$  has small coefficients.
- 3. Compute the response  $(\mathbf{z}, \mathbf{\Delta}) = (\mathbf{y} + \mathbf{s} \cdot c, \mathbf{e}' + \mathbf{e} \cdot c)$ .

Finally, the verification checks if  $(\mathbf{z}, \boldsymbol{\Delta})$  are short and  $c = \mathsf{H}(\mathbf{A}\mathbf{z} - \mathbf{b} \cdot c + \boldsymbol{\Delta} \| \mathsf{msg} \| \mathsf{vk})$ . There are several ways to ensure that  $(\mathbf{z}, \boldsymbol{\Delta})$  does not leak  $(\mathbf{s}, \mathbf{e})$ 's information:

- (i) rejection sampling [Lyu09,Lyu12,DKL+18];
- (ii) noise flooding [GKPV10,ASY22,CTZ24];
- (iii) the Hint-MLWE assumption [KLSS23,dPKM<sup>+</sup>24,BKL<sup>+</sup>25].

Raccoon uses the Hint-MLWE assumption, which says that the MLWE problem of  $(\mathbf{A}, \mathbf{b})$  is still hard with the knowledge of many hints  $(\mathbf{z}, \boldsymbol{\Delta})$ .

Three-Round Threshold Signatures. The transformation to a three-round threshold signing protocol [dPKM<sup>+</sup>24,KRT24] can be done without relying on any heavy tools and thus naturally supports a large number of participants. The signing algorithms proceed as follows.

Assume that the secret **s** is shared among n signers with Shamir's secret sharing scheme. That is, **s** is splitted into  $\{\mathbf{s}_i\}_{i\in[n]}$  such that  $\mathbf{s} = \sum_{i\in S} \lambda_i \mathbf{s}_i$  for any |S| = t,  $S \subseteq [n]$ , where  $\lambda_i$  is the ith Lagrange coefficient. Signer j's public key share is  $\mathbf{b}_j = \mathbf{A}\mathbf{s}_j + \mathbf{e}_j$ . The three-round signing protocol proceeds as follows.

- **Round 1:** Each signer j samples ephemeral randomness  $(\mathbf{y}_j, \mathbf{e}'_j)$  from a suitable distribution and computes the commitment share  $\mathbf{w}_j = \mathbf{A}\mathbf{y}_j + \mathbf{e}'_j$ . Broadcast  $\mathsf{H}_0(\mathbf{w}_j)$  with  $\mathsf{H}_0(\cdot)$  being a random oracle.
- Round 2: Broadcast  $\mathbf{w}_j$  and run a view-authentication process to ensure that honest parties have the identical view of the first-round messages.
- Round 3: Each signer j computes the challenge  $c = \mathsf{H}(\sum_{i \in S} \mathbf{w}_i \| \mathsf{msg} \| \mathsf{vk})$  and the response share  $(\mathbf{z}_j, \boldsymbol{\Delta}_j) = (\mathbf{y}_j + c\lambda_j \mathbf{s}_j + \mathbf{m}_j, \mathbf{e}'_j + c\mathbf{e}_j)$  where  $\mathbf{m}_j$ 's are random shares of zero. Broadcast  $(\mathbf{z}_j, \boldsymbol{\Delta}_j)$ .

The sum of any t or more than t shares  $(\mathbf{z}_j, \boldsymbol{\Delta}_j)$  is a Raccoon signature  $(\mathbf{z}, \boldsymbol{\Delta})$ . As the secret share  $\mathbf{s}_j$  is amplified by the large Lagrange coefficient  $\lambda_j$ , here the zero's random share  $\mathbf{m}_j$  is employed to hide the secret shares.

Improve to Two-Round. It should be emphasized at the outset that merely removing the first round of transmitting  $H_0(\mathbf{w}_j)$  would compromise security. This would allow the adversary to manipulate  $\mathbf{w}$  to mount attacks such as the concurrent attack originally described by Drijvers et al. [DEF<sup>+</sup>19]. Concretely, the unforgeability of Fiat-Shamir-type signature schemes is proved in

{7}------------------------------------------------

two steps. First, unforgeability under the chosen message attack (CMA) is reduced to unforgeability under NMA is reduced to the underlying hardness assumption. The first step can be proved by invoking honest-verifier zero-knowledge (HVZK) to simulate the signature  $(c, \mathbf{z}, \Delta)$ , and by programming the random oracle to output c on input  $\mathbf{w} = \mathbf{A}\mathbf{z} - \mathbf{b} \cdot c + \Delta$ . When adapted to the threshold scenario, it indicates that  $\mathbf{w}$  should not be generated before c for the programming to succeed. For this reason, in the first round,  $\mathbf{w}_j$  could not be transmitted explicitly.

Nevertheless, the above argument does not imply that a two-round protocol is impossible. Similar to threshold Schnorr, lattice-based threshold signatures can also be realized in two rounds using suitable techniques.

A reliable approach, employed in [EKT24,BKL+25], utilizes a technique that requires multiple  $\mathbf{w}$  values and then linearly combines them into  $\widetilde{\mathbf{w}}$ , which replaces  $\mathbf{w}$  to act as the hash input to generate c. A similar approach has also appeared in the threshold Schnorr scheme [NRS21] and lattice-based multisignature [BTT22]. Concretely, each signer j generates multiple values  $\{\mathbf{w}_{j,i}\}_{i\in[k]}$  in the first round. Then, a random oracle  $\mathsf{H}_{\beta}(\cdot)$  is employed to generate coefficients  $\{\beta_i\}_{i\in[k]} = \mathsf{H}_{\beta}(\{\mathbf{w}_{j,i}\}_{j\in S,i\in[k]})$ , where S is the signer set. These coefficients are used to form the linear combination  $\widetilde{\mathbf{w}}_j = \sum_{i\in[k]} \beta_i \mathbf{w}_{j,i}$ . Finally, the combined  $\widetilde{\mathbf{w}} = \sum_{j\in S} \widetilde{\mathbf{w}}_j$  is taken as input to generate the challenge c. In this approach, the adversary is prevented from manipulating the aggregated  $\widetilde{\mathbf{w}}$ , as the coefficients  $\beta_i$ 's for the linear combination are determined by the random oracle  $\mathsf{H}_{\beta}$ . However, the security proof of such a two-round construction is not straightforward, as directly invoking HVZK followed by programming is infeasible. Instead, existing approaches either rely on the non-standard AOM-MLWE assumption [EKT24] or involve embedding a trapdoor into  $\{\mathbf{w}_{j,i}\}_{i\in[k]}$  [BKL+25].

Whether introducing a new assumption or embedding a trapdoor with the above approach, one must set the parameter k sufficiently large to ensure the security proof. From an efficiency standpoint, this results in high communication costs for both schemes, amounting to 275 KB [EKT24] and 618 KB [BKL<sup>+</sup>25], for t = 1024.

#### 2.1 Our Solutions

Another idea for achieving a two-round protocol is to use a homomorphic trapdoor commitment scheme (HTDC), as in [DOTT21]. That is, in the first round, it sends a commitment to  $\mathbf{w}_j$  and opens it in the second round upon revealing the response share  $\mathbf{z}_j$ . This inherently prevents the adversary from manipulating  $\mathbf{w}$ , as it can only tamper with the commitment, which provides no advantage, especially when the commitment key is message-dependent rather than fixed. In this way, when arguing the reduction from CMA security to NMA security, the reduction works in the trapdoor mode: it can firstly generate a commitment and later open it to a pre-determined value of  $\mathbf{Az}_j - c\mathbf{b}_j + \mathbf{\Delta}_j$ . However, as noted in prior work [Che23], an HTDC-based signature must include the opening randomness  $\mathbf{r}$ , which significantly increases the signature size.

{8}------------------------------------------------

Our scheme TalonG adopts the concept of HTDC while addressing its limitations.

Weaken the Binding Requirement. A key observation is that the binding property in the standard commitment definition (which we refer to as standard binding) is not necessary; instead, a form of "weak" binding suffices for the commitment used in our construction. Concretely, we allow the adversary without trapdoor to find pairs of distinct (w(1) , w(2)) such that Com(w(1); r (1)) = Com(w(2); r (2)) with distinct randomness (r (1) , r (2)), but neither w(b) can be expressed in the MLWE form Az(b) − c (b)b + ∆(b) , where (A, b) are predefined as public key components and (c (b) , z (b) , ∆(b) ) are short tuples of signatures (b = 1, 2). From an intuitive standpoint, weak binding is enough, since it prevents the adversary from committing to an arbitrary value and later opening it to a well-structured value of signature-verification form, which would lead to a forgery.

We take a closer look to the reduction of NMA security to the underlying MSIS problem. By applying the forking lemma [\[BN06\]](#page-28-8), the reduction could find an MSIS solution via two valid forged signatures (c (1) , z (1) , ∆(1) , r (1)) and (c (2) , z (2) , ∆(2) , r (2)). However, standard binding is not necessary; weak binding suffices: any PPT adversary capable of producing values satisfying eq. [\(1\)](#page-8-0) has directly broken the weak binding property, since the two committed values are both expressed in the MLWE form.

A straightforward way to achieve such weak binding is to use the form Com(w; r) = w + Hr by simplifying [\[DOTT21\]](#page-29-5)'s HTDC, where H is a uniformly random public matrix as the commitment key and r is randomness of small norm. The scheme satisfies weak binding: when a PPT adversary generates values satisfying eq. [\(1\)](#page-8-0), it follows that

$$\mathbf{A}\mathbf{z}^{(1)} - c^{(1)}\mathbf{b} + \mathbf{\Delta}^{(1)} + \mathbf{H}\mathbf{r}^{(1)} = \mathbf{A}\mathbf{z}^{(2)} - c^{(2)}\mathbf{b} + \mathbf{\Delta}^{(2)} + \mathbf{H}\mathbf{r}^{(2)}.$$

As long as H is pre-set by the reductionist as the input to the MSIS problem (with a certain non-negligible probability), the above equation also leads to an MSIS solution (∆(1) −∆(2) , z (1) −z (2), c(1) −c (2) , r (1) −r (2)) w.r.t. matrix [A∥ − b∥H].

By removing the dependence on the standard binding, we achieve a more streamlined form of commitment, as illustrated above. More significantly, this opens the door to a more efficient trapdoor type that can further reduce the sizes of the commitment and the randomness.

Efficient NTRU-Type Trapdoor Semi-Commitment. Following the trapdoor type [MP12] used in [DOTT21], embedding the trapdoor into the matrix **H** requires **H** to be of large dimension, which in turn increases both the communication cost and the signature size. Therefore, we explore a new type of trapdoor.

In this paper, we employ a Falcon-style function  $f_h(r_1, r_2) = r_1 + hr_2$  with the two short polynomials  $(r_1, r_2)$  as input, where Falcon [PFH+22] is NIST's standard signature based on the NTRU problem. As a well-studied lattice assumption, NTRU [SS11,PS21] assumes that the distribution of  $h = f^{-1}g \in \mathcal{R}_q$  is computationally indistinguishable from the uniform distribution over  $\mathcal{R}_q$ . Using the trapdoor  $\mathbf{B}_{f,g}$  constructed with short f,g, we can get the preimage  $(r_1,r_2)$  of any function output. Based on such an NTRU-type trapdoor, we can construct a trapdoor semi-commitment scheme. We refer to this as a semi-commitment because it satisfies only weak binding rather than the standard binding property. On the other hand, it has much more compact commitment and randomness sizes than the scheme presented in [DOTT21].

- Commit: Under the commitment key h with  $h \leftarrow \mathcal{R}_q$ , for a message  $\mu \in \mathcal{R}_q$  and randomness  $\boldsymbol{\rho} = (r_1, r_2) \in \mathcal{R}^2$  sampled from distribution  $D^2_{\sigma_r}$ , compute  $\tilde{w} = \mu + f_h(r_1, r_2)$ . Output com  $= \tilde{w}$ .
- Open: Given  $com = \tilde{w}$ , message  $\mu$ , and randomness  $\rho = (r_1, r_2)$ , determine that com is valid if and only if  $\tilde{w} = \mu + f_h(r_1, r_2)$  and  $(r_1, r_2)$  is short.
- Trapdoor-based Equivocability: When h is generated along with the trapdoor  $\mathsf{td} = \mathbf{B}_{f,g}$ , given  $(\mathsf{td}, \mathsf{com} = \tilde{w}, \mu)$ , there exists a Falcon preimage sampling algorithm, which can sample  $(r_1, r_2) \leftarrow D_{\sigma_r}^2$  s.t.  $f_h(r_1, r_2) = \tilde{w} \mu$ .

Specifically, the semi-commitment is hiding since  $f_h(r_1, r_2)$  is statistically close to uniform over  $\mathcal{R}_q$ . It is also weakly binding: constructing an equation of the form  $az - bc + \Delta + f_h(r_1, r_2) = az' - bc' + \Delta' + f_h(r_1', r_2')$  is difficult under the RSIS assumption, where (a, b) will be defined as the public key and  $(c, z, \Delta)$  as short components of signatures; see Section 5.3 for details. Its commitment com consists of a single polynomial, and the randomness involves only two short polynomials. We believe that this NTRU-type trapdoor commitment scheme can be directly leveraged to enhance the HTDC-based constructions in [DOTT21,GKS24].

Our Two-Round Construction. To ensure compatibility with the NTRU-type commitment scheme, our TalonG is based on the Ring LWE and Ring SIS assumptions, rather than the Module variants of LWE and SIS used in prior works, in which the committed message is exactly a polynomial<sup>4</sup>. The outline of TalonG proceeds as follows:

1. **Key Generation:** A trusted party samples  $a \leftarrow \mathcal{R}_q$  uniformly and (s, e) from a bounded distribution over  $\mathcal{R}_q^2$ . It computes b = as + e and uses Shamir's secret sharing to distribute s and e into  $\{s_j\}_{j\in[n]}$  and  $\{e_j\}_{j\in[n]}$ .

and outputs the signature  $(c, z, \Delta, r_1, r_2)$ .

5. **Verify:** The verifier computes  $\tilde{w} = az - bc + \Delta + r_1 + hr_2$ , and accepts the signature iff  $c = \mathsf{H}_2(\tilde{w} \| \mathsf{msg} \| \mathsf{vk})$  and  $(z, \Delta, r_1, r_2)$  is short.

Reduce the size via Modulus Switching. During the computation of  $\tilde{w} = az - bc + \Delta + r_1 + hr_2$ , we observe that the term  $\Delta + r_1$  is small. We can remove  $\Delta + r_1$  and only output the difference between the high-order bits of  $\tilde{w}$  and  $az - bc + hr_2$ . This can help us further significantly reduce the signature size. To realize it, we compress  $\tilde{w}$  and  $az - bc + \Delta$  via modulus switching. Specifically, the input to  $H_2$  becomes  $(\lfloor \tilde{w} \rceil_p \| \text{msg} \| \text{vk})$ , where  $\lfloor \tilde{w} \rceil_p$  denotes the result of applying modulus switching to  $\tilde{w}$  by dropping its p least significant bits. Note that both modulus switching and modulus rounding can achieve the effect of dropping p bits, but our use of the former allows for additional bits of compression in the public key and signature sizes compared to the latter (see Lemma 2). #### 3.1 Notations

Let  $\lambda \in \mathbb{N}$  be the security parameter. Given  $n \in \mathbb{N}$ , we denote [n] the set  $\{1,2,\ldots,n\}$ . Given a distribution  $\mathcal{D}$ , we write  $x \leftarrow \mathcal{D}$  to denote that x is sampled from  $\mathcal{D}$ . Given a set R, we write  $x \leftarrow R$  to denote that x is sampled from the uniform distribution over R. For a modulus  $q \in \mathbb{N}$  and a power-of-two polynomial degree  $N \in \mathbb{N}$ , we define  $\mathbb{Z}_q \coloneqq \mathbb{Z}/q\mathbb{Z} = \{x + q\mathbb{Z} | x \in \mathbb{Z}\}$ ,  $\mathcal{R} \coloneqq \mathbb{Z}[X]/(X^N+1)$  and  $\mathcal{R}_q \coloneqq \mathcal{R}/q\mathcal{R}$ . Scalars, vectors and matrices over  $\mathbb{Z}_q$  or  $\mathcal{R}_q$  are typeset in italic (i.e., x), bold lowercase (i.e., x) and bold uppercase (i.e., x) fonts, respectively. We denote a vector with all components equal to 1 using the bold lowercase 1. For any vector  $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^m$ , the  $l_1$ -norm,  $l_2$ -norm and  $l_{\infty}$ -norm are denoted by  $\|\mathbf{x}\|_1$ ,  $\|\mathbf{x}\|$  and  $\|\mathbf{x}\|_{\infty}$ . When considering vectors  $\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{R}^m$ , these norms are interpreted through the coefficient embedding of  $\mathbf{x}$  into a standard Euclidean space. For  $\mathbf{a} \in \mathbb{Z}_q^m$ , where  $\mathbb{Z}_q^m = \{\mathbf{x} + q\mathbb{Z}^m | \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{Z}^m\}$  denotes the m-dimensional integer ring modulo q, we define the norm of a vector class  $\|\mathbf{a}\|$  as  $\min_{\mathbf{x} \in \mathbf{a} + q\mathbb{Z}^m} \|\mathbf{x}\|$ . This definition extends naturally to the quotient ring  $\mathcal{R}_q$  through its coefficient embedding. We now show the triangle inequality:

**Lemma 1.** For any  $q, m \in \mathbb{N}$ , and  $\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \in \mathbb{Z}_q^m$ , we have  $||\mathbf{x}|| - ||\mathbf{y}|| \le ||\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{y}|| \le ||\mathbf{x}|| + ||\mathbf{y}||$ .

*Proof.* We represent  $\mathbf{x}$  and  $\mathbf{y}$  as equivalence classes  $\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{x}_0 + q\mathbb{Z}^m$  and  $\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{y}_0 + q\mathbb{Z}^m$  with  $\mathbf{x}_0, \mathbf{y}_0 \in \mathbb{Z}^m$  satisfying  $\|\mathbf{x}_0\|_{\infty}, \|\mathbf{y}_0\|_{\infty} \leq q/2$ . By construction,  $\|\mathbf{x}_0\| = \|\mathbf{x}\|, \|\mathbf{y}_0\| = \|\mathbf{y}\|$ . For the upper bound:

<span id="page-11-0"></span>
$$\|\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{y}\| = \min_{\mathbf{z} \in \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{y}} \|\mathbf{z}\| \le \|\mathbf{x}_0 + \mathbf{y}_0\| \le \|\mathbf{x}_0\| + \|\mathbf{y}_0\| = \|\mathbf{x}\| + \|\mathbf{y}\|.$$

For the lower bound, observe that  $\|\mathbf{x}\| - \|\mathbf{y}\| = \|(\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{y}) + (-\mathbf{y})\| - \|\mathbf{y}\| \le \|\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{y}\|$ .

This result extends naturally to the space  $\mathbb{Q}^m/q\mathbb{Z}^m = \{\mathbf{x} + q\mathbb{Z}^m | \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{Q}^m\}.$

#### 3.2 Modulus Switching

In contrast to the modulus rounding techniques employed by del Pino et al. [dPKM<sup>+</sup>24,BKL<sup>+</sup>25], our construction instead leverages modulus switching to achieve a more compact bound of signatures. Let  $q, q' \in \mathbb{N}$  and q > q'. We define the modulus switching function:

<span id="page-12-0"></span>
$$\lfloor \cdot \rceil_{q'/q} : \mathbb{Z}_q \to \mathbb{Z}_{q'} \text{ s.t. } \lfloor x + q \mathbb{Z} \rceil_{q'/q} \coloneqq \lfloor q' x/q \rceil + q' \mathbb{Z},$$

where  $\lfloor \cdot \rfloor$  denotes the rounding operator. The function extends naturally to the vector space  $\mathbb{Z}_q^m$ ,  $\mathcal{R}_q$  and  $\mathbb{Q}^m/q\mathbb{Z}^m$ . When  $q' = \lfloor q/2^p \rfloor$  for some  $p \in \mathbb{N}$ , we adopt the short notation  $\lfloor \cdot \rceil_p := \lfloor \cdot \rceil_{q'/q}$ .

We now present several lemmas that facilitate bound calculations in our constructions.

**Lemma 2.** Let q, p be positive integers such that  $q > 2^p$  and set  $q' = \lfloor q/2^p \rfloor$ . Then, for any  $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{Z}_q^m$ , we have  $\left\| \mathbf{x} - \frac{q}{q'} \cdot \lfloor \mathbf{x} \rceil_p \right\| \leq \frac{q}{q'} \cdot \left\| \frac{1}{2} \right\|$ .

Furthermore, for any  $\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \in \mathbb{Z}_q^m$ , the following inequality holds:

$$\left| \left\| \mathbf{x} - \mathbf{y} \right\| - \frac{q}{q'} \cdot \left\| \lfloor \mathbf{x} \rceil_p - \lfloor \mathbf{y} \rceil_p \right\| \right| \le \frac{q}{q'} \cdot \|\mathbf{1}\|.$$

Proof. We first establish a preliminary claim, for any  $\mathbf{z} \in \mathbb{Z}_{q'}^m$ ,  $\frac{q}{q'} \cdot \|\mathbf{z}\| = \left\| \frac{q}{q'} \cdot \mathbf{z} \right\|$ . Proof of claim: decompose  $\mathbf{z}$  into its equivalence classes representative  $\mathbf{z} = \mathbf{z}_0 + q'\mathbb{Z}^m$  with  $\mathbf{z}_0 \in \mathbb{Z}^m$  satisfying  $\|\mathbf{z}_0\|_{\infty} \leq q'/2$ . Since  $\left\| \frac{q}{q'} \cdot \mathbf{z}_0 \right\|_{\infty} \leq q/2$ ,

$$\frac{q}{q'} \cdot \|\mathbf{z}\| = \frac{q}{q'} \cdot \|\mathbf{z}_0\| = \left\| \frac{q}{q'} \cdot \mathbf{z}_0 \right\| = \left\| \frac{q}{q'} \cdot \mathbf{z} \right\|.$$

For the first inequality, decompose  $\mathbf{x}$  into its equivalence classes representative  $\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{x}_0 + q\mathbb{Z}^m$  with  $\mathbf{x}_0 \in \mathbb{Z}^m$  satisfying  $\|\mathbf{x}_0\|_{\infty} \leq q/2$ . Then:

$$\|\mathbf{x} - \frac{q}{q'} \cdot \lfloor \mathbf{x} \rceil_p \| = \|\mathbf{x}_0 - \frac{q}{q'} \cdot \lfloor \frac{q' \mathbf{x}_0}{q} \rceil + q \mathbb{Z}^m \|$$

$$= \|\frac{q}{q'} \cdot \left(\frac{q' \mathbf{x}_0}{q} - \lfloor \frac{q' \mathbf{x}_0}{q} \rceil\right) \| \le \frac{q}{q'} \cdot \|\frac{1}{2}\|.$$

For the second inequality, apply the triangle inequality (Lemma 1):

$$\left| \left\| \mathbf{x} - \mathbf{y} \right\| - \frac{q}{q'} \cdot \left\| \left[ \mathbf{x} \right]_p - \left[ \mathbf{y} \right]_p \right\| \right| = \left| \left\| \mathbf{x} - \mathbf{y} \right\| - \left\| \frac{q}{q'} \cdot \left[ \mathbf{x} \right]_p - \frac{q}{q'} \cdot \left[ \mathbf{y} \right]_p \right\| \right|$$

$$\leq \left\| \mathbf{x} - \frac{q}{q'} \cdot \left[ \mathbf{x} \right]_p + \frac{q}{q'} \cdot \left[ \mathbf{y} \right]_p - \mathbf{y} \right\|$$

$$\leq 2 \cdot \frac{q}{q'} \cdot \left\| \frac{1}{2} \right\| = \frac{q}{q'} \cdot \left\| \mathbf{1} \right\|.$$

Remark 1. Compared with the modulus rounding techniques employed by del Pino et al. [dPKM<sup>+</sup>24,BKL<sup>+</sup>25], our modulus switching reduces the rounding error term from  $2^p$  to  $q/(2q') \approx 2^{p-1}$ . This improvement allows for additional bits to be dropped from the signature construction.

#### 3.3 Lattice and Discrete Gaussian

A lattice is a discrete set  $\Lambda(\mathbf{b}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{b}_n) = \{\sum_{i=1}^n x_i \mathbf{b}_i | x_i \in \mathbb{Z}\}$ . For  $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^m$  or  $\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{R}_q^m$ , and  $\sigma > 0$ , define the Gaussian function with parameter  $\sigma$  as  $\rho_{\sigma}(\mathbf{x}) := \exp(-\pi \|\mathbf{x}\|^2/\sigma^2)$ . The discrete Gaussian distribution with parameter  $\sigma$  over lattice  $\Lambda$  is defined as  $D_{\Lambda,\sigma}(\mathbf{x}) := \frac{\rho_{\sigma}(\mathbf{x})}{\sum_{\mathbf{x}' \in \Lambda} \rho_{\sigma}(\mathbf{x}')}$ . When  $\Lambda = \mathcal{R}_q$  (resp.  $\mathcal{R}_q^m$ ), we simply denote  $D_{\sigma}$  (resp.  $D_{\sigma}^m$ ).

<span id="page-13-0"></span>Lemma 3 (Adapted from [Lyu12], Lemma 4.4). Let  $\sigma > 0, k > 1/\sqrt{2\pi}$ , it holds that

1.  $\Pr[|z| > k\sigma : z \leftarrow D_{\mathbb{Z},\sigma}] \le 2\exp(-\pi k^2)$ .

2. 
$$\Pr[\|z\| > k\sigma\sqrt{N} : z \leftarrow D_{\sigma}] \le (\sqrt{2\pi}k)^N \cdot \exp(\frac{N}{2}(1 - 2\pi k^2)).$$

The  $\epsilon$ -smoothing parameter of a lattice  $\Lambda$ , denoted by  $\eta_{\epsilon}$ , is defined as  $\eta_{\epsilon}(\Lambda) \coloneqq \inf \left\{ s > 0 : \rho_{1/s}(\Lambda^*) \le 1 + \epsilon \right\}$ .

### 3.4 Hardness Assumptions

We first recall a set of computational assumptions.

**Definition 1 (RSIS).** Let  $\mathcal{R}, k, N, q, \beta$  be parametrised by security parameter  $\lambda$ . The  $\mathbf{RSIS}_{\mathcal{R},q,k,\beta}$  assumption states that for any PPT algorithm  $\mathcal{A}$ , the following probability is negligible in  $\lambda$ :

$$\mathsf{Adv}^{\mathrm{rsis}}_{\mathcal{R},q,k,\beta}(\lambda) = \left| \Pr \left[ \mathbf{y} \leftarrow \mathcal{A}(\mathbf{a}) : \frac{\mathbf{a} \leftarrow \mathcal{R}^{k-1}_q, \mathbf{y} \in \mathcal{R}^k_q,}{\|\mathbf{y}\| \leq \beta, [1 \ \mathbf{a}^T] \mathbf{y} = 0} \right] \right|$$

**Definition 2 (NTRU).** Let  $\mathcal{R}, N, q, \sigma$  be parametrised by security parameter  $\lambda$ . The  $\mathbf{NTRU}_{\mathcal{R},q,\sigma}$  assumption states that for any PPT algorithm  $\mathcal{A}$ , the following probability is negligible in  $\lambda$ :

$$\mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{ntru}}_{\mathcal{R},q,\sigma}(\lambda) = \left| \Pr \left[ 1 \leftarrow \mathcal{A}(h) : (f,g) \leftarrow D_{\sigma}^{2}, h = f^{-1}g \right] - \Pr \left[ 1 \leftarrow \mathcal{A}(h) : h \leftarrow \mathcal{R} \right] \right|$$

**Definition 3 (RLWE).** Let  $\mathcal{R}, k, N, q, \sigma$  be parametrised by security parameter  $\lambda$ . The **RLWE**<sub> $\mathcal{R},q,k,\sigma$ </sub> assumption states that for any PPT algorithm  $\mathcal{A}$ , the following probability is negligible in  $\lambda$ :

$$\mathsf{Adv}^{\mathrm{rlwe}}_{\mathcal{R},q,k,\sigma}(\lambda) = \left| \Pr \left[ 1 \leftarrow \mathcal{A}(\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}) : \frac{\mathbf{a} \leftarrow \mathcal{R}_q^k, s \leftarrow D_{\sigma},}{\mathbf{e} \leftarrow D_{\sigma}^k, \mathbf{b} = \mathbf{a}s + \mathbf{e}} \right] \right.$$
$$\left. - \Pr \left[ 1 \leftarrow \mathcal{A}(\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}) : \frac{\mathbf{a} \leftarrow \mathcal{R}_q^k, s \leftarrow D_{\sigma},}{\mathbf{e} \leftarrow D_{\sigma}^k, \mathbf{b} \leftarrow \mathcal{R}_q^k} \right] \right|$$

We recall the hint-LWE problem that allows an adversary to obtain noisy leakages of the LWE secrets. This problem was introduced in [KLSS23].

**Definition 4 (Hint-RLWE).** Let  $\mathcal{R}, k, N, q, \sigma_1, \sigma_2, \tau, Q$  be parametrized by the security parameter  $\lambda$ . Let C denote a subset of  $\mathcal{R}_q$  such that any  $c \in \mathcal{R}$  satisfies  $\|c\|_1 = \tau$ . The **Hint-RLWE**<sub> $\mathcal{R},q,k,\sigma_1,\sigma_2,\tau,Q$ </sub> assumption states that for any PPT algorithm  $\mathcal{A}$ , the following probability is negligible in  $\lambda$ :

$$\begin{aligned} \mathsf{Adv}^{\mathrm{hint-rlwe}}_{\mathcal{R},q,k,\sigma_{1},\sigma_{2},\tau,Q}(\lambda) \\ &= \left| \Pr \left[ 1 \leftarrow \mathcal{A}(\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}, \left\{ c_{i}, \mathbf{ht}_{i} \right\}_{i \in [Q]}) : \begin{array}{c} \mathbf{a} \leftarrow \mathcal{R}_{q}^{k}, s \leftarrow D_{\sigma_{1}}, \mathbf{e} \leftarrow D_{\sigma_{1}}^{k}, \\ y_{i} \leftarrow D_{\sigma_{2}}, \mathbf{e}_{i}' \leftarrow D_{\sigma_{2}}^{k} \\ \mathbf{b} = \mathbf{a}s + \mathbf{e}, \mathbf{ht}_{i} = \begin{pmatrix} y_{i} \\ \mathbf{e}_{i}' \end{pmatrix} + c_{i} \begin{pmatrix} s \\ \mathbf{e} \end{pmatrix} \right] \\ - \Pr \left[ 1 \leftarrow \mathcal{A}(\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}, \left\{ c_{i}, \mathbf{ht}_{i} \right\}_{i \in [Q]}) : \begin{array}{c} \mathbf{a} \leftarrow \mathcal{R}_{q}^{k}, s \leftarrow D_{\sigma_{1}}, \mathbf{e} \leftarrow D_{\sigma_{1}}^{k}, \\ y_{i} \leftarrow D_{\sigma_{2}}, \mathbf{e}_{i}' \leftarrow D_{\sigma_{2}}^{k} \\ \mathbf{b} \leftarrow \mathcal{R}_{q}^{k}, \mathbf{ht}_{i} = \begin{pmatrix} y_{i} \\ \mathbf{e}_{i}' \end{pmatrix} + c_{i} \begin{pmatrix} s \\ \mathbf{e} \end{pmatrix} \right] \right| \end{aligned}$$

Under appropriate parameters, the hardness of Hint-RLWE can be reduced to that of RLWE [KLSS23,dPKM<sup>+</sup>24]. The concrete requirement is as follows.

This allows for choosing smaller values of  $\sigma_1, \sigma_2$  to reduce the size of the signature while maintaining the same security level of  $\mathbf{RLWE}_{\mathcal{R},q,k,\sigma}$ .

#### 3.7 Threshold Signatures

We show the syntax of the R-round threshold signature following the definition.  $n \in \mathbb{N}$  is the number of all signers.  $t \in \mathbb{N}$  is the reconstruction threshold s.t.  $t \leq n$ . We use act to denote an active signer set such that  $\mathsf{act} \subseteq [N]$  with size t. Each signer  $j \in [n]$  keeps a state  $\mathsf{st}_j$  to retain the information specified for the short session. Formally, an R-round (t,n)-threshold signature scheme is a tuple of PPT algorithms (KGen, {ShareSign}\_i)\_{i \in [R]}, Combine, Vf).

- $\mathsf{KGen}(1^\lambda,t,n) \to (\mathsf{vk},\{\mathsf{sk}_j\}_{j\in[n]})$ : A trusted party takes as inputs security parameters  $1^\lambda$ , reconstruction threshold t and total number of signers n. Then it generates a verification key  $\mathsf{vk}$  and secret key shares  $\{\mathsf{sk}_j\}_{j\in[n]}$  for each signer. Each signer's state is initialized as  $\mathsf{st}_j \coloneqq \varnothing$ .
- ShareSign $_i(\mathsf{sk}_j,\mathsf{st}_j,\mathsf{act},\mathsf{msg},\mathsf{pm}_{i-1}) \to (\mathsf{st}_j,\mathsf{pm}_i[\check{j}])$ : In the i-th  $(i \in [R])$  round signing protocol, the user j uses secret key  $\mathsf{sk}_j$ , current state  $\mathsf{st}_j$ , an active signer set  $\mathsf{act}$ , a message  $\mathsf{msg}$  and protocol message in previous round  $\mathsf{pm}_{i-1} = \{\mathsf{pm}_{i-1}[j]\}_{j \in [\mathsf{act}]}$  to produce a new protocol message  $\mathsf{pm}_i[j]$  and updated state  $\mathsf{st}_j$ . Especially, the protocol messages  $\mathsf{pm}_0$  are empty.
- Combine(vk, msg,  $\{pm_i\}_{i\in[R]}$ )  $\to$  sig: The combiner aggregates all last round protocol messages of all signers in act into a final signature sig.

# <span id="page-18-1"></span>4 Weakly Binding Commitment (Semi-Commitment)

We construct a homomorphic trapdoor commitment scheme with a weak binding property (i.e., semi-commitment) based on the aforementioned NTRU-type trapdoor in Section 3.6. Following [DOTT21]'s syntax, it has the following algorithms:

- $\mathsf{CGen}(1^{\lambda}) \to h$ : The key generation algorithm outputs  $h \leftarrow \mathcal{R}_q$  as the commitment key.
- $\mathsf{Com}_h(\mu; \boldsymbol{\rho}) \to \mathsf{com}$ : For a message  $\mu \in \mathcal{R}_q$  and randomness  $\boldsymbol{\rho} = (r_1, r_2) \leftarrow D_{\sigma_r}^2$ , the committing algorithm outputs  $\mathsf{com} = \mu + f_h(r_1, r_2) \in \mathcal{R}_q$ .
- $\operatorname{Open}_h(\operatorname{com}, \mu, \rho) \to \{0, 1\}$ : Given  $\operatorname{com}, \mu, \rho = (r_1, r_2)$ , the opening algorithm outputs 1 if and only if  $\operatorname{com} = \mu + f_h(r_1, r_2)$  and the vector  $(r_1, r_2)$  satisfies the required norm bounds. Otherwise, it outputs 0.
- $\mathsf{TCGen}(1^{\lambda}) \to (h, \mathbf{B}_{f,g})$ : The trapdoor key generation algorithm runs Falcon.  $\mathsf{GenTrap}(1^{\lambda}) \to (h, \mathbf{B}_{f,g})$ .
- $\mathsf{Eqv}_h(\mathbf{B}_{f,g},\mathsf{com},\mu) \to (r_1,r_2)$ : If h is generated together with the trapdoor  $\mathbf{B}_{f,g}$ , the trapdoor-based equivocation algorithm outputs  $(r_1,r_2) \leftarrow \mathsf{Falcon}.\mathsf{SamplePre}(h,\mathbf{B}_{f,g},\mathsf{com}-\mu)$ .

Given the properties of the Falcon function  $f_h(\cdot)$ , it is evident that the commitment is homomorphic and satisfies the hiding property. The equivocability can be obtained directly from the property of Falcon.SamplePre in Theorem 2. However, it does not have the standard binding. One can trivially find collisions by setting  $\mu_1 = 0$  and  $\mu_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & h \end{bmatrix} (\rho_1 - \rho_2)$ , which satisfies  $\mathsf{Com}_h(\mu_1; \rho_1) = \mathsf{Com}_h(\mu_2; \rho_2)$ .

<span id="page-18-0"></span>Specifically, the scheme satisfies the *weak binding* property. We begin by defining this property.

**Definition 8 (Weak Binding).** A commitment scheme (CGen, Com, Open) is weakly binding if for any PPT adversary A, a norm bound  $\beta \in \mathbb{R}^+$ ,

$$\Pr\left[\begin{array}{ll} \mu_i = az_i - bc_i + \Delta_i & (a,b) \leftarrow \mathcal{R}_q^2, h \leftarrow \mathsf{CGen}(1^\lambda) \\ \mathsf{Com}_h(\mu_1; \boldsymbol{\rho}_1) = \mathsf{Com}_h(\mu_2; \boldsymbol{\rho}_2) : & (z_i, c_i, \Delta_i, \boldsymbol{\rho}_i) \leftarrow \mathcal{A}(a,b,h) \\ \|(z_i, c_i, \Delta_i, \boldsymbol{\rho}_i)\| \leq \beta & i \in \{1,2\} \end{array}\right] = \mathsf{negl}(\lambda)$$

Our commitment scheme is weakly binding. Suppose an adversary outputs  $(z_1, c_1, \Delta_1, \boldsymbol{\rho}_1)$  and  $(z_2, c_2, \Delta_2, \boldsymbol{\rho}_2)$  such that  $\|(z_i, c_i, \Delta_i, \boldsymbol{\rho}_i)\| \leq \beta$  and

$$az_1 - bc_1 + \Delta_1 + [1 \ h] \rho_1 = az_2 - bc_2 + \Delta_2 + [1 \ h] \rho_2.$$

Rearranging yields  $az' - bc' + hr' + \Delta' = 0$  with  $||(z', -c', r', \Delta')|| \le 2\beta$ . This directly gives an RSIS solution for the public vector  $[1 \ a \ b \ h]$ .

It is important to note that the constraints imposed on  $\mu_i$  within Definition 8 are highly tailored to the specific lattice-based signature structure. As such, it is a primitive designed specifically for our threshold signature scheme. However, by applying different algebraic constraints on  $\mu_i$ , this definition could be adapted for broader use cases. Establishing a more generalized definition and exploring these wider applications remain subjects for our future work.

**Two-Round Signing.** Our signing protocol follows a lattice-based Fiat-Shamir framework. To realize two-round communication in a distributed manner, we use weakly binding commitment defined in Section 4 to mask each party's "commit" component  $ay_j + e'_j$ , where the corresponding NTRU trapdoor helps us in completing the security proof, instead of relying on an additional round to perform a hash-and-open paradigm as  $[dPKM^+24]$ .

In detail, the first round signing algorithm proceeds as follows:

- 1. Commitment Generation: Each signer j first generates a message-specific key  $h = \mathsf{H}_1(\mathsf{msg}||\mathsf{vk}) \in \mathcal{R}_q$ , then generates its share  $w_j = ay_j + e'_j + r_{j,1} + hr_{j,2} \mod q$ . The ephemeral randomness  $(y_j, e'_j)$  are sampled from  $D^2_{\sigma_2}$ , which are utilized to mask the private key (s, e). Another randomness  $(r_{j,1}, r_{j,2})$  are sampled from  $D^2_{\sigma_r}$ , which are utilized to mask the fresh RLWE sample  $ay_j + e'_j \mod q$ .
- 2. State Management: The tuple  $(\mathsf{msg}, w_j, y_j, r_{j,2})$  is stored in  $\mathsf{st}_j$ . The first round protocol message  $\mathsf{pm}_1[j]$  is defined as  $w_j$ .

The second round signing algorithm proceeds as follows:

- 1. Consistency Check: Each signer j takes as input its secret key  $\mathsf{sk}_j$ , current state  $\mathsf{st}_j$ , active signer set  $\mathsf{act}$ , message  $\mathsf{msg}$  and the first round protocol message  $\mathsf{pm}_1$ . It confirms  $|\mathsf{act}| \geq t$  and the existence of the session state  $(\mathsf{msg}, \mathsf{pm}_1[j], *)$ , ensuring participation in the first round but not in the second round.
- 2. Challenge Derivation: Each signer j computes the rounded aggregated commitment  $w = \lfloor \sum_{i \in \mathsf{act}} w_i \rceil_{p_2}$  and the challenge  $c = \mathsf{H}_2(w \| \mathsf{msg} \| \mathsf{vk})$ .
- 3. Response Generation: Each signer j constructs zero-sum masking term  $\sum_{i \in \mathsf{act}} (p_{i,j} p_{j,i})$  through pairwise PRF seeds with the session-specific input  $\mathsf{act} \| \mathsf{msg} \| \mathsf{pm}_1^6$ . The signer further computes the response share  $z_j \coloneqq y_j + \lambda_{\mathsf{act},j} s_j c + \sum_{i \in \mathsf{act}} (p_{i,j} p_{j,i}) \mod q$ .

Verification times remain stable and efficient across all thresholds, consistently staying below 1 ms. This is because verification involves only a small, constant number of polynomial multiplications independent of t, with minor fluctuations arising from sampling noise.

<span id="page-32-2"></span>In this section, we prove the indistinguishability of Falcon.SamplePre in Section 3.6 via a sequence of hybrid distributions. First, we set the primal distribution

$$\mathcal{D}_{0} = \left\{ (h, w, r_{1}, r_{2}) : \begin{array}{c} h \leftarrow \mathcal{R}_{q}, (r_{1}, r_{2}) \leftarrow D_{\sigma_{r}}^{2}, \\ w = r_{1} + h \cdot r_{2} \end{array} \right\},$$

which is generated without using any trapdoor.

Define the first distribution:

$$\mathcal{D}_1 = \left\{ (h, w, r_1, r_2) : \frac{(h, \mathbf{B}_{f,g}) \leftarrow \mathsf{Falcon.GenTrap}(1^{\lambda}),}{(r_1, r_2) \leftarrow D_{\sigma_r}^2, w = r_1 + h \cdot r_2} \right\}.$$

The distribution  $\mathcal{D}_1$  differs from  $\mathcal{D}_0$  only in how h is sampled. Under NTRU assumption [SS11], h from  $\mathcal{D}_0$  is computationally distinguishable from h in  $\mathcal{D}_1$ . For any PPT adversary  $\mathcal{A}$ ,

$$|\Pr[\mathcal{A}(\mathcal{D}_0) = 1] - \Pr[\mathcal{A}(\mathcal{D}_1) = 1]| \leq \mathsf{Adv}^{\mathrm{ntru}}_{\mathcal{R},q,\sigma_{td}}(\lambda).$$

Define the second distribution:

$$\mathcal{D}_2 = \left\{ (h, w, r_1, r_2) : \frac{(h, \mathbf{B}_{f,g}) \leftarrow \mathsf{Falcon.GenTrap}(1^\lambda), w \leftarrow \mathcal{R}_q}{(r_1, r_2) \leftarrow D_{\sigma_r}^2 \big| r_1 + h \cdot r_2 = w} \right\}.$$

Here, w is sampled uniformly and  $(r_1, r_2)$  is sampled from the conditional distribution. For fixed w, the conditional distributions of  $\mathcal{D}_2$  and  $\mathcal{D}_1$  are identical. Thus, the statistical distance between  $\mathcal{D}_2$  and  $\mathcal{D}_1$  arises only from the marginal distribution of w. $$\Lambda_h^{\perp} = \{(s_1, s_2) \in \mathcal{R}_q^2 : s_1 + h \cdot s_2 = 0\}.$$

By [GPV08, lemma 3.1],

$$\eta_{\epsilon}(\Lambda_h^{\perp}) \leq \|\tilde{\mathbf{B}}_{f,g}\|_{\mathsf{GS}} \cdot \sqrt{\log(4N(1+1/\epsilon))/\pi}.$$

*Proof.* We construct the reduction  $\mathcal{B}$  as follows:

- 1. Computing  $(\mathsf{vk}, \{\mathsf{sk}_i\}_{i \in [n]})$  identically to  $\mathsf{Hybrid}_0$  and provide  $(\mathsf{vk}, \{\mathsf{sk}_i\}_{i \in \mathcal{C}})$  to  $\mathcal{A}$ .
- 2. For all oracle queries except  $OS_2(\cdot)$ , simulate identically to  $Hybrid_0$ .
- 3. For  $\mathsf{OS}_2(\cdot)$  queries, simulate identically to  $\mathsf{Hybrid}_0$  except:

  - If  $i \in \mathcal{H}_{act}$ , query the PRF oracle with inputs  $(i * n + j, act || msg || pm_1)$  and  $(j * n + i, act || msg || pm_1)$ , obtaining outputs y and y'. Set  $p_{i,j} = y$ ,  $p_{j,i} = y'$ .

If PRF uses  $\mathcal{O}_{prf}(\cdot)$ ,  $\mathcal{B}$  perfectly simulates Hybrid<sub>0</sub>. Thus

$$\mathsf{Adv}^{\operatorname{hybrid}_0}_{\mathcal{A}}(\lambda) = \Pr[1 \leftarrow \mathcal{B}^{\mathcal{O}_{\mathsf{prf}}(\cdot)}(1^{\lambda}) : \mathsf{seed}_i \leftarrow \{0,1\}^{\lambda}].$$

**Lemma 8.** For any PPT adversary A against Hybrid,

$$\mathsf{Adv}_{\mathcal{A}}^{\text{hybrid}_7}(\lambda) \leq \frac{Q_H^2}{|\mathcal{C}|} + Q_H^{3/2} \cdot \sqrt{\mathsf{Adv}_{\mathcal{R},q,4,\beta_{\mathsf{SIS}}}^{\mathrm{rsis}}(\lambda)},$$

,  $\beta_\delta \coloneqq \frac{q}{q_2}(\sqrt{N} + 2\beta_\Delta) + \frac{q\tau}{q_1}\sqrt{N}$ .

*Proof.* We construct the reduction  $\mathcal{B}$  against  $\mathbf{RSIS}_{\mathcal{R},q,4,\beta_{SIS}}$  as follows:
