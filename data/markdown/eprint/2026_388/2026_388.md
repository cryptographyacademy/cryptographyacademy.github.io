# Necessary and Sufficient Conditions for the Existence of Ideal Linear Secret Sharing Schemes for Arbitrary Access Structures Zheng Chen<sup>1</sup> , Qiuxia Xu2\*, Chunming Tang1\*

1\* School of Mathematics and Information Science, Guangzhou University, Guangzhou, Guangdong, 510006, China . School of Mathematics and Systems Science, Guangdong Polytechnic Normal University, Guangzhou, Guangdong, 510665, China .

#### Abstract

Determining whether an arbitrary access structure can be realized by an ideal linear secret sharing scheme is an important research topic. we use linear codes as the main tool to construct matrices H and G over a finite field F<sup>q</sup> for a given access structure Γmin, and show that a necessary and sufficient condition for the existence of an ideal linear secret sharing scheme realizing Γmin is that the equation GH<sup>T</sup> = 0 has a solution. If this equation has a solution, then H serves as the parity-check matrix of a linear code that realizes Γmin, and G is the corresponding generator matrix. Furthermore, we prove that the result is equivalent to the following statement: there exists an ideal linear code for realizing the Γmin if and only if it is the port of a matroid that is representable over Fq.

Keywords: ideal linear secret sharing scheme, linear codes, access structure, dual access structure, matroid

MSC Classification: 94A62 , 94B05 , 05B35

#### 1 Introduction

Secret sharing, as an important branch of modern cryptography, distributes a secret into multiple shares among participants such that any authorized subset of participants can collaboratively recover the secret, while any unauthorized subset obtains no information about it.The schemes proposed by Shamir [\[1\]](#page-28-0) and Blakley [\[2\]](#page-28-1), who independently introduced the concept in 1979, laid the foundation for secret sharing theory and are both linear in nature. However, a precise formulation of linear secret sharing schemes was first provided by Beimel [\[3\]](#page-28-2) in his 1996 Ph.D. dissertation, where a secret sharing scheme is defined to be linear if its sharing function is linear. As the schemes of Shamir and of Blakley, designed for threshold access structures, are often inadequate for handling complex practical scenarios, this limitation prompted research into secret sharing schemes for general access structures. In 1989, Ito et al. [\[4\]](#page-28-3) constructed the first linear secret sharing scheme for general access structures, a theoretical breakthrough that spurred subsequent research [\[5,](#page-28-4) [6\]](#page-28-5). Monotone span programs (MSPs), a primary tool for constructing linear secret sharing schemes, were formally defined by Karchmer et al. [\[7\]](#page-29-0), who also established the equivalence between MSPs and linear secret sharing schemes. Beimel [\[3\]](#page-28-2) further elaborated this correspondence, and Xiao et al. [\[8\]](#page-29-1) utilized MSPs to construct linear secret sharing schemes for general access structures.

Due to the one-to-one correspondence between linear codes and monotone span programs [\[9\]](#page-29-2), linear codes have also become a fundamental tool for constructing linear secret sharing schemes. As early as 1981, the connection between Reed–Solomon codes and Shamir's threshold scheme was noted [\[10\]](#page-29-3). In 1993, Massey [\[11\]](#page-29-4) pointed out the correspondence between minimal authorized sets and minimal codewords in the dual code whose first component is 1, and proposed secret sharing schemes based on linear codes. Building on Massey's framework, Anderson et al. [\[12\]](#page-29-5) further investigated robust secret sharing schemes with sparse access structures and applied linear codes such as 

{2}------------------------------------------------

Reed–Muller codes to secret sharing. Recently, Tang et al. [\[13\]](#page-29-6) employed Massey's theory to construct optimal binary linear codes realizing arbitrary access structures. Meanwhile, minimal linear codes play a significant role in secret sharing, as they give rise to secret sharing schemes with determinable access structures; thus the study of minimal linear codes is of great importance. In 1998, Ashikhmin and Barg [\[14\]](#page-29-7) investigated the properties of minimal vectors in general linear codes and established a sufficient condition for a linear code to be minimal. This condition has become an important tool in subsequent research (e.g., [\[15,](#page-30-0) [16\]](#page-30-1)). Cohen et al.[\[17\]](#page-30-2) observed that the sufficient condition proposed by Ashikhmin and Barg is merely sufficient but not necessary, and they further constructed counterexamples to illustrate this. Later, Chang et al. [\[18\]](#page-30-3) constructed the first infinite family of minimal binary linear codes violating this sufficient condition. Ding et al. [\[19\]](#page-30-4) derived necessary and sufficient conditions for a binary linear code to be minimal and constructed three infinite families of minimal binary linear codes. Furthermore, based on Ding et al.'s characterization, Li et al. [\[20\]](#page-30-5) employed Boolean functions to construct four infinite families of minimal binary linear codes that also fail to meet the Ashikhmin–Barg condition.

Another line of research relevant to this work concerns representable matroids. Matroids, which abstract the notion of independence in combinatorial mathematics, were first defined by Whitney [\[21\]](#page-30-6) in 1935. The concept of matroids has since provided a key theoretical foundation for characterizing ideal access structures. In 1991, Brickell and Davenport [\[22\]](#page-30-7) established a partial characterization of ideal access structures using matroids. Their central result states that if an access structure is the port of a representable matroid, then it is ideal; conversely, if an access structure is ideal, then it is the port of a unique matroid. Following this line, Martin [\[23\]](#page-31-0) developed an algorithm to construct the corresponding matroid from an ideal access structure. Nevertheless, a complete characterization of ideal access structures remains an open problem, and only specific families have been fully characterized, such as hierarchical threshold

{3}------------------------------------------------

access structures [\[24\]](#page-31-1), access structures defined by graphs [\[22,](#page-30-7) [25,](#page-31-2) [26\]](#page-31-3), bipartite access structures [\[27\]](#page-31-4), and access structures with three or four minimal authorized sets [\[28\]](#page-31-5). These characterizations share a common feature: all such access structures are realized by ideal linear secret sharing schemes. Although, in theory, an ideal access structure does not necessarily imply the existence of a linear secret sharing scheme that realizes it, linear schemes are more efficient in practice. Therefore, necessary and sufficient conditions for an arbitrary access structure to be realized by an ideal linear secret sharing scheme are still of practical significance to explore.

In this direction, our work makes the following main contributions:

- Correction of constraints in the original theorem. Tang et al.[\[29\]](#page-31-6) first established that the existence of an ideal linear secret sharing scheme realizing an access structure Γmin is equivalent to the solvability of a system of quadratic congruences GH<sup>T</sup> = 0 over some finite field F<sup>q</sup> [\[29,](#page-31-6) Theorem 2]. Let the collection of all maximal unauthorized sets R<sup>i</sup> corresponding to Γmin be denoted the adversary structure R(Γmin). Tang et al. asserted that when constructing the matrix G = (gij ) associated with R(Γmin), if j /∈ R<sup>i</sup> , then gij ∈ F<sup>q</sup> is an unknown that may possibly be zero.
  - In Section [3,](#page-11-0) we revisit this theorem from the novel perspective of access structures and their duals, and we prove that the unknowns gij must in fact be nonzero elements of Fq. This correction refines and completes the original theorem.
- Revelation of the duality between matrices G and H. During the proof of the theorem, we elucidate the dual relationship between the matrices G and H in an ideal linear secret sharing scheme that realizes the access structure, namely, that the row space of G is the orthogonal complement of the row space of H (and vice versa). This relationship was not explicitly clarified in the original work [\[29\]](#page-31-6). Based on this new insight, we present a more comprehensive version of the theorem (Theorem [7\)](#page-18-0).

{4}------------------------------------------------

• Bridging three perspectives on the same mathematical object. From the matroid point of view, we uncover a profound connection among ideal linear secret sharing schemes, linear codes, and representable matroids for a given access structure. This provides an equivalent criterion that bridges cryptography, algebraic coding theory, and combinatorial mathematics in the study of the same underlying object.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section [2](#page-4-0) reviews basic concepts and relationships among access structures, linear secret sharing schemes, linear codes, and matroids. Section [3](#page-11-0) presents the necessary and sufficient conditions for an access structure to be realized by an ideal linear code. Section [4](#page-19-0) discusses the equivalence among the existence of an ideal linear secret sharing scheme, the solvability of a system of quadratic congruences under a specific linear code construction, and the representability of a matroid for the same mathematical object; illustrative examples are also provided. Section [5](#page-27-0) concludes the paper and outlines future research directions.

### <span id="page-4-0"></span>2 Preliminaries

#### 2.1 Access Structures and Secret Sharing Schemes

For convenience, we denote the cardinality of a finite set X by |X|, the set difference of X and Y by X − Y , and the power set of a finite set X by P(X).

Definition 1 (Access structure) Let P = {P1, P2, . . . , Pn} be a set of n participants and let AS ⊆ P(P) be a non-empty subset of P(P). We call AS an access structure on P if it satisfies the following monotonicity property:

if 
$$A \in \mathcal{AS}$$
, then for every  $A' \in \mathcal{P}(P)$  with  $A \subseteq A'$ , we have  $A' \in \mathcal{AS}$ .

Any set in AS is called an authorized set on P. Moreover, an authorized set S is said to be a minimal authorized set on P

if for every 
$$S' \in \mathcal{P}(P)$$
 with  $S' \subsetneq S$ , we have  $S' \notin \mathcal{AS}$ .

{5}------------------------------------------------

The collection of all minimal authorized sets on P is defined as the minimal access structure Γmin. When no confusion arises, we may also refer to Γmin simply as the access structure.

Any set in P(P)−AS is called an unauthorized set on P. Furthermore, an unauthorized set R is said to be a maximal unauthorized set

> if for every R ′ ∈ P(P) with R ⊊ R ′ , we have R ′ ∈ AS.

The collection of all maximal unauthorized sets on P is defined as the adversary structure R(Γmin) corresponding to Γmin.

Remark 1 Throughout this paper, we assume that access structures are non-redundant, that is, each participant appears in at least one minimal authorized set.

To analyze the duality of the above structures, we introduce the dual access structure (introduced by van Dijk [\[9\]](#page-29-2)). The dual access structure of Γmin is defined as

$$\Gamma_{\min}^* = \{ S^* \subseteq P \mid P - S^* \in \mathcal{R}(\Gamma_{\min}) \}.$$

Correspondingly, the adversary structure associated with Γ<sup>∗</sup> min is defined as

$$\mathcal{R}^*(\Gamma_{\min}^*) = \{ R^* \subseteq P \mid P - R^* \in \Gamma_{\min} \}.$$

Definition 2 (Secret sharing scheme) Let the secret s ∈ Fq, and a secret sharing scheme realizing an access structure Γmin on the participant set P is called perfect if it satisfies the following two conditions:

- Correctness: Every minimal authorized set A ∈ AS can uniquely reconstruct the secret s from its shares s<sup>i</sup> .
- Perfect Security: Any unauthorized set B ∈ P(P) − AS obtains no information about the secret s.

A perfect secret sharing scheme realizing AS is said to be ideal if the share length of each participant is equal to the length of the secret. Furthermore, if the process of sharing the secret is linear, then the scheme is called an ideal linear secret sharing scheme.

{6}------------------------------------------------

This paper aims to investigate necessary and sufficient conditions for an arbitrary access structure to be realizable by an ideal linear secret sharing scheme.

#### 2.2 Secret Sharing Schemes Based on Linear Codes

With linear codes serving as a fundamental tool for constructing linear secret sharing schemes, this paper employs linear codes to investigate necessary and sufficient conditions for an arbitrary access structure to be realizable by an ideal linear secret sharing scheme.

Definition 3 (Linear code) A k-dimensional subspace C of the vector space F n <sup>q</sup> is called an [n, k] linear code if it satisfies

$$a\mathbf{c} + \tilde{a}\tilde{\mathbf{c}} \in C, \quad \forall a, \tilde{a} \in \mathbb{F}_q, \ \forall \mathbf{c}, \tilde{\mathbf{c}} \in C,$$

where each element c = (c1, c2, . . . , cn) ∈ C is called a codeword.

Since a linear code C is a k-dimensional subspace of F n <sup>q</sup> , taking k linearly independent codewords from C as row vectors yields a k × n matrix G, which is called a generator matrix of C.

The dual code of C is defined as

$$C^{\perp} = \{ \boldsymbol{c}' \in \mathbb{F}_q^n \mid \forall \boldsymbol{c} \in C, \ \langle \boldsymbol{c}, \boldsymbol{c}' \rangle = 0 \},$$

where ⟨c, c ′ ⟩ denotes the Euclidean inner product. It is well known that if C is an [n, k] linear code over Fq, then C <sup>⊥</sup> is an [n, n − k] linear code over Fq.

Similar to C, the dual code C <sup>⊥</sup> also has a generator matrix. Let H be a generator matrix of C <sup>⊥</sup>; then H is also called a parity-check matrix of C.

Let C be an [n, k] linear code over F<sup>q</sup> and let c = (c1, c2, . . . , cn) ∈ C be a codeword. The support of c is defined as supp(c) = {i ∈ [1, n] | c<sup>i</sup> ̸= 0}. A codeword c is said to cover another codeword v if supp(v) ⊆ supp(c).

A nonzero codeword c ∈ C is called a minimal codeword whose first component is 1 if it satisfies the following two conditions:

Now let  $P = \{P_1, \ldots, P_n\}$  be a set of n participants and let  $\Gamma_{\min} = \{S_1, \ldots, S_m\}$  be the access structure consisting of all minimal authorized sets on P. Suppose there exists an [n+1,k] linear code C over  $\mathbb{F}_q$  whose parity-check matrix H is constructed as follows:

$$H = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & h_{11} & h_{12} & \cdots & h_{1n} \\ 1 & h_{21} & h_{22} & \cdots & h_{2n} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 1 & h_{m1} & h_{m2} & \cdots & h_{mn} \end{pmatrix},$$

where  $h_{ij} \neq 0$  if and only if  $P_j \in S_i$ . Each row of H corresponds to a minimal authorized set  $S_i \in \Gamma_{\min}$ . The **labeling map**  $\rho$  is defined as

$$\rho = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 3 & \cdots & n+1 \\ P_1 & P_2 & \cdots & P_n \end{pmatrix},$$

where the first row lists the column indices of H and the second row indicates the corresponding participants.

Following Massey [11], we introduce the notion of an **ideal linear code** as defined below.

**Definition 4** (Ideal linear code) For the given  $\Gamma_{\min}$ , we say that C is an **ideal linear code** realizing  $\Gamma_{\min}$  if and only if there exists a set of nonzero elements  $h_{ij} \in \mathbb{F}_q$  that yields the parity-check matrix H as above such that each row vector of H corresponds bijectively to a minimal codeword with first component 1 in the dual code  $C^{\perp}$ .

{8}------------------------------------------------

Such an ideal linear code directly induces a secret sharing scheme realizing Γmin. Let G = (g<sup>0</sup> , g<sup>1</sup> , . . . , gn) be a k × (n + 1) generator matrix of C, where each g<sup>i</sup> is a column vector over Fq.

Secret Distribution. The dealer P<sup>0</sup> first randomly selects a vector u = (u1, u2, . . . , uk) with u<sup>i</sup> ∈ F<sup>q</sup> satisfying ug<sup>0</sup> = s, computes uG = (ug<sup>0</sup> ,ug<sup>1</sup> , . . . ,ugn) and then distributes the share s<sup>i</sup> = ug<sup>i</sup> to participant P<sup>i</sup> .

Secret Reconstruction. Let S = {P<sup>i</sup><sup>1</sup> , P<sup>i</sup><sup>2</sup> , . . . , P<sup>i</sup>m} ⊆ P be a minimal authorized set. By construction, the vector

$$\mathbf{v} = (1, 0, \dots, 0, h_{i_1}, 0, \dots, 0, h_{i_m}, 0, \dots, 0)$$

is a minimal codeword with first component 1 in the dual code C <sup>⊥</sup>. Consequently,

$$\boldsymbol{g}_0 = -\sum_{j=1}^m h_{i_j} \boldsymbol{g}_{i_j},$$

and thus

$$s = ug_0 = -u\sum_{j=1}^m h_{i_j}g_{i_j} = -\sum_{j=1}^m h_{i_j}(ug_{i_j}) = -\sum_{j=1}^m h_{i_j}s_{i_j}.$$

The access structure realized by this scheme is exactly Γmin, and the scheme is linear. Moreover, by the labeling map, the share length of each participant is equal to the secret length, hence the scheme is also ideal. Therefore, the existence of an ideal linear secret sharing scheme for an arbitrary access structure Γmin is equivalent to the existence of an ideal linear code realizing Γmin as defined above.

 The members of I are called independent sets of M. A maximal independent set in M is called a basis of M, denoted by B. If X /∈ I, then X is called a dependent set of M. A minimal dependent set of M is called a circuit of M; that is, a dependent set C is a circuit if and only if every proper subset of C is independent. Furthermore, the rank

 function r<sup>M</sup> : P(E) → N of M is defined as the cardinality of a maximal independent set

$$r_{\mathcal{M}}(X) = \max\{|I| : I \subseteq X, I \in \mathcal{I}\}.$$

If rM(E) = r, then M is called a matroid of rank r.

Definition 6 (Representable matroid) Let M = (E, I) be a matroid of rank r. M is said to be representable over a field F if there exists a mapping ϕ : E → V , where V is some r-dimensional vector space over F, such that for every subset X ⊆ E, the rank of X in M equals the dimension of the subspace spanned by ϕ(X) in V , i.e.,

$$r_{\mathcal{M}}(X) = \dim_{\mathbb{F}} \langle \phi(X) \rangle.$$

Such a mapping ϕ is called an F-coordinatization of M.

Definition 7 (Connected matroid) Let M = (E, I) be a matroid. If for any two distinct elements x, y ∈ E, there exists a circuit C ∈ C(M) such that {x, y} ⊆ C, where C(M) is the family of circuits of M, then M is called a connected matroid.

Definition 8 (Dual matroid) Let M = (E, I) be a matroid and let B(M) denote the family of bases of M. The dual matroid M<sup>∗</sup> = (E, I ∗ ) of M is defined by its family of independent sets I ∗ as follows:

$$\mathcal{I}^* = \{ I^* \subseteq E \mid \exists B \in \mathcal{B}(M) \text{ such that } B \subseteq E - I^* \}.$$

That is, a subset I <sup>∗</sup> ⊆ E is independent in M<sup>∗</sup> if and only if its complement E −I ∗ contains a basis of M. From the definition of the dual matroid, the following properties hold:

- (M<sup>∗</sup> ) <sup>∗</sup> = M.
- For X ⊆ E, rM<sup>∗</sup> (X) = |X| − rM(E) + rM(E − X). In particular, rM<sup>∗</sup> (E) = |E| − rM(E).
- M<sup>∗</sup> is representable over a field F if and only if M is representable over F.
- M<sup>∗</sup> is connected if and only if M is connected.

Definition 9 (Matroid isomorphism) Let M<sup>1</sup> = (E1, I1) and M<sup>2</sup> = (E2, I2) be two matroids. If there exists a bijection ϕ : E<sup>1</sup> → E<sup>2</sup> such that for every X ⊆ E1,

$$X \in \mathcal{I}_1 \iff \phi(X) \in \mathcal{I}_2,$$

then ϕ is called an isomorphism from M<sup>1</sup> to M2, and M<sup>1</sup> and M<sup>2</sup> are said to be isomorphic, denoted by M<sup>1</sup> ∼= M2. Intuitively, the independence structures of M<sup>1</sup> and M<sup>2</sup> are essentially the same.

Let G = (g<sup>0</sup> , g<sup>1</sup> , . . . , gn) be a k × (n + 1) matrix over F<sup>q</sup> that serves as a generator matrix of an [n+1, k] linear code C. The column vectors of G define an Fq-representable matroid M on the point set P = {P0, P1, . . . , Pn}, whose rank function r<sup>M</sup> : P(P) → N is given by

$$r_{\mathcal{M}}(A) = \operatorname{rank}_{\operatorname{LIN}} (\{ \boldsymbol{g}_i \mid i \in A \}), \qquad A \subseteq P,$$

where rankLIN denotes the linear algebraic rank of the set of vectors (i.e., the dimension of the subspace they span).

{11}------------------------------------------------

It is worth noting that the matroid M depends only on the linear code C, not on the particular choice of the generator matrix G. We say that M is the matroid associated with C, and that C is an Fq-representation of M. It is easy to verify that the rank of the matroid equals the rank of the generator matrix, i.e., rM(P) = rankLIN(G).

Remark 2 Different linear codes may represent the same matroid.

Furthermore, if the secret sharing scheme based on the linear code C realizes an access structure Γmin on P (not necessarily ideal), then the secret sharing scheme based on the dual code C <sup>⊥</sup> realizes the dual access structure Γ<sup>∗</sup> min, and both schemes have the same information rate.

Under this correspondence, the matroid M<sup>1</sup> associated with the dual code C <sup>⊥</sup> is precisely the dual matroid of the matroid M<sup>2</sup> associated with C. Since the dual of an Fq-representable matroid is also Fq-representable, the duality of access structures is naturally reflected in the duality of matroids: If Γmin is the port of the matroid M<sup>1</sup> at P<sup>0</sup> — that is, a subset S<sup>i</sup> ⊆ P − {P0} is a minimal authorized set in Γmin if and only if Si∪ {P0} is a circuit of M<sup>1</sup> (the notion of the port of a matroid throughout the sequel follows this definition) — then Γ<sup>∗</sup> min is the port of the dual matroid M<sup>2</sup> at P0.

#### <span id="page-14-1"></span>3.2 From Algebraic Conditions to the Construction of Schemes

In Section 3.1, we proved that if an access structure  $\Gamma_{\min}$  is realized by an ideal linear code, then the system of quadratic equations  $GH^{\mathsf{T}} = 0$  has a solution over  $\mathbb{F}_q$ . We now turn to the converse problem: assuming that the matrices H and G are constructed as in Section 3.1 and that  $GH^{\mathsf{T}} = 0$  has a solution over  $\mathbb{F}_q$ , how can we construct a concrete ideal linear code that realizes the given access structure? That is, we seek an ideal linear code C with parity-check matrix H that realizes  $\Gamma_{\min}$ .

## <span id="page-19-0"></span>4 Unified Framework and Illustrative Examples

#### <span id="page-19-1"></span>4.1 Construction of a Unified Framework

Brickell and Davenport [\[22\]](#page-30-7) provided a partial characterization of ideal access structures, namely, if an access structure is the port of a matroid representable over some finite field, then the access structure is realized by an ideal linear secret sharing scheme over that field (sufficient condition); if an access structure is ideal, then it is the port of some (unique up to isomorphism) matroid (necessary condition). These sufficient and necessary conditions do not fully coincide; there exists a gap. For example, Seymour [\[33\]](#page-32-1) proved that the access structure induced by the V´amos matroid is not ideal, demonstrating that the necessary condition is not sufficient.

In fact, the converse of the sufficient condition given by Brickell and Davenport also holds: if an access structure can be realized by an ideal linear secret sharing scheme over a finite field Fq, then the matrix corresponding to this scheme defines an Fq-representable matroid, and consequently the access structure is the port of this representable matroid over that field. Moreover, monotone span programs are equivalent to linear secret sharing schemes [\[3,](#page-28-2) [7\]](#page-29-0), and there exists a one-to-one correspondence 

{20}------------------------------------------------

between linear codes and monotone span programs [9]. Therefore, we can prove this converse statement from the perspective of ideal linear codes (see Theorem 8).

Although the connection between an ideal linear secret sharing scheme realizing an access structure and a corresponding representable matroid was already implicit from the perspective of linear codes and matroids in Section 2.3, we still provide an independent proof of this statement (see Theorem 8), mainly in view of the unified framework we are about to establish. Before giving the proof, we present an alternative definition of a matroid, different from Definition 5.

**Definition 10** (Matroid (rank function definition)) A matroid  $\mathcal{M} = (V, r_{\mathcal{M}})$  consists of a finite set V and a function  $r_{\mathcal{M}} : \mathcal{P}(V) \to \mathbb{N}$  satisfying the following three axioms:

- Non-negativity and Boundedness: For any  $X \subseteq V$ ,  $0 \le r_{\mathcal{M}}(X) \le |X|$ .
- Monotonicity: For any  $X \subseteq Y \subseteq V$ ,  $r_{\mathcal{M}}(X) \leq r_{\mathcal{M}}(Y)$ .
- Submodularity:  $r_{\mathcal{M}}(X \cup Y) + r_{\mathcal{M}}(X \cap Y) \leq r_{\mathcal{M}}(X) + r_{\mathcal{M}}(Y)$ .

In a matroid  $\mathcal{M}$ , a subset  $X \subseteq V$  is **independent** if  $r_{\mathcal{M}}(X) = |X|$ ; it is **dependent** if  $r_{\mathcal{M}}(X) < |X|$ . A subset  $A \subseteq V$  is a **circuit** if it is a minimal dependent set (i.e., for every  $a \in A$ ,  $r_{\mathcal{M}}(A) = |A| - 1$  and  $r_{\mathcal{M}}(A - \{a\}) = |A| - 1$ ).

An access structure is realized by an ideal linear secret sharing scheme over F<sup>q</sup>

⇐⇒ GH<sup>T</sup> = 0 has a solution over F<sup>q</sup>

⇐⇒ The access structure is the port of an Fq-representable matroid.

 The significance of this unified framework lies in the fact that, theoretically, analyzing whether an access structure (or a family thereof) can be realized by an ideal linear secret sharing scheme is equivalent to studying whether its corresponding matroid is representable over some field (e.g., the (t, n)-threshold access structure corresponds to the uniform matroid Ut,n+1, and Ut,n+1 is representable over F<sup>q</sup> whenever q ≥ n([\[30,](#page-31-7) p. 202]), and the existence of an ideal linear secret sharing scheme is also equivalent to solving GH<sup>T</sup> = 0 over Fq. This means that we can study the same mathematical object using different tools from different perspectives, thereby providing a computable and verifiable practical tool for systematically addressing the problem of the existence of ideal linear secret sharing schemes for arbitrary access structures.

 Since the necessary and sufficient conditions proposed in this paper are intended to determine whether an access structure can be realized by an ideal linear secret sharing scheme, it follows that for the access structure induced by the V´amos matroid, one can theoretically verify that the matrices G and H constructed from this access structure yield no solution to GH<sup>T</sup> = 0 over any field. This implies that the access structure induced by the V´amos matroid cannot be realized by an ideal linear secret sharing

The problem is 12701271 reduced to the solvability of the system of equations  $GH^{\mathsf{T}} = 0$  associated with a linear 12721273 code of a specific construction over a finite field, thereby providing a universal alge-1274 braic criterion. This condition simultaneously reveals an essential equivalence among 12751276 cryptography, algebraic coding theory, and combinatorics in characterizing the same 12771278 mathematical object. However, this criterion is applicable only to determining whether 1279 a given access structure can be realized by an ideal linear secret sharing scheme; it 12801281 does not address the gap in the sufficient and necessary conditions given by Brickell 12821283 and Davenport [22] concerning access structures that are ports of a matroid but are 1284 not ideal (e.g., the access structure induced by the Vámos matroid), and thus cannot 12851286 determine whether such structures can be realized by ideal nonlinear schemes. Finally, the proposed conditions do not provide a method for selecting an appropriate finite 1288
