# **New Techniques for Information-Theoretic Asynchronous MPC with Abort**

Xiaoyu Ji<sup>1</sup> , Yifan Song<sup>2</sup>

1 jixy23@mails.tsinghua.edu.cn, Tsinghua University 2 yfsong@mail.tsinghua.edu.cn, Tsinghua University and Shanghai Qi Zhi Institute

**Abstract.** We study the communication complexity of information-theoretic asynchronous multiparty computation (AMPC) with optimal resilience *n* = 3*t* + 1 and malicious security. In this setting, the only known result with linear communication per gate is due to Goyal, Liu-Zhang, and Song [CRYPTO '24]. However, their construction incurs a large communication overhead of *Ω*(*n* <sup>14</sup>) elements that is independent of the circuit size, rendering their result only of theoretical interest. By additionally assuming a random oracle, Bandarupalli et al. [CCS '25] reduce the communication overhead to O(*n* 3 ) while maintaining the linear communication per gate, at the cost of only achieving malicious security with fairness.

In this work, we remove the random oracle assumption and design an information-theoretic AMPC protocol that achieves malicious security with abort. The communication complexity of our construction is O(|*C*|*n* + *Dn*<sup>2</sup> + *n* 3 ) field elements for an arithmetic circuit of size |*C*| and depth *D*, assuming a functionality for Agreement on Common Set (ACS).

Our main technical contribution is a novel verification mechanism with the following guarantee: whenever verification succeeds, there exists a subset of at least *t*+ 1 honest parties whose local computations are mutually consistent, and the final output is correct with respect to their computation. In contrast to prior approaches that require all honest parties to hold consistent states and execute identical computations before verification, our mechanism tolerates inconsistencies among honest parties while still ensuring that the verified computation is correct for at least one such honest subset, if the verification succeeds.

# <span id="page-0-0"></span>**1 Introduction**

Secure multi-party computation (MPC) [\[Yao82,](#page-19-0) [GMW87,](#page-19-1) [BGW88,](#page-18-0) [CCD88,](#page-18-1) [RB89\]](#page-19-2) allows a set of *n* parties to jointly evaluate a function over their private inputs while revealing nothing beyond the prescribed output, assuming that at most *t* parties may be corrupted by an adversary.

In the literature, most MPC protocols are designed assuming a synchronous network, where a global synchronous clock is available to all participants and every message is guaranteed to be delivered within a bounded amount of time. This feature simplifies the protocol construction and parties can rely on the strong timing guarantees to coordinate protocol steps. In the real world, however, communication is asynchronous: messages may be arbitrarily delayed due to unstable network connections. The nature of asynchrony introduces a fundamental challenge in the protocol construction: an honest party cannot distinguish between slow but honest participants and faulty participants. As a result, most of MPC protocols in the synchronous setting are no longer secure when running in the asynchronous network. Even for tasks that do not involve secrets, such as Byzantine agreements, asynchronous protocols are much more difficult to construct than the synchronous counterparts. In addition, the optimal resilience also differs between synchronous and asynchronous settings. Specifically, in the information-theoretic setting, statistical security can be achieved in the synchronous model when *t < n/*2 assuming broadcast [\[RB89\]](#page-19-2). In contrast, in the asynchronous model, statistical security requires *t < n/*3 [\[BKR94,](#page-18-2) [ADS20\]](#page-18-3).

Over the past decades, a long line of work [\[BKR94,](#page-18-2) [PCR10,](#page-19-3) [CP17,](#page-18-4) [CP23,](#page-18-5) [AAPP24,](#page-18-6) [GLZS24\]](#page-19-4) has focused on reducing the communication complexity of information-theoretic AMPC with guaranteed output delivery (GOD). In particular, [\[GLZS24\]](#page-19-4) gives the first construction with amortized linear communication per gate. Concretely, for an arithmetic circuit of size |*C*| and depth *D*, the communication complexity of their 

{1}------------------------------------------------

construction is  $\mathcal{O}(|C|n + Dn^2 + n^{14})$  field elements. However, the large communication overhead of  $\mathcal{O}(n^{14})$  elements renders their result a pure theoretic interest, making their construction impractical even for a small number of parties.. While a recent work [JS26] improves [GLZS24] by reducing the communication overhead to  $\mathcal{O}(n^5)$ , it is still the main bottleneck of using AMPC in practice, especially for applications involving many participants.

To achieve a better concrete efficiency, one direction is to consider a weaker notion of malicious security with abort. In this setting, the protocol may abort prematurely but the secrecy of honest parties' inputs and outputs remains. Malicious security with abort has been extensively studied in the synchronous setting. In particular, the best-known information-theoretic synchronous protocols [GS20, GLO+21] with optimal resilience can even achieve  $\mathcal{O}(Cn+n^2)$  elements of communication for security with abort, compared to the best-known result with  $\mathcal{O}(Cn+n^6)$  [GSZ20] for GOD. On the other hand, there has been very little study of asynchronous MPC for malicious security with abort in the literature. One exception is the recent work [BJK+25], which constructs a malicious AMPC with abort<sup>3</sup> and achieves  $\mathcal{O}(|C|n+Dn^2+n^3)$  elements of communication assuming a random oracle, demonstrating the potential of this direction to address the issue of concrete efficiency of AMPC. We note that this  $\mathcal{O}(n^3)$  communication overhead is nearly optimal: intuitively, in case each party needs to perform a broadcast, the communication overhead is at least  $\mathcal{O}(n^3)$  since the lower bound of broadcast in the asynchronous setting is  $\mathcal{O}(n^2)$  [DR85].

This leads to our following question:

Can we construct an information-theoretic AMPC protocol that achieves malicious security with abort in the optimal resilience setting, with linear communication and  $\mathcal{O}(n^3)$  overhead?

### 1.1 Our Contributions

In this work, we answer this question affirmatively. Concretely, we obtain the following result.

**Theorem 1.** Let n=3t+1,  $\kappa$  denote the security parameter,  $\mathbb{F}$  be a finite field of size  $2^{\Omega(\kappa)}$ , and C be an arithmetic circuit over  $\mathbb{F}$  of size |C| and depth D. Let  $\mathcal{F}_{\mathsf{acs}}$  be a functionality for Agreement on Common Set. There is a fully malicious asynchronous MPC protocol in the  $\mathcal{F}_{\mathsf{acs}}$ -hybrid model that securely computes C against at most t corrupted parties with security-with-abort. The achieved communication complexity is  $\mathcal{O}(|C|n+Dn^2+n^3)$  field elements plus  $\mathcal{O}(1)$  instances of  $\mathcal{F}_{\mathsf{acs}}$ .

Main Technical Contributions. In prior constructions of maliciously secure asynchronous MPC [GLZS24, JS26], the verification procedures crucially relies on the primitive of asynchronous complete secret sharing (ACSS), which allows a dealer to distribute degree-t Shamir sharings to all parties and ensures that all honest parties' shares are consistent (in the sense that their shares lie on a valid degree-t polynomial). However, the communication overhead of ACSS also becomes the main bottleneck in the previous constructions. For example, the  $\mathcal{O}(n^{14})$  communication overhead in [GLZS24] is due to  $\mathcal{O}(n^2)$  instances of ACSS [JLS24], each incurs  $\mathcal{O}(n^{12})$  communication overhead. The recent work [JS26] improves [JLS24] and obtain a variant of ACSS that directly generates random degree-t Shamir sharings to all parties with  $\mathcal{O}(n^5)$  communication overhead, and uses it to construct an AMPC protocol with the same communication overhead.

Our construction deviates from [GLZS24, JS26] and introduces a new verification mechanism without relying on ACSS. Very informally, we guarantee that whenever the verification succeeds, there exists a subset of at least t+1 honest parties whose local computations are mutually consistent, and the final output is correct with respect to their computation. Our mechanism thus tolerates inconsistency among honest parties' shares and intermediate states, while still providing a well-defined correctness guarantee. This new perspective enables a communication-efficient verification protocol in the security-with-abort setting.

## <span id="page-2-1"></span>2 Technical Overviews

In this section, we provide a high-level overview of our technique. We use n to denote the number of parties and t to denote the number of corrupted parties. We assume that n=3t+1. We write  $[s]_t$  for a degree-t Shamir sharing of a secret s. The security parameter is denoted by  $\kappa$ , and we require the field size to be  $2^{\Omega(\kappa)}$ . Our goal is to construct an information-theoretic maliciously secure asynchronous MPC protocol in the  $\mathcal{F}_{acs}$ -hybrid model with linear communication per gate and  $\mathcal{O}(n^3)$  communication overhead in terms of field elements.

We begin in Section 2.1 with a variant of the AMPC in [JS26] that achieves only semi-honest security with send omission, where corrupted parties always follow the protocol but may drop their messages sent to honest parties, and the adversary can arbitrarily delay messages between honest parties. In Section 2.2, we identify the main obstacles that arise when extending the above protocol to the malicious setting. Sections 2.3 and 2.4 present our main technical contributions. Finally, in Section 2.5, we discuss some issues in the input/output phase and give our solutions.

# <span id="page-2-0"></span>2.1 Starting Point: Semi-Honest Variant of AMPC in [JS26]

The construction in [JS26] follows from the standard paradigm of constructing AMPC: (1) In the offline phase, all parties prepare a sufficient number of random Beaver triples, each shared using degree-t Shamir sharings. (2) Then in the online phase, all parties follow the standard Beaver triple technique to evaluate the circuit gate by gate. Notably, the online computation only requires public reconstruction of degree-t Shamir sharings, which can be performed with amortized linear communication per gate. Thus, we only focus on the preparation of random Beaver triples below.

We first introduce the semi-honest version of ACSS in [JS26], which follows from the standard technique of using bivariate polynomials. We refer to this protocol as  $\Pi_{Sh}$ .

**Distributing Degree**-t Shamir sharings. The following protocol  $\Pi_{Sh}$  allows a dealer D to distribute  $\mathcal{O}(n)$  degree-t Shamir sharings with  $\mathcal{O}(n^2)$  communication. Thus, the communication per sharing is linear. Suppose D wishes to distribute t+1 degree-t Shamir sharings  $[s_0]_t, \ldots, [s_t]_t$ . All parties do the following steps.

- 1. D samples a random degree-(t, 2t) bivariate polynomial F(x, y) such that  $F(x, -\ell)$  is the degree-t polynomial associated with  $[s_{\ell}]_t$  for all  $\ell \in [0, t]$ . Define F(x, i), F(i, y) as each  $P_i$ 's row and column polynomials.
- 2. **Distributing Columns.** D sends F(i, y) to each  $P_i$ . Parties then execute a reliable agreement protocol to guarantee that at least t+1 honest parties have received their columns, and then terminate the sharing phase.
- 3. Reconstructing Rows. Each  $P_i$  sends the value F(i,j) to party  $P_j$ .  $P_j$  collects values from at least t+1 parties, which suffice to interpolate the degree-t row polynomial F(x,j).
- 4. **Reconstructing Columns.** After reconstructing F(x,i), each  $P_i$  sends F(j,i) to  $P_j$ .  $P_j$  collects values from at least 2t+1 parties, which suffice to interpolate the degree-2t column polynomial F(j,y). This allows  $P_j$  to compute  $F(j,-\ell)$  as his share of  $[s_\ell]_t$  for all  $\ell \in [0,t]$ .

**Preparation of Random Beaver Triples.** Recall that a random Beaver triple is in the form of  $([a]_t, [b]_t, [c]_t)$ , where a, b are random field elements, and  $c = a \cdot b$ . In [JS26], the authors propose an efficient approach for preparing random Beaver triples with linear communication and  $\mathcal{O}(n^3)$  overhead by combining the party virtualization technique [Bra87] and the triple extraction technique [CP17]. We give a brief review as follows.

Triple Extraction from [CP17]: Suppose the parties jointly hold N random Beaver triples  $\{([a_i]_t, [b_i]_t, [c_i]_t)\}_{i=1}^N$ , among which at most  $T \leq (N-1)/2$  are known to the adversary. The technique of [CP17] shows how to extract fresh random Beaver triples from this pool.

Let  $L = \frac{N-1}{2}$ . The protocol uses the N input triples to define three shared polynomials f, g, h such that  $h = f \cdot g$ , where f and g have degree L and h has degree 2L. Then, for any evaluation point  $\alpha$ ,  $(f(\alpha), g(\alpha), h(\alpha))$  forms a valid Beaver triple.

{3}------------------------------------------------

To be more concrete, the polynomial f and g are defined by  $f(i) = a_i, g(i) = b_i$  for all  $i \in \{1, \ldots, L+1\}$ . All parties can locally compute shared polynomials  $[f(\cdot)]_t, [g(\cdot)]_t$  from  $\{([a_i]_t, [b_i]_t)\}_{i=1}^{L+1}$ . As for the polynomial h, all parties set  $[h(i)]_t = [c_i]_t$  for all  $i \in \{1, \ldots, L+1\}$ , and then compute  $[h(i)]_t = [f(i) \cdot g(i)]_t$  for all  $i \in \{L+2, \ldots, N\}$  by using the i-th random Beaver triple  $([a_i]_t, [b_i]_t, [c_i]_t)$ . In this way, all parties can locally compute the shared polynomial  $[h(\cdot)]_t$  as well.

Since f and g are determined by L+1 points and the adversary learns at most T random Beaver triples, the parties can extract at most L+1-T fresh random Beaver triples by evaluating the polynomials at another L+1-T points (different from  $1,\ldots,N$ ). The achieved amortized communication per fresh random Beaver triple is  $\mathcal{O}\left(\frac{Nn}{N-2T+1}\right)$  field elements. In [CP17], this yields a quadratic communication per triple since their construction only guarantees N=2t+1 and T=t.

Techniques in [JS26]: Building on [CP17], the authors of [JS26] show how to increase the ratio of unknown triples to the adversary so that T/N < 1/4, which suffices to achieve linear communication. Their main technique is party virtualization [Bra87].

Specifically, each ordered pair of parties  $(P_i, P_j)$  simulates a virtual party  $V_{i,j}$  whose view is additively shared between  $P_i$  and  $P_j$ . Note that as long as one of  $P_i$  or  $P_j$  is honest, the view of  $V_{i,j}$  remains hidden from the adversary. This results in  $N' = n^2$  virtual parties, among which at most  $T' = t^2$  are corrupted. Using these virtual parties, the protocol generates a pool of random Beaver triples as follows.

- Stage 1: Each virtual party  $V_{i,j}$  prepares a random Beaver triple and invokes  $\Pi_{Sh}$  to distribute it to all parties. In [JS26], this step can be emulated by  $(P_i, P_j)$  with the help of all other parties with O(n) communication.
- Stage 2: The parties agree on a subset of N virtual parties that successfully completed the distribution, and apply the extraction procedure of [CP17] to obtain  $\Theta(N)$  fresh random Beaver triples.

Regarding the achievable value of N, note that a virtual party  $V_{i,j}$  may terminate only when both  $P_i$  and  $P_j$  are honest. Since at least 2t+1 parties are honest, N can be set to be  $\frac{4}{9}N'$ . Consequently, the protocol yields  $N = \frac{4}{9}N'$  triples, among which at most  $T = T' \leq \frac{1}{9}N' = \frac{1}{4}N$  are known to the adversary.

For simplicity, we defer the details and some optimizations for the whole protocol to Section 4. In summary, assuming a functionality  $\mathcal{F}_{acs}$  that helps the parties to agree on a common set of N virtual parties in **Stage 2**, the construction in [JS26] realizes a triple generation protocol with linear communication per triple and  $\mathcal{O}(n^3)$  overhead. Together with the online phase, this yields an AMPC with linear communication and  $\mathcal{O}(n^3)$  overhead that achieves semi-honest security with send omission.

### <span id="page-3-0"></span>2.2 Towards Malicious Security

In [JS26], the authors show that the above template can achieve malicious security with guaranteed output delivery when replace  $\Pi_{Sh}$  by a maliciously secure ACSS. To this end, the authors improve the ACSS construction in [JLS24] and obtain a variant of ACSS that directly generates random degree-t Shamir sharings to all parties with  $\mathcal{O}(n^5)$  communication overhead, which leads to their final AMPC protocol with linear communication and  $\mathcal{O}(n^5)$  overhead.

Recall that our focus is the malicious security with abort. To maintain the  $\mathcal{O}(n^3)$  overhead as the semi-honest variant, a natural idea is to lift the security of  $\Pi_{\mathtt{Sh}}$  to malicious security with abort while maintaining the linear communication and  $\mathcal{O}(n^2)$  overhead. Unfortunately, we do not know how this can be achieved. When running  $\Pi_{\mathtt{Sh}}$  in the fully malicious setting, a corrupted party  $P_i$  may send a wrong common point F(i,j) (or F(j,i)) to  $P_j$ . As a result, honest parties may end up with incorrect shares even if the dealer D is honest.

One may attempt to adapt the synchronous approach to verify the shares of honest parties: first invoke the semi-honest secure  $\Pi_{Sh}$  to distribute shares, and then let all parties publicly reconstruct a random linear combination of their shares so that each party can locally check the correctness of his shares. Unfortunately, this approach fails in the asynchronous setting due to the adversary's ability to delay honest parties' messages.

In the asynchronous model, if the random challenge r is released before all honest parties' shares are received, then an adversary may decide the errors added on honest parties' shares based on the random

{4}------------------------------------------------

challenge *r* so that honest parties still receive incorrect shares but the verification passes. However, to ensure liveness, parties can only wait for responses from 2*t* + 1 parties. Thus, in the worst case, the adversary can selectively delay the delivery of up to *t* honest parties' shares while still allowing the protocol to proceed and generate the challenge. As a result, only *t* + 1 honest parties will obtain their shares before the random challenge *r* is generated, while the rest of honest parties may only receive their shares after *r* is released. This issue essentially nullifies all verifications based on random challenges.

Our attempt is to directly use *Π*Sh without ensuring consistency of shares among honest parties.

**Problems of Using** *Π*Sh **Directly.** However, this attempt brings new difficulties in achieving malicious security.

*Attack on the Public Reconstruction Procedure*: Since honest parties may hold inconsistent shares, they can no longer rely on the error-correction property of degree-*t* Shamir sharings to guarantee a unique public reconstruction. This issue becomes particularly harmful in the online phase, where parties must perform public reconstruction when using Beaver triples to evaluate multiplication gates. If honest parties proceed with different reconstruction results, the authors in [\[GLS19\]](#page-19-10) show a *double dipping* attack, which allows the adversary to extract information about honest parties' private inputs. We discuss how to address this issue in Section [2.4](#page-7-0) later.

*Attack on the Triple Verification*: The triple generation protocol in [\[JS26\]](#page-19-5) does not naturally achieve malicious security. To verify the correctness of all random Beaver triples, the idea in [\[JS26\]](#page-19-5) is to reuse the shared polynomials *f, g, h* and verify the multiplication relation on a random evaluation point. Similar to the attempt of verifying honest parties' shares, this verification no longer works in our attempt either. We illustrate a concrete attack below in which verification succeeds at a random point *r* even though *h* ̸≡ *f* · *g*. Without loss of generality, suppose that the first *t* + 1 honest parties receive their shares from *Π*Sh, while the adversary delays the remaining honest parties' shares.

- 1. All parties generate a random challenge *r* and reconstruct [*f*(*r*)]*t,* [*g*(*r*)]*t,* [*h*(*r*)]*t*.
- 2. The adversary collects the shares of [*f*(*r*)]*t,* [*g*(*r*)]*t,* [*h*(*r*)]*<sup>t</sup>* from these *t*+ 1 honest parties and interpolates the corresponding secrets.
- 3. The adversary computes *e* = *f*(*r*) · *g*(*r*) − *h*(*r*) and defines a degree-*t* sharing [*e*]*<sup>t</sup>* whose secret is *e* and whose shares for the first *t* honest parties are zero.
- 4. The adversary adds the remaining share of [*e*]*<sup>t</sup>* to the last honest party's share of [*h*(*r*)]*t*, resulting in a sharing [*h* ′ (*r*)]*<sup>t</sup>* = [*h*(*r*)]*<sup>t</sup>* + [*e*]*<sup>t</sup>* = [*f*(*r*) · *g*(*r*)]*t.* Then the adversary inserts errors on the last honest party's shares so that he would obtain his share of [*h* ′ (*r*)]*t*.
- 5. During verification, the adversary delays messages from honest parties except the first *t* honest parties and the last honest party. Then the adversary uses corrupted parties' shares of [*f*(*r*)]*t,* [*g*(*r*)]*t,* [*h* ′ (*r*)]*t*. As a result, the first *t* honest parties and the last honest party will reconstruct *f*(*r*)*, g*(*r*), and *h* ′ (*r*), and therefore incorrectly accept the triples.

*Remark 1.* A careful reader may notice that in the above attack, with overwhelming probability, the (*t* + 1) th honest party will observe inconsistency with his share of [*h*(*r*)]*<sup>t</sup>* and the one [*h* ′ (*r*)]*<sup>t</sup>* reconstructed by all parties. Then this honest party would abort and notify other parties. However, the notification may be delayed by the adversary. As a result, even if an honest party observes incorrect computation, other honest parties may continue the computation.

**Our Idea.** Instead of attempting to enforce global consistency among all honest parties, which is fundamentally difficult in the asynchronous setting, we design verification protocols that certify the correctness of computation with respect to a subset of *t* + 1 honest parties.

Concretely, successful verification guarantees that there exists a subset of *t* + 1 honest parties such that all triples produced in the offline phase are correct with respect to their shares. In the online phase, there also exists a subset of *t* + 1 honest parties such that the online evaluation proceeds correctly using these triples, with all public reconstruction results consistent with the shares held by this subset.

{5}------------------------------------------------

Crucially, the protocol neither identifies such a subset nor allows parties to distinguish which subset satisfies this property. Rather, verification only establishes the existence of such a subset. The subset of honest parties during the offline and online verification can also be different. Moreover, if multiple subsets of t+1 honest parties satisfy the verification conditions, then the secrets interpolated from all such subsets must be identical.

Additive Errors. Before moving on to the details of our construction, we clarify that although  $\Pi_{Sh}$  allows the adversary to insert errors on honest parties' shares *after* the sharing phase, when the secret is unknown to the adversary (e.g., a random sharing distributed by an honest dealer), any modification can only introduce *additive errors*. Concretely, if an honest party reconstructs a row or column polynomial, a corrupted party may alter some of the shares it receives from this party. Since the reconstruction is a linear combination of these shares, the effect of the adversary's modification manifests as an additive error on the resulting polynomial, and consequently, on the party's output shares.

### <span id="page-5-0"></span>2.3 Offline Verification

Our goal is to guarantee that once all parties pass the verification, there exists t+1 honest parties such that the triples interpolated by their shares are valid. Then we define the whole sharing of the verified triples based on these t+1 honest parties' shares, and for the rest of the honest parties who may hold inconsistent shares, the adversary can only insert additive errors at any time later. Our verification will ensure that if multiple subsets of t+1 honest parties satisfy the verification conditions, the triples interpolated from all such subsets are identical.

The Key Observation. Revisiting the triple verification procedure, during public reconstruction parties obtain 2t + 1 shares of  $\{[f(r)]_t, [g(r)]_t, [h(r)]_t\}$ , among which at least t + 1 shares come from honest parties. If these t + 1 honest parties receive their triple shares before the random challenge r is generated, then this verification is sound since the polynomials f, g, h determined by these t + 1 honest parties' shares are already fixed before r is generated.

For the attack on verification in Section 2.2, it succeeds because the adversary can insert additive errors into the shares of an honest party who receives his triple shares after the random challenge r is generated. Once an honest party has received his shares, the adversary can no longer modify them by introducing new additive errors.

This observation suggests that if the adversary aims to prevent the existence of any subset of t+1 honest parties whose shares interpolate to correct triples, it must inject additive errors into the shares of a new honest party who has not yet received his triple shares at the time the random challenge is generated. Thus, our idea is to repeat the above verification t+1 times sequentially, each with a fresh random challenge. Then in each repetition, the adversary has to deliver the shares to a new honest party (or otherwise f(r), g(r), h(r) would not satisfy the multiplication relation with overwhelming probability and all honest parties would abort). Since there are 2t+1 honest parties and at least t+1 honest parties have received their shares before the first repetition, the adversary can carry out this attack for at most t repetitions. After all honest parties have received their shares, the attack no longer applies.

Ignoring the cost of generating random coins, each verification requires public reconstruction of the sharings  $\{[f(r)]_t, [g(r)]_t, [h(r)]_t\}$ , which costs  $\mathcal{O}(n^2)$  field elements. Repeating this procedure for  $t+1 = \mathcal{O}(n)$  rounds results in a total communication complexity of  $\mathcal{O}(n^3)$  field elements.

A Subtle Issue. For a computationally unbounded adversary, after learning the challenge r in a verification, the adversary can check whether there exists a subset of t+1 honest parties whose shares have been delivered, and the secrets  $\{f(r),g(r),h(r)\}$  interpolated from their shares satisfy the multiplication relation. If such a subset exists, the adversary no longer needs to insert errors into a new honest party's shares, but can ensure that  $\{f(r),g(r),h(r)\}$  are reconstructed using shares from these t+1 honest parties and still pass the verification. When  $|\mathbb{F}|=2^{\Omega(\kappa)}$ , the probability of this event in a single verification is at most  $\binom{2t+1}{t+1}/|\mathbb{F}|\approx 2^{n-\kappa}$ .

{6}------------------------------------------------

To make this probability negligible in *κ*, each verification now has to test multiple independent random challenges instead of a single one. Concretely, parties use *n*+*κ κ* random challenges in each verification. For any fixed subset of *t* + 1 honest parties, the probability that accidental consistency occurs is then at most 2 −(*n*+*κ*) . Taking a union bound over all such subsets yields an overall soundness error of at most 2 −*κ* .

For communication complexity, the above modification introduces additional O(*n* 4 )-bit communication. In the following, we present an optimization that eliminates this overhead.

**Initial Attempt.** We first consider the following verification procedure, which is sufficient to achieve our goal for communication complexity. We also no longer need to generate public random coins.

- **– Proposing Random Challenges.** Upon obtaining their shares of triples, each party *P<sup>i</sup>* reliably broadcasts *n*+*κ κ* distinct random challenges.
- **– Verification.** Upon receiving a challenge *r* from *P<sup>i</sup>* , each party sends its shares of {[*f*(*r*)]*t,* [*g*(*r*)]*t,* [*h*(*r*)]*t*} to *P<sup>i</sup>* . Once *P<sup>i</sup>* receives 2*t* + 1 shares (including its own), it checks whether these shares lie on unique degree-*t* polynomials and the secrets satisfy the correct multiplication relation. If not, *P<sup>i</sup>* sends a failure symbol (⊥*, Pi*) to all parties and aborts. Otherwise, *P<sup>i</sup>* sends (OK*, Pi*) to all parties.
- **– Termination.** Upon receiving (OK*, Pi*) from 2*t*+ 1 distinct parties *P<sup>i</sup>* , a party terminates with Success. If a failure symbol (⊥*, Pi*) is received first, the party aborts.

However, this construction is insecure due to the fact that all verifications are performed in parallel rather than sequentially. As a result, the adversary can first learn the random challenges proposed by multiple honest parties and then adaptively inject additive errors into honest parties' shares so as to satisfy all verifications simultaneously. Then different honest verifiers may rely on different subsets of shares, and the protocol may terminate successfully even though no single set of *t* + 1 honest parties holds consistent triple shares.

To rule out this attack, we keep the same local verification procedure but add a structured acceptance process on top of it. Each successful verification is assigned an explicit acceptance level, and advancement to higher levels is permitted only after collecting sufficiently many acceptances at the previous level. As we show next, this iterative structure restores the existential guarantee that was missing in the above attempt.

**Iterative Acceptance.** Each party executes the same verification procedure as before (excluding the termination condition). In the following, whenever a party fails in the verification or receives a failure symbol ⊥, it forwards ⊥ to all parties and aborts. Each party *P<sup>i</sup>* initializes a local counter cnt*<sup>i</sup>* ← 0.

- 1. **Initialization.** Whenever *P<sup>i</sup>* successfully completes a verification for all its own *n*+*κ κ* distinct challenge *r*, it reconstructs and locally records the whole degree-*t* sharings {[*f*(*r*)]*t,* [*g*(*r*)]*t,* [*h*(*r*)]*t*}. It then sends (OK*, P<sup>i</sup> ,* cnt*i*) to all parties.
- 2. **Iterative Acceptance.** Upon receiving (OK*, P*∗*,* cnt∗) together with shares of {[*f*(*r*)]*t,* [*g*(*r*)]*t,* [*h*(*r*)]*t*} from 2*t* + 1 distinct parties *P*<sup>∗</sup> that all report the same counter value cnt<sup>∗</sup> as cnt*<sup>i</sup>* , party *P<sup>i</sup>* checks whether these shares are consistent with the degree-*t* sharings fixed during its own initialization. If so, *Pi* increments its counter cnt*<sup>i</sup>* ← cnt*<sup>i</sup>* + 1 and sends (OK*, P<sup>i</sup> ,* cnt*i*) to all parties. This step may repeat multiple times and does not rely on synchrony.
- 3. **Termination.** When *P<sup>i</sup>* reaches cnt*<sup>i</sup>* = *t* + 2, it terminates with Success.

**Correctness and Termination.** We show that if an honest party *P<sup>i</sup>* terminates with Success, then there exists an honest party *P<sup>k</sup>* whose verification relies on at least *t* + 1 honest parties who received their shares before *P<sup>k</sup>* proposes the random challenge.

Suppose an honest party *P<sup>i</sup>* reaches cnt*<sup>i</sup>* = *t* + 2, consider the set S of 2*t* + 1 parties *P*<sup>∗</sup> whose messages (OK*, P*∗*, t* + 1) caused *P<sup>i</sup>* to increment its counter from *t* + 1 to *t* + 2. Let *P<sup>k</sup>*<sup>0</sup> be the honest party in S that is the *last* (according to the adversarially controlled delivery order) to receive his shares. Since *P<sup>k</sup>*<sup>0</sup> sent (OK*, P<sup>k</sup>*<sup>0</sup> *, t* + 1), then there exists a set S ′ of 2*t* + 1 parties whose messages (OK*, P*∗*, t*) caused this increment at *P<sup>k</sup>*<sup>0</sup> .

If at least *t* + 1 honest parties in S ′ received their shares before *P<sup>k</sup>*<sup>0</sup> , then *P<sup>k</sup>*<sup>0</sup> is the target party *Pk*. Otherwise, there exists an honest party *P<sup>k</sup>*<sup>1</sup> in S ′ that receives its shares after *P<sup>k</sup>*<sup>0</sup> . In this case, we can apply the same argument recursively to *P<sup>k</sup>*<sup>1</sup> . In the *ℓ*th iteration, either the honest party *P<sup>k</sup><sup>ℓ</sup>* is the target 

{7}------------------------------------------------

party  $P_k$  or there exists another honest party  $P_{k_{\ell+1}}$  that receives shares after  $P_{k_{\ell}}$ . At each step, the counter value strictly decreases by one, and there are at most t honest parties that will receive their shares after  $P_{k_0}$ . Therefore, after at most t+1 iterations, the process must reach an honest party whose acceptance relies on at least t+1 honest parties that received their shares earlier.

Termination follows since all honest parties eventually respond to each counter value. For the communication complexity, it costs  $\mathcal{O}(n^3)$  field elements plus  $\mathcal{O}(n)$  instances of reliably broadcast, and the input size for each broadcast protocol is  $n \left\lceil \frac{n+\kappa}{\kappa} \right\rceil$  field elements. Refer to section 4.4 for detailed construction.

**Summary.** The above procedure establishes the following guarantee for offline triple verification: whenever the verification succeeds, there exists a subset of at least t+1 honest parties whose shares define polynomials f, g, h satisfying the multiplication relation  $f \cdot g = h$ . Consequently, the triples are well-defined with respect to this subset, while the remaining honest parties' shares can be interpreted as evaluations of these polynomials with additive errors that may be introduced by the adversary.

Importantly, we do not require global consistency among all honest parties. Correctness is certified solely through the existence of such a consistent subset. In the subsequent online phase, all parties rely on these well-defined triples for circuit evaluation. Since global consistency is not enforced, parties perform another verification in the online phase to ensure that the triples used in computation are indeed correct, which will be described later.

Remark 2. We stress that the above verification technique can not be used in the semi-honest secure  $\Pi_{Sh}$  to achieve a maliciously secure ACSS. In  $\Pi_{Sh}$ , the dealer's secret is not uniquely fixed at the end of the sharing phase: due to possible additive errors inserted by the adversary, different subsets of t+1 honest parties may interpolate to different secrets. Our verification mechanism only guarantees the existence of a subset of t+1 honest parties whose views are mutually consistent.

In contrast, during offline triple verification, we do not aim to fix the specific values of the polynomials f, g, h. Instead, we only verify that there exists a subset of t+1 honest parties whose shares can interpolate valid polynomials f, g, h such that  $f \cdot g = h$ . The correctness guarantee is therefore relational rather than value-fixing. This distinction is crucial: while existential consistency suffices for verifying multiplication relations, it is insufficient for achieving maliciously secure secret sharing, where a unique dealer secret must be determined.

Omitted Difficulty in the Security Proof. Let f,g,h be the polynomials that are used to extract the random triples during the offline phase, and we use L to denote the degree of f,g. During the generation of f,g,h in the semi-honest secure construction, for  $\ell \in [L+2,2L+1]$ , the adversary can choose values  $\delta_{\ell}^{(f)}, \delta_{\ell}^{(g)}, \delta_{\ell}^{(h)}$  and insert an error  $\delta_{\ell}^{(f)} \cdot f(\ell) + \delta_{\ell}^{(g)} \cdot g(\ell) + \delta_{\ell}^{(h)}$  to  $h(\ell)$ . Then, during the simulation of the offline verification, before learning the final verification result, it does not know whether  $f \cdot g = h$  holds. But it needs to respond to the adversary's chosen challenge r, which contains the information a linear combination of  $\{\delta_{\ell}^{(f)} \cdot f(\ell) + \delta_{\ell}^{(g)} \cdot g(\ell) + \delta_{\ell}^{(h)}\}_{\ell \in [L+2,2L+1]}$ . In the security proof, we carefully analyze that this linear combination would not reveal any information about the output triples or give the adversary a noticeable advantage to let honest parties' verification succeed, but  $f \cdot g \neq h$ .

### <span id="page-7-0"></span>2.4 Online Evaluation and Verification Procedure

Recall that when parties use a Beaver triple  $\{[a]_t, [b]_t, [c]_t\}$  to evaluate a multiplication gate with input wires  $([x]_t, [y]_t)$ , they only publicly reconstruct the values x + a and y + b. Our goal in the online phase is to guarantee that, once all parties pass the verification, there exist t + 1 honest parties such that their shares of triples interpolate valid triples fixed by the offline verification, and their shares of  $[x + a]_t$  and  $[y + b]_t$  interpolate the public reconstruction values x + a and y + b used throughout the computation.

To improve the efficiency, we first defer the verification until the final output reconstruction and do verification for all circuit layers in parallel. As discussed in Section 2.2, directly using the semi-honest secure public reconstruction is not sufficient in the malicious setting, since there is no guarantee that all honest parties use the same public reconstruction values x+a and y+b during the computation. This may lead to the double dipping attack [GLS19], which compromises security. This issue also appears in prior work [BJK<sup>+</sup>25],

{8}------------------------------------------------

where it is shown that as long as all honest parties use the same reconstruction values *x* + *a* and *y* + *b* for computation, privacy is preserved even if the adversary injects errors into these values to break the correctness.

Therefore, we first focus on modifying the public reconstruction procedure so that all parties either terminate with the same reconstruction results or abort. Once this is ensured, the remaining verification can be safely deferred to the final stage.

**Guaranteeing a Unique Public Reconstruction.** To illustrate the idea, consider first the reconstruction of a single value *x*. Each party sends its reconstructed value to all parties and accepts *x* if it receives the same value from 2*t*+ 1 parties. Then, for any two honest parties that terminate, there must exist at least one honest party whose message is received by both, implying that they accept the same reconstruction result.

When multiple reconstruction values need to be verified, we reduce the communication complexity by checking a random linear combination of these values. To ensure soundness, each party uses fresh pairwise randomness: party *P<sup>i</sup>* samples a random value for each *P<sup>j</sup>* , sends the randomness together with the corresponding linear combination of its reconstruction results, and *P<sup>j</sup>* independently verifies consistency using its own reconstruction results. Since the pairwise randomness between two honest parties is unknown to the adversary at the time of reconstruction, the adversary cannot adaptively forge inconsistent reconstruction values that pass all checks, which guarantees soundness. Refer to Section [4.2](#page-11-0) for detailed construction.

**Online Verification.** Now all parties can first securely evaluate the circuit using the above technique, during which all public reconstruction values *x* + *a, y* + *b* are fixed. To achieve our verification goal, it is sufficient to check a random linear combination of all sharings {[*x* + *a*]*t,* [*y* + *b*]*t*} generated in the online phase and verify that the used Beaver triples are valid.

For the verification of the random linear combination of all sharings {[*x* + *a*]*t,* [*y* + *b*]*t*}, although the adversary can still inject additive errors into some honest parties' shares, the procedure is analogous to triple verification in the offline phase: If a subset of *t* + 1 honest parties received their shares before the random challenge is generated, then either the public reconstruction results are correct with respect to their shares, or the verification fails for this subset of parties with overwhelming probability. Thus, if the adversary aims to prevent the existence of any subset of *t* + 1 honest parties whose shares are correct with respect to the publication results, it must inject additive errors into the shares of a new honest party and deliver the shares to this party. We can therefore apply the same technique to perform online verification. In addition, to ensure that the online computation and the Beaver triples are correct with respect to the *same* subset of *t* + 1 parties. All parties will verify the random Beaver triples again in parallel with the verification of the public reconstruction.

With more details, each party proposes a random challenge *r* and verifies a linear combination of all {[*x* + *a*]*t,* [*y* + *b*]*t*} in the online phase based on *r* and the relation *f*(*r*)*, g*(*r*)*, h*(*r*), where *f, g, h* are the polynomials generated in the offline phase. They follow the same iterative acceptance to decide the verification results. Once verification succeeds, we know that there exist *t*+1 honest parties whose computation is correct, meaning their output shares are all correct. For the remaining honest parties, the adversary can only insert additive errors into their output shares. In this way, the secret of the output is well-defined by the *t*+1 honest parties with correct shares, and all parties can securely reconstruct the output later. Refer to Section [5.1](#page-16-0) for the detailed construction.

**Summary.** Once the online verification succeeds, all parties learn that there exist *t*+ 1 honest parties whose shares can interpolate both correct triples {*a, b, c*} and the intermediate values {*x* + *a, y* + *b*}. With correct triples and these consistent public reconstruction values, the secret {*x, y*} of each circuit wire is also well defined. Thus, these *t* + 1 honest parties' computations are all correct throughout the whole computation, and the output is well-defined as the secret interpolated from these *t* + 1 honest parties. Note that if there exist multiple such subsets of honest parties that can pass the verification, their shares must interpolate the same secrets values.

**A Subtle Issue.** One subtle point is that, when using a triple {*a, b, c*} to evaluate the circuit, the randomness of {*a, b, c*} is consumed when the public reconstruction values {*x* + *a, y* + *b*} are released. Consider the worst case where all inputs *x, y* are known to the adversary (e.g. circuit's inputs are all from corrupted parties), so 

{9}------------------------------------------------

the adversary can learn the full triples {*a, b, c*}. During triple verification, although we allow the adversary to insert additive errors, these errors can now depend on the secrets.

As a result, the adversary can inject additive errors *δ* (*a*) *, δ*(*b*) to *a* and *b*, and *δ* (*b*) · *a*+*δ* (*a*) · *b*+*δ* (*a*) · *δ* (*b*) to *c*, effectively shifting secrets of the triple from {*a, b, c*} to {*a* ′ *, b*′ *, c*′}. By construction, {*a* ′ *, b*′ *, c*′} is still a valid triple. However, if online verification only guarantees the existence of *t* + 1 honest parties interpolating a valid triple, this is insufficient: the valid triple {*a* ′ *, b*′ *, c*′} may differ from the triple {*a, b, c*} used to compute *x* + *a* and *y* + *b*. In that case, the adversary could arbitrarily adjust the secrets of all circuit wires, and the output would not be well-defined.

Therefore, parties must verify that the *t* + 1 honest parties not only interpolate a valid triple, but also interpolate the triples fixed in the offline phase. This ensures that all circuit wires *x* and *y* are fixed since all *x* + *a, y* + *b, a, b* are fixed. To achieve this, we rely on the fact that there are sufficiently many random points on *f, g, h* unknown to the adversary. Concretely, let *L* denote the degree of *f, g* and *h* = *f* · *g*. As shown in Section [2.1,](#page-2-0) given *T < N/*4, there are approximately *L* + 1 − *T* ≈ *N/*4 random points on *f, g, h* unknown to the adversary. If we select only half of these points as output triples, then even after the adversary learns all *N/*8 evaluation points used, there remain *N/*8 unknown points, preventing the adversary from interpolating *f, g, h* completely. Since each party verifies a random point on *f, g, h* to check *f* · *g* = *h*, the verification of triples remains sound, and learning the used triples does not compromise it.

# <span id="page-9-0"></span>**2.5 Input and Output Phase**

**Input Phase.** A subtle issue here is that using *Π*Sh is insufficient for distributing inputs because the adversary can insert additive errors to bias an honest dealer's input. Moreover, *Π*Sh does not fix a corrupted dealer's input secret after the sharing phase, so the simulator cannot extract a corrupted dealer's input during simulation.

To address this, we assign an unused Beaver triple {[*a*]*t,* [*b*]*t,* [*c*]*t*} from the offline phase to each party *P<sup>i</sup>* 's input *x*. All parties send their shares of this triple to *P<sup>i</sup>* . Using any *t* + 1 received shares, *P<sup>i</sup>* interpolates the secrets {*a, b, c*} and checks that *a* · *b* = *c*. If the check fails, *P<sup>i</sup>* detects a deviation from the protocol by some corrupted party and aborts. Otherwise, *P<sup>i</sup>* reliably broadcasts the value *x* + *a*, and each party can locally compute its share of *x* as [*x*]*<sup>t</sup>* := (*x* + *a*) − [*a*]*t*.

During the online verification phase, the assigned triple is also verified. If some honest parties successfully completes the online verification, then, as argued in Section [2.4,](#page-7-0) the secret *a* is fixed during the offline phase, so the input *x* is uniquely determined by the public value *x* + *a*. In particular, when *P<sup>i</sup>* is honest, since the Input phase happens before the Evaluation phase, a correct triple {[*a*]*t,* [*b*]*t,* [*c*]*t*} must be the one fixed from the offline verification.

**Output Phase.** We assume all parties receive the same output. As shown in Section [2.4,](#page-7-0) once parties succeed in the online verification, the output is well-defined (with respective to some subset of *t* + 1 honest parties). Since *Π*Sh is secure up to additive errors, the secret interpolated from any subset of *t* + 1 honest parties can only deviate from the correct secret by an error learnt by the adversary. Thus, it is safe to perform public reconstruction to obtain the circuit output without any secrecy issue. However, to ensure the correctness of the reconstruction, all parties will execute the online verification procedure again.

# 3.2 Shamir Secret Sharing Scheme

In this work, we will use the standard Shamir Secret Sharing Scheme [Sha79]. Let n be the number of parties and  $\mathbb{F}$  be a finite field of size  $|\mathbb{F}| \geq 2n$ . Let  $\alpha_0, \ldots, \alpha_n$  be n+1 distinct non-zero elements in  $\mathbb{F}$ .

A degree-d Shamir sharing of  $x \in \mathbb{F}$  is a vector  $(x_1, \ldots, x_n)$  which satisfies that there exists a polynomial  $f(\cdot) \in \mathbb{F}[X]$  of degree at most d such that  $f(\alpha_0) = x$  and  $f(\alpha_i) = x_i$  for  $i \in \{1, \ldots, n\}$ . Each party  $P_i$  holds a share  $x_i$  and the whole sharing is denoted by  $[x]_d$ . We recall the properties of the Shamir secret sharing scheme:

- Linear Homomorphism:

$$\forall [x]_d, [y]_d, [x+y]_d = [x]_d + [y]_d.$$

- Multiplying two degree-d sharings yields a degree-2d sharing. The secret value of the new sharing is the product of the original two secrets.

$$\forall [x]_d, [y]_d, [x \cdot y]_{2d} = [x]_d \cdot [y]_d.$$

# <span id="page-10-0"></span>4 Triple Generation

In this section, we give the construction of our triple generation protocol  $\Pi_{\text{Triple}}$ , which achieves amortized linear communication per triple plus  $\mathcal{O}(n^3)$  additive overhead in terms of field elements. We first introduce the detailed construction of the semi-honest secure secret sharing protocol  $\Pi_{\text{Sh}}$  in Section 2.1, then give the consistent public reconstruction protocol  $\Pi_{\text{PubRec}}$  in Section 4.2, finally define  $\mathcal{F}_{\text{Triple}}$ , and the detailed construction of  $\Pi_{\text{Triple}}$ .

### 4.1 Secret Sharing Protocol

Let N be the number of degree-t Shamir secret sharings to be distributed, the communication complexity of  $\Pi_{Sh}$  is  $\mathcal{O}(Nn+n^2)$  field elements. As we discussed in Section 2.3, during the execution of  $\Pi_{Sh}$ , the malicious adversary can insert additive errors to honest parties' shares even *after* the sharing phase.

<span id="page-10-1"></span>In this work, we do not count the cost of  $\mathcal{F}_{acs}$  since our construction is built in  $\mathcal{F}_{acs}$ -hybrid model.

{11}------------------------------------------------

## Protocol $\Pi_{\operatorname{Sh}}$

**Parameter:** Let N be the number of degree-t Shamir sharings to be distributed, N' = N/(t+1), and  $\beta_0, \ldots, \beta_t, \alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_n$  be distinct field elements.

**Input:** Dealer D takes N degree-t Shamir secret sharing  $[s_1]_t, \ldots, [s_N]_t$  as inputs.

## Distributing Phase

- 1: D divides  $[s_1]_t, \ldots, [s_N]_t$  into N' groups, each of size t+1. Let  $[s_0^{(\ell)}]_t, \ldots, [s_t^{(\ell)}]_t$  be the sharing in the  $\ell$ th group, D randomly samples a degree-(t, 2t) bivariate polynomials  $F_\ell(x, y)$  such that  $F_\ell(x, \beta_k) = [s_k^{(\ell)}]_t$  for all  $k \in [0, t]$ .
- 2: **Distributing Column Polynomials.** For each  $\ell \in [N']$  and  $P_i \in \mathcal{P}$ , D computes degree-2t column polynomial  $g_{\ell,i}(y) = F_{\ell}(\alpha_i, y)$  and sends it to  $P_i$ .
- 3: **Termination.** All parties jointly invoke an instance of  $\mathcal{F}_{ra}$ . Upon receiving the degree-2t column polynomials from the dealer D, all parties set their input as 1 to  $\mathcal{F}_{ra}$ . Upon terminating  $\mathcal{F}_{ra}$  with 1, all parties terminate the distributing phase and proceed to the recovery phase.

### **Recovery Phase**

- 4: Reconstruction of Row Polynomials. Upon receiving  $\{g_{\ell,i}(y)\}_{\ell\in[N']}$  from D, each party  $P_i$  sends  $\{g_{\ell,i}(\alpha_j)\}_{\ell\in[N']}$  to each party  $P_j$ . Upon receiving  $\{g_{\ell,j}(\alpha_i)\}_{\ell\in[N']}$  from t+1 distinct  $P_j$ ,  $P_i$  interpolates a degree-t polynomial  $f_{\ell,i}(x)$  based on these t+1 distinct  $g_{\ell,j}(\alpha_i)$  for all  $\ell\in[N']$ . Then  $P_i$  sends  $f_{\ell,i}(\alpha_j)$  to each party  $P_j$  for all  $\ell\in[N']$ .
- 5: Reconstruction of Column Polynomials. Upon receiving  $\{f_{\ell,j}(\alpha_i)\}_{\ell\in[N']}$  from 2t+1 distinct  $P_j$ ,  $P_i$  interpolates a degree-2t polynomial  $g_{\ell,i}(y)$  based on these 2t+1 distinct  $f_{\ell,j}(\alpha_i)$  for all  $\ell\in[N']$ . Then  $P_i$  computes  $\{g_{\ell,i}(\beta_k)\}_{\ell\in[N'],k\in[0,t]}$  as his share of  $[s_1]_t,\ldots,[s_N]_t$  and terminates.

## <span id="page-11-0"></span>4.2 Consistent Public Reconstruction.

As we discussed in Section 2.4, parties first invoke an efficient public reconstruction protocol [DN07] to reconstruct the secrets, then execute a pair-wise verification to determine the output. The communication complexity is  $\mathcal{O}(Nn+n^2)$  field elements for reconstructing N values.

## Protocol $\Pi_{\mathtt{PubRec}}$

**Parameter:** Let N denote the number of input sharings.

**Input:** All parties take their shares of  $[x_1]_t, \ldots, [x_N]_t$  as inputs.

If one party's input share is a failure symbol  $\bot$ , then he will send  $\bot$  to all parties. Parties that receive  $\bot$  will forward it to all parties and terminate with Fail.

- 1: Divide  $[x_1]_t, \ldots, [x_N]_t$  into N/(2t+1) groups, each of size 2t+1. For each group,  $P_i$  does the following.
  - (1). Let  $[s^{(0)}]_t, \ldots, [s^{(2t)}]_t$  denote the degree-t Shamir secret sharings in this group. We define the degree-t polynomial  $f(X) = s^{(0)} + s^{(1)} \cdot X + \cdots + s^{(2t)} \cdot X^{2t}$ .  $P_i$  sends his share of  $[f(\alpha_j)]_t = [s^{(0)}]_t + [s^{(1)}]_t \cdot \alpha_j + \cdots + [s^{(2t)}]_t \cdot \alpha_j^{2t}$  to each  $P_j$ .
  - (2). Upon receiving 2t + 1 shares of  $[f(\alpha_i)]_t$ ,  $P_i$  interpolates the secret  $f(\alpha_i)$  and sends it to all parties.
  - (3). Upon receiving  $f(\alpha_j)$  from 2t+1 distinct  $P_j$ ,  $P_i$  interpolates the degree-2t polynomial f(X) and gets the coefficients  $s^{(0)}, \ldots, s^{(2t)}$  from f(x).
- 2: Upon getting the reconstitution result  $x_1, \ldots, x_N$ , each party  $P_i$  locally samples a random value  $r_{i,j}$  for each  $P_j$  and computes  $X_{i,j} = \sum_{\ell=1}^N r_{i,j}^\ell \cdot x_\ell$ . Then  $P_i$  sends  $(r_{i,j}, X_{i,j})$  to each party  $P_j$ .
- 3: Pair-Wise Verification. Upon receiving  $(r_{j,i}, X_{j,i})$  from 2t+1 distinct  $P_j$ ,  $P_i$  checks whether  $X_{j,i} = \sum_{\ell=1}^{N} r_{j,i}^{\ell} \cdot x_{\ell}$  is hold for all these 2t+1 distinct  $P_j$ . If true,  $P_i$  terminates with  $x_1, \ldots, x_N$ . Otherwise,  $P_i$  terminates with a failure symbol Fail.

### 4.3 Triple Extraction

We modify the triple generation technique in [JS26] and give the following construction  $\Pi_{\texttt{GenTriple}}$ , which allows all parties to generate random Beaver triples. We first recap a simplified version in [JS26], which allows each virtual party  $V_{i,j} = (P_i, P_j)$  to distribute a random triple to all parties.

{12}------------------------------------------------

- **Step 1.**  $P_i$  and  $P_j$  invoke an instance of  $\Pi_{Sh}$  to share random degree-t sharings  $\{[a^{(i)}]_t, [b^{(i)}]_t\}$  and  $\{[a^{(j)}]_t, [b^{(j)}]_t\}$ , respectively. Upon receiving shares, all parties locally compute their shares of  $[a]_t := [a^{(i)}]_t + [a^{(j)}]_t, [b]_t := [b^{(i)}]_t + [b^{(j)}]_t$ . These two sharings are the first two random sharings in the output triple.
- Step 2. For each  $P_k \in \mathcal{P}$ , let  $a_k, b_k$  denote  $P_k$ 's shares of  $[a]_t, [b]_t$ . Then  $P_k$  computes  $e_k = a_k \cdot b_k$  and randomly samples an additive secret sharing  $\langle e_k \rangle = (e_k^{(i)}, e_k^{(j)})$  such that  $e_k = e_k^{(i)} + e_k^{(j)}$ . Then  $P_k$  distributes this additive sharing to  $P_i$  and  $P_j$ .
- **Step 3.**  $P_i$  and  $P_j$  agree on a common set  $\mathcal{Q}$  of 2t+1 parties that have distributed the additive shares to both of them. Then they interpolate a degree-2t polynomial based on their additive shares, respectively. As a result, the secrets of their degree-2t polynomials form an additive secret sharing  $\langle a \cdot b \rangle$ .
- Step 4. Let  $\langle a \cdot b \rangle = (c^{(i)}, c^{(j)})$ , then  $P_i$  and  $P_j$  invoke  $\Pi_{Sh}$  to distribute the sharings  $[c^{(i)}]_t, [c^{(j)}]_t$ , respectively. Upon receiving shares, all parties locally compute their shares of  $[c]_t := [c^{(i)}]_t + [c^{(j)}]_t$ . All parties hold their shares of triple  $([a]_t, [b]_t, [c]_t)$ .

**Optimization.** Directly execute the above procedure cause  $n^2$  instances of  $\Pi_{Sh}$  and results in  $\mathcal{O}(n^4)$  field elements additive overhead. For each  $V_{i,j}$ , parties set  $[a]_t = [a^{(i,j)}]_t + [a^{(j,i)}]_t$ ,  $[b]_t = [b^{(i,j)}]_t + [b^{(j,i)}]_t$ . Upon getting the additive shares of  $\langle a \cdot b \rangle$  in Step 4,  $P_j$  will use his secret  $r^{(j,i)}$  to mask his additive share  $c^{(j)}$  and send it to  $P_i$ . Then  $P_i$  can compute the secret  $z^{(i,j)} = a \cdot b + r^{(i,j)} + r^{(j,i)}$ . Upon getting  $z^{(i,j)}$  for 2t+1 distinct  $P_j$ ,  $P_i$  reliably broadcasts them in parallel, and all parties can compute their shares of triples for these 2t+1 distinct virtual parties  $V_{i,j}$  in parallel. As a result, each party only invokes one instance of  $\Pi_{Sh}$  and reliably broadcasts, achieving  $\mathcal{O}(n^3)$  additive overhead.

Finally, to avoid  $P_i$  and  $P_j$  performing  $\mathcal{O}(n)$  round communication to agree on the set  $\mathcal{Q}$ , we let  $P_j$  choose his additive share of each  $P_k$ 's  $\langle e_k \rangle$ . Upon receiving the additive share from  $P_j$ , each party  $P_k$  computes the additive share for  $P_i$ . Later,  $P_i$  only needs to send the set  $\mathcal{Q}$  to  $P_j$ , and they can get their additive shares of the  $\langle a \cdot b \rangle$ .

## 4.5 Offline Phase

Finally, we define the functionality  $\mathcal{F}_{\mathsf{Triple}}$  and the construction of  $\Pi_{\mathsf{Triple}}$ . Let N be the number of triples to be prepared, L be each party's maximum number of requests for verifying the random triples. The first **Generation of the Random Triples** is the triple generation protocol invoked in the offline phase, and the second **Request for Verifying the Random Triples** is invoked later in the online phase to verify the used triples.

Remark 3. Recall that later in the online phase, all parties still need to verify the correctness of the used triples for circuit evaluation. The total communication complexity of  $\Pi_{\text{Triple}}$  is  $\mathcal{O}(Nn + Ln^3)$  field elements for generating N random Beaver triples. ### **Evaluation Phase**

- 1: Evaluating the Circuit. All parties do the following to evaluate the circuit layer by layer:
  - **Addition Gate:** With input sharings  $[x]_t$ ,  $[y]_t$ , all parties locally compute the output sharing  $[z]_t = [x]_t + [y]_t$ .

  - If the output is Success, then this party proceeds to the output phase.

- 1: Reconstruct the Output. All parties invoke  $\Pi_{\text{PubRec}}$  with their output shares  $[y]_t$  to reconstruct the circuit output y.

Agreement on a Common Subset. The agreement on a common subset (ACS) primitive allows the parties to agree on a set of at least n-t parties that satisfy a certain property (a so-called ACS property).

**Definition 1.** Let  $\mathcal{P}$  be a set of n parties and let Q be a property that can be influenced by multiple protocols running in parallel. Every party  $P_i \in \mathcal{P}$  can decide for every party  $P_j \in \mathcal{P}$  based on the protocols running in parallel whether  $P_j$  satisfies the property towards  $P_i$  or not. If it does, we say  $P_i$  likes  $P_j$  for Q or simply  $P_i$ likes  $P_i$  if the property Q is clear from the context. We require that once a party likes another party, it cannot unlike it. Such a property Q is called an ACS property if for every pair of uncorrupted parties  $(P_i, P_j) \in \mathcal{P}^2$ we have that  $P_i$  will eventually like  $P_i$ .

We state the traditional property-based formalization of ACS

**Definition 2.** Let  $\Pi$  be an n-party protocol where all parties take as input a global ACS property Q and each party  $P_i$  outputs a set  $S_i$  of parties. We say that  $\Pi$  is a t-resilient ACS protocol for Q if the following holds whenever up to t parties are corrupted:

- Consistency: Each honest party outputs the same set  $S_i = S$ .
- Set quality: Each output set has size at least n-t, and for each  $P_i \in S$  there exists at least one honest party  $P_i$  that likes  $P_i$  for Q.

- Termination: All honest parties eventually terminate.

We also describe a functionality for ACS. In the functionality, each party can input  $k \in [n]$ . And it is guaranteed that every party receives at least n-t such indices. Moreover, any index k input by a party  $P_i$  will also be eventually input by  $P_j$ .

For an ACS property Q, we will say that the parties invoke  $\mathcal{F}_{acs}$ , meaning that each party  $P_i$  inputs k to the functionality as soon as  $P_i$  likes  $P_k$ .

  - Otherwise, arbitrarily choose  $k^*$  from  $P_i$ .

- 2. If  $D_i^{\text{out}} = 0$  and  $Y = \bot$ , then set Y as follows:

  - Otherwise, set Y to be the set of all eligible values (which is of size at least n-t).

In any case, send (output, sid, Y) to  $P_i$ .

Then  $\mathcal{S}$  interpolates the secrets  $\{u'_{\ell}, v'_{\ell}\}_{\ell \in [C]}$  from shares  $\{[u_{\ell}]_t, [v_{\ell}]_t\}_{\ell \in [C]}$  of for honest parties in  $\mathcal{M}$ , and checks whether  $\{u'_{\ell}, v'_{\ell}\} = \{u_{\ell}, v_{\ell}\}$  for all  $\ell \in [C]$ . If not,  $\mathcal{S}$  aborts the simulation.

### Simulator $\mathcal{S}$

In the following simulation, when S learns that an honest party  $P_i$  terminates Fail or abort in any sub-protocols, it will send (Fail,  $P_i$ ) to  $\mathcal{F}_{\mathsf{AMPC-Ab}}$  in the end.

### Offline Phase

- 1: S emulates  $\mathcal{F}_{\mathsf{Triple}}$  as follows.
  - During the generation of random triples. S only generates shares of corrupted parties and no longer generates the secrets of triples. The rest of the steps are the same.
  - During the request for verifying the random triples.
    - In Step 3, it honestly emulates  $\mathcal{F}_{\mathsf{Triple}}$ .
    - In Step 4: in Step (1), instead of verifying whether the output shares of honest parties in  $\mathcal{M}$  lie on degree-t polynomials,  $\mathcal{S}$  checks whether the additive errors of these honest parties on their shares of triples lie on degree-t polynomials  $\{\delta_{\ell}^{(a)}(x), \delta_{\ell}^{(b)}(x), \delta_{\ell}^{(c)}(x)\}$ ; in Step (2), instead of verifying whether each  $\{a'_{\ell}, b'_{\ell}, c'_{\ell}\} = \{a_{\ell}, b_{\ell}, c_{\ell}\}$ ,  $\mathcal{S}$  directly checks whether  $\delta_{\ell}^{(a)}(\alpha_0) = \delta_{\ell}^{(b)}(\alpha_0) = \delta_{\ell}^{(c)}(\alpha_0) = 0$ .

As a result,  $\mathcal{S}$  learns corrupted parties' shares of  $\{[a_{\ell}]_t, [b_{\ell}]_t, [c_{\ell}]_t\}_{\ell \in [C_I + C]}$ . For each honest party that has received his shares from  $\mathcal{F}_{\mathsf{Triple}}$ ,  $\mathcal{S}$  learns his additives errors on his shares of  $\{[a_{\ell}]_t, [b_{\ell}]_t, [c_{\ell}]_t\}_{\ell \in [C_I + C]}$ .

### Input Phase

1: **Distributing Inputs.** S does the following steps.

{34}------------------------------------------------

- (1). In Step 1, for each corrupted  $P_i$ , S randomly samples values  $a^{(i)}_{t}$ ,  $b^{(i)}_{t}$  and computes  $c^{(i)}_{t} = a^{(i)}_{t} \cdot b^{(i)}_{t}$ . Then S randomly samples the whole sharings  $\{[a^{(i)}]_{t}, [b^{(i)}]_{t}, [c^{(i)}]_{t}\}$  based on shares of corrupted parties and the secrets. With the whole sharings  $\{[a^{(i)}]_{t}, [b^{(i)}]_{t}, [c^{(i)}]_{t}\}$  and the additive errors on honest parties' shares, S honestly executes each honest party. For each honest  $P_i$ , S simulates the behavior of sending messages from honest parties to  $P_i$ . S also records the shares he received from corrupted parties, and computes the difference between the received values and the corrupted parties' shares sampled by S before.
- (2). In Step 2, for each honest party  $P_i$ , let  $\mathcal{H}_i$  denote the set of the identities of the first t+1 parties who sent shares to  $P_i$ . Then S interpolates a degree-t polynomial  $\delta_i^{(a)}(x)$  such that: for each corrupted party  $P_j \in \mathcal{H}_i$ ,  $\delta_i^{(a)}(\alpha_j)$  is the difference computed above. For each honest party  $P_j \in \mathcal{H}_i$ ,  $\delta_i^{(a)}(\alpha_j)$  is  $P_j$ 's additive error on his shares of  $[a^{(i)}]_t$ . S does the same thing to interpolate  $\delta_i^{(b)}$ ,  $\delta_i^{(c)}$ . Then S checks whether  $\delta_i^{(a)}(\alpha_0) = \delta_i^{(b)}(\alpha_0) = \delta_i^{(c)}(\alpha_0) = 0$ . If not, S reliably broadcasts  $\bot$  on behalf of  $P_i$ . Otherwise, S randomly samples a value as  $x^{(i)} + a^{(i)}$  and reliably broadcasts it on behalf of  $P_i$ .

### **Evaluation Phase**

- 1: Evaluating the Circuit. S does the following steps.
  - For addition gate, S computes corrupted parties' share of  $[z]_t$ , and each honest party's additive error on his share of  $[z]_t$ .
  - For every t+1 multiplication gates in one layer, S first randomly samples the whole sharings  $([x_i + a_i]_t, [y_i + b_i]_t)_{i=1}^{t+1}$  based on shares of corrupted parties. Then it invokes  $S_{\text{PubRec}}$  to do simulation, with the sharings  $([x_i + a_i]_t, [y_i + b_i]_t)_{i=1}^{t+1}$  and honest parties' additive errors on their shares of  $([x_i + a_i]_t, [y_i + b_i]_t)_{i=1}^{t+1}$  as inputs. Upon getting the public reconstruction results, S computes corrupted parties' shares of  $\{[z_i]_t\}_{i\in[t+1]}$ , and each honest party's additive error on his share of  $\{[z_i]_t\}_{i\in[t+1]}$ .
- 2: Verification of the Evaluation. S invokes  $S_{\text{VerifyEval}}$  and learns honest parties' output of  $\Pi_{\text{VerifyEval}}$  at the end of  $S_{\text{VerifyEval}}$ . If S learns that any honest party gets Success at the end of  $S_{\text{VerifyEval}}$ , then S also learns that there exists a set M of size t+1 such that the parties in M are all honest, and they use valid triples to evaluate the circuit correctly.
- 3: Extract Corrupted Parties' Input. For the input sharing  $[x_i]_t$  distributed by each corrupted  $P_i \in \mathcal{D}$ ,  $\mathcal{S}$  uses shares of all honest parties in  $\mathcal{M}$  to interpolate the secret  $x_i$ .

### **Output Phase**

- 1: S sends the set D and the inputs  $x_i$  for all corrupted  $P_i \in D$  to  $\mathcal{F}_{\mathsf{AMPC-Ab}}$  and gets the output y. Then S randomly samples the whole sharing  $[y]_t$  based on the secret y and shares of corrupted parties. Then S invokes  $S_{\mathsf{PubRec}}$  with sharing  $[y]_t$  and honest parties' additive errors as inputs.
- 2: Upon terminating  $\Pi_{\text{PubRec}}$ ,  $\mathcal{S}$  invokes  $\mathcal{S}_{\text{VerifyEval}}$  again and learns honest parties' output of  $\Pi_{\text{VerifyEval}}$  at the end of  $\mathcal{S}_{\text{VerifyEval}}$ .
  - For each honest party terminates with Success, S deliveries the output from  $\mathcal{F}_{AMPC-Ab}$  to this honest party.
  - Otherwise, S sends (Fail,  $P_i$ ) to  $\mathcal{F}_{AMPC-Ab}$ .

- In Step 2, it records corrupted parties' shares of triples, the additive errors on honest parties' shares of triples, and the  $(abort, P_i)$  instruction.

**Hyb**<sub>2</sub>: In the following hybrid arguments, we focus on the simulation of the Input Phase.

 $\mathbf{Hyb}_{2.1}$ : In this hybrid, for each honest dealer  $P_i$ ,  $\mathcal{S}$  change the generation of the secret  $a^{(i)}$  by first randomly samples  $x^{(i)} + a^{(i)}$  and recomputes  $x^{(i)} = x^{(i)} + a^{(i)} - a^{(i)}$ . The distribution of  $\mathbf{Hyb}_{2.1}$  and  $\mathbf{Hyb}_2$  is identical.

**Hyb**<sub>2.2</sub>: In this hybrid,  $\mathcal{S}$  delays the generation of the whole sharings  $\{[a^{(i)}]_t, [b^{(i)}]_t, [c^{(i)}]_t\}$  which is assigned to an honest dealer  $P_i$  until the end of the input phase. For honest parties who should send their shares of  $\{[a^{(i)}]_t, [b^{(i)}]_t, [c^{(i)}]_t\}$  to  $P_i$ ,  $\mathcal{S}$  only simulates this behavior of sending messages. Then for each honest party  $P_i$ , instead of verifying  $a^{(i)} \cdot b^{(i)} = c^{(i)}$ ,  $\mathcal{S}$  interpolates degree-t polynomials  $\delta^{(a)}(x), \delta^{(b)}(x), \delta^{(c)}(x)$  as shown in the construction of simulator, and only checks whether  $\delta^{(a)}(\alpha_0) = \delta^{(b)}(\alpha_0) = \delta^{(c)}(\alpha_0) = 0$ . If not,  $\mathcal{S}$  reliably broadcasts  $\perp$  on behalf of  $P_i$ .

The difference between  $\mathbf{Hyb}_{2.2}$  and  $\mathbf{Hyb}_{2.1}$  is that  $P_i$  will not reliably broadcasts  $\bot$  in case not all  $\delta^{(a)}(\alpha_0), \delta^{(b)}(\alpha_0), \delta^{(c)}(\alpha_0)$  are zero but the following equation hold.

$$(a^{(i)} + \delta^{(a)}(\alpha_0)) \cdot (b^{(i)} + \delta^{(b)}(\alpha_0)) = c^{(i)} + \delta^{(c)}(\alpha_0).$$

This is equivalent to the following equation.

$$b^{(i)} = (-\delta^{(b)}(\alpha_0) \cdot a^{(i)} + \delta^{(c)}(\alpha_0) - \delta^{(a)}(\alpha_0) \cdot \delta^{(b)}(\alpha_0)) / \delta^{(a)}(\alpha_0).$$

Since honest  $P_i$  will reliably broadcast  $x^{(i)} + a^{(i)}$ , then if  $x^{(i)}$  is not random, then the adversary can learn the distribution of  $a^{(i)}$ . However,  $b^{(i)}$  is still random and is uniformly distributed from the view of  $\mathcal{A}$ . Therefore, the probability that the above equation holds is at most  $1/|\mathbb{F}|$ , which is negligible in the security parameter  $\kappa$  if  $|\mathbb{F}| = 2^{\Omega(\kappa)}$ . By taking the union bound for all honest  $P_i$ , this probability is at most  $n/|\mathbb{F}|$ . Therefore, the distribution of  $\mathbf{Hyb}_{2,2}$  and  $\mathbf{Hyb}_{2,1}$  is statistically close.

 $\mathbf{Hyb}_3$ : In this hybrid, during the evaluation phase, for every t+1 multiplication gates, we change the generation of the secrets  $\{a_i,b_i\}_{i=1}^{t+1}$ .  $\mathcal{S}$  first randomly samples values as  $\{x_i+a_i,y_i+b_i\}_{i=1}^{t+1}$ , then recomputes  $a_i=x_i+a_i-x_i$ ,  $b_i=y_i+b_i-y_i$ . Since both of the secrets are randomly sampled, the distribution of  $\mathbf{Hyb}_3$  and  $\mathbf{Hyb}_{2,2}$  is identical.

 $\mathbf{Hyb}_4$ : In this hybrid, during the evaluation phase, for every t+1 multiplication gates, we change the generation of the sharings  $\{[x_i+a_i]_t, [y_i+b_i]_t\}_{i=1}^{t+1}$  by interpolating it from the secret  $x_i+a_i, y_i+b_i$  and shares of corrupted parties.  $\mathbf{Hyb}_5$ : In the following hybrid arguments, we focus on the simulation of  $\Pi_{\mathtt{VerifyEval}}$ . We set  $P'_i$  to be the identity of the party who broadcast his challenge  $r_i$  in the *i*th time order,  $\mathcal{S}$  initializes a set  $\mathcal{H}_i = \emptyset$  for each honest party  $P'_i$ .

From i=1 to n, we consider the following hybrid argument. Note that each party will broadcast his challenge after he gets his shares of  $\{[u_\ell]_t, [v_\ell]_t\}_{\ell \in [C]}$ .

 $\mathbf{Hyb}_{5.i}$ : In this hybrid, if  $P_i'$  is corrupted,  $\mathcal{S}$  honestly execute honest parties to response his challenge. If  $P_i'$  is honest,  $\mathcal{S}$  sets  $\mathcal{H}_i$  to be the set of honest parties who have received their shares of  $\{[u_\ell]_t, [v_\ell]_t\}_{\ell \in [C]}$ . Upon  $P_i'$  getting the set  $\mathcal{M}$  (the set of parties who sent shares of  $[S_i]_t$  to  $P_i'$ ),  $\mathcal{S}$  checks whether  $|\mathcal{M} \cap \mathcal{H}_i| \geq t + 1$ . If not, then during the emulation of  $\mathcal{F}_{\mathsf{Triple}}$ ,  $\mathcal{S}$  allows the adversary  $\mathcal{A}$  to change the output bit b for  $P_i'$ .

In case  $|\mathcal{M} \cap \mathcal{H}_i| \geq t+1$ ,  $\mathcal{S}$  interpolates the secrets  $\{u'_\ell, v'_\ell\}_{\ell \in [C]}$  from shares  $\{[u_\ell]_t, [v_\ell]_t\}_{\ell \in [C]}$  of for honest parties in  $\mathcal{M}$ , and checks whether  $\{u'_\ell, v'_\ell\} = \{u_\ell, v_\ell\}$  for all  $\ell \in [C]$ . If not, but  $\mathcal{S}$  finds that the output of  $\mathcal{F}_{\mathsf{Triple}}$  for  $P'_i$  is 1 and the equation  $S_i(\alpha_0) = \sum_{\ell \in [C]} r_i^{\ell-1} (u_\ell + r_i^C \cdot v_\ell)$  holds for  $P'_i$ ,  $\mathcal{S}$  aborts the simulation. The difference between  $\mathbf{Hyb}_{5.i}$  and  $\mathbf{Hyb}_{5.i-1}$  is that  $\mathcal{S}$  will not abort the simulation in  $\mathbf{Hyb}_{5.i-1}$  if the following condition happens:

$$\sum_{\ell \in [C]} r_i^{\ell-1}(u_\ell + r_i^C \cdot v_\ell) = \sum_{\ell \in [C]} r_i^{\ell-1}(u'_\ell + r_i^C \cdot v'_\ell), \exists \ell \in [C], \{u_\ell, v_\ell\} \neq \{u'_\ell, v'_\ell\}.$$

We rewrite the above equation as follows.

$$\sum_{\ell \in [C]} r_i^{\ell-1} (u_\ell - u'_\ell + r_i^C \cdot (v_\ell - v'_\ell)) = 0$$

There exists  $\ell \in [C]$  such that the coefficient  $u_{\ell} - u'_{\ell}$  or  $v_{\ell} - v'_{\ell}$  is non-zero. Therefore,  $r_i$  can be viewed as a root for the equation  $f(x) = \sum_{\ell \in [C]} x^{\ell-1} (u_{\ell} - u'_{\ell} + x^C \cdot (v_{\ell} - v'_{\ell}))$ . Since the coefficients are fixed before  $P_i$  proposes his challenge  $r_i$ . Therefore, for a fixed set  $\mathcal{M}$ , the probability (for a single challenge  $r_i$ ) that the adversary can guess the root  $r_i$  is at most  $C/|\mathbb{F}|$ , which is negligible in the security parameter  $\kappa$  if  $|\mathbb{F}| = 2^{\Omega(\kappa)}$ . By taking the union bound for all subsets  $\mathcal{M}$  and  $\left\lceil \frac{n+\kappa}{\kappa} \right\rceil$  distinct random challenges, the probability that the adversary can correctly guess is at most:

$$2^n \cdot (C/|\mathbb{F}|)^{\left\lceil \frac{n+\kappa}{\kappa} \right\rceil} = C/2^{\kappa}.$$

which is negligible in the security parameter  $\kappa$ . Therefore, the distribution of  $\mathbf{Hyb}_{5.i}$  and  $\mathbf{Hyb}_{5.i-1}$  is statistically close.

**Hyb**<sub>6</sub>: In this hybrid, when  $\mathcal{S}$  learns that one honest party  $P_k$  terminates  $\Pi_{\text{VerifyEval}}$  with Success, we argue that there exists at least one honest  $P_i$  such that he sends request (VerifyEnd,  $P_i$ ,  $\mathcal{M}$ ) to  $\mathcal{F}_{\text{Triple}}$ , where  $\mathcal{M} \subseteq \mathcal{M}_i$  (defined in  $\mathcal{F}_{\text{Triple}}$ ) and  $|\mathcal{M} \cap \mathcal{H}_i| \ge t + 1$ . Thus, the adversary cannot change the output bit b for this honest party  $P_i'$ . The argument is as follows.

*Proof.* In this case, we know that this honest party  $P_k$  who outputs Success has received  $(t+1, P_j)$  from 2t+1 distinct  $P_j$ .

Let  $\mathcal{H}_0^{(i)}$ ,  $\mathcal{H}_1^{(i)}$  denote the set of parties who receive their shares of triples before/after an honest party  $P_i$ . For the last honest party  $P_i$  who sent  $(t+1, P_i)$  among these 2t+1 parties, we know that he has received  $(t, P_j)$  from 2t+1 distinct  $P_j$ , each of them considers the verification result to be true. Then we know that:

- If t+1 honest parties of them come from  $\mathcal{H}_0^{(i)}$ , we have met the requirement, the party  $P_i$  is the desired party and these t+1 honest parties in  $\mathcal{H}_0^{(i)}$  form the set  $\mathcal{H}_i$ .
- Otherwise, at least one honest party of them comes from  $\mathcal{H}_1^{(i)}$ , and we denote this honest party by  $P_k$ .

For this honest party  $P_k$ , similarly, we know that he has received  $(t-1, P_j)$  from 2t+1 distinct  $P_j$ . Then either t+1 honest parties of them come from  $\mathcal{H}_0^{(k)}$ , or at least one honest  $P'_k$  comes from  $\mathcal{H}_1^{(k)}$ . Since the size of  $\mathcal{H}_1^{(k)}$  is bounded by t, in the worst case, after at most t times of recursion, we know that there exists an honest party who receives his shares after all the other honest parties, and this honest party meets our requirement.

The distribution of  $\mathbf{Hyb}_6$  and  $\mathbf{Hyb}_5$  is identical.

 $\mathbf{Hyb}_7$ : In this hybrid, we change the computation of the output sharing  $[y]_t$ . Instead of computing it by  $\mathcal{S}$  with honest parties' inputs,  $\mathcal{S}$  first extracts corrupted parties' input based on shares of honest parties in  $\mathcal{M}$ , then  $\mathcal{S}$  sends the set  $\mathcal{D}$  and all corrupted parties' inputs to  $\mathcal{F}_{\mathsf{AMPC-Ab}}$  and gets the circuit output y.  $\mathbf{Hyb}_8$ : In this hybrid, during the  $\Pi_{\mathtt{VerifyEval}}$  in the Output phase,  $\mathcal{S}$  does a similar thing as in  $\mathbf{Hyb}_6$  to learn the identity party  $P_i$ , his challenge  $r_i$  and the request set  $\mathcal{M}$ . Following the same argument in  $\mathbf{Hyb}_5$ , the distribution of  $\mathbf{Hyb}_8$  and  $\mathbf{Hyb}_7$  is statistically close.
