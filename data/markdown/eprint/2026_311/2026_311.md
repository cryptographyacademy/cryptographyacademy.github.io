# <span id="page-0-0"></span>Security of the Fischlin Transform in Quantum Random Oracle Model

Christian Majenz and Jaya Sharma

Department of Applied Mathematics and Computer Science, Technical University of Denmark {chmaj, jaysh}@dtu.dk

Abstract. The Fischlin transform yields non-interactive zero-knowledge proofs with straight-line extractability in the classical random oracle model. This is done by forcing a prover to generate multiple accepting transcripts through a proof-of-work mechanism. Whether the Fischlin transform is straight-line extractable against quantum adversaries has remained open due to the difficulty of reasoning about the likelihood of query transcripts in the quantum-accessible random oracle model (QROM), even when using the compressed oracle methodology. In this work, we prove that the Fischlin transform remains straight-line extractable in the QROM, via an extractor based on the compressed oracle. This establishes the post-quantum security of the Fischlin transform, providing a post-quantum straight-line extractable NIZK alternative to Pass' transform with smaller proof size. Our techniques include tail bounds for sums of independent random variables and for martingales as well as symmetrization, query amplitude and quantum union bound arguments.

## <span id="page-1-0"></span>1 Introduction

Non-interactive zero-knowledge proofs (NIZKs) obtained from interactive protocols via the Fiat–Shamir paradigm form a central tool in modern cryptography. Beyond zero-knowledge, many applications require straight-line extractability, namely the ability to efficiently extract a witness from any prover that convinces the verifier, without rewinding. The Fischlin transform [\[Fis05\]](#page-31-2) provides a prominent approach for achieving straight-line extractable NIZKs from Σ-protocols in the random oracle model. It constitutes an alternative to Pass' transform [\[Pas03\]](#page-31-3), replacing the use of commitments by a rejection-sampling mechanism based on oracle queries. As a result, the Fischlin transform yields small non-interactive proofs with strong extraction guarantees. The key constructive element in the Fischlin transform is what would now be called a proof-of-work-like mechanism. The challenges that are chosen uniformly at random by the verifier in the interactive setting can now be chosen freely by the prover, but need to fulfil a hash condition, e.g., the hash of the transcript needs to start with l zeroes. Modeling the hash function as a random oracle now implicitly randomizes the challenge as the pre-image of the hash predicate is random.

With the advent of quantum computing, it has become necessary to analyze the security of random-oracle-based constructions in the presence of quantum adversaries. This has led to the development of the quantum random oracle 

{2}------------------------------------------------

model (QROM) [\[BDF](#page-30-1)+11], in which adversaries may query the random oracle in superposition. Over the past decade, a substantial body of work has established a versatile technical toolbox for proving the post-quantum security of classical constructions in the QROM. These techniques have been successfully applied to a wide range of Fiat–Shamir-type transforms, including Pass' transform [\[CMS19](#page-30-2)[,DFMS22,](#page-31-4)[RT25\]](#page-31-5) and related primitives. However, despite its importance, the Fischlin transform has so far resisted security analysis in the QROM. Existing proof techniques do not readily extend to this setting, and no QROM extractability proof for the Fischlin transform was previously known.

The main difficulty in analyzing the Fischlin transform in the QROM stems from the structure of its extraction mechanism. Informally, a prover succeeds only if it finds an oracle query whose output satisfies a stringent acceptance condition, which in turn requires many attempts, except with tiny probability. A classical extractor can thus read off two accepting transcripts from the adversarial prover's oracle query inputs, allowing extraction of a witness via special soundness. In the quantum setting, however, this reasoning becomes problematic. The adversary's output depends only on the single accepting query, the unsuccessful ones are in some sense counterfactual. This stands in sharp contrast to Pass' transform, where accepting transcripts contain explicit commitments to responses for all challenges that can be leveraged for extraction even in the quantum setting. As a result, proving that a quantum adversary must invest substantial query complexity in order to succeed requires new techniques.

Our results. In this work, we prove the straight-line extractability of the Fischlin transform in the QROM. Our result provides concrete bounds for a wide range of parameter choices that include an infinite family of parameter sets for asymptotic security. In more detail, we have the following

Theorem 1 (Informal version of Corollary [1\)](#page-26-1). Let Σ be a Σ-protocol for a witness relation R with special soundness and unique responses. Under suitable conditions on the parameters of the Fischlin transform that allow unbounded values for the parallel repetition parameter of the Fischlin transform, k, the Fischlin transform Fis[Σ] is a proof of knowledge with straight-line extractability. The simulation can be made perfect and the extractor succeeds whenever a q-query malicious prover does, except with probability no more than

$$\varepsilon_{\rm ex} \le q^2 \cdot {\rm negl}(k).$$

Technical Overview. Defining the straight-line extractor for the Fischlin transform is straightforward. We simulate the random oracle towards the prover using a compressed oracle [\[Zha19\]](#page-31-6). Once the prover has finished and its proof is verified, the extractor measures the compressed oracle database register. If the measurement result contains two accepting transcripts for the same repetition with different challenges, the special soundness extractor of the Σ-protocol is used to produce a witness.

{3}------------------------------------------------

The main work goes into proving that no prover can succeed with significant probability at outputting a proof that involves a fixed commitment vector chosen ahead of time ("deterministic prover") so that the extractor still fails. This result is then lifted to a general prover that chooses the commitment vector in a randomized manner using an intricate but compact query amplitude argument using a quantum union bound.

To show the result for deterministic provers, our strategy is reminiscent of the Monty Hall paradox. Here, a game master presents three doors. Behind a random one is a car, while there are goats behind the other two. The participant can now choose a door, which is, however, not opened. The game master now opens a different door which reveals a goat. The participant can now decide: stick to their choice of door, or choose the only remaining door instead. The optimal strategy is to switch, which reveals the car with probability 2/3. What makes this situation appear paradoxical is the fallacy of regarding the remaining door as a fixed object instead of a random variable. The game master chooses the door they open depending on the randomness of the placement of the car, which correlates the car's placement with the remaining doors.

When a prover outputs a valid Fischlin proof, a number of hash values are now known (to evaluate to 0). This can only be achieved by correlating the choice of challenges with the random oracle. Clearly this can only happen if the probability that some hash outputs not involved in proof verification are 0 is now less than suggested by the uniform distribution, otherwise the oracle output distribution would be biased. In the compressed oracle, this means that the oracle registers corresponding to valid transcripts not involved in the verification of the proof output by the adversarial prover cannot contain the uniform superposition of outputs anymore. This deviation is then exploited by the extractor.

From a technical perspective, the task of making this argument rigorous is formidable. The output length of the random oracle is logarithmic, preventing the usual tight relationship between oracle register content and adversarial view up to negligible error. We therefore need to explicitly analyze every switch between the "compressed" and "uncompressed" bases of the superposition oracle using Chernoff bounds. The high-level strategy is now as follows. Pick a number η such that (i) it is unlikely that there are η oracle inputs in a certain sub-domain that map to zero (by an additional standard Chernoff bound), but (ii) it is likely to find roughly η − k zeroes, where k is the number of parallel repetitions in the Fischlin transform and thus the number of hash values that need to be zero for a Fischlin proof to be accepted. We then use the fact that it is unlikely to find η zeroes, but likely to find η zeroes given that the prover has succeeded and the extractor has failed, to conclude that the probability that the prover succeeds and the extractor fails is small.

Proving that it is likely to find η zeroes given that the prover has succeeded and the extractor has failed is non-trivial. Conditioning (or rather projecting) on these events renders the oracle register non-uniform and concentration inequalities for independent Bernoulli random variables no longer apply. Fortunately, symmetrizing the state and using properties of the "extractor fails" projector, we 

{4}------------------------------------------------

can define a suitable *martingale* based on the outcomes of measuring the different database registers and apply the Azuma-Hoeffding concentration inequality to get the desired bound.

Overall, our analysis establishes that, even in the presence of quantum superposition queries, success in the Fischlin transform implicitly certifies the existence of many "hidden" accepting transcripts. While these transcripts may not be classically observable, they can be detected through concentration phenomena in the prover's measurement process, enabling straight-line extraction in the QROM.

## <span id="page-4-1"></span>2 Preliminaries

For asymptotic security analysis, protocol parameters are expressed as a function of the security parameter  $\lambda \in \mathbb{N}$ . A (possibly negative) function  $f(\lambda)$  is called negligible if  $|f(\lambda)| \leq 1/p(\lambda)$  for any polynomial  $p(\lambda)$  and all sufficiently large  $\lambda$ . We use negl to denote a negligible function. A function which is not negligible is called noticeable. For any witness relation  $R \subset \mathcal{X} \times \mathcal{W}$  we call the elements of  $\mathcal{X}$  instances, the elements of  $\mathcal{W}$  witnesses, and define the associated language  $\mathcal{L}_R = \{x \in \mathcal{X} | \exists w \in \mathcal{W} : (x, w) \in R\}$ . We denote by  $\delta(\cdot, \cdot)$  the maximal 1-sample computational distinguishing advantage between two probability distributions. We allow algorithms as arguments here, which indicate computational distinguishability of their outputs.

A common idealization is the random oracle model where parties have access to a random function H with some domain and range depending on  $\lambda$ . Our random oracles have output length at most polynomial in the security parameter.

**Definition 1** ( $\Sigma$ -Protocol). A  $\Sigma$ -protocol  $\Sigma = (\mathcal{P}, \mathcal{V})$  for a relation  $R \subseteq \mathcal{X} \times \mathcal{W}$  is a three-round two-party interactive protocol between a Prover  $\mathcal{P} = (\mathcal{P}_1, \mathcal{P}_2)$  and a verifier  $\mathcal{V}$  where the prover produces  $(a, st) \leftarrow \mathcal{P}_1(x, w)$  and sends the commitment  $a \in \mathcal{M}$  to the verifier, the verifier sends a random challenge  $c \leftarrow \mathcal{C}$  (public-coin) and the prover sends a response  $z \leftarrow \mathcal{P}_2(st, c)$ . Finally, the verifier outputs the verdict  $b \leftarrow \mathcal{V}(x, a, c, z)$ , with b = 1 meaning "valid". We denote the part of the interaction that produces the communication transcript as  $(a, c, z) \leftarrow \langle \mathcal{P}(x, w), \mathcal{V} \rangle$ . It has to satisfy the following properties:

- 1. Completeness. For any  $(x, w) \in R$ , any  $(a, c, z) \leftarrow \langle \mathcal{P}(x, w), \mathcal{V} \rangle$  it holds  $\mathcal{V}(x, a, c, z) = 1$ .
- 2. Special Soundness. There exists a probabilistic polynomial-time (PPT) extractor algorithm  $\mathsf{Ext}_{\mathsf{ss}}$  such that for all  $x \in \mathcal{X}$  and for all pairs of transcripts (a,c,z) and (a,c',z') with  $c \neq c'$  such that  $\mathcal{V}(x,a,c,z) = \mathcal{V}(x,a,c',z') = 1$ ,

the extractor produces a witness  $w \leftarrow \mathsf{Ext}_{\mathsf{ss}}\left(x, a, c, z, c', z'\right)$  satisfying  $(x, w) \in R$ .

**Definition 2 (Min-Entropy).** The **min-entropy** of a discrete random variable X over a finite set X is defined as:

$$H_{\infty}(X) = -\log_2\left(\max_{x \in \mathcal{X}} \Pr[X = x]\right)$$

where Pr[X = x] denotes the probability that X takes the value x.

We now list some additional properties required by our  $\Sigma$ -protocol the make the transformation from interactive to non-interactive, work:

**Definition 3 (Commitment Entropy).** Let  $\Sigma$  be a  $\Sigma$ -protocol. We say  $\Sigma$  has commitment entropy  $\alpha \in \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$  if, for any  $(x, w) \in R$  and  $a \leftarrow \mathcal{P}_1(x, w)$  it holds that  $H_{\infty}(a) \geq \alpha$ .

**Definition 4 (Unique Responses).** Let  $\Sigma$  be a  $\Sigma$ -protocol. We say  $\Sigma$  has unique responses, if for all tuples  $(x, a, c, z, z') \leftarrow A(\lambda)$  with  $z \neq z'$ , either  $\mathcal{V}_1(x, a, c, z) = 0$  or  $\mathcal{V}_1(x, a, c, z') = 0$ .

**Definition 5 (Honest-Verifier Zero-Knowledge (HVZK)).** Let  $\Sigma$  be a  $\Sigma$ -protocol for relation R. We say  $\Sigma$  has honest-verifier zero-knowledge (HVZK) if there exists a PPT algorithm  $\mathsf{Sim}_{\mathsf{HVZK}}$ , the zero-knowledge simulator, such that for any quantum polynomial-time (QPT) algorithm  $\mathcal{D} = (\mathcal{D}_0, \mathcal{D}_1)$ , it holds that

$$\begin{vmatrix}
\Pr_{\substack{(x,w,\mathsf{st})\leftarrow\mathcal{D}_0(1^\lambda)\\(a,c,z)\leftarrow\langle\mathcal{P}(x,w),\mathcal{V}\rangle}} [1\leftarrow\mathcal{D}_1(a,c,z,\mathsf{st})\land(x,w)\in R] \\
-\Pr_{\substack{(x,w,\mathsf{st})\leftarrow\mathcal{D}_0(1^\lambda)\\(a,c,z)\leftarrow\mathsf{Sim}_{\mathsf{HVZK}}(x)}} [1\leftarrow\mathcal{D}_1(a,c,z,\mathsf{st})\land(x,w)\in R] \Big| \leq \operatorname{negl}(\lambda).$$

Below we sometimes use a stronger zero-knowledge property where the simulator is able to generate proofs for a specific challenge.

**Definition 6 (Special Honest Verifier Zero-Knowledge).** A  $\Sigma$ -protocol  $\Sigma$  is special honest-verifier zero-knowledge (SHVZK) if it is HVZK and there exists a simulator  $\mathsf{Sim}_{\mathsf{SHVZK}}$  such that  $\mathsf{Sim}_{\mathsf{HVZK}}(x)$  simulates transcripts (a,c,z) by sampling  $c \leftarrow \mathcal{C}$  and running  $(a,z) \leftarrow \mathsf{Sim}_{\mathsf{SHVZK}}(x,c)$ .

**Completeness.** For any  $(x, w) \in R$  and any  $\pi \leftarrow \mathcal{P}^H(x, w)$  we have  $\Pr\left[\mathcal{V}^H(x, \pi) = 1\right] = 1 - \operatorname{negl}(\lambda)$ .

In the QROM, straight-line extractability can be defined as follows, following [DFMS22]. Let  $\mathcal{P}^*$  be a dishonest prover that potentially outputs some additional auxiliary (possibly quantum) output Z next to  $\pi$ . We then consider an interactive algorithm Ext, called *online extractor*, which takes the security parameter  $\lambda$  as input and simulates the answers to the oracle queries in the execution of  $\mathcal{V}^H \circ \mathcal{P}^{*H}(x)$ , which we define to run  $(\pi, Z) \leftarrow \mathcal{P}^{*H}(x)$  followed by  $v \leftarrow \mathcal{V}^H(x, \pi)$ ; furthermore, at the end, Ext outputs  $w \in \mathcal{W}$ . We denote the execution of  $\mathcal{V}^H \circ \mathcal{P}^{*H}(x)$  with the calls to H simulated by Ext, and considering Ext's final output w as well, as  $(\pi, Z; v; w) \leftarrow \mathcal{V}^{\mathsf{Ext}} \circ \mathcal{P}^{*\mathsf{Ext}}(x)$ .

Definition 8 (Non-adaptive variant of Definition 3.1 in [DFMS22]). A NIZK in the (QROM) for relation  $R \subset \mathcal{X} \times \mathcal{W}$  is a proof of knowledge with straight-line extractability against non-adaptive adversaries if there exists an online extractor Ext, and functions  $\varepsilon_{\text{sim}}$  (the simulation error) and  $\varepsilon_{\text{ex}}$  (the extraction error), with the following properties. For any  $\lambda \in \mathbb{N}$ , for any  $x \in \mathcal{X}$  and for any dishonest prover  $\mathcal{P}^*$  making no more than q queries,

$$\delta([(\pi, Z, v)]_{\mathcal{V}^{H} \circ \mathcal{P}^{*H}(x)}, [(\pi, Z, v)]_{\mathcal{V}^{\mathsf{Ext}} \circ \mathcal{P}^{*\mathsf{Ext}}(x)}) \leq \varepsilon_{\mathrm{sim}}(\lambda, q)$$

and

$$\Pr \big[ v = \mathtt{accept} \, \wedge \, (x,w) \not \in R : (\pi,Z;v;w) \leftarrow \mathcal{V}^{\mathsf{Ext}} \circ \mathcal{P}^{*\mathsf{Ext}}(x) \big] \leq \varepsilon_{\mathrm{ex}}(\lambda,q) \, .$$

In this section we summarize the mathematical tools and notational conventions used throughout the paper. We review basic facts about Hilbert spaces and operators, introduce the computational and Fourier bases for finite Abelian groups, and recall the standard encoding of functions into quantum oracles. These foundations are needed to formalize the compressed oracle technique and to analyze the behavior of quantum adversaries making oracle queries.

**Hilbert Spaces and Operators.** We work over finite-dimensional complex Hilbert spaces. Unless stated otherwise,  $\mathcal{H} = \mathbb{C}^d$  for some d, with the usual braket notation. Linear operators between spaces  $\mathcal{H}$  and  $\mathcal{H}'$  are denoted  $\mathcal{L}(\mathcal{H}, \mathcal{H}')$ , with  $\mathcal{L}(\mathcal{H})$  for endomorphisms. A (pure) quantum state is a unit vector  $|\psi\rangle \in \mathcal{H}$ .

**Norms.** For  $A \in \mathcal{L}(\mathcal{H}, \mathcal{H}')$ , ||A|| denotes the operator norm. If  $\mathcal{H} = \bigoplus_{i=1}^m \mathcal{H}_i$  and A acts as  $B_i$  on each  $\mathcal{H}_i$ , then  $||A|| = \max_i ||B_i||$ . When A is defined only on a subspace, it can be extended by zero outside without ambiguity in its norm.

Any finite set  $\mathcal{Y}$  defines an associated quantum register with state space  $\mathbb{C}\mathcal{Y}$  computational basis  $\{|y\rangle\}_{y\in\mathcal{Y}}$ . We will consider the extension  $\overline{\mathcal{Y}} = \mathcal{Y} \cup \{\bot\}$  and the superspace  $\mathbb{C}\overline{\mathcal{Y}} \supset \mathbb{C}\mathcal{Y}$ .

We will denote the function table stored in a quantum register by  $|H\rangle = \bigotimes_x |H(x)\rangle \in (\mathbb{C}\mathcal{Y})^{\otimes \mathcal{X}}$ . We implicitly consider the different registers to be labeled by  $x \in \mathcal{X}$  in the obvious way.

Compressed Oracle Technique. The compressed oracle formalism introduced by Zhandry [Zha19] is a way to simulate a quantum-accessible random oracle in a way that provides additional features for reductions. Instead of storing the full random function, we maintain a *compressed database D* that records only the points queried so far and their outputs. This suffices for simulating any adversary's interaction while simplifying the analysis.

Instead of a uniformly random choice of function, consider a superposition  $\frac{1}{\sqrt{|\mathfrak{H}|}}\sum_{H}|H\rangle$ , where  $\mathfrak{H}$  is the set of functions H is sampled uniformly from. This is the purified oracle which is indistinguishable from the original random oracle for any (quantum) query algorithm since the queries commute with measuring the superposition. The initial state of the oracle registers is given by

$$|\Pi_0\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{|\mathfrak{H}|}} \sum_{H} |H\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{|\mathfrak{H}|}} \bigotimes_{x} \left(\sum_{y} |y\rangle_{D_x}\right) =: \bigotimes_{x} |+_{\mathcal{Y}}\rangle_{D_x}.$$

$$\operatorname{Comp}_{D_S} = \bigotimes_{x \in S} \operatorname{Comp}_{D_x}$$

Query algorithms. We now outline the structure of a quantum algorithm interacting with a compressed oracle. The adversary has registers XYZO, where X is the query input, Y the query output, Z is the local workspace of the algorithm and O is the register that is measured to produce the final output. D is the compressed oracle database register that is inaccessible to the algorithm except via oracle queries.

Let  $|\phi_i\rangle$  denote the adversary's state before the (i+1)-th query, and  $|\phi_i'\rangle$  the state immediately after that query,

$$|\phi_i'\rangle = O_{XYD}|\phi_i\rangle.$$

The adversary then applies a unitary  $U^2$  on (x, u, z), leaving D unchanged, to obtain

$$|\phi_{i+1}\rangle = U_{XYZO}|\phi_i'\rangle.$$

Thus, after q queries, the overall computation evolves as:

- 1. Start in some initial state  $|\phi_0\rangle$ .
- 2. For each  $1 \le i \le q$ :
  - (a) Apply the oracle  $O_{XYD}$  to obtain  $|\phi_i'\rangle$ .
  - (b) Apply  $U_{XYZO}$  to obtain  $|\phi_i\rangle$ .
- 3. At the end, perform a computational basis measurement of register O.

## <span id="page-9-0"></span>2.2 Reprogramming Quantum Random Oracle

We need an adaptive reprogramming lemma from [GHHM21].

Let  $\mathcal{X}, \mathcal{X}', \mathcal{Y}$  be finite sets. Consider the following game  $\mathsf{Repro}_b$ , where  $b \in \{0,1\}$ :

- 1. Sample an initial random oracle  $O_0: \mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{Y}$ .
- 2. Define  $O_1$  to be  $O_0$ , but with access to a Reprogram oracle: on input a probability distribution p on  $\mathcal{X} \times \mathcal{X}'$ , it samples  $(x, x') \leftarrow p$  and  $y \leftarrow \mathcal{Y}$ , sets  $O_1(x) := y$ , and returns (x, x').
- 3. An adversary A is given oracle access to  $O_b$  (quantum) and to Reprogram, and outputs a bit b'.

The distinguishing advantage is

$$\left|\Pr[\mathtt{Repro}_1^A \implies 1] - \Pr[\mathtt{Repro}_0^A \implies 1]\right|.$$

Let  $p_X$  be the marginal of p on X. Define

$$p_{\max}^{(r)} := \mathbb{E}\left[\max_{x} p_X^{(r)}(x)\right],$$

where  $p^{(r)}$  is the adversary's r-th input to the Reprogram oracle, and the expectation is over the adversary's randomness up to round r.

<span id="page-9-2"></span><span id="page-9-1"></span>Without loss of generality, this unitary does not depend on i: We can include a counter register  $Z_N$  in Z which keeps track of the number of queries that have been applied thusfar, and effectively apply a unitary  $U_i$  after the i-th query by defining U as a controlled unitary with control register  $Z_N$ 

{10}------------------------------------------------

**Lemma 1.** [Simplified from Theorem 1 in [GHHM21]] For any adversary D making R reprogramming calls and q quantum queries in total,

$$\left|\Pr[\textit{Repro}_1^D \implies 1] - \Pr[\textit{Repro}_0^D \implies 1]\right| \le \sum_{r=1}^R \left(\sqrt{q \cdot p_{\max}^{(r)}} + \frac{1}{2}q \cdot p_{\max}^{(r)}\right). \tag{2}$$

### <span id="page-10-0"></span>2.3 Quantum union bound

We need a lemma referred to as the quantum union bound.

Lemma 2 (Theorem 1.1 in [OV22]). Let  $|\psi\rangle \in \mathcal{H}$  be a quantum state and let  $P_i$ , i = 1, ..., N be projectors on  $\mathcal{H}$  such that  $\langle \psi | P_i | \psi \rangle \geq 1 - \epsilon_i$ . Then

<span id="page-10-4"></span>
$$\left\| \left( \prod_{i=1}^{N} P_i \right) |\psi\rangle \right\|^2 \ge 1 - 4 \sum_{i=1}^{N} \epsilon_i.$$

#### <span id="page-10-1"></span>2.4 Chernoff Bounds

<span id="page-10-2"></span>The following lemma is taken from combining Theorem 4.4 and 4.5 from [MU05].

**Lemma 3 (Chernoff Bounds).** Let  $X_1, ..., X_n$  be independent Bernoulli variables such that  $Pr(X_i = 1) = p_i$ . Let

$$X = \sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i$$
 and  $\mu = \mathbb{E}[X]$ .

Then the following Chernoff bounds hold:

1. (Upper tail) For  $0 < \delta \le 1$ ,

$$\Pr(X \ge (1+\delta)\mu) \le e^{-\mu\delta^2/3}.$$
 (4.2)

2. (Lower tail) For  $0 < \delta \le 1$ ,

<span id="page-10-3"></span>
$$\Pr(X \le (1 - \delta)\mu) \le e^{-\mu\delta^2/2}.$$
 (4.4)

The random variables  $X_i$  defined by sequential measurements form a martingale difference sequence rather than independent trials. We therefore need to apply the following martingale Chernoff bound derived from Azuma-Hoeffding Inequality below, from Theorem 12.6 in [MU05]

Lemma 4 (Azuma–Hoeffding Inequality). Let  $(Y_0, Y_1, ..., Y_n)$  be a martingale such that for all i = 1, ..., n,

$$|Y_i - Y_{i-1}| \le c_i$$
 almost surely.

Then for any t > 0,

$$\Pr[Y_n - Y_0 \ge t] \le \exp\left(-\frac{t^2}{2\sum_{i=1}^n c_i^2}\right).$$

## <span id="page-11-0"></span>3 Proof of Post Quantum Security of the Fischlin Transform

We are now set to prove that the Fischlin transform is a NIZK (Definition [7\)](#page-5-0) with straight-line extractability (Definition [8\)](#page-6-0) and zero knowledge in Quantum-Random Oracle Model.

## <span id="page-11-1"></span>3.1 Proof Intuition: From Classical Counting to Quantum Extraction

We begin by providing some additional detail about the proof strategy to guide the reader.

We start with the strategy for the deterministic prover. Upper-bounding the probability of getting η outcomes 0<sup>l</sup> when applying a hypothetical measurement (used for analysis) to the part of the superposition oracle register corresponding to valid transcripts starting with a fixed commitment vector is straightforward (Lemma [5\)](#page-13-0). The difficult part is lower-bounding that same probability after applying two projectors sequentially, one that ensures the success of the prover, and one that ensures the failure of the extractor. The prover success projector leaves the oracle registers corresponding to the inputs queried by verification in the state |0 l ⟩. Changing to the compressed basis leaves most of the k registers in the state |0 l ⟩ (in superposition, see Lemma [6\)](#page-14-0). Applying the extractor failure projector now correlates the part of the database register that is used for verifying the proof (and that still contains many 0<sup>l</sup> 's) and the remaining database register. The extractor-fail projector ensures, however, that in superposition, most registers are in the state |⊥⟩ (the compressed basis initial state). Switching back to the uncompressed basis now (i) transforms the |⊥⟩ states into uniform superpositions, and again (ii) leaves most of the remaining |0 l ⟩'s intact (via another application of Lemma [5,](#page-13-0) see Eq. [\(21\)](#page-25-0) in the proof of Lemma [11\)](#page-23-0). Measuring in the computational basis now should yield many 0<sup>l</sup> 's from the registers used to verify the proof, and also many 0<sup>l</sup> 's as the remaining registers have been projected by the extractor fail projector onto a coarse approximation of the uniform superposition. Showing the latter requires us to symmetrize the state (see Eq. [\(13\)](#page-17-0)). This facilitates a lower bound on the probability of obtaining outcome 0<sup>l</sup> when measuring the i'th register conditioned on the measurement results of the first i−1 registers (Lemma [7\)](#page-17-1) and consequently on the cumulative mean of our super-martingale of measurement results (Lemma [9\)](#page-20-0). This lower bound eventually allows the application of martingale techniques (Lemma [8\)](#page-19-0).

The most involved part of the proof is now done. We lift the result to general malicious provers in Section [3.3.](#page-26-0) The proof of the main lifting theorem, Theorem [2,](#page-26-2) involves decomposing a prover's final state according to when it has first queried an input starting with a fixed commitment vector. As the relevant part of the compressed oracle database is untouched before that query, we can post-select on that query with input starting with a fixed commitment vector actually being made, without query cost. This allows the application of the result for deterministic provers.

with the convention that  $\langle +^l|_i|\psi_I\rangle_{I^cE}=0$ . We can use this decomposition to derive expressions for the parts of the state where the first subregister is in state  $|+^l\rangle$ , and orthogonal to it, respectively,

$$|\psi\rangle_{\tilde{D}E} = \sum_{\substack{I\subset[m]\\|I|\geq n}} |+^{l}\rangle_{I}^{\otimes|I|} |\psi_{I}\rangle_{I^{C}E} = \sum_{\substack{I\subset[m]\\|I|\geq n\\1\in I}} |+^{l}\rangle_{I}^{\otimes|I|} |\psi_{I}\rangle_{I^{C}E} + \sum_{\substack{I\subset[m]\\|I|\geq n\\1\notin I}} |+^{l}\rangle_{I}^{\otimes|I|} |\psi_{I}\rangle_{I^{C}E}$$

$$= |+^{l}\rangle_{1} \left(\sum_{\substack{I'\subset[m]\backslash 1\\|I'|\geq n-1}} |+^{l}\rangle_{I'}^{\otimes|I'|} |\psi_{I'\cup\{1\}}\rangle_{(I'\cup\{1\})^{C}E}\right) + \sum_{\substack{I\subset[m]\\|I|\geq n\\1\notin I}} |+^{l}\rangle_{I}^{\otimes|I|} |\psi_{I}\rangle_{I^{C}E}$$

$$=: |+^{l}\rangle_{1} |\psi^{+}\rangle_{1^{C}E} + |\psi^{-}\rangle_{\tilde{D}E},$$
(14)

where  $\langle +^l |_1 | \psi^- \rangle_{\tilde{D}E} = 0$ . By the assumed permutation symmetry of  $|\psi\rangle$ , the norm of  $|\psi_I\rangle$  only depends on |I|. We thus define  $\zeta_i = ||\psi_{[i]}\rangle||^2$  and note that by the normalization of  $|\psi\rangle$  we have

$$\sum_{i=n}^{m} {m \choose i} \zeta_i = 1.$$

We can now bound

$$\| |\psi_{+}\rangle \|^{2} = \sum_{\substack{I' \subset [m] \setminus 1 \\ |I'| \ge n-1}} \| |\psi_{I' \cup \{1\}}\rangle \|^{2}$$

$$= \sum_{i=n}^{m} \binom{m-1}{i-1} \zeta_{i}$$

$$= \sum_{i=n}^{m} \frac{i}{m} \binom{m}{i} \zeta_{i}$$

$$\geq \sum_{i=n}^{m} \frac{n}{m} \binom{m}{i} \zeta_{i}$$

$$= \frac{n}{m} =: p$$

We can now bound for some  $\alpha \in [0, 1]$ ,

$$\begin{aligned} & \left\| |0^{l}\rangle_{1} |\psi\rangle_{\tilde{D}E} \right\|^{2} = \left\| \langle 0|+\rangle |\psi^{+}\rangle_{1^{c}E} + \langle 0|_{1} |\psi^{-}\rangle_{\tilde{D}E} \right\|^{2} \\ & \geq \left( 2^{-l/2} \| |\psi^{+}\rangle \| - \alpha \| |\psi^{-}\rangle \| \right)^{2} \\ & \geq \left( 2^{-l/2} \sqrt{p} - \alpha \sqrt{1-p} \right)^{2} = 2^{-l}p + \alpha^{2}(1-p) - \alpha 2^{1-l/2} \sqrt{p(1-p)} \\ & \geq \frac{2^{-l}n}{m} - 2^{1-l/2} \sqrt{\frac{n(m-n)}{m^{2}}}. \end{aligned}$$

Bounding  $||(P_{\eta})_{\tilde{D}}||\psi^{\mathbf{x}}\rangle||$  using martingale tail bound. We will now follow the same strategy as in Lemma 5 to show that after applying the extractor failure projector, there are, in fact, more than  $\eta$  registers in state  $|0^{l}\rangle$  with high probability, with one crucial difference: the random variables obtained by measuring the sub-registers of  $\tilde{D}$  in the state  $|\psi^{\mathbf{x}}\rangle$  are not independent. This renders the standard Chernoff bound we used in Lemma 5 inapplicable. We therefore resort to a martingale tail bound for our analysis.

In the following, for ease of notation, let

$$|\psi\rangle = \frac{1}{\||\psi_{\text{sym}}^{\mathbf{x}}\rangle\|} |\psi_{\text{sym}}^{\mathbf{x}}\rangle.$$

Let  $X_i$ ,  $0 \le i \le k(N-1)$  be the random variables defined analogously to the proof of Lemma 5, i.e.

$$X_i = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if measuring register } \tilde{D}_i \text{ of state } |\psi\rangle \text{ yields } 0^l \\ 0 & \text{else.} \end{cases}$$

We define  $X_{\leq i} = (X_1, \dots X_{i-1})$ , and

$$Z_i = \mathbb{E}\left[X_i | X_{\le i}\right],\tag{15}$$

$$\hat{X}_i = Z_i - X_i, \text{ and} (16)$$

$$\hat{Z}_i = \sum_{j=1}^i \hat{X}_j. \tag{17}$$

Note that conditioning on  $X_{\leq i}$ ,  $Z_{\leq i}$ ,  $\hat{X}_i$  and  $\hat{Z}_{\leq i}$  are all equivalent. Let the value of the standard cumulative mean be denoted as

<span id="page-19-1"></span>
$$\mu' = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \mathbb{E}[X_i \mid X_{\le i}] = \sum_{i=1}^{n} Z_i.$$
 (18)

<span id="page-19-0"></span>**Lemma 8.** We have the following probability bound

$$\|(P_{\eta})_{\tilde{D}} |\psi\rangle\|^2 \ge 1 - \exp\left(-\frac{{\delta'}^2 \underline{\mu'}^2}{2m}\right) =: 1 - \varepsilon'$$

Here,

$$\underline{\mu}' \coloneqq 2^{-l}k \left[ N - 1 - \gamma \left( 1 + 4 \cdot 2^l + \log \left( \frac{\log k}{4\gamma} \right) \right) - 4\sqrt{2^l \gamma N} \right]$$

and

$$\eta = (1 - \delta')\mu'.$$

*Proof.* As in the proof of Lemma 5, we use the fact that

$$\|(P_{\eta})_{\tilde{D}} |\psi\rangle\|^2 = \Pr\left[\sum_{i=1}^m X_i \ge \eta\right].$$

{20}------------------------------------------------

Note that conditioning on  $X_{\leq i}, Z_{\leq i}, \hat{X}_i$  and  $\hat{Z}_{\leq i}$  are all equivalent. We get

$$\mathbb{E}\left[\hat{X}_{i} \mid \hat{Z}_{< i}\right] = \mathbb{E}\left[Z_{i} - X_{i} \mid X_{< i}\right]$$

$$= \mathbb{E}\left[\mathbb{E}\left[X_{i} \mid X_{< i}\right] - X_{i} \mid X_{< i}\right]$$

$$= \mathbb{E}\left[X_{i} \mid X_{< i}\right] - \mathbb{E}\left[X_{i} \mid X_{< i}\right]$$

$$= 0$$

$$\implies \mathbb{E}\Big[\hat{Z}_i \mid \hat{Z}_{< i}\Big] = \hat{Z}_{i-1} + \mathbb{E}\Big[\hat{X}_i \mid \hat{Z}_{< i}\Big] = \hat{Z}_{i-1}$$

Hence  $\hat{Z}_i$  is a martingale. Additionally,  $\hat{Z}_0 = 0$  and  $|\hat{Z}_i - \hat{Z}_{i-1}| = |\hat{X}_i| \leq 1$ . Applying Lemma 4 we thus get

$$\Pr\left[\sum_{j=1}^{m} X_j \le \mu' - \epsilon\right] = \Pr\left[\sum_{j=1}^{m} X_j \le \sum_{j=1}^{m} Z_j - \epsilon\right]$$
$$= \Pr\left[0 - \hat{Z}_m \le -\epsilon\right]$$
$$\le \exp\left(-\frac{\epsilon^2}{2m}\right).$$

Using the lower bound on the cumulative mean from Lemma 9 we obtain the desired bound,

$$\Pr\left[\sum_{j=1}^{m} X_j \le \underline{\mu}' - \epsilon\right] \le \exp\left(-\frac{\epsilon^2}{2m}\right).$$

We continue by providing the needed lower bound on the cumulative mean.

<span id="page-20-0"></span>**Lemma 9.** The cumulative mean defined in Eq. (18) is bounded as

$$\mu' \ge \underline{\mu}' = 2^{-l}k \left[ N - 1 - \gamma \left( 1 + 4 \cdot 2^l + \log \left( \frac{4\gamma}{\log k} \right) \right) - 4\sqrt{2^l \gamma N} \right].$$

Proof. Let  $|\beta\rangle_{\tilde{D}_{[i+1:m']}}$  be the post-measurement state after the first i-1 sub-registers of  $\tilde{D}$  have been measured and fixed outcomes  $y_1, \ldots y_{i-1}$  have been obtained. $$\Pr[X_i = 1 | y_1, \dots, y_{i-1}] \ge \frac{2^{-l}(n-i+1)}{(m-i+1)} - 2^{1-l/2} \sqrt{\frac{(n-i+1)(m-n)}{(m-i+1)^2}}$$

{21}------------------------------------------------

by Lemma [7](#page-17-1) and therefore in particular

$$\mathbb{E}[X_i|X_{< i}] = \Pr[X_i = 1|X_{< i}] \ge \frac{2^{-l}(n-i+1)}{(m-i+1)} - 2^{1-l/2} \sqrt{\frac{(n-i+1)(m-n)}{(m-i+1)^2}}$$
$$\ge \frac{2^{-l}(n-i)}{(m-i)} - 2^{1-l/2} \sqrt{\frac{(n-i)(m-n)}{(m-i)^2}}.$$

We can now lower-bound now for any n ′ ≤ n,

$$\mu' = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \mathbb{E}[X_i | X_{< i}]$$

$$\geq \sum_{i=1}^{n'} \mathbb{E}[X_i | X_{< i}]$$

$$\geq \sum_{i=1}^{n'} \left(\frac{n-i}{m-i}\right) 2^{-l} - 2^{1-l/2} \sqrt{\frac{(n-i)(m-n)}{(m-i)^2}}$$

$$= \sum_{i=1}^{n'} \left(1 - \frac{m-n}{m-i}\right) 2^{-l} - 2^{1-l/2} \sqrt{\frac{(n-i)(m-n)}{(m-i)^2}}.$$

$$= n' - 2^{-l}(m-n) \sum_{i=1}^{n'} \frac{1}{m-i} - \sum_{i=1}^{n'} 2^{1-l/2} \sqrt{\frac{(n-i)(m-n)}{(m-i)^2}}.$$

We have the following inequalities for n ′ < m on the above terms in the sum:

1. On the second term: 
$$\sum_{i=1}^{n'} \frac{1}{m-i} \le \int_{1}^{n'} \frac{dx}{m-x} = \log\left(\frac{m-1}{m-n'}\right),$$
2. On the third term: 
$$\sum_{i=1}^{n'} \sqrt{\frac{(n-i)}{(m-i)^2}} \le \sum_{i=1}^{n'} \sqrt{\frac{(m-i)}{(m-i)^2}}$$

$$\le \int_{1}^{n'} \frac{dx}{\sqrt{m-x}} = 2\left[\sqrt{(m-1)} - \sqrt{(m-n')}\right]$$

Hence we get:

$$\mu' \ge 2^{-l} \left[ n' - (m-n) \log \left( \frac{m-1}{m-n'} \right) \right] - 2^{2-l/2} \sqrt{m-n} \left[ \sqrt{(m-1)} - \sqrt{(m-n')} \right]$$

$$\ge 2^{-l} \left[ n' + (m-n) \log \left( \frac{m-n'}{m-1} \right) \right] - 2^{2-l/2} \sqrt{m(m-n)}$$

$$= 2^{-l} \left[ k(N-1-\gamma) - 2^{2+l} k\gamma + (k\gamma) \log \left( \frac{\gamma(1+2^{2+l})}{2^l \log k - 1} \right) \right] - 2^{2-l/2} \sqrt{m(m-n)}$$

$$\ge 2^{-l} k \left[ (N-(1+\gamma+2^{2+l}\gamma) + (\gamma) \log \left( \frac{4\gamma}{\log k} \right) \right] - 2^{2-l/2} \sqrt{k(N-1)\gamma k}$$

$$\ge 2^{-l} k \left[ (N-1-\gamma) \left( 1+2^{2+l} + \log \left( \frac{4\gamma}{\log k} \right) \right) - 2^{2+l/2} \sqrt{N\gamma} \right]$$

Here, we have chose n' so that we do not unnecessarily lower-bound non-negative terms by negative ones,

$$\left[\frac{n-i}{m-i}\right] 2^{-l} - \frac{2^{1-l}\sqrt{(n-i)(m-n)}}{m-i} \ge 0$$

$$\Longrightarrow (n-i)2^{-l} \ge 2^{1-l/2}\sqrt{(n-i)(m-n)}$$

$$\Longrightarrow (n-i)^2 2^{-2l} \ge 2^{2-l}(n-i)(m-n)$$

$$\Longrightarrow (n-i) \ge 2^{2+l}(m-n)$$

$$\Longrightarrow i \le n-2^{2+l}(m-n) =: n'.$$

## <span id="page-26-0"></span>3.3 Extractability – lifting to general prover.

We now go on to apply our bound for a prover outputting a proof with a fixed value for **a** to obtain a bound for a general prover. **Theorem 2.** Let  $\mathcal{P}$  be a dishonest prover for the Fischlin transform of a special-sound  $\Sigma$ -protocol for a relation R with unique responses, in the QROM, making no more than q quantum queries to the random oracle. We have

<span id="page-26-4"></span><span id="page-26-2"></span>
$$\Pr_{(x,\pi,w)\leftarrow\langle\mathcal{P},\mathcal{E}\rangle}[\mathcal{V}(x,\pi)=1] = \Pr_{(x,\pi)\leftarrow\mathcal{P}^H}[\mathcal{V}(x,\pi)=1]$$
 (22)

and

$$\Pr_{(x,\pi,w)\leftarrow\langle\mathcal{P},\mathcal{E}\rangle}[\mathcal{V}(x,\pi)=1\land(x,w)\notin R]\leq 4(q+k)^2\cdot\delta,\tag{23}$$

where  $\delta$  is such that for any prover  $\mathcal{P}'$  and any  $\mathbf{a}_0 \in \mathcal{M}^k$ ,

<span id="page-26-5"></span>
$$\Pr_{(x,\pi=(\mathbf{a},\mathbf{c},\mathbf{z}),w)\leftarrow\langle\mathcal{P}',\mathcal{E}\rangle}[\mathbf{a}=\mathbf{a}_0\wedge\mathcal{V}(x,\pi)=1\wedge(x,w)\notin R]\leq\delta.$$
 (24)

Combining with Lemma 10, we get our main result.

Corollary 1 (straight-line extractability of the Fischlin transform in the QROM). Let  $\Sigma$  be a  $\Sigma$ -protocol for a relation R with special soundness and unique responses. If the challenge space is of size  $N=c\cdot 2^l\cdot \log(k)$  for some constant c and parameters k,l of the Fischlin transform,  $2^{\frac{1}{c}} \leq k \leq 2^{\frac{2^l}{256 \cdot c}}$ ,  $l \geq 14$ , and  $l = O(\log \log k)$  then  $\operatorname{Fis}[\Sigma]$  is a proof of knowledge with straight-line extractability. The extractor's simulation of the quantum-accesible random oracle can be made perfect  $(\varepsilon_{\text{sim}} = 0)$  and for a q-query adversarial prover,

<span id="page-26-1"></span>

## <span id="page-29-0"></span>3.4 Proof of Zero Knowledge

We prove that the Fischlin transform is zero-knowledge in the quantum random oracle model (QROM). Our proof follows standard techniques for adaptive oracle reprogramming in the QROM [GHHM21]. We begin by defining the zero-knowledge simulator. Let  $\mathsf{Sim}_{\Sigma}$  denote the honest-verifier zero-knowledge simulator for the underlying  $\Sigma$ -protocol.

<span id="page-29-1"></span>**Definition 11 (Simulator**  $Sim_{Fis}$ ). The simulator  $Sim_{Fis}^H$  proceeds as follows.

**Setup.** Sample a random function

$$\tilde{H}: [k] \times \mathcal{C} \to \{0, 1\}^l.$$

For each  $i \in [k]$ , sample  $c_i \leftarrow^{\$} C$  such that  $\tilde{H}(i, c_i) = 0^l$ . If no such value exists, abort.

**Proof Generation.** For each  $i \in [k]$ , run

$$(a_i, z_i) \leftarrow \mathsf{Sim}_{\Sigma}(x, c_i).$$

Set  $\mathbf{a} = (a_i)_{i \in [k]}$  and similarly  $\mathbf{c}$ ,  $\mathbf{z}$ .

**Oracle Programming.** For any query  $(\mathbf{a}, i, c, z)$ , it answers as follows:

$$H'(\mathbf{a}, i, c, z) = \begin{cases} \tilde{H}(i, c) & \text{if } V(a_i, c, z) = 1 \text{ and } H \text{ is undefined,} \\ H(\mathbf{a}, i, c, z) & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases}$$

Output. Define the proof

$$\pi = (\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{c}, \mathbf{z}).$$

and output  $(\pi, H')$ .

With this simulator in hand, we can prove zero-knowledge.

**Theorem 3.** For a  $\Sigma$ -protocol with commitment entropy, the Fischlin transform is zero-knowledge in the QROM.

*Proof.* The simulator clearly runs in expected polynomial time.

{30}------------------------------------------------

Hybrid Argument. We prove indistinguishability by a sequence of hybrid experiments.

**Hybrid**  $H_0$ . The honest prover executes the Fischlin protocol using an unmodified quantum random oracle.

**Hybrid**  $H_1$ . The prover generates commitments  $(a_i)_{i \in [k]}$  honestly. The oracle is reprogrammed on all accepting transcripts with prefix  $(\mathbf{a}, i)$  as in Definition 11. Challenges  $c_i$  are sampled uniformly, and responses  $z_i$  are computed honestly.

By the adaptive reprogramming lemma of [GHHM21] (Lemma 1),  $H_1$  is computationally indistinguishable from  $H_0$ , except with probability negligible in the min-entropy of the commitment vector.

**Hybrid**  $H'_1$ . As in  $H_1$ , but challenges  $c_i$  are sampled conditioned on  $\tilde{H}(i, c_i) = 0^l$ .

Since  $\tilde{H}$  is uniformly random, this conditioning affects the distribution only negligibly. Hence  $H'_1$  is indistinguishable from  $H_1$ .

**Hybrid**  $H_2$ . As in  $H'_1$ , but responses are generated using the simulator  $Sim_{\Sigma}$  instead of the honest prover.

By the honest-verifier zero-knowledge property of the  $\Sigma$ -protocol,  $H_2$  is indistinguishable from  $H'_1$  up to advantage at most  $k\varepsilon$ , where  $\varepsilon$  is the distinguishing advantage for a single repetition.

Hybrid  $H_2$  corresponds exactly to the output of  $Sim_{Fis}$ . Since each transition introduces only negligible distinguishing advantage, the simulator output is computationally indistinguishable from a real proof. This establishes zero knowledge in the QROM.
