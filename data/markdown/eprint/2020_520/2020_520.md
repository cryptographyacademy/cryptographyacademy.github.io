## <span id="page-0-0"></span>MMSAT: A Scheme for Multimessage Multiuser Signature Aggregation

Yarkın Dor¨oz<sup>1</sup> , Jeffrey Hoffstein<sup>2</sup> , Joseph H. Silverman<sup>2</sup> , and Berk Sunar<sup>1</sup>

<sup>1</sup> Worcester Polytechnic Institute, Worcester MA, USA, {ydoroz,sunar}@wpi.edu <sup>2</sup> Brown University, Providence RI, USA, {jhoff,jhs}@math.brown.edu

Abstract. Post-Quantum (PQ) signature schemes are known for large key and signature sizes, which may inhibit their deployment in real world applications. In this work, we construct a PQ signature scheme MMSAT that is the first such scheme capable of aggregating unrelated messages signed individually by different parties. Our proposal extends the notion of multisignatures, which are signatures that support aggregation of signatures on a single message signed by multiple parties. Multisignatures are especially useful in blockchain applications, where a transaction may be signed by multiple users. The proposed construction achieves significant gains in bandwidth and storage requirements by allowing aggregation of unrelated transactions. Our construction is derived by extending the PASSRS scheme, and thus the security of our scheme relies on the hardness of the Vandermonde-SIS problem. When aggregated, a signature consists of two parts. The first part is a post-quantum size signature that grows very slowly, scaling by on the order of log K bits for K signatures. The second part scales linearly with K, with a very short fixed cost, roughly twice the bit security. Thus even when aggregating a modest number of signatures, the per signature cost of MMSAT is in line with that of traditional pre-quantum signature schemes such as ECDSA. As an extension to MMSAT, we describe a variant called MMSATK in which it the public keys required to verify an aggregated signature are compressed by a factor of 20 to 30.

Keywords: Digital signature, PASS, signature aggregation, compressed key

## <span id="page-2-0"></span>1 Introduction

Traditional cryptographic schemes providing encryption, key encapsulation, and signature services are expected to be replaced by quantum-resistant schemes in deployments during the next decade. The threat is so urgent that the US National Institute of Standards and Technology started a standardization competition in 2018 to select one or more so-called Post-Quantum schemes. In the meantime, companies such as Google have already started experimenting with PQ cryptography, including the New Hope scheme [\[5,](#page-56-0) [11\]](#page-56-1) and an NTRU derivate [\[23,](#page-57-0) [25\]](#page-57-1).

PQ Signature Schemes are expected to play a vital role in protecting the integrity of data in storage, during transmission, and even during computation [\[9,](#page-56-2) [19\]](#page-56-3). Indeed, in recent years there has been increasing interest in signature schemes having extended features. For example, multisignatures are a class of signature schemes that allow aggregation into a single signature of signatures produced by many parties on a single message [\[24\]](#page-57-2). Some multisignature schemes even support aggregation of the public keys, thereby greatly saving bandwidth and making them ideal for Blockchain applications [\[29\]](#page-57-3), where transactions may be signed by multiple users and where reducing the block size is crucial in improving the throughput of the P2P network. Targeting the same application domain, a recent proposal by Bansarkhani and Sturm [\[7\]](#page-56-4) proposed a PQ multisignature scheme. This is important, since the emergence of quantum computers will have a disastrous impact on current blockchain implementations. A significant feature offered by blockchains is that all past transactions are recorded on a ledger whose integrity is strongly protected, i.e., the ledger is expected to provide longterm security. The vast majority of Blockchains currently use traditional elliptic curve-based signatures (ECDSA), which is vulnerable to quantum attacks.

While techniques such as multisignatures are useful for compressing multiply signed individual transactions, the bulk of the transactions on Bitcoin and other networks are signed by different users. Therefore, new blocks are mostly made up of transactions with separate signatures that are not compressible by existing multisignature schemes. An early aggregation scheme capable of compressing independent messages signed by different users was proposed by Boneh et al. in [\[10\]](#page-56-5). The scheme uses bilinear maps constructed over a suitably chosen elliptic curve to achieve aggregation. Although at that time the primary proposed 

{3}------------------------------------------------

application was to certificate chains, the aggregation scheme can be effectively used to compress signatures on transactions in a block. A more recent scheme by Boneh et al. [8] supports both signature compression and public-key aggregation with the primary goal being to reduce the size of the Bitcoin Blockchain. Their aggregation scheme is derived from Schnorr and BLS signatures. From an efficiency perspective, the methods in [8] appear to provide an ideal solution, since the scheme supports not only signature compression, but is also able to compress multiple public-keys into a single one. If deployed, an aggregation scheme such as [8] would greatly reduce the crypto overhead in Blockchains.

However, all of the aforementioned schemes use traditional cryptographic primitives that assume hardness in the traditional non-quantum model. There is an urgent need for PQ signature schemes that allow aggregation. This problem is exacerbated by the fact that most PQ schemes typically have signature and key sizes significantly larger than ECDSA, on the order of several thousand bits compared to the several hundred bits in ECDSA. For example, signatures in BLISS are 625 bytes, while those in ECDSA are 64 bytes [15]. Compression of individual PQ signatures and aggregation of public keys remain a challenge.

#### Our Contribution. The present work includes the following:

• We propose the first PQ signature scheme supporting aggregation across unrelated signatures signed by different users. It comes in several versions in which increased algorithm complexity yields improved operating characteristics. We denote these schemes by:

MMSA = Multi-message Multi-user Signature Aggregation Scheme,

MMSAT = MMSA with signature compression,

MMSATK = MMSA with both signature and public key compression.

(The T in MMSAT refers to a linear map T used for signature compression.) An aggregated MMSAT signature has size roughly equal to a single PQ signature plus  $2\lambda$ -bits per signature aggregated. From a practical perspective, even for a modest number of signatures, the aggregate signature size of MMSAT represents an improvement over traditional signature schemes such as ECDSA; e.g. it is 18-times smaller than BLISS and 1.9 times smaller than ECDSA for 1000 signatures at 128-bit security.

- We reduce the aggregate signature security of MMSAT to the forgery security of  $PASS_{RS}^*$ , a variant of  $PASS_{RS}$ .
- We use an ∞-norm analysis to give (slightly) improved estimates for the forgery probability from lattice reduction, leading to optimized parameters. This may be of independent interest for optimizing other lattice-based schemes.
- We analyze the lattice security of MMSAT and provide concrete parameters secure against BKZ and Sieving.

### <span id="page-4-1"></span>2 Background, Notation, and the PASS<sub>RS</sub> Scheme

In this section we set notation and discuss the hard lattice problem underlying  $\mathsf{PASS}_{\mathtt{RS}}$  and our various amalgamation schemes.

#### <span id="page-4-2"></span>2.1 The SIS problem

The Short Integer Solution problem (SIS), introduced by Ajtai in 1996 [1], has been the basis of a number of cryptographic constructions. SIS is the problem of of finding a vector  $\mathbf{y}$  in the kernel of a given linear transformation  $\mathbf{A}: \mathbb{Z}_q^n \mapsto \mathbb{Z}_q^m$  such that  $\mathbf{y}$  is small with respect to a prescribed norm. Ajtai showed a remarkable worst/average case equivalency property, i.e. for uniform random  $\mathbf{A}$ , for m = Poly(n) with n also serving as the security parameter, the ability to solve random SIS instances with non-negligible probability implies an ability to find short vectors in any lattice within a certain approximation parameter. In [32] Micciancio and Peikert show that SIS retains its hardness even for small moduli  $q \geq \beta n^{\delta}$  for any constant  $\delta > 0$ , where  $\beta$  is the bound on the Euclidean norm of the solution.

In practice, generic SIS instances do not yield compact and efficient implementations, so further assumptions are commonly made. An efficient class of schemes is obtained by replacing the matrix A with a list of elements  $a_1, \ldots, a_k \in \mathbb{Z}_q[x]/\psi(x)$ , where typically  $\psi(x)$  is a cyclotomic polynomial. This gives rise to the Ideal-SISproblem (Ideal-SIS) of finding small  $y_i \in \mathbb{Z}_q[x]/\psi(x)$  satisfying  $\sum a_i y_i = 0$ . The security of the PASS<sub>RS</sub> scheme relies on a Vandermonde version of SIS(Vandermonde-SIS), in which A is a submatrix of the Fourier transform matrix, so A takes the form of a partial Vandermonde matrix. In the literature, Vandermonde-SIS is also referred to as the Partial Fourier Recovery Problem.

component-wise multiplication of vectors in  $\mathbb{Z}_q^N$ .  $\odot$ 

We often lift vectors in  $\mathbb{Z}_q^N$  to vectors in  $\mathbb{Z}^N$  with coordinates in [-q/2, q/2].

**Definition 1.** The discrete Fourier transform over  $\mathbb{Z}_q$  is the linear transformation

$$\mathcal{F}: \mathbb{Z}_q^N \longrightarrow \mathbb{Z}_q^N, \quad \boldsymbol{f} \longmapsto \mathcal{F}(\boldsymbol{f}) = \widehat{\boldsymbol{f}}, \quad \textit{given by the matrix} \quad (\mathcal{F})_{i,j} = g^{ij}.$$

Alternatively, as a map  $R_q \to \mathbb{Z}_q^N$ , the map  $\mathcal{F}$  is given by the formula

$$\mathcal{F}(f) = (f(1), f(g), f(g^2), \dots, f(g^{N-1})).$$

The discrete Fourier transform is a ring isomorphism  $(\mathbb{Z}_q^N, \star) \to (\mathbb{Z}_1^N, \odot)$ ,

$$\mathcal{F}(a+b) = \mathcal{F}(a) + \mathcal{F}(b)$$
 and  $\mathcal{F}(a \star b) = \mathcal{F}(a) \odot \mathcal{F}(b)$ .

The hard problem underlying  $\mathsf{PASS}_{\mathtt{RS}}$  is an underdetermined linear inversion problem that is sometimes called the *Partial Fourier Recovery Problem*. We fix a subset

$$\Omega = {\Omega_1, \dots, \Omega_t} \subset {0, 1, \dots, N-1},$$

and we use it to define a linear transformation  $\mathcal{F}_{\Omega}$  that projects  $\mathcal{F}(\boldsymbol{f})$  onto the coordinates specified by  $\Omega$ . Thus  $\mathcal{F}_{\Omega}: R_q \to \mathbb{Z}_q^t$  is the map

$$\mathcal{F}_{\Omega}(\mathbf{f}) = \widehat{\mathbf{f}}|_{\Omega} = \begin{pmatrix} \text{the coordinates of } \widehat{\mathbf{f}} \text{ that are } \\ \text{specified by the index set } \Omega \end{pmatrix} = (\mathbf{f}(g^i))_{i \in \Omega}.$$

**Definition 2.** The Partial Fourier Recovery Problem is:

Given 
$$\widehat{\boldsymbol{\kappa}}|_{\Omega} \in \mathbb{Z}_q^t$$
, find  $\boldsymbol{\xi} \in R_q$  with small norm such that  $\widehat{\boldsymbol{\xi}}|_{\Omega} = \widehat{\boldsymbol{\kappa}}|_{\Omega}$  in  $\mathbb{Z}_q^t$ .

Remark 1. The problem of recovering a signal from a restricted number of its Fourier coefficients is a well-studied problem that is known to be difficult in general. See Section 2.3 for a reformulation as a lattice problem. The original PASS<sub>RS</sub> paper |21| contains a more detailed discussion of the difficulty of solving the partial Fourier recovery problem.

Remark 2. PASS<sub>RS</sub> is an improved version of the original PASS scheme [22]. It uses a rejection sampling method pioneered by Lyubashevsky [27] to negate attacks based on transcript analysis. Lyubashevsky originally constructed a latticebased transcript-secure identification scheme using a technique that he called "aborting," and which is often called rejection sampling. Lyubashevsky et al. later improved his technique and constructed a signature scheme via the Fiat-Shamir method, with hardness based on the Ring-SIS problem [15, 20, 28].

#### <span id="page-7-0"></span>2.3 The Partial Fourier Recovery Problem as a Lattice Problem

We write the coordinates of  $\widehat{\kappa}|_{\Omega}$  as  $\widehat{\kappa}_1, \dots, \widehat{\kappa}_t$ , and then the Partial Fourier Problem asks for a short vector  $\boldsymbol{\xi} = (\xi_0, \dots, \xi_{N-1}) \in \mathbb{Z}^N$  satisfying

$$\xi_0 + g^i \xi_1 + g^{2i} \xi_2 + \dots + g^{(N-1)i} \xi_{N-1} \equiv \widehat{\kappa}_i \pmod{q} \quad \text{for all } i \in \Omega.$$

This gives t linear congruences in the N unknowns  $\xi_0, \ldots, \xi_{N-1}$ , and the fact that g is a primitive Nth root of unity in  $\mathbb{Z}_q$  implies that the congruences are independent. (Indeed, the full set of congruences for  $0 \le i < N$  is given by a vanderMonde matrix, which is invertible.) This means that we can solve for the first t unknowns in terms of the later ones. So for  $0 \le k < t$ , we have

$$\xi_k \equiv \sum_{j=t}^{N-1} h_{jk} \xi_j - c_k \pmod{q}$$
 with known  $h_{jk}, c_k \in \mathbb{Z}_q$ .

Writing  $I_n$  for the n-by-n identity matrix, we consider the lattice

<span id="page-7-3"></span>
$$\Lambda = \operatorname{RowSpan}\begin{pmatrix} qI_t & 0 \\ H & I_{N-t} \end{pmatrix}, \quad \text{where} \quad H = \begin{pmatrix} h_{t0} & \cdots & h_{t,t-1} \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ h_{N-1,0} & \cdots & h_{N-1,t-1} \end{pmatrix}.$$
(3)

Then  $\Lambda$  contains the vector

$$(\xi_0 + c_0, \dots, \xi_{t-1} + c_{t-1}, \xi_t, \dots, \xi_{N-1})$$

which is close to the known vector  $(c_0, \ldots, c_{t-1}, 0, \ldots, 0)$ , so the Partial Fourier Recovery Problem can be formulated as a Closest Vector Problem in the lattice  $\Lambda$ . And as usual, a CVP in dimension N can be solved by embedding it into an SVP in dimension N+1, so throughout this paper, we simply identify the CVP problem associated to partial Fourier recovery with an SVP problem in the same lattice.

<span id="page-7-2"></span>**Definition 3.** We define the space of (N, q, t)-SIS lattices to be set of lattices

$$\Lambda_{N,q,t} = \left\{ \operatorname{RowSpan} \begin{pmatrix} qI_t & 0 \\ H & I_{N-t} \end{pmatrix} : H \in \operatorname{Mat}_{(N-t)\times t}(\mathbb{Z}_q) \right\}.$$

Elements  $a \in R_q$  are represented as polynomials

$$\mathbf{a} = a_0 + a_1 x + a_2 x^2 + \dots + a_{N-1} x^{N-1}$$
 with  $a_i \in \mathbb{Z}_q$ ,

When convenient, we freely transition between these representations. For numerical calculations, we consistently identify  $a_i \in \mathbb{Z}_q$  with an integer satisfying  $|a_i| \leq q/2$ . With this convention, the  $L_{\infty}$ -norm and  $L_2$ -norm of  $\boldsymbol{a} \in R_q$  are

$$\|\boldsymbol{a}\|_{\infty} = \max_{0 \le i \le N} |a_i|$$
 and  $\|\boldsymbol{a}\|_2 = \sqrt{|a_0|^2 + \dots + |a_{N-1}|^2}$ .

(The  $L_{\infty}$ -norm is also called the *sup norm*.) We define sets of norm-bounded vectors and trinary vectors by

$$\mathcal{B}^{\infty}(k) = \left\{ \boldsymbol{a} \in R_q : \|\boldsymbol{a}\|_{\infty} \leq k \right\},$$

$$\mathcal{T}_N(d) = \left\{ \begin{array}{l} \text{polynomials in } R_q \text{ with } d \text{ coefficients equal to } 1, \\ d \text{ coefficients equal to } -1, \text{ and the rest equal to } 0 \end{array} \right\}.$$

Finally, we write [K] for the set  $\{1, 2, ..., K\}$ , and  $\mathbb{1}_{\mathcal{S}}(x)$  for the indicator function that equals 1 if  $x \in \mathcal{S}$  and 0 otherwise.

#### <span id="page-8-2"></span>3.2 PASS<sub>RS</sub> Key Generation

In the original formulation of PASS<sub>RS</sub> [22], the secret key was a randomly chosen polynomial  $f \in \mathcal{B}^{\infty}(1)$ , so its coefficients were chosen independently and uniformly from  $\{-1,0,1\}$ . Here we add flexibility by taking f to be a randomly chosen element of  $\mathcal{T}_N(d_f)$ . The parameter  $d_f$  is selected so that that the key space has more than  $2^{2\lambda}$  elements, and so that various lattice security estimates are satisfied. #### <span id="page-9-0"></span>3.3 PASS<sub>RS</sub> Signing

Signing is an iteratative process consisting of the generation of a candidate signature followed by a rejection sampling step to prevent the publication of signatures that could leak secret key information.

The signer has a secret key f and wants to sign a message digest  $\mu$ . She first selects a commitment polynomial y uniformly at random from  $\mathcal{B}^{\infty}(k)$ . The commitment y serves to mask the private key and must be treated with the same care as the private key itself. The signer then computes and stores the partial Fourier transform  $\widehat{y}|_{\Omega} = \mathcal{F}_{\Omega} y$ . The quantity  $\widehat{y}|_{\Omega}$  will ultimately be made public if the candidate signature passes rejection sampling.

Next, the signer computes a challenge polynomial  $c \in \mathcal{T}(d_c)$  that is used to bind  $\hat{y}|_{\Omega}$  to  $\mu$ . To do this she uses a public hash/encoder function:<sup>3</sup>

$$\mathsf{Hash}_{\mathsf{C}}: \{0,1\}^* \to \mathcal{T}_N(d_c) \quad \text{to compute} \quad \boldsymbol{c} = \mathsf{Hash}_{\mathsf{C}}(\widehat{\boldsymbol{y}}|_{\Omega}, \widehat{\boldsymbol{f}}|_{\Omega}, \mu).^4$$

#### <span id="page-10-0"></span>3.4 PASS<sub>RS</sub> Verification

To check the validity of the signature  $(\hat{\boldsymbol{y}}|_{\Omega}, \boldsymbol{z}, \mu)$  for the public key  $\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}|_{\Omega}$ , the verifier first computes

$$\boldsymbol{c} = \mathsf{Hash}_\mathsf{C}(\widehat{\boldsymbol{y}}|_\Omega, \widehat{\boldsymbol{f}}|_\Omega, \mu).$$

The verifier accepts the signature as valid if

$$\boldsymbol{z} \in \mathcal{B}^{\infty}(k-b) \quad \text{and} \quad \widehat{\boldsymbol{z}}|_{\Omega} = \widehat{\boldsymbol{f}}|_{\Omega} \cdot \widehat{\boldsymbol{c}}|_{\Omega} + \widehat{\boldsymbol{y}}|_{\Omega}.$$

#### <span id="page-10-1"></span>3.5 PASS<sub>RS</sub> Correctness

Since  $\mathcal{F}_{\Omega}$  is a ring homomorphism that changes  $\star$  multiplication to  $\odot$  multiplication, we see that

$$\boldsymbol{z} = \boldsymbol{f} \star \boldsymbol{c} + \boldsymbol{y} \quad \text{implies that} \quad \widehat{\boldsymbol{z}}|_{\Omega} = \widehat{\boldsymbol{f}}|_{\Omega} \odot \widehat{\boldsymbol{c}}|_{\Omega} + \widehat{\boldsymbol{y}}|_{\Omega}.$$

Hence any signature that comes out of the signing algorithm will be verified as valid by the verification algorithm.

#### <span id="page-11-0"></span>Differences from the Original $PASS_{RS}$ 3.6

The version of  $PASS_{RS}$  that we use in this paper differs from the original version of  $PASS_{RS}$  in |21| in three ways:

1. The original PASS<sub>RS</sub> signature was  $(c, z, \mu)$ , while we are using  $(\widehat{y}|_{\Omega}, z, \mu)$ . This is irrelevant from a security perspective, since the formulas

$$c = \mathsf{Hash}_{\mathsf{C}}(T(\widehat{\boldsymbol{y}}|_{\Omega}), \mu, \widehat{\boldsymbol{f}}|_{\Omega}) \quad \text{and} \quad \widehat{\boldsymbol{y}}|_{\Omega} = \widehat{\boldsymbol{f}}|_{\Omega} \odot \widehat{\boldsymbol{c}}|_{\Omega} - \widehat{\boldsymbol{z}}|_{\Omega}$$

show that an attacker can pass from one form of the signature to the other using public knowledge. In practice, the vector  $\boldsymbol{c}$  tends to require fewer bits to describe than  $\widehat{\boldsymbol{y}}|_{\Omega}$ , which is why one would use it for single signatures. However, our amalgamation algorithm MMSAT is going to require the  $\hat{y}|_{\Omega}$ values, so it is easier (at least for the sake of exposition) if we take take the individual signatures to already be in the form  $(\widehat{\boldsymbol{y}}|_{\Omega}, \boldsymbol{z}, \mu)$ .

- 2. The original PASS<sub>RS</sub> scheme did not include the public key  $f|_{\Omega}$  as input to the hash function  $\mathsf{Hash}_\mathsf{C}$  used to create the challenge polynomial c. By including  $f|_{\Omega}$  or  $T(f|_{\Omega})$  in the input to  $\mathsf{Hash}_{\mathsf{C}}$ , we tie c irrevocably to the key f. This can only help the security of the scheme.
- 3. The original  $PASS_{RS}$  scheme did not us a a linear transformation T. For individual  $PASS_{RS}$  signatures, there is no reason to use T, but for the amalgamate signatures produced by MMSA, the map T can be used to compress the amalgamate signature and the associated public keys.

## <span id="page-12-0"></span>4 From PASS<sub>RS</sub> to MMSA: Aggregating Signatures

In this section we describe MMSA, which is our basic signature aggregation scheme. Later we describe modified versions of MMSA called MMSAT and MM-SATK that allow compressed aggregated signatures and compressed public keys

#### <span id="page-12-1"></span>4.1 Signature Aggregation

Suppose that K individual signers use their private keys  $f_1, \ldots, f_K$  to sign message digests  $\mu_1, \ldots, \mu_K$ . They do this by randomly choosing commitment polynomials  $y_1, \ldots, y_K \in \mathcal{B}^{\infty}(k)$ , using the hash function  $\mathsf{Hash}_{\mathsf{C}}$  to create their challenge polynomials  $c_1, \ldots, c_K$ , and creating their signatures  $z_1, \ldots, z_K \in \mathcal{B}^{\infty}(k-b)$ . More precisely, the signature of individual i is the triple  $(\widehat{y}_i|_{\Omega}, z_i, \mu_i)$  associated to the public key  $\widehat{f}_i|_{\Omega}$ . A crucial aspect of the Fiat–Shamir construction is that the signer cannot influence the value of the challenge  $c_i$ , since  $c_i$  is the output of a hash function. The verification of the i'th signature is via a proof of knowledge of the private key  $f_i$  and the fact, demonstrated via  $c_i$ , that  $\widehat{y}_i|_{\Omega}$  really is a collection of values of a short polynomial y. The goal in forming an aggregate signature is to have the verification of the aggregate signature imply, with high probability, the validity of all the individual signatures.

A crucial piece of the aggregate signature is a random linear combination of the  $z_i$ . In order to determine which linear combination to use, the aggregator applies a hash/encoder function to the challenge polynomials and computes a vector  $\boldsymbol{\beta}$  with  $\pm 1$  coordinates,<sup>5</sup>

$$\boldsymbol{\beta} = (\beta_1, \dots, \beta_K) = \mathsf{Hash}_\mathsf{B}(\boldsymbol{c}_1, \dots, \boldsymbol{c}_K) \in \{-1, 1\}^K.$$

Then one piece of the aggregate signature is the sum

$$\boldsymbol{z} = \sum_{i=1}^K \beta_i \boldsymbol{z}_i.$$

The aggregator checks that z satisfies<sup>6</sup>

<span id="page-12-4"></span>
$$\|\boldsymbol{z}\|_{\infty} \le B_k \sqrt{K}(k-b) \tag{5}$$

for an appropriately chosen public parameter  $B_k$ ; see Section 4.2. The inequality (5) is then one of the conditions checked by the verifier.

Each coordinate of z is more-or-less a K-step random walk, with each step having length uniformly distributed in [-k+b,k-b], which is why it is easy for the aggregator to construct z satisfying (5) for, say  $B_k = 3$ . It follows from (5)

<span id="page-12-3"></span><sup>&</sup>lt;sup>6</sup> If (5) fails, the aggregator can permute the order of the signatures being aggregated and try again, since this changes the value of  $\beta$ . We note, however, that increasing  $B_k$  to make it easier for the aggregator will also make it easier for a forger to forge an aggregate signature. In particular, we must take  $B_k\sqrt{K}(k-b)$  sufficiently small compared to q, so we define a new parameter  $B_q$  satisfying  $B_k\sqrt{K}(k-b)\approx B_qq$ , and we require that  $B_q$  be chosen from some suitable range, say  $\frac{1}{4} \leq B_q \leq \frac{2}{5}$ . To verify the aggregated signature, the verifier first recomputes the  $c_i = \mathsf{Hash}_{\mathsf{C}}(\widehat{\boldsymbol{y}}_i|_{\Omega},\mu_i,\widehat{\boldsymbol{f}}_i|_{\Omega})$ . The verifier then uses the  $\boldsymbol{c}_i$  to recompute the  $\beta_i$  via  $\boldsymbol{\beta} = \mathsf{Hash}_{\mathsf{B}}(\boldsymbol{c}_1,\ldots,\boldsymbol{c}_K)$ . He then checks that

<span id="page-13-3"></span>
$$\widehat{\boldsymbol{z}}|_{\Omega} = \sum_{i=1}^{K} \beta_i \left( \widehat{\boldsymbol{f}}_i |_{\Omega} \odot \widehat{\boldsymbol{c}}_i |_{\Omega} + \widehat{\boldsymbol{y}}_i |_{\Omega} \right). \tag{6}$$

For a valid aggregate of valid signatures, the formula (6) is true, since  $z = \sum \beta_i z_i$  and the partial Fourier transform is a ring homomorphism.

To summarize, an aggregate signature is valid if z is short and  $\widehat{z}|_{\Omega}$  takes on a value determined by the public keys  $\widehat{f}_i|_{\Omega}$ , message digests  $\mu_i$ , and commitments  $\widehat{y}_i|_{\Omega}$ .

## <span id="page-15-0"></span>5 From MMSA to MMSAT: Compressing the Aggregate Signature

MMSA aggregates multi-message multi-user signatures, but the aggregate signature includes one <sup>y</sup>b<sup>i</sup> |<sup>Ω</sup> for each signature in the aggregate. To curb this growth, we introduce MMSAT, a variant of MMSA that significantly reduces the per signature overhead. We do this using a linear map

$$T: (\mathbb{Z}/q\mathbb{Z})^t \mapsto (\mathbb{Z}/q\mathbb{Z})^{t_0},$$

so <sup>T</sup>(yb<sup>i</sup> |Ω) requires only t<sup>0</sup> log<sup>2</sup> (q) bits to describe. Choosing t<sup>0</sup> so that

$$t_0 \log_2(q) \approx 2^{\lambda},$$

<span id="page-15-1"></span><sup>10</sup> To ease the calculation, we take a continuous distribution on the interval [−k + b, k − b], rather than a discrete one. This means that we are ignoring some lower order terms.

{16}------------------------------------------------

we will achieve combinatorial security for  $T(\hat{y}_i|_{\Omega})$ .

However, if the signature were to include only the  $T(\widehat{\boldsymbol{y}}_i|\Omega)$  values, then the validity check on  $\boldsymbol{z}$  would involve only  $T(\widehat{\boldsymbol{z}}|\Omega)$ , which would lead to a lattice attack in which the lattice discriminant is reduced from  $q^t$  to  $q^{t_0}$ . This would make it easier for a forger to find a sufficiently small vector, since the expected solution to the SVP would be smaller. To preclude this, we include some additional information in the signature; specifically, we include the quantity

$$Y = \beta_1 \widehat{y}_1|_{\Omega} + \cdots + \beta_K \widehat{y}_K|_{\Omega}.$$

This allows the verifier to do two things:

- 1. The verifier can check that T(Y) is equal to  $\sum \beta_i T(\widehat{y}_i|_{\Omega})$ , thereby ensuring that Y was correctly generated from  $\widehat{y}_1|_{\Omega}, \ldots, \widehat{y}_K|_{\Omega}$ .
- 2. The verifier can check that z has the correct value by comparing  $\widehat{z}|_{\Omega}$  with  $\left(\sum \beta_i \widehat{f}_i|_{\Omega} \odot \widehat{c}_i|_{\Omega}\right) + Y$ . This verification takes place in  $\mathbb{Z}_q^t$ , not  $\mathbb{Z}_q^{t_0}$ , so the lattice used to create a forgery has dimension that depends on t.

We note that this explains why we modified  $PASS_{RS}$  so that the input to  $Hash_C$  includes  $T(\widehat{\boldsymbol{y}}|_{\Omega})$ , instead of  $\widehat{\boldsymbol{y}}|_{\Omega}$ , since the person verifying the aggregate signature only has access to the  $T(\widehat{\boldsymbol{y}}_i|_{\Omega})$  values.

An MMSAT aggregate of K signatures consists of one  $\mathbf{z} \in \mathbb{Z}_q^N$ , one  $\mathbf{Y} \in \mathbb{Z}_q^t$ , and K elements  $T(\widehat{\mathbf{y}}_i|_{\Omega}) \in \mathbb{Z}_q^{t_0}$ , so ignoring the message digests, the cryptographic part of the aggregate signature has length approximately

$$\underbrace{N \log_2(B_k k \sqrt{K})}_{\text{storage for } \boldsymbol{z}} + \underbrace{t \log_2(q)}_{\text{storage for } \boldsymbol{Y}} + \underbrace{Kt_0 \log_2(q)}_{\text{storage for } T(\widehat{\boldsymbol{y}_1}|_{\Omega}), \dots, T(\widehat{\boldsymbol{y}_K}|_{\Omega})}_{\text{bits.}}$$

#### <span id="page-23-0"></span>8.2 Key Swap Attacks on Aggregate Signatures

A serious threat to multisignatures and signature aggregation schemes is the possibility of swapping the forger's key for the legitimate key. In this attack, the adversary does not conform to the usual key generation process, and instead choses public keys in way to gain advantage in forging aggregate signatures [30]. To demonstrate the vulnerability consider an aggregating scheme built on the PASS<sub>RS</sub> (single signature) signing and verification primitives where Alice has a legitimately generated public key  $\hat{f}_A|_{\Omega}$ . During verification Alice's public key is used to recover the public version of y, i.e.  $\hat{y}|_{\Omega} = \hat{z}|_{\Omega} - \hat{f}_A|_{\Omega} \odot \hat{c}|_{\Omega}$  as the key step to enable the check in the verification algorithm. In the aggregation scheme, again we use the same mechanism but this time simultaneously over multiple keys in the aggregated signature z, i.e.  $\sum \hat{y}|_{\Omega} = \hat{z}|_{\Omega} - \sum \hat{f}_i|_{\Omega} \odot \hat{c}_i|_{\Omega}$ . For simplicity assume only two signatures are aggregated and the forger is contributing the second public key  $\hat{f}_B|_{\Omega}$ . A malicious party can craft a fake public key  $\hat{f}_B|_{\Omega} =$  $\widehat{f}|_{\Omega} - \widehat{f}_A|_{\Omega}$  and eliminate the effect of Alice's key during aggregate verification by also forcing  $\widehat{c_A}|_{\Omega} = \widehat{c_B}|_{\Omega}$ . This is easily done in the original PASS<sub>RS</sub> scheme, since in that version the challenge polynomial is computed from  $\mathsf{Hash}(\widehat{\boldsymbol{y}}|_{\Omega},\mu)$ and thus does not depend on the public key. Note that the forger does not even know the actual secret key that yields the public key  $\widehat{f}_{B}|_{\Omega}$ , since he does not know the short  $f_A$  related to  $\widehat{f_A}|_{\Omega}$ , but this does not hinder the success of this aggregate forgery attack:

$$2\widehat{\boldsymbol{y}}|_{\Omega} = \widehat{\boldsymbol{z}}|_{\Omega} - \widehat{\boldsymbol{f}}_{A}|_{\Omega} \odot \widehat{\boldsymbol{c}}|_{\Omega} - \widehat{\boldsymbol{f}}_{B}|_{\Omega} \odot \widehat{\boldsymbol{c}}|_{\Omega}$$

$$= \widehat{\boldsymbol{z}}|_{\Omega} - \widehat{\boldsymbol{f}}_{A}|_{\Omega} \odot \widehat{\boldsymbol{c}}|_{\Omega} - (\widehat{\boldsymbol{f}}|_{\Omega} - \widehat{\boldsymbol{f}}_{A}|_{\Omega}) \odot \widehat{\boldsymbol{c}}|_{\Omega}$$

$$= \widehat{\boldsymbol{z}}|_{\Omega} - \widehat{\boldsymbol{f}}|_{\Omega} \odot \widehat{\boldsymbol{c}}|_{\Omega}$$

The values  $\widehat{f}_A|_{\Omega}$  and  $\widehat{y}|_{\Omega}$  are completely decoupled, with Alice's public key eliminated from the right-hand side, although the verification primitive does use Alice's public key  $\widehat{f}_A|_{\Omega}$ . Since the forger has the short version of  $\widehat{f}|_{\Omega}$  and also gets to choose the short y, he can freely forge an aggregate signature.

To overcome this particularly strong threat, Micali et al. [30] propose using complex schemes such as Zero Knowledge Proofs that ensure the correct generation of the public keys. Boneh et al. in [10] chose a simpler route by requiring that the individual messages in an aggregate signature to be distinct.

In  $\mathsf{PASS}_{\mathsf{RS}}^{13}$  and  $\mathsf{MMSAT}$  we opt for a simple countermeasure by requiring the challenge polynomial  $c_i$  to be computed not only from the the commitment polynomial  $\widehat{y}_i|_{\Omega}$  and message  $\mu_i$ , but also from the public key  $\widehat{f}_i|_{\Omega}$ . To overcome this countermeasure, a key-forging adversary would have to find a hash collision, or a pseudo-collision with the linear sum of a number of colliding hashes, without significantly increasing the norm of the resulting vector.

<span id="page-23-1"></span>As noted earlier, the original version of  $\mathsf{PASS}_{RS}$  did not include the public key as the input to the hash function that generates c, but as also noted earlier, including the public key cannot hurt the security of the scheme, and it only minimally affects efficiency.

We now quote from [\[13\]](#page-56-12): "Experiments in [\[17\]](#page-56-13) show that in practice, the Hermite root factor [for BKZ-β] is typically δ(β, n), where δ(β, n) converges rapidly as n grows to infinity for fixed β, and it is shown in [\[12\]](#page-56-14) that the data supports the assumption that the HRF follows the asymptotic formula [\(13\)](#page-24-3)."

<span id="page-25-2"></span>Proposition 4 (Experimental/Extrapolated). Fix a blocksize β ≥ 50, and let L ⊂ R <sup>N</sup> be a (random) lattice of dimension N β. Then a conservative estimate for the cost of running BKZ-β on L (using a quantum computer ) is

<span id="page-25-1"></span>Cost of 
$$BKZ-\beta \approx 2^{0.265\beta}$$
. (14)

Thus in order to achieve bit security λ, it must be impossible to break the system by running BKZ-β with β ≤ λ/0.265.

Proof (Justification). Various implementations suggest that the cost of running BKZ-β is roughly 20.29<sup>β</sup> , although there would be nontrivial parallelism and memory issues in scaling such an implementation up to large blocksizes; cf. #### <span id="page-25-0"></span>9.1 A Note on the General Sieve Kernel

Recently more efficient algorithms for solving Short Vector Problem (SVP) have appeared in the literature. Especially sieving, which is asymptotically slower, yields better run times on real time experiments compared to popular SVP solving algorithms, i.e. BKZ-2.0. In [\[2\]](#page-55-1), Ducas et. al shares experimental results of the sieving technique. From a practical point of view, the method achieves a 400 fold speedup compared to BKZ-2.0 for low dimensional lattices. This translates roughly to a 9-bit reduction in the security level. In order to estimate the security reduction for higher dimensional lattices, we also performed similar experiments using the G6K sieving software. However, it is hard to solve SVP on lattices with dimensions over 120 in practice. In [\[2\]](#page-55-1), the authors note that the performance observed for low dimensional lattices does not match with the asymptotic analysis, and they therefore refrain from extrapolating to higher dimensions. As 

{26}------------------------------------------------

it stands now, while useful, using low dimensional G6K runtime estimates to extrapolate the runtime for higher lattice dimensions does not appear to be a viable option.

The authors of [2] also suggest a conservative security bound of  $2^{0.292d}$  for solving the SVP in dimension d. Assuming this estimate, this gives a security bound of  $2^{0.292d}$  for finding an entire KZ-reduced basis in dimension d, since the first element of such a basis is a solution to SVP. We note that since BKZ- $\beta$  needs to find many KZ-reduced bases for many sublattices of dimension  $\beta$ , the SVP security estimate in [2] says that the security of BKZ- $\beta$  is at least  $2^{0.292\beta}$ . (And presmuably considerably higher.)

In conclusion: Current conservative estiamtes for the practical security of BKZ- $\beta$  on a classical computer suggest that it has bit security greater than  $0.292\beta$ . This is far greater than the highly conservative quantum bit security of  $0.265\beta$  for BKZ- $\beta$  that we use in this paper.

#### <span id="page-28-0"></span>9.3 $L_2$ -Norm Bounds Versus $L_{\infty}$ -Norm Bounds

In this section we let L be a lattice of dimension N and discriminant D. Suppose that the forger is required to find a vector  $z \in L$  satisfying  $||v||_{\infty} \leq C$ . The triangle inequality gives the trivial estimate

$$\left\| \boldsymbol{v} \right\|_{2} \leq \sqrt{N} \cdot \left\| \boldsymbol{v} \right\|_{\infty},$$

with equality if and only if all of the coordinates of v have the same magnitude. In particular, a necessary condition for success is that the vector  $z \in L$  satisfy

<span id="page-28-5"></span><span id="page-28-3"></span>
$$\|\boldsymbol{v}\|_2 \le \sqrt{N} \cdot C. \tag{15}$$

However, this is far from sufficient, since it is quite unlikely that the coordinates of a random v will all be of the same size.

We can give an upper bound for the probability that a random point  $\boldsymbol{v}$  with  $L_2$  norm  $\|\boldsymbol{v}\|_2 \leq R$  has  $L_{\infty}$  norm bounded by  $\|\boldsymbol{v}\|_{\infty} \leq C$  by calculating the following ratio of volumes:

$$\operatorname{Prob}_{\boldsymbol{v} \leftarrow \{\boldsymbol{w} \in \mathbb{R}^{N} : ||\boldsymbol{w}||_{2} \leq R\}} (||\boldsymbol{v}||_{\infty} \leq C) \approx \frac{\operatorname{Vol}(\{\boldsymbol{v} \in \mathbb{R}^{N} : ||\boldsymbol{v}||_{\infty} \leq C \text{ and } ||\boldsymbol{v}||_{2} \leq R\})}{\operatorname{Vol}(\{\boldsymbol{v} \in \mathbb{R}^{N} : ||\boldsymbol{v}||_{\infty} \leq C\})}$$

$$\leq \frac{\operatorname{Vol}(\{\boldsymbol{v} \in \mathbb{R}^{N} : ||\boldsymbol{v}||_{\infty} \leq C\})}{\operatorname{Vol}(\{\boldsymbol{v} \in \mathbb{R}^{N} : ||\boldsymbol{v}||_{2} \leq R\})}$$

$$= \frac{(2C)^{N}}{R^{N} \mu_{N}}.$$

$$(16)$$

Suppose that the attacker runs BKZ- $\beta$  on L. We denote the run-time of BKZ- $\beta$  by Time $_{\beta}$  and the HRF of the output by  $\delta_{\beta}$ . The definition of HRF says that the output is a lattice vector  $\boldsymbol{z}$  whose  $L_2$ -norm is approximately

<span id="page-28-2"></span><span id="page-28-1"></span>
$$\|\boldsymbol{z}\|_2 \approx \delta_{\beta}^{N-1} \cdot D^{1/N}. \tag{18}$$

An upper bound for the probability that such a vector has  $L_{\infty}$ -norm smaller than C is given by (17). Hence the (probable) run-time to find a  $z \in L$  satisfying  $\|z\|_{\infty} \leq C$  is

$$\begin{aligned} & \text{Time}_{\text{total}} = \frac{\text{Run-time for BKZ-}\beta}{\text{Prob}(\text{BKZ-}\beta \text{ output satisfies } \|\boldsymbol{z}\|_{\infty} \leq C)} \\ & = \frac{\text{Time}_{\beta}}{\text{Prob}\big(\|\boldsymbol{z}\|_{\infty} \leq C \mid \|\boldsymbol{z}\|_{2} = \delta_{\beta}^{N-1} \cdot D^{1/N}\big)} \quad \text{from (18),} \\ & \geq \frac{\text{Time}_{\beta}}{\big(2C/(\delta_{\beta}^{N-1} \cdot D^{1/N})\big)^{N} \cdot \mu_{N}^{-1}} \quad \text{from (17).} \end{aligned}$$

Taking logs and doing a little algebra yields

<span id="page-28-4"></span>
$$\log_{2}(\text{Time}_{\text{total}}) \ge \log_{2}(\text{Time}_{\beta}) + N \left\{ \log_{2}\left(\underbrace{\frac{\delta_{\beta}^{N-1} \cdot D^{1/N}}{\sqrt{N}C}}_{\text{must be } \le 1 \atop \text{from (15) and (18)}}\right) + \log_{2}\left(\underbrace{\frac{1}{2}\mu_{N}^{1/N} \cdot \sqrt{N}}_{\approx \sqrt{\pi e/2}}\right) \right\}.$$

$$(19)$$

{29}------------------------------------------------

(The second subnote uses  $\mu_N = \pi^{N/2}/\Gamma(1+N/2) \approx (2\pi e/N)^{N/2}$ .)

We note that (19) gives a lower bound for the <u>logarithmic</u>-run-time, while the second term in the lower bound is a multiple of  $\overline{N}$ . So the attacker's total run-time will be huge unless the quantity in braces is non-positive. Hence to have any chance of success, the forger must choose  $\beta$  so that

$$\delta_{\beta}^{N-1} \cdot D^{1/N} \le \left(\frac{1}{2}\mu_N^{1/N} \cdot \sqrt{N}\right)^{-1} \sqrt{N}C.$$

Comparing this with (15) and (18), we see that the actual  $L_2$ -norm required to forge is roughly  $\sqrt{2/\pi e}$  times smaller than the naive estimate (15) coming from the (implausible) assumption that the coordinates of a random vector are all of the same size. Hence in order to find a vector whose  $L_{\infty}$ -norm is less than C, i.e., to find a forgery, the forger needs to achieve an HRF that is at most

<span id="page-29-0"></span>
$$\left( \left( \frac{1}{2} \mu_N^{1/N} \cdot \sqrt{N} \right)^{-1} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{N}C}{D^{1/N}} \right)^{1/(N-1)} = \left( \frac{2C}{\mu_N^{1/N} \cdot D^{1/N}} \right)^{1/(N-1)}.$$
(20)

Thus the forger needs to run BKZ- $\beta$  with a sufficiently large blocksize  $\beta$  so that  $\delta_{\beta}$  is at most the quantity given in (20).

<span id="page-29-2"></span>Remark 8. We note that since  $2\Gamma(1+N/2)^{1/N}/\sqrt{\pi} \approx \sqrt{2/\pi e} \cdot \sqrt{N}$ , the attacker's HRF forgery goal is roughly  $(2/\pi e)^{1/(2N-2)}$  times harder than suggested using the naive  $L_2$ -norm bound.

We summarize the preceding discussion as a proposition.

<span id="page-29-1"></span>**Proposition 5.** Let L be a lattice of dimension N. To find a vector  $\mathbf{v} \in L$  whose  $L_{\infty}$ -norm satisfies  $\|\mathbf{v}\|_{\infty} \leq C$ , a lattice reduction algorithm needs to achieve a Hermite root factor  $\delta$  satisfying

$$\delta \le \left(\frac{2C\Gamma(1+N/2)^{1/N}}{\sqrt{\pi}\operatorname{Disc}(L)^{1/N}}\right)^{1/(N-1)}.$$

*Proof.* This is a restatement of (20).

Experimental Estimates for  $L_2$ -Norm Versus  $L_{\infty}$ -Norm The inequality (16) is only an estimate since for any given C and R, a portion of the  $L_{\infty}$ -box may lie outside the  $L_2$ -ball.

<span id="page-30-0"></span>**Proposition 6.** Let  $\mathcal{X}$  be a (well-behaved) random variable with values in [0,1], and suppose that the distribution function  $F_{\mathcal{X}}$  of  $\mathcal{X}$  satisfies

$$F_{\mathcal{X}}(x) \leq Ax^N$$
 for all  $x \leq A^{-1/N}$ .

Then the expectation of X satisfies

$$\operatorname{Mean}(\mathcal{X}) \ge \frac{NA^{-1/N}}{N+1}.$$

*Proof.* We compute

$$\operatorname{Mean}(\mathcal{X}) = \int_0^1 x F_{\mathcal{X}}'(x) \, dx = x F_{\mathcal{X}}(x) \Big|_0^1 - \int_0^1 F_{\mathcal{X}}(x) \, dx$$
$$= 1 - \int_0^1 F_{\mathcal{X}}(x) \, dx \ge 1 - \int_0^{A^{-1/N}} Ax^N \, dx - \int_{A^{-1/N}}^1 1 \, dx = \frac{NA^{-1/N}}{N+1}.$$

#### <span id="page-31-0"></span>9.4 The Gaussian Heuristic

**Definition 9.** We denote the volume of a unit ball in  $\mathbb{R}^N$  by

$$\mu_N = \operatorname{Vol}\left(\left\{ \boldsymbol{v} \in \mathbb{R}^N : \left\| \boldsymbol{v} \right\|_2 \le 1 \right\} \right) = \frac{\pi^{N/2}}{\Gamma(1+N/2)} \approx \left(\frac{2\pi e}{N}\right)^{N/2},$$

where the approximation comes from Stirling's formula  $\Gamma(1+x) \sim (x/e)^x$  and is valid for large values of N. In general, the volume of a ball of radius R in  $\mathbb{R}^N$  is  $is^{16}$ 

$$\operatorname{Vol}\left(\left\{ \boldsymbol{v} \in \mathbb{R}^{N} : \left\| \boldsymbol{v} \right\|_{2} \leq R \right\}\right) = R^{N} \mu_{N}.$$

**Definition 10 (Gaussian Heuristic Assumption).** The Gaussian Heuristic Assumption says that a smallest non-zero vector in a random N-dimensional lattice  $\Lambda$  has  $L_2$ -norm approximately equal to the Gaussian Heuristic Value

$$\mathsf{GH}(\Lambda) = \frac{\mathrm{Disc}(\Lambda)^{1/N}}{\mu_N^{1/N}} \approx \sqrt{\frac{N}{2\pi e}} \cdot \mathrm{Disc}(\Lambda)^{1/N}. \tag{22}$$

More generally, for  $\epsilon > 0$ , it is expected that there are no non-zero vectors of length  $(1 - \epsilon) \operatorname{GH}(\Lambda)$ , and that there are lots of vectors of length  $(1 + \epsilon) \operatorname{GH}(\Lambda)$ .

Remark 10. The justification for the Gaussian Heuristic Assumption is that the volume of a ball of radius  $\mathsf{GH}(\Lambda)$  is equal to the volume of a fundamental domain for  $\Lambda$ .

#### <span id="page-31-1"></span>9.5 The Unique Shortest Vector Problem (UniqueSVP)

**Definition 11.** The Unique Shortest Vector Problem (UniqueSVP) refers to finding a shortest vector in a lattice whose shortest vector is significantly shorter than the next smallest linearly independent vector. The difficulty of the UniqueSVP is measured by the ratio of the first and second successive minima of the lattice. Since in practice it is difficult to compute the second minimum, we approximate it by the Gaussian heuristic value, so we define the SVP-Gap of the lattice L to be the quantity

$$\mathsf{SVP\_Gap}(L) = \frac{\mathsf{GH}(L)}{\lambda_1(L)} \approx \frac{\lambda_2(L)}{\lambda_1(L)}.$$

<span id="page-31-3"></span> $<sup>\</sup>overline{^{16}}$  See [40] for a nice exposition of several ways to compute the volumes of the N-ball.

In [\[4\]](#page-56-17) the authors conclude that the 2016-Estimate predicts that UniqueSVP is easier to solve than predicted by the 2008-Estimate, a prediction that they confirm by experiments on lattices using typical LWE parameters. #### <span id="page-33-0"></span>10.1 The Fake Key Hermite Root Factor for PASS<sub>RS</sub> and MMSAT

In this section we analyze the security of  $PASS_{RS}$  and MMSAT against a lattice attack designed to find a fake signing key. The analysis of MMSAT key security uses a slightly fancier version of the central limit theorem, which we state here, as well as an elementary calculus calculation about points on spheres.

<span id="page-33-4"></span>Theorem 4 (Generalized (Lyapunov) Central Limit Theorem). Suppose that  $\mathcal{X}_1, \mathcal{X}_2, \ldots$  are independent random variables with finite means  $\mu_1, \mu_2, \ldots$  and finite variances  $\sigma_1^2, \sigma_2^2, \ldots$  For  $K \geq 1$ , let

$$s_K^2 = \sigma_1^2 + \dots + \sigma_K^2$$
 and  $\mathcal{Z}_K = (\mathcal{X}_1 - \mu_1) + \dots + (\mathcal{X}_K - \mu_K).$ 

Then under a technical condition that is satisfied in our applications,<sup>20</sup>

$$\lim_{K \to \infty} \operatorname{Prob}\left(|\mathcal{Z}_K| \le C s_K\right) = \operatorname{erf}\left(C/\sqrt{2}\right).$$

*Proof.* See for example [38, Theorem 1.15 and equation (197)].

<span id="page-33-3"></span>**Lemma 1.** Let  $S^{N-1} \subset \mathbb{R}^N$  denote the unit sphere taken with the uniform probability measure, and let  $X_i$  denote any one of the coordinate functions on  $\mathbb{R}^N$ . Then the mean of  $X_i$  is 0, and the variance of  $X_i$  is 1/N.

*Proof.* More generally, it is an elementary calculus exercise to compute all of the moments of  $X_i$ . For the convenience of the reader, we include the proof in Section B.

<span id="page-33-5"></span>Proposition 8 (Key Security HRF). Let  $\widehat{f}|_{\Omega}$  be a given PASS<sub>RS</sub> public key. We give Hermite root bounds  $\delta_{\text{fake-}F}$  with the following property: A lattice reduction algorithm must achieve an HRF smaller than  $\delta_{\text{fake-}F}$  on the PASS<sub>RS</sub> lattice of dimension N and discriminant  $q^t$  associated to  $\widehat{f}|_{\Omega}$  in order to find a fake key F that can be used to (a) forge a PASS<sub>RS</sub> signature, or to (b) add a forged PASS<sub>RS</sub> signature to an existing MMSAT aggregate signature.

(a) The fake key HRF for the PASS<sub>RS</sub> parameter set  $(\lambda, N, q, t, d_c, k, b_k)$  is the quantity<sup>21</sup>

$$\delta_{\text{fake-}\boldsymbol{F}} = \left\lceil \frac{(k-b)\sqrt{N}}{2\sqrt{d_c}q^{t/N}\operatorname{erf}^{-1}(2^{-\lambda/N})} \right\rceil^{1/(N-1)}.$$

(b) The fake key HRF for the MMSAT parameter set  $(\lambda, K, N, q, t, d_c, k, b, B_k)$  is the quantity

$$\delta_{\text{fake-}F} = \left[ \frac{N}{2d_c q^{2t/N}} \left( \frac{B_k^2 K(k-b)^2}{2 \operatorname{erf}^{-1}(2^{-\lambda/N})^2} - \frac{1}{3} (K-1)(k-b)^2 \right) \right]^{1/(2N-2)}.$$

(We note that the estimate in (a) is simply the estimate in (b) with  $K = B_k = 1$ , i.e., forging a PASS<sub>RS</sub> signature is essentially the same as appending a forged PASS<sub>RS</sub> signature to an empty aggregate signature.)

<span id="page-33-1"></span>The required condition is that there is a  $\epsilon > 0$  so that  $s_K^{-2-\epsilon} \sum_{i=1}^K E[\mathcal{X}_i - \mu_i|^{2+\epsilon}] \to 0$  as  $K \to \infty$ .

<span id="page-33-2"></span><sup>&</sup>lt;sup>21</sup> Note that erf<sup>-1</sup> is the inverse of error function, not the reciprocal.

{34}------------------------------------------------

*Proof.* (a) Let  $\mathbf{f} \in \mathcal{T}_N(f_f)$  be a PASS<sub>RS</sub> signing key, with corresponding verification key  $\widehat{\mathbf{f}}|_{\Omega}$ . A polynomial  $\mathbf{F} \in R_q$  can be used to forge an individual PASS<sub>RS</sub> signature if  $\widehat{\mathbf{F}}|_{\Omega} = \widehat{\mathbf{f}}|_{\Omega}$ , and further if for every message digest  $\mu$ , it is feasible to find a  $\mathbf{y} \in R_q$  so that

<span id="page-34-1"></span>
$$\|\mathbf{F} \star \mathbf{c} + \mathbf{y}\|_{\infty} \le k - b \quad \text{with} \quad \mathbf{c} = \mathsf{Hash}_{\mathsf{C}}(\widehat{\mathbf{y}}|_{\Omega}, \widehat{\mathbf{F}}|_{\Omega}, \mu)$$
 (23)

Note that there is no requirement that  $\mathbf{F}$  have coefficients in  $\{-1,0,1\}$ , nor is there any condition imposed on the coefficients of  $\mathbf{y}$ . Thus for example, the forger could choose the coefficients of  $\mathbf{y}$  from  $\{-1,0,1\}$ , i.e.,  $\mathbf{y} \in \mathcal{B}^{\infty}(1)$ , in which case the norm bound (23) is essentially a norm bound on  $\mathbf{F} \star \mathbf{c}$ . So we will say that the forgery succeeds if  $\|\mathbf{F} \star \mathbf{c}\|_{\infty}$  is small enough with probability at least  $2^{-\lambda}$ , i.e., if

$$\operatorname{Prob}_{\boldsymbol{c} \leftarrow \mathcal{T}_{N}(d)} \Big( \| \boldsymbol{F} \star \boldsymbol{c} \|_{\infty} \le k - b \Big) \ge 2^{-\lambda}.$$

Each coefficient of  $\mathbf{F} \star \mathbf{c}$  is a sum of  $2d_c$  coefficients of  $\pm \mathbf{F}$ , so if we view  $\mathbf{F}$  as a random point on a sphere of radius  $\|\mathbf{F}\|_2$ , then each coefficient of  $\mathbf{F} \star \mathbf{c}$  is roughly a sum of  $2d_c$  iid random variables chosen from the coordinates of points on that sphere.<sup>23</sup>

Thus the distribution of the sum may be approximated by a normally distributed random variable of mean 0 and variance

$$\|\boldsymbol{F}\|_{2}^{2}/N,\tag{24}$$

where the variance follows from Lemma 1. Treating the coefficients of  $F \star c$  as independent, the central limit theorem gives the approximation

$$\begin{split} \underset{\boldsymbol{c} \leftarrow \mathcal{T}_{N}(d_{c})}{\operatorname{Prob}} \Big( \| \boldsymbol{F} \star \boldsymbol{c} \|_{\infty} \leq C \Big) &\approx \underset{\boldsymbol{c} \leftarrow \mathcal{T}_{N}(d_{c})}{\operatorname{Prob}} \Big( \big| \big( \boldsymbol{F} \star \boldsymbol{c} \big)_{0} \big| \leq C \Big)^{N} \quad \text{independence assumption,} \\ &\approx \operatorname{erf} \left( \frac{C\sqrt{N}}{2\sqrt{d_{c}} \left\| \boldsymbol{F} \right\|_{2}} \right)^{N} \quad \text{Theorem 1 with } K = 2d_{c} \\ &\text{and } \sigma = \left\| \boldsymbol{F} \right\|_{2} / \sqrt{N}. \end{split}$$

Taking C = k - b, which is the  $L_{\infty}$ -norm bound for a valid signature, we see that the forgery is successuff if and only if the forger has found a vector  $\mathbf{F}$  satisfying

<span id="page-34-3"></span>
$$\operatorname{erf}\left(\frac{(k-b)\sqrt{N}}{2\sqrt{d_c}\left\|\boldsymbol{F}\right\|_2}\right)^N \ge 2^{-\lambda}.\tag{25}$$

<span id="page-34-0"></span>Although taking y = 0 would not be a good idea, since then  $\hat{y}|_{\Omega} = 0$ , which would suggest that the signature is forged.

<span id="page-34-2"></span>It would be more accurate to say that the  $\mathcal{Y}_i$  are determined by first choosing a random point on  $S^{N-1}(R_{\delta})$  and then choosing distinct random coordinates of that point. However, the coordinates of the points of  $S^{N-1}(R_{\delta})$  are only slightly correlated, so since in practice  $d_c$  is quite small compared to N, it suffices to take the  $\mathcal{Y}_i$  to be random coordinates of independently chosen random points of  $S^{N-1}(R_{\delta})$ .

{35}------------------------------------------------

By definition of HRF, the smallest vector  $\mathbf{F}$  obtained by a lattice reduction algorithm (on a PASS<sub>RS</sub> lattice of dimension N and discriminant  $q^t$ ) has length

<span id="page-35-0"></span>
$$\|\boldsymbol{F}\|_{2} = \mathrm{HRF}^{N-1} \cdot q^{t/N}. \tag{26}$$

Substituting (26) into (25) and solving for the HRF gives the stated result.

(b) Let  $\widehat{f}|_{\Omega}$  be a public key. If the forger is able to achieve an HRF of  $\delta$  on the associated PASS<sub>RS</sub> lattice of dimension N and discriminant  $q^t$ , then she can find a lattice vector  $\mathbf{F}$  satisfying

<span id="page-35-1"></span>
$$\widehat{\boldsymbol{F}}|_{\Omega} = \widehat{\boldsymbol{f}}|_{\Omega} \quad \text{and} \quad \|\boldsymbol{F}\|_{2} \approx R_{\delta} = \delta^{N-1} \cdot q^{t/N}.$$
 (27)

Next suppose that the forger wants to append a forged signature to z, which is an existing aggregate of K-1 signatures. To do this, she chooses an arbitrary y of her choice, she hashes  $y |\widehat{F}|_{\Omega}$ , and a document digest  $\mu$  to create c, and then she hopes that

$$z' = z + F \star c + y$$

is a valid aggregate signature. The partial Fourier transform for z' works, so the forger just needs  $||z'||_{\infty}$  to be sufficiently small. It is in the forger's best interest to choose y to be small, say  $y \in \mathcal{B}^{\infty}(1)$ , so we ignore y and declare the forgery a success if

$$z' = z + F \star c$$
 satisfies  $\|z'\|_{\infty} \leq B_k \sqrt{K}(k-b)$ .

As usual, we treat the coefficients of z' as being essentially independent, so

$$\operatorname{Prob}(\|\boldsymbol{z}'\|_{\infty} \leq B_k \sqrt{K}(k-b)) = \operatorname{Prob}(|(\boldsymbol{z}')_0| \leq B_k \sqrt{K}(k-b))^N.$$

The 0th coefficient of z' has the form

$$(z')_0 = \sum_{i=1}^{K-1} \beta_i(z_i)_0 + \sum_{i=0}^{N-1} F_{N-i}c_i.$$

The  $\beta_i$  are  $\pm 1$ , while the  $c_i$  consist of  $2d_c$  copies of  $\pm 1$  and  $N-2d_c$  copies of 0. The coefficients of  $F_i$  are the coefficients of a random point on the sphere of radius  $R_{\delta}$ . Hence we can model the distribution of  $(z')_0$  by the random variable

$$\mathcal{Z} = \sum_{i=1}^{K-1} \mathcal{X}_i + \sum_{i=1}^{2d_c} \mathcal{Y}_i,$$

where the  $\mathcal{X}_i$  and  $\mathcal{Y}_i$  are independent random variables satisfying

 $\mathcal{X}_i = \text{random variable uniformly distributed on } [-(k-b), k-b],$ 

 $\mathcal{Y}_i$  = first coordinate of point uniformly distributed on  $S^{N-1}(R_\delta)$ .

Then  $\mathcal{X}_i$  and  $\mathcal{Y}_i$  have mean 0, and their variances are given by

$$\sigma^2(\mathcal{X}_i) = \frac{1}{3}(k-b)^2$$
 and  $\sigma^2(\mathcal{Y}_i) = \frac{1}{N}R_{\delta}^2$ ,

{36}------------------------------------------------

where the former is an easy computation and the latter follows from Lemma [1.](#page-33-3)

We are going to use the generalized central limit theorem (Theorem [4\)](#page-33-4), which says that if we set

<span id="page-36-1"></span>
$$s^{2} = \sum_{i=1}^{K-1} \sigma^{2}(\mathcal{X}_{i}) + \sum_{i=1}^{2d_{c}} \sigma^{2}(\mathcal{Y}_{i}) = \frac{K-1}{3}(k-b)^{2} + \frac{2d_{c}}{N}R_{\delta}^{2},$$
 (28)

then Z/s is approximately a standard normal distribution with mean 0 and variance 1. Hence for all C,

<span id="page-36-0"></span>
$$\operatorname{Prob}\!\left(|\mathcal{Z}| \leq Cs\right) \approx \operatorname{erf}\left(C/\sqrt{2}\right).$$

Putting all of this together, we find that the probability of a successful forgery is approximately

$$\operatorname{Prob}\left(\|\boldsymbol{z}'\|_{\infty} \leq B_k \sqrt{K}(k-b)\right)$$

$$\approx \operatorname{erf}\left[\frac{B_k \sqrt{K}(k-b)}{\sqrt{2} \cdot \sqrt{((K-1)/3)(k-b)^2 + (2d_c/N)R_{\delta}^2}}\right]^N. \quad (29)$$

We substitute R<sup>δ</sup> = δ N−1 · q t/N from [\(27\)](#page-35-1), set [\(29\)](#page-36-0) equal to 2<sup>−</sup><sup>λ</sup> , and solve for δ to find the HRF for λ-bit lattice key security for MMSAT.

<span id="page-36-2"></span>Remark 12. The tail of the error function erf is rapidly decreasing, but on the other hand, the value of erf in the right-hand side of [\(29\)](#page-36-0) is raised to a large power. This means that as R<sup>δ</sup> decreases, there will be a fairly abrupt boundary at which forgery goes from being infeasible to being easy. This occurs roughly when the two terms in the sum [\(28\)](#page-36-1) defining s <sup>2</sup> are equal.

#### <span id="page-37-0"></span>10.2 Solving for the PASS<sub>RS</sub> Private Key as a UniqueSVP

In this section we analyze the difficulty of finding a PASS<sub>RS</sub> private key formulated as a UniqueSVP. We note that the private key f is a vector length  $\sqrt{2d_f}$  in the PASS<sub>RS</sub> lattice (3) of dimension N and discriminant  $q^t$ , so the SVP-Gap is

<span id="page-37-2"></span>
$${\sf SVP\_Gap} = \frac{{\sf GH}}{\lambda_1} \approx \sqrt{\frac{N}{4d_f\pi e}} \cdot q^{t/N},$$

which will be significantly larger than 1. (Note that we always have  $d_f < \frac{1}{3}N$ .)

We recall from Proposition 7 that there are two competing estimates for the difficulty of solving UniqueSVP. The conclusion of [4] is that the 2016-Estimate predicts that UniqueSVP is easier to solve than predicted by the 2008-Estimate, and that this prediction is confirmed by experiments on lattices using typical LWE parameters. We were thus somewhat surprised that for the parameters used by PASS<sub>RS</sub> and MMSAT, the opposite is true, i.e., the 2016-Estimate says that the UniqueSVP for finding a PASS<sub>RS</sub> secret key is harder than predicted by the 2008-Estimate! To see why, we solve the two estimates for the HRF  $\delta$  required to solve the UniqueSVP:

2008-Estimate 
$$\delta = \left[ \frac{1}{\tau \sqrt{2\pi e}} \cdot \sqrt{N} \cdot D^{1/N} \cdot \lambda_1(L)^{-1} \right]^{1/N}, \quad (30)$$

{38}------------------------------------------------

2016-Estimate 
$$\delta = \left[\frac{1}{\sqrt{\beta}} \cdot \sqrt{N} \cdot D^{1/N} \cdot \lambda_1(L)^{-1}\right]^{1/(N-2\beta)}.$$
 (31)

The estimates differ in two ways. First, the 1/τ<sup>√</sup> 2πe ≈ 0.6 factor in the 2008- Estimate is replaced by 1/ √ β in the 2016-Estimate. For security levels 128 and 256, as given for example in Table [19,](#page-59-0) the allowed blocksizes are, respectively, β = 484 and β = 966, so 1/ √ β is 0.046 and 0.032. This means that the quantity in square brackets is roughly 13 to 18 times smaller for the 2016-Estimate.

<span id="page-38-1"></span>Second, the 2008-Estimate is raised to the 1/N power, while the 2016-Estimate is raised to the smaller 1/(N − 2β) power. If β is large, this will definitely tend to make the 2016-Estimate larger than the 2008-Estimate, but there is a delicate balance of whether it compensates for the fact that the 2008-Estimate started 13 to 18 times smaller before taking the roots. #### <span id="page-38-0"></span>10.3 HRF Required to Forge the z Part of an MMSAT Signature

We use the result in Proposition [5](#page-29-1) to estimate the HRF needed to forge an MMSAT signature z. We note that this estimate includes the L∞-norm versus L2-norm correction discussed in Section [9.3.](#page-28-0)

<span id="page-38-3"></span>Proposition 9. For the PASSRS lattice [\(3\)](#page-7-3) of dimension N and discriminant q t , in order to find a z that forges an MMSAT signature, the forger needs to achieve an HRF smaller than

<span id="page-38-2"></span>
$$\delta_{L_{\infty}\text{-forge}} = \left(\frac{2\Gamma(1+N/2)^{1/N}B_q q^{1-t/N}}{\sqrt{\pi}}\right)^{1/(N-1)}.$$
 (32)

{39}------------------------------------------------

*Proof.* For the MMSAT aggregate signatures, the  $L_{\infty}$ -norm bound in the PASS<sub>RS</sub> lattice is  $C = B_q q$ , and the discriminant of the lattice is  $\text{Disc} = q^t$ . Substituting these values into Proposition 5 gives the stated result.

Remark 13. For PASS<sub>RS</sub> and MMSAT, it is not literally true that the attacker succeeds if they find a vector satisfying (32), since the given basis for the lattice includes the q-vectors  $qe_1, \ldots, qe_t$ . Thus the lattice contains t mutually orthogonal vectors whose  $L_2$ -norms are equal to q, so it contains lots of vectors satisfying (32), specifically all linear combinations

$$n_1 q \mathbf{e}_1 + \dots + n_t q \mathbf{e}_t$$
 with  $n_1^2 + \dots + n_t^2 \le 2N B_q^2 / \pi e$ .

Of course, these vectors are useless for forging, since their  $L_{\infty}$ -norms are larger than  $B_q q$ . So when a forger uses BKZ to try to find a vector satisfying (32) or some similar inequality, at the very least they are actually looking for a vector that is not built solely out of the known q-vectors.

# <span id="page-39-0"></span>10.4 A Summary of the Hermite Root Factors for MMSAT Lattice Security

For a given desired bit security level  $\lambda$ , we use Proposition 4 to set the achievable block-size  $\beta = \lceil \lambda/0.265 \rceil$ , and then Proposition 3 tells us the HRF that is achievable by BKZ- $\beta$ .

We have described a number of ways in which a lattice reduction algorithm such as BKZ- $\beta$ , applied to the lattice (3), can be used to attack PASS<sub>RS</sub> or MMSAT. These include forging the signature z, finding a vector F that can be used in place of the private key f, or directly searching for f. Each of these lattice problems has an associated HRF, and the security of PASS<sub>RS</sub> and MMSAT requires that the HRFs for the attacks be smaller than the HRF achievable by BKZ  $\beta$ . We summarize these requirements in the next proposition.

#### <span id="page-39-2"></span>Proposition 10 (Lattice Security of PASS<sub>RS</sub> and MMSAT). Let

$$(\lambda, K, N, q, t, d_c, k, b, B_k)$$

be an MMSAT parameter set, or alternatively a PASS<sub>RS</sub> parameter set in which  $K = B_k = 1$ . Set  $\beta = \lceil \lambda/0.265 \rceil$ , and define<sup>24</sup>

 $\delta_{\beta}$  = the HRF achieved by BKZ- $\beta$  for blocksize  $\beta = \lambda/0.265$ 

$$= \left[ \frac{\beta}{2\pi e} \cdot (\beta \pi)^{1/\beta} \right]^{1/(2\beta - 2)},$$

 $\delta_{\text{forge-}z} = \text{the HRF required to forge an amalgamate signature } z$ 

$$= \left[ \frac{2\Gamma(1+N/2)^{1/N} B_q q^{1-t/N}}{\sqrt{\pi}} \right]^{1/(N-1)},$$

<span id="page-39-1"></span>Here  $\tau$  is an experimentally determined constant. A conservative choice is  $\tau = 1/3$ .

{40}------------------------------------------------

δfake-<sup>F</sup> = the HRF required to find an F that serves a a fake signing key

$$= \left[ \frac{N}{2d_c q^{2t/N}} \left( \frac{B_k^2 K(k-b)^2}{2 \operatorname{erf}^{-1}(2^{-\lambda/N})^2} - \frac{1}{3} (K-1)(k-b)^2 \right) \right]^{1/(2N-2)},$$

δUSVP-08 = the HRF required to solve the UniqueSVP and find f (2008-Estimate)

$$= \left[ \frac{1}{\tau \sqrt{4d_f \pi e}} \cdot \sqrt{N} \cdot q^{t/N} \right]^{1/N},$$

δUSVP-16 = the HRF required to solve the UniqueSVP and find f (2016-Estimate)

$$= \left[ \frac{1}{\sqrt{2d_f\beta}} \cdot \sqrt{N} \cdot q^{t/N} \right]^{1/(N-2\beta)}.$$

Then the given MMSAT (or PASSRS) parameter set is secure against (known) lattice attacks provided that

$$\delta_{\beta} > \max\{\delta_{\text{forge-}z}, \delta_{\text{fake-}F}, \delta_{\text{USVP-}08}, \delta_{\text{USVP-}16}\}.$$

Proof. For the derivation of these HRF formulas, see Proposition [3](#page-24-4) for δβ, see Proposition [9](#page-38-3) for δforge-z, see Proposition [8\(](#page-33-5)b) for δfake-<sup>F</sup> , see Proposition [7\(](#page-32-4)a) and [\(30\)](#page-37-2) for δUSVP-08, and see Proposition [7\(](#page-32-4)b) and [\(31\)](#page-38-4) for δUSVP-16.

## <span id="page-40-0"></span>11 Choosing Parameters for MMSA and MMSAT

<span id="page-40-3"></span>
$$\delta(\text{BKZ-}\beta) \approx \left[\frac{\beta}{2\pi e} \cdot (\pi\beta)^{1/\beta}\right]^{1/(2\beta-2)}.$$

# <span id="page-45-0"></span>13 MMSATK Bit Security and Choosing MMSATK Parameters

The lattice forgery problem underlying MMSA is to find a sufficiently short vector z such that the t coordinates of  $\widehat{z}|_{\Omega}$  take on specified values in  $\mathbb{Z}_q$ . The lattice has dimension N and discriminant  $q^t$ .

The lattice forgery problem for MMSATK is similar, except that the forger now only needs to match t' of the coorindates of  $\hat{z}|_{\Omega}$  to specified values, where t' < t. However, the forger does not know in advance which t' coordinates need to match. Indeed, the t' indices are chosen as a hash of z, thereby making it impossible for the forger to preselect any of the t' indices.

However, the MMSATK forger gains some advantage over the MMSA forger. Specifically, she may choose to solve an easier lattice problem in which she finds a sufficiently short z such that t'' of the coordinates of  $\hat{z}|_{\Omega}$  are correct. If t'' is fairly large compared to t', then there is a reasonable possibility that she will be lucky and the t' indices chosen by hashing z will be a subset of the good t'' coordinates that she forced to be correct.<sup>26</sup> Hence

Expected run time to forge an MMSATK signature z

$$= \begin{pmatrix} \text{Expected run time to find } \boldsymbol{z} \\ \text{satisfying } \|\boldsymbol{z}\|_{\infty} \leq B_q q \text{ in a} \\ \text{PASS}_{RS} \text{ lattice of dimension } N \text{ and discriminant } q^{t''} \end{pmatrix} \text{Prob} \begin{pmatrix} \text{Choosing } t' \text{ samples from a set of size } t \text{ containing } t'' \text{ winning tickets and getting all winning tickets} \end{pmatrix}^{-1}. \tag{44}$$

The probability in (44) is easy to compute. There are  $\binom{t}{t'}$  ways to choose the sample, and of those, exactly  $\binom{t''}{t'}$  of them consist entirely of winning tickets, so the probability is

<span id="page-45-2"></span>Prob 
$$\begin{pmatrix} \text{Choosing } t' \text{ samples from} \\ \text{a set of size } t \text{ containing} \\ t'' \text{ winning tickets and} \\ \text{getting all winning tickets} \end{pmatrix} = \frac{\begin{pmatrix} t'' \\ t' \end{pmatrix}}{\begin{pmatrix} t \\ t' \end{pmatrix}}$$

To estimate the first factor in (44), we let  $\delta_{BKZ}(\beta)$  be the expected HRF of BKZ- $\beta$  as given by the formula (13) in Proposition 3, and we will also use the fact that, according to Proposition 4, the cost of running BKZ- $\beta$  is approximately  $2^{0.265\beta}$ . On the other hand, the formula (32) in Remark 8 says that the HRF to find a forgery is

$$\delta_{\text{forge}}(t'') = \left(\frac{2\Gamma(1+N/2)^{1/N}B_q q^{1-t''/N}}{\sqrt{\pi}}\right)^{1/(N-1)}.$$

<span id="page-45-1"></span>Even if a few of the t' indices are not in the forger's good set, she might be lucky and have the extra coordinates match by chance. But each such coordinate has only a 1/q chance of being correct, and q is quite large, so we will ignore this small error term in our calculation.

{46}------------------------------------------------

Note that this formula uses t'' in place of t. This means that the  $\delta_{\text{forge}}$  for MMSATK is larger than the  $\delta_{\text{forge}}$  for MMSA, making MMSATK somewhat easier to forge than MMSA, as we would expect.

For each t'' let  $\beta(t'')$  be the smallest  $\beta$  so that so that

$$\delta_{\mathrm{BKZ}}(\beta(t'')) \leq \delta_{\mathrm{forge}}(t'').$$

Then the bit security of MMSATK against forgery lattice attacks is obtained by taking the optimal (for the forger) value of t'',

$$\mathsf{MMSATK} \text{ bit security} = \min_{t' \leq t'' \leq t} \left[ 0.265 \beta(t'') + \log_2 \binom{t}{t'} - \log_2 \binom{t''}{t'} \right].$$

Table 16 gives some examples showing that when  $B_t = \frac{1}{3}$ , i.e.,  $t \approx \frac{1}{3}N$ , this attack decreases the bit security by only a few bits. However, for  $B_t = \frac{1}{2}$ , the decrease in security is more substantial, bringing the security level below the desired  $\lambda$  value. In practice, the security level can easily be raised back to the desired level by increasing t' slightly, which in turn will make the MMSATK key a bit larger.

## <span id="page-46-0"></span>A Proof of the Reduction of MMSA to PASS<sub>RS</sub>

Proof (of Theorem 3 in Section 8). The goal of the Challenger  $\mathcal{C}$  is to interact with the Chosen Key Forger  $\mathcal{A}$  to forge a Single Signature on  $\mu_K$  using Alice's key  $\widehat{f}_A|_{\Omega}$ . Note that  $\mathcal{C}$  does not know the secret key  $f_A$  and has no influence over how  $\mu_K$  is chosen. The Challenger  $\mathcal{C}$  simulates the forger  $\mathcal{A}$  by presenting a challenge key, returning signature and hash queries. In response the Forger  $\mathcal{A}$  returns an aggregate signature forgery. The Challenger  $\mathcal{C}$  uses the aggregate forgery to extract a PASS<sub>RS</sub> signature. To handle the hash (random oracle) queries,  $\mathcal{C}$  maintains a list H. In MMSA hashes are computed with input  $(\widehat{y}|_{\Omega}, \mu, \widehat{f}|_{\Omega})$ . Therefore,  $\mathcal{C}$  simulates the random oracle H with inputs  $(\widehat{y}|_{\Omega}, \mu, \widehat{f}|_{\Omega})$ .

The simulation proceeds as follows:

Challenge PK: The Challenger  $\mathcal C$  gives the aggregate forger  $\mathcal A$  a challenge public key  $\widehat{f_K}|_{\Omega} = \widehat{f_{\mathcal C}}|_{\Omega} + \widehat{f_A}|_{\Omega}$  where  $\widehat{f_A}|_{\Omega}$  is Alice's public key and  $\widehat{f_{\mathcal C}}|_{\Omega}$  is the public key of the Challenger. Hence,  $\mathcal C$  knows  $f_{\mathcal C}$  but not  $f_{\mathcal K}$ .

**Signature Queries:**  $\mathcal{A}$  requests signatures on  $\mu_1, \ldots, \mu_{N_S}$  under  $\widehat{\boldsymbol{f}}_K|_{\Omega}$ .  $\mathcal{C}$  prepares signatures  $\boldsymbol{z}_i, \boldsymbol{c}_i$  and returns them to  $\mathcal{A}$  by iterating the following steps for  $i = 1, \ldots, N_S$ :

- 1. Sample  $\boldsymbol{y}_{i}^{\mathcal{C}} \in \mathcal{B}^{\infty}(k)$  and  $\boldsymbol{c}_{i} \in \mathcal{B}^{\infty}(b)$ .
- 2. Compute  $\widehat{\boldsymbol{y}}_i|_{\Omega} = \widehat{\boldsymbol{y}_i^{\mathcal{C}}}|_{\Omega} \widehat{\boldsymbol{f}}_A|_{\Omega} \odot \widehat{\boldsymbol{c}}_i|_{\Omega}$ .
- 3. If entry with matching  $\widehat{\boldsymbol{y}}_i|_{\Omega}$ ,  $\mu_i$ ,  $\widehat{\boldsymbol{f}}_1|_{\Omega}$  exists in H, restart from Step 1.
- 4. Compute  $z_i = f_{\mathcal{C}} \star c_i + y_i^{\mathcal{C}}$ .
- 5. If  $\|\boldsymbol{z}_i\| > k b$  then return to Step 1 (rejection sampling).
- 6. Uniformly sample  $\beta_i \in \{-1, 1\}$ .
- 7. Insert  $\langle \widehat{\boldsymbol{y}}_i |_{\Omega}, \mu_i, \widehat{\boldsymbol{f}}_K |_{\Omega}, \boldsymbol{c}_i, \boldsymbol{z}_i, \beta_i \rangle$  into H.
- 8. Return signature  $(z_i, c_i, \mu_i)$  to A.

Note that Steps 1 and 5 ensure that the signatures are indistinguishable from authentic signatures to the forger A.

Message Hash Queries: The forger  $\mathcal{A}$  requests  $\mathsf{H}(\widehat{\boldsymbol{y}}_i|_{\Omega}, \mu_i, \widehat{\boldsymbol{f}}_i|_{\Omega})$  for forged keys  $\{\widehat{\boldsymbol{f}}_i|_{\Omega}\}_{i=1}^{N_H}$ . The challenger  $\mathcal{C}$  handles the hash queries as follows for  $i \neq N_H$ :

- 1. If entry for  $\widehat{\boldsymbol{y}}_i|_{\Omega}$ ,  $\mu_i$ ,  $\widehat{\boldsymbol{f}}_i|_{\Omega}$  exists in H, lookup and return  $\boldsymbol{c}_i$ .
- 2. Otherwise C samples  $c_i \in \mathcal{B}^{\infty}(b)$  and  $\beta_i \in \{-1, +1\}$ .
- 3. Return  $c_i$  and insert  $\langle \widehat{\boldsymbol{y}}_i |_{\Omega}, \mu_i, \widehat{\boldsymbol{f}}_i |_{\Omega}, c_i, \text{null}, \beta_i \rangle$  into random oracle H. In the last iteration  $\mathcal{A}$  queries for  $\mathsf{H}(\widehat{\boldsymbol{y}}_{N_H}|_{\Omega}, \mu_{N_H}, \widehat{\boldsymbol{f}}_{N_H}|_{\Omega})$ . The Challenger follows these steps instead:
- 1. If  $\widehat{\boldsymbol{f}}_{N_H}|_{\Omega} \neq \widehat{\boldsymbol{f}}_K|_{\Omega}$  then ABORT.
- 2. If  $\mu_K, f_K|_{\Omega}$  is found in H and corresponding  $z_i$  is not null then ABORT.
- 3. Challenger C retrieves  $\widehat{\boldsymbol{f}}_i|_{\Omega}$ ,  $\widehat{\boldsymbol{c}}_i|_{\Omega}$ ,  $\widehat{\boldsymbol{y}}_i|_{\Omega}$  from H for  $i=1,\ldots,N_H-1$ .
- 4. Challenger C uniformly samples  $\beta_i \in \{-1, 1\}$  and inserts this value into  $\mathsf{H}(\widehat{\boldsymbol{f}}_i|_{\Omega}, \widehat{\boldsymbol{c}}_i|_{\Omega}, \widehat{\boldsymbol{y}}_i|_{\Omega})$ .
- 5. Challenger  $\mathcal{C}$  computes  $\widehat{\boldsymbol{y}_A}|_{\Omega}$ :

$$\widehat{\boldsymbol{y}_A}|_{\Omega} = \widehat{\boldsymbol{y}_K}|_{\Omega} + \beta_K \sum_{i=1}^{N_H-1} \beta_i(\widehat{\boldsymbol{f}_i}|_{\Omega} \odot \widehat{\boldsymbol{c}_i}|_{\Omega} + \widehat{\boldsymbol{y}_i}|_{\Omega}).$$

{48}------------------------------------------------

6. Challenger C computes and returns  $c_A = \mathsf{Hash}(\widehat{y_A}|_{\Omega}, \mu_K, \widehat{f_A}|_{\Omega})$ .

**Aggregation Hash Query:** For aggregation  $\mathcal{A}$  needs the  $\beta_i$  values, and therefore needs make another random oracle query to  $\mathsf{H}$  with  $\{\widehat{y}_i|_{\Omega}, \mu_i, \widehat{f}_i|_{\Omega}\}_{i=1}^K$  as input. For  $(\widehat{y}_i|_{\Omega}, \mu_i, \widehat{f}_i|_{\Omega})$  entries that were requested earlier and stored in the hash table, the Challenger  $\mathcal{C}$  can look up the corresponding  $\beta_i$  value. Otherwise,  $\mathcal{C}$  creates a new entry in  $\mathsf{H}$  by uniformly sampling  $\beta_i \in \{-1, 1\}$ .  $\mathcal{C}$  returns  $\beta_1, \ldots, \beta_K$ .

**Aggregate Forgery:**  $\mathcal{A}$  forges an aggregate signature  $(\boldsymbol{z}, \{\mu_i, \widehat{\boldsymbol{y}}_i|_{\Omega}\}_{i=1}^K)$ , that verifies for  $\{\widehat{\boldsymbol{f}}_i|_{\Omega}\}_{i=1}^K$  and messages  $\{\mu_i\}_{i=1}^K$ . If the forger fails so does  $\mathcal{C}$  and returns ABORT.

#### Signature Extraction:

- 1. Challenger  $\mathcal{C}$  retrieves  $\boldsymbol{z}_i, \beta_i$  from  $\mathsf{H}(\widehat{\boldsymbol{y}}_i|_{\Omega}, \mu_i, \widehat{\boldsymbol{f}}_K|_{\Omega})$  for  $i = 1, \ldots, N_S$ .
- 2. Challenger C eliminates signatures obtained from the signing oracle for  $\widehat{f}_K|_{\Omega}$  by computing

$$\boldsymbol{z}' = \beta_K (\boldsymbol{z} - \sum_{i=1}^{N_S} \beta_i \boldsymbol{z}_i)$$

3. Recover signature by computing

$$\boldsymbol{z}_A = \boldsymbol{z}' - \boldsymbol{f}_\mathcal{C} \star \boldsymbol{c}_A$$

4. Return  $(z_A, c_A, \mu_K)$  as the PASS<sub>RS</sub> signature.

Correctness. The challenger C returns  $(z_A, c_A, \mu_K)$  as the PASS<sub>RS</sub> signature and succeeds only if  $\widehat{f}_{N_H}|_{\Omega} = \widehat{f}_K|_{\Omega}$ , i.e. the last hash query is performed with the challenge key. This happens with probability  $1/N_H$ . Otherwise the the simulation ends with an ABORT. For the remaining analysis we assume this to hold:  $\widehat{f}_{N_H}|_{\Omega} = \widehat{f}_K|_{\Omega}$ . To verify the signature we run the PASS<sub>RS</sub> verification process for instantiation with parameters (q, N, k', b'). There are two checks in the verification process: check of the norm of the signature  $z_A$  and the check on the recomputed challenge c. Starting with the latter the challenge polynomial is recomputed from the signature and public key as follows. We first compute

$$\widehat{\bm{y}_A'}|_{\Omega} = \widehat{\bm{z}_A}|_{\Omega} - \widehat{\bm{f}_A}|_{\Omega} \odot \widehat{\bm{c}_A}|_{\Omega}.$$

and then the challenge

$$\boldsymbol{c}' = \mathsf{FormatC}(\mathsf{Hash}(\widehat{\boldsymbol{y}'_A}|_\Omega, \mu_K, \widehat{\boldsymbol{f}_A}|_\Omega))$$

Then we check if the newly computed challenge matches the one received in the signature  $c' = c_A$ . Remember that

$$\boldsymbol{z}_A = \boldsymbol{z}' - \boldsymbol{f}_{\mathcal{C}} \star \boldsymbol{c}_A \quad \text{and} \quad \boldsymbol{z}' = \boldsymbol{z} - \sum_{i=1}^{N_S} \beta_i \boldsymbol{z}_i.$$

{49}------------------------------------------------

By construction the signatures returned during the signature queries satisfy

$$\boldsymbol{z}_i = \boldsymbol{f}_K \star \boldsymbol{c}_i + \boldsymbol{y}_i = (\boldsymbol{f}_{\mathcal{C}} + \boldsymbol{f}_A) \star \boldsymbol{c}_i + \boldsymbol{y}_i = \boldsymbol{f}_{\mathcal{C}} \star \boldsymbol{c}_i + \boldsymbol{f}_A \star \boldsymbol{c}_i + \boldsymbol{y}_i$$

where z' denotes the remaining part of the aggregate signature with the signature queries removed,

$$\boldsymbol{z}' = \boldsymbol{z} - \sum_{i=1}^{N_S} \beta_i \boldsymbol{z}_i.$$

Since

$$\boldsymbol{z}' = \beta_K(\boldsymbol{f}_K \star \boldsymbol{c}_A + \boldsymbol{y}_K) + \sum_{i=1}^{N_H-1} \beta_i(\boldsymbol{f}_i \star \boldsymbol{c}_i + \boldsymbol{y}_i),$$

and since  $y_A$  was defined to satisfy

$$\beta_K \boldsymbol{y}_A = \beta_K \boldsymbol{y}_K + \sum_{i=1}^{N_H-1} \beta_i (\boldsymbol{f}_i \star \boldsymbol{c}_i + \boldsymbol{y}_i),$$

we can write

$$\beta_K \boldsymbol{z}' = (\boldsymbol{f}_K \star \boldsymbol{c}_A) + \boldsymbol{y}_A.$$

Let us recall that  $\boldsymbol{f}_K = \boldsymbol{f}_{\mathcal{C}} + \boldsymbol{f}_A$ . We expand the equation

$$\boldsymbol{z}' = \big((\boldsymbol{f}_{\mathcal{C}} + \boldsymbol{f}_{A}) \star \boldsymbol{c}_{A}\big) + \boldsymbol{y}_{A} = \boldsymbol{f}_{\mathcal{C}} \star \boldsymbol{c}_{A} + \boldsymbol{f}_{A} \star \boldsymbol{c}_{A} + \boldsymbol{y}_{A}.$$

Since we know  $f_{\mathcal{C}} \star c_A$ , we can subtract it from the short version of the signature z':

$$\boldsymbol{z}_A = \boldsymbol{z}' - f_{\mathcal{C}} \star \boldsymbol{c}_A = \boldsymbol{f}_A \star \boldsymbol{c}_A + \boldsymbol{y}_A.$$

Now we have a valid signature  $(z_A, c_A)$  for Alice for the message  $\mu_K$  and public key  $\widehat{f}_A|_{\Omega}$ . One can verify the signature by computing and comparing if the challenges  $c_A$  match:

$$|\widehat{\boldsymbol{y}_A}|_{\Omega} = \widehat{\boldsymbol{z}_A}|_{\Omega} - \widehat{\boldsymbol{f}_A}|_{\Omega} \odot \widehat{\boldsymbol{c}_A}|_{\Omega}$$

This reveals

$$\boldsymbol{c}_A = \mathsf{Hash}(\widehat{\boldsymbol{y}_A}|_{\Omega}, \mu_K, \widehat{\boldsymbol{f}_A}|_{\Omega})$$

In addition to the check on the challenge, the norm bound on the signature  $z_A$  also needs to be satisfied:

$$\|z_A\| = \|z' - f_C c_A\| \le \|z'\| + \|f_C c_A\| = \|z'\| + b$$

and

$$\|\boldsymbol{z}'\| = \left\|\boldsymbol{z} - \sum_{i=1}^{N_S} \beta_i \boldsymbol{z}_i \right\| = \left\|\sum_{i=1}^{N_H} \boldsymbol{z}_i \right\| \leq N_H(k-b).$$

The Central Limit Theorem implies that  $\|\mathbf{z}'\| \approx \sqrt{N_H}(k-b)$ . To compensate for the infinity norm check, we add a constant  $\mathcal{O}(1)$ , and then  $\mathbf{z}_A$  satisfies

$$\boldsymbol{z}_A \in \mathcal{B}^{\infty}\left(\mathcal{O}(1)\sqrt{N_H}(k-b)\right).$$

{50}------------------------------------------------

**Efficiency.** The computations during the simulation performed by the Challenger C are summarized as follows:

- Challenge PK: 1  $\mathcal{F}_{\Omega}$ -transformation, 1 polynomial addition.
- Signature Query: 2  $\mathcal{F}_{\Omega}$ -transformation, 2 addition/subtraction, 1 coefficient multiplication, 1 polynomial multiplication, 1 table insert for each signature query  $(N_S)$ .
- Message Hash Query: 1  $\mathcal{F}_{\Omega}$ -transformation, 1 coefficient multiplication and 1 coefficient addition for  $N_H-1$  queries. In addition 1 coefficient addition and 1 Hash calculation.
- Single Extraction: 1 subtraction for each  $N_S$ , 1 multiplication and 1 additional subtraction.

Hence in terms of primitive operations, the total computation time  $\tau$  is given by

$$\tau = (2N_S + 1)\tau_{\mathcal{F}_{\Omega}} + (4N_S + 2N_H + 3)\tau_{\odot} + (N_S + 1)\tau_{\times} + (N_S + N_H)\tau_{\mathsf{H}} + \tau_{\mathsf{Hash}}.$$

#### <span id="page-50-0"></span>B The Coordinate Moment of Points on a Sphere

In this section we compute the moments of the coordinates of points on a sphere, which is an elementary calculus exercise.

**Lemma 2.** Let  $S^{N-1} \subset \mathbb{R}^N$  denote the unit sphere taken with the uniform probability measure, and let  $X_i$  denote any one of the coordinate functions on  $\mathbb{R}^N$ . Then the odd moments of  $X_i$  are 0, and the moments of  $|X_i|$  are given by

$$E(|X_i|^{\ell}) = \frac{\Gamma\left(\frac{\ell+1}{2}\right)\Gamma\left(\frac{N}{2}\right)}{\Gamma\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)\Gamma\left(\frac{N+\ell}{2}\right)}$$

In particular, the variance is  $E(X_i^2) = 1/N$ .

*Proof.* The sphere is invariant under  $X_i \to -X_i$ , so it is clear that the odd moments of  $X_i$  vanish. For the moments of  $|X_i|$  we compute

$$\int_{S^{N-1}} |X_1|^{\ell} dV_{S^{N-1}} 
= \int_0^{\pi} \cdots \int_0^{\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} |\cos \theta_1|^{\ell} \prod_{i=1}^{N-2} \sin^{N-1-i} \theta_i d\theta_1 \cdots d\theta_{N-1} 
= \left( \int_0^{\pi} |\cos \theta_1|^{\ell} \cdot \sin^{N-2} \theta_1 d\theta_1 \right) \left( \prod_{i=2}^{N-2} \int_0^{\pi} \sin^{N-1-i} \theta_i d\theta_i \right) \left( \int_0^{2\pi} d\theta_{N-1} \right) 
= \left( 2 \int_0^{\pi/2} \cos^{\ell} \theta_1 \cdot \sin^{N-2} \theta_1 d\theta_1 \right) \left( \prod_{i=2}^{N-2} 2 \int_0^{\pi/2} \sin^{N-1-i} \theta_i d\theta_i \right) \left( 4 \int_0^{\pi/2} d\theta_{N-1} \right)$$

{51}------------------------------------------------

$$= B\left(\frac{\ell+1}{2}, \frac{N-1}{2}\right) \cdot \prod_{i=2}^{N-2} B\left(\frac{1}{2}, \frac{N-i}{2}\right) \cdot 2B\left(\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2}\right)$$

$$= \frac{\Gamma\left(\frac{\ell+1}{2}\right) \Gamma\left(\frac{N-1}{2}\right)}{\Gamma\left(\frac{N+\ell}{2}\right)} \cdot \prod_{i=2}^{N-2} \frac{\Gamma\left(\frac{1}{2}\right) \Gamma\left(\frac{N-i}{2}\right)}{\Gamma\left(\frac{N-i+1}{2}\right)} \cdot 2\frac{\Gamma\left(\frac{1}{2}\right) \Gamma\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)}{\Gamma(1)}$$

$$= 2\Gamma\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{N-1} \Gamma\left(\frac{\ell+1}{2}\right) \Gamma\left(\frac{N+\ell}{2}\right)^{-1}.$$

In particular, taking  $\ell = 0$  gives the well-known formula

<span id="page-51-1"></span>
$$Vol(S^{N-1}) = 2\Gamma(1/2)^{N}/\Gamma(N/2)$$
(45)

for the volume of the sphere, and thus the probability measure on  $S^{N-1}$  is  $dV_{S^{N-1}}$  divided by (45). Hence

$$E(|X_i|^{\ell}) = \operatorname{Vol}(S^{N-1})^{-1} \int_{S^{N-1}} |X_1|^{\ell} dV_{S^{N-1}} = \frac{\Gamma(\frac{\ell+1}{2}) \Gamma(\frac{N}{2})}{\Gamma(\frac{1}{2}) \Gamma(\frac{N+\ell}{2})},$$

which is the desired formula.

To compute the variance, we use  $\Gamma(z+1)=z\Gamma(z)$  to compute

$$E(X_i^2) = \frac{\Gamma(3/2)\Gamma(N/2)}{\Gamma(1/2)\Gamma(N/2+1)} = \frac{(1/2)\Gamma(1/2)\Gamma(N/2)}{\Gamma(1/2)(N/2)\Gamma(N/2)} = \frac{1}{N}.$$

More generally if  $\ell$  is even, one can expand the gamma functions to get

$$E(X_i^{\ell}) = \prod_{j=0}^{\ell/2-1} \frac{2j+1}{N+2j},$$

so for example,  $E(X_1^4) = 3/(N^2 + 2N)$  and  $E(X_1^6) = 15/(N^3 + 6N^2 + 8N)$ .

## <span id="page-53-0"></span>D Average L2-Norm of F ? c

Lemma 3. Let F ∈ Z <sup>N</sup> . Then for all N ≥ 1 and N/2 ≥ d ≥ 1,

$$\operatorname{Mean}_{\boldsymbol{c} \leftarrow \mathcal{T}_{N}(d)} (\|\boldsymbol{F} \star \boldsymbol{c}\|_{2}^{2}) = 2d \|\boldsymbol{F}\|_{2}^{2}.$$

$$\| \boldsymbol{F} \star \boldsymbol{c} \|_2 \approx \sqrt{2d} \cdot \| \boldsymbol{F} \|_2 \,, \tag{46}$$

which accords with viewing the coefficients of F ?c as summing a 2dc-step random walk through the coefficients of F.

Proof. To ease notation, we write T for T<sup>N</sup> (d). Then

$$\operatorname{Mean}_{\boldsymbol{c} \leftarrow \mathcal{T}_{N}(d)} (\|\boldsymbol{F} \star \boldsymbol{c}\|_{2}^{2}) = (\#\mathcal{T})^{-1} \sum_{\boldsymbol{c} \in \mathcal{T}} \|\boldsymbol{F} \star \boldsymbol{c}\|_{2}^{2}$$

$$= (\#\mathcal{T})^{-1} \sum_{\boldsymbol{c} \in \mathcal{T}} \sum_{k=0}^{N-1} (\boldsymbol{F} \star \boldsymbol{c})_{k}^{2}$$

$$= (\#\mathcal{T})^{-1} \sum_{\boldsymbol{c} \in \mathcal{T}} \sum_{k=0}^{N-1} \left(\sum_{i=0}^{N-1} F_{k-i} c_{i}\right)^{2}$$
