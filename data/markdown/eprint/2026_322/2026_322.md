# Multi-key Fully Homomorphic Encryption with Non-Interactive Setup in the Plain Model

Seonhong Min<sup>1</sup> , Jeongeun Park<sup>2</sup> , and Yongsoo Song<sup>1</sup>

<sup>1</sup> Seoul National University <sup>2</sup> Norwegian University of Science and Technology

Abstract. Multi-key fully homomorphic encryption (MKFHE) enables computation over encrypted data under multiple different keys. Constructing MKFHE without any trusted or interactive setup remains an open problem. In the context of MKFHE, a trusted setup is often assumed to mean the use of a common random string (CRS).

In this paper, we present the first MKFHE scheme in the plain model (i.e., without any trusted or interactive setup) based solely on the RLWE assumption. Our design yields a 2-round multi-party computation (MPC) in the plain model against semi-malicious adversaries. Moreover, it can be applied to transform existing FHE schemes that rely on RGSW in their construction into a multi-key variant. We also provide concrete conversions for widely-used FHE schemes, including BGV, BFV, CKKS, FHEW, TFHE, and Carousel.

Finally, we implement our scheme and present experimental results for the expansion algorithm from a single-key ciphertext to a multi-key ciphertext and the multi-key homomorphic multiplication algorithm.

# 1 Introduction

Over the last decade, fully homomorphic encryption (FHE), which enables computation over encrypted data, has emerged as a promising solution for privacypreserving computation. Its non-interactive nature makes it particularly wellsuited for cloud-based applications, enabling secure processing of sensitive data without requiring interaction between parties [\[25,](#page-31-0)[46,](#page-33-0)[26,](#page-31-1)[34,](#page-32-0)[24\]](#page-31-2). Notably, recent advances have brought FHE to a level of practicality where it is not only adopted in research prototypes but also deployed in real-world systems, for example, Apple's FHE-based caller ID system [\[53\]](#page-33-1).

Despite recent promising progress, most of the development has focused on the single-user setting, whereas real-world cloud-based applications often involve data from multiple owners. Examples include collaborative analysis, federated learning, joint financial risk assessments, and secure multi-party healthcare data sharing. Recognizing the importance of this field, the U.S. National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) recently issued a call [\[55\]](#page-33-2) to promote research on extending FHE to support multi-party settings.

Multi-key fully homomorphic encryption (MKFHE) [\[44\]](#page-32-1) directly supports computation across multiple parties without requiring interaction between participants. In other words, MKFHE enables computation on inputs encrypted 

{1}------------------------------------------------

under different keys, allowing a function to be evaluated over multiple parties' data while ensuring that each party's input remains secret from the others.

Such a strong functionality of MKFHE can be applied to a wide range of realworld scenarios, including blockchain platforms. In fact, FHE has been applied to blockchain in works to enable privacy-preserving smart contracts. However, all of these constructions are designed for the single-user setting [\[54\]](#page-33-3) or rely on a global secret key distributed to trusted MPC nodes [\[62\]](#page-34-0). Therefore, to support multi-user computation in a truly decentralized manner, it is essential to achieve MKFHE without a trusted setting.

Indeed, the first ever construction of MKFHE [\[44\]](#page-32-1), namely the LTV scheme, requires no interaction among parties and does not rely on any shared randomness, such as a common random/reference string (CRS), which is generated from a trusted setup. This feature was mainly due to the employment of the DSPR (Decisional Small Polynomial Ratio) problem, which is a milder variant of the NTRU problem. On the other hand, its disadvantage lies in the multi-round decryption, which stems from the multiplicative joint key structure. This issue was later resolved by Ananth, Jain, Jin, and Malavolta, from a generic transformation for constructing MKFHE in the plain model (without a trusted setup), which supports a one-round decryption protocol [\[4\]](#page-29-0). The transformation requires the following three building blocks:

- 1. An MKFHE scheme with a trusted setup and one-round decryption,
- 2. An MKFHE scheme without a trusted setup but with an arbitrary round decryption,
- 3. A two-round semi-malicious oblivious transfer protocol without a trusted setup.

Concretely, the first building block can be instantiated easily with existing RLWE-based MKFHE schemes in the CRS model [\[16,](#page-30-0)[17,](#page-30-1)[37](#page-32-2)[,39\]](#page-32-3), as they inherently support one-round decryption. The LTV scheme can be used for the second building block, and finally, the third block can be constructed from LWE-based two-round OT protocols, for instance, [\[10,](#page-30-2)[7\]](#page-30-3).

Later, however, the DSPR problem was found to be insecure when scaled to larger parameters [\[19,](#page-31-3)[1\]](#page-29-1), making the LTV scheme vulnerable, particularly in scenarios involving a large number of keys in the MKFHE setting. Therefore, subsequent developments in this field have shifted toward employing a different hardness assumption, namely Ring Learning with Errors (RLWE), in order to avoid the known attacks and allow more flexible parameter choices.

Nevertheless, all secure constructions [\[18,](#page-31-4)[48,](#page-33-4)[37,](#page-32-2)[39,](#page-32-3)[16,](#page-30-0)[17\]](#page-30-1) based on RLWE rely on a CRS to achieve practical efficiency and simplify key entanglement for correct decryption. While there have been a few attempts [\[11,](#page-30-4)[35\]](#page-32-4) to remove reliance on a CRS by introducing an interactive setup among a fixed number of parties, this approach sacrifices the dynamic property, namely, the ability to support ad hoc participants on-the-fly, which was inherently supported by the original MKFHE construction. Several recent works [\[59,](#page-33-5)[50,](#page-33-6)[57\]](#page-33-7) attempt to revive DSPR in the context of MKFHE with carefully chosen small parameters to ensure security, but all of them ultimately rely on (R)LWE ciphertexts for correctness, thereby 

{2}------------------------------------------------

requiring a CRS. Furthermore, the scheme proposed in [\[59\]](#page-33-5) was later shown to be insecure due to its use of a fixed Hamming weight, as demonstrated in [\[36\]](#page-32-5). Consequently, it leads us to the following fundamental research question:

> Can we construct a secure MKFHE scheme in the plain model without any pre-computation, and with one-round decryption?

Importantly, even under a relaxed version of the problem where one-round decryption is not required and it suffices to support an arbitrary number of decryption rounds, no existing secure MKFHE scheme without a trusted setup is known to fulfill this requirement [3](#page-2-0) .

Our Contribution. In this paper, we resolve the research question by constructing a multi-key variant of Gentry-Sahai-Waters fully homomorphic encryption scheme [\[29\]](#page-31-5), which we call multi-key RGSW, based solely on RLWE in the plain model with no interactive setup. As a direct implication of a transform from [\[4\]](#page-29-0), we achieve one-round decryption as well.

In previous papers, the use of a CRS appears in RGSW ciphertexts, which consist of vectors of RLWE ciphertexts. For example, an RGSW ciphertext can be written as (b, A), where A is a random matrix of polynomials in ZQ[X]/(X<sup>N</sup> + 1) for some positive integer Q and power-of-two N, and it is assumed to be a CRS shared among the parties.

In contrast, we construct a multi-key RGSW scheme without a common polynomial matrix, such as A. This results in a multi-key FHE scheme that does not rely on a CRS. In more detail, at the core of our MKFHE, there lies a new single-key encryption called the compact LEV (cLEV), which offers a multiplicative operation between a vector of ring elements and an encrypted message. We extend the cLEV cryptosystem to a multi-key variant of the RGSW cryptosystem via homomorphic operations in Section [3.](#page-12-0) Then we show that our multi-key RGSW cryptosystem satisfies the definition of MKFHE in Section [4.](#page-19-0) However, since the entanglement is no longer linear, decryption lacks the clean structure typically seen in CRS-based MKFHE schemes.

As a result, the server must explicitly follow the non-linear relation of the joint key[4](#page-2-1) for correctness, without learning its actual value. In our solution, the server follows this relation by keeping track of the order of multiplications, which poses little overhead in an outsourced computation setting where the circuit information is already known. This tracked information is used by the server for key switching and bootstrapping, which performs decryption homomorphically to enable unlimited operations.

#### 1.1 Technical Overview

For some positive integer Q > 0 and a power-of-two N (which is a fixed parameter throughout the paper), we write  $R := \mathbb{Z}[X]/(X^N + 1)$  and  $R_Q := R/QR$ . Also,  $\mathbf{g} = (g_0, \dots, g_{d-1}) \in \mathbb{Z}^d$  is a gadget vector, and  $h : R_Q \to R^d$  is the corresponding decomposition function, such that  $||h(a)||_{\infty}$  is small and  $a = h(a)^{\top} \cdot \mathbf{g}$  (mod Q) for any  $a \in R_Q$ .

**Compact Leveled Encryption.** Consider a multi-key RLWE ciphertext  $\mathbf{c} = (c_0, \dots, c_{k-1}) \in R_Q^{1 \times k}$  of a plaintext  $m \in R_Q$  under a joint key  $\mathsf{sk} = (s_0, \dots, s_{k-1}) \in R^{k \times 1}$  of parties  $1, \dots, k-1$ . In other words,

$$\sum_{i=0}^{k-1} c_i s_i = \mathbf{c} \cdot \mathsf{sk} \approx m \pmod{Q}.$$

We remark that the plaintext m is encrypted in a noisy manner, which we neglect in this overview. In later sections, we discuss how one can keep the noise level small during the homomorphic computation, ensuring the correct decryption result. Now, assume that the k-th party wants to multiply some plaintext  $\mu \in R_Q$  with this ciphertext. In the conventional RLWE-RGSW multiplication, the linear decryption formula is homomorphically computed, but the secret is multiplied by the plaintext. Specifically, we homomorphically evaluate

$$\sum_{i=0}^{k-1} c_i \cdot (\mu \cdot s_i) = \mathbf{c} \cdot (\mu \cdot \mathsf{sk}) \approx \mu \cdot m \pmod{Q}$$

given the encryptions of  $g_j \cdot \mu \cdot s_i$  for  $0 \le i < k$  and  $0 \le j < d$ . However, this is challenging to realize in the conventional multi-key scenario, as the parties do not have access to all  $s_i$ 's and therefore cannot generate the encryption of  $g_j \cdot \mu \cdot s_i$ 's directly. In prior works [48,51,16,39], this challenge was resolved by using the CRS. Roughly speaking, the encryptions of  $g_j \cdot \mu \cdot s_i$  can be homomorphically generated from the pre-computed arithmetic relation between the CRS and each party's key.

Instead, we homomorphically compute a vector  $\mu \cdot \mathbf{c} \in R_Q^{1 \times k}$  packed in a single ciphertext. Our approach does not require any information on the other parties' secrets, removing the need for additional arithmetic relations using CRS. However, the challenge here is that a conventional multi-key ciphertext can encrypt only a single ring element, not a vector. Therefore, we need a new cryptosystem that can encrypt a plaintext vector into a single ciphertext. For this, we utilize the shared-A RLWE. Briefly speaking, the shared-A RLWE is identical to several RLWE ciphertexts that share the randomness part, encrypted under different keys. To be specific, given the secret key  $\mathbf{t} = (t_1, \dots, t_k) \in R^{k \times 1}$ , a

{5}------------------------------------------------

shared-A RLWE encryption of a vector  $\boldsymbol{\mu} = (\mu_1, \dots, \mu_k) \in R_Q^{1 \times k}$  is given by  $(b_1, \dots, b_k, a) \in R_Q^{1 \times (k+1)}$  where

$$\forall 1 \le i \le k, \quad b_i + a \cdot t_i \approx \mu_i \pmod{Q}.$$

It is worth noting that the shared-A RLWE has been explicitly and implicitly considered in many works [52,12,5,6], including MKFHE [16,17,37,39].

On top of shared-A RLWE, we construct a new encryption scheme, called the *compact leveled encryption*, cLEV for short. Let k be a positive integer, the dimension of the encryption. Concretely, a cLEV encryption of  $\mu$  of dimension kis composed of shared-A RLWE encryptions of each row of the matrix  $\mu \cdot \mathbf{g} \otimes \mathbf{I}_k$ , where  $\otimes$  denotes the tensor product of matrices and  $\mathbf{I}_k$  denotes the identity matrix of the size  $k \times k$ . Consequently, a cLEV encryption of dimension k is a matrix of ring elements of the size  $kd \times (k+1)$ , where the i-th row is a shared-A RLWE encryption of the i-th row of  $\mu \mathbf{g} \otimes \mathbf{I}_k$ .

We also propose a new multiplicative operation called the *vector gadget product*, defined between a vector of ring elements of size k and a cLEV encryption of dimension k. It homomorphically computes the following linear equation:

$$[h(c_0)^{\top} h(c_1)^{\top} \dots h(c_{k-1})^{\top}] \cdot (\mu \mathbf{g} \otimes \mathbf{I}_k) = [c_0 \ c_1 \dots c_{k-1}] \cdot (\mu \mathbf{I}_k)$$
$$= \mu \cdot \mathbf{c} \pmod{Q}$$

for the input vector  $\mathbf{c} = (c_0, \dots, c_{k-1}) \in R_Q^{1 \times k}$ . In ciphertext level, the vector gadget product between  $\mathbf{c} \in R_Q^{1 \times k}$  and a cLEV ciphertext  $\mathbf{C} \in R_Q^{kd \times (k+1)}$  of dimension k, which we denote by  $\mathbf{c} \boxtimes_v \mathbf{C}$ , is as follows:

$$\mathbf{c} \boxtimes_v \mathbf{C} = [h(c_0)^\top h(c_1)^\top \dots h(c_{k-1})^\top] \cdot \mathbf{C} \pmod{Q}.$$

Now, assume that  $\mathbf{c}$  is a multi-key encryption of m under the secret  $\mathsf{sk}$ . Then, the resulting ciphertext of the vector gadget product can be seen as an encryption of  $\mu \cdot m$  under a 'joint' secret of  $\mathsf{sk}$  and  $\mathsf{t}$ . Let us delve into detail. Observe that the decryption of a shared-A RLWE encryption  $(b_1,\ldots,b_k,a) \in R_Q^{1\times(k+1)}$  of a vector  $(\mu_1,\ldots,\mu_k) \in R^{1\times k}$  under the secret  $\mathsf{t}=(t_1,\ldots,t_k) \in R^{k\times 1}$  can be written as the following linear equation:

$$[b_1 \dots b_k \ a] \cdot \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{I}_k \\ \mathbf{t}^{\top} \end{bmatrix} \approx [\mu_1 \dots \mu_k] \pmod{Q}.$$

This directly follows from the definition of shared-A RLWE. Being a stack of shared-A RLWE encryptions, a cLEV encryption  $\mathbf{C} \in R_Q^{kd \times (k+1)}$  of  $\mu \in R$  is decrypted with a linear relation, as follows:

$$\mathbf{C} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{I}_k \\ \mathbf{t}^{\top} \end{bmatrix} \approx \mu \cdot \mathbf{g} \otimes I_k \pmod{Q}.$$

Therefore, for the output ciphertext  $\mathbf{c}' = \mathbf{c} \boxtimes_v \mathbf{C} \in R_Q^{1 \times (k+1)}$  of the vector gadget product, we have the following equation:

$$\mathbf{c}' \cdot \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{I}_k \\ \mathbf{t}^\top \end{bmatrix} \cdot \mathsf{sk} = \begin{bmatrix} h(c_0)^\top \ h(c_1)^\top \ \dots \ h(c_{k-1})^\top \end{bmatrix} \cdot \mathbf{C} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{I}_k \\ \mathbf{t}^\top \end{bmatrix} \cdot \mathsf{sk}$$

{6}------------------------------------------------

$$= [h(c_0)^\top \ h(c_1)^\top \dots h(c_{k-1})^\top] \cdot (\mu \mathbf{g} \otimes \mathbf{I}_k) \cdot \mathsf{sk}$$
$$\approx \mu \cdot \mathbf{c}^\top \cdot \mathsf{sk} \approx \mu \cdot m \pmod{Q}.$$

The above relation implies that the vector gadget product outputs a ciphertext encrypted under a joint key  $\begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{I}_k \\ \mathbf{t}^{\top} \end{bmatrix} \cdot \mathsf{sk} = \begin{bmatrix} \mathsf{sk} \\ \mathbf{t}^{\top} \cdot \mathsf{sk} \end{bmatrix}$ . In our scenario, each *i*-th party outputs cLEV encryption of dimension *i* and performs the vector gadget product sequentially in order to compute a product of their messages. More specifically,  $\mathbf{C}_i \in R_Q^{id \times (i+1)}$  be a cLEV ciphertext of dimension *i* encrypting some message  $\mu_i \in R_Q$  under  $\mathbf{s}_i \in R^i$ , the secret key of the *i*-th party for  $1 \leq i \leq n$ , where *n* denotes the number of participants. Then, for some plaintext  $m \in R_Q$ ,  $m \cdot \prod_{i=1}^n \mu_i \in R_Q$  can be homomorphically computed as  $\mathbf{c} := (\dots((m \boxtimes_v \mathbf{C}_1) \boxtimes_v \mathbf{C}_2) \boxtimes_v \dots) \boxtimes_v \mathbf{C}_n$ , encrypted under the secret  $\overline{\mathbf{sk}} := \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{I}_n \\ \mathbf{s}_n^{\top} \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{I}_{n-1} \\ \mathbf{s}_{n-1}^{\top} \end{bmatrix} \dots \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{I}_1 \\ \mathbf{s}_1^{\top} \end{bmatrix}$ . We remark that the final joint secret has the form  $\overline{\mathbf{sk}} = (1, \overline{s}_1, \dots, \overline{s}_n) \in R^{(n+1) \times 1}$  for some polynomials  $\overline{s}_i \in R$  for  $1 \leq i \leq n$ .

Multi-key RGSW. The cLEV encryption and vector gadget product enable multiple parties to compute the product of private data, however, its functionality is still limited as it only allows the parties to multiply once and no further homomorphic multiplications. Therefore, it is important to achieve full homomorphism, i.e., an unlimited number of homomorphic multiplications and additions. We achieve this by extending the cLEV encryption into a multi-key RGSW ciphertext.

Previously, the joint secret of the resulting ciphertext was given by  $\overline{\mathbf{sk}} = (1, \overline{s}_1, \dots, \overline{s}_n)$ . To facilitate RGSW-RGSW multiplication under this secret, we require MLWE encryptions of each entry of  $\mu \cdot (\mathbf{g} \otimes \overline{\mathbf{sk}})$ , i.e., MLWE encryptions of  $g_j \mu \overline{s}_i$  and  $g_j \mu$  for  $1 \leq i \leq n$  and  $0 \leq j < d$ . Our core observation is that  $\overline{s}_i$  is essentially a multivariate polynomial of  $s_{j,k}$ 's, where  $s_{j,k}$  denotes the k-th entry of the secret  $\mathbf{s}_j$  of j-th party, for  $1 \leq j \leq n$  and  $0 \leq k < j$ .

Without loss of generality, assume that  $\mu$  belongs to the *i*-th party. Naïvely, we can compute all the terms of each multivariate polynomial  $g_m \mu \bar{s}_\ell$  using the cLEV encryptions of  $s_{j,k}$ 's for  $1 \leq i \neq j \leq n$  and  $0 \leq k < j$ ,  $\mu \cdot s_{i,k}$  for  $0 \leq k < i$ , and finally  $\mu$ , then add them together to obtain an MLWE encryption of  $g_m \mu \bar{s}_\ell$  for  $0 \leq m < d$  and  $1 \leq \ell \leq n$ . However, this naïve approach may require an unnecessarily large amount of vector gadget multiplications, leading to poor performance. Instead, we propose a circuit-like approach that can inductively generate each row of RGSW encryptions.

<span id="page-6-0"></span>Let 
$$\overline{\mathsf{sk}}_i := \prod_{j=1}^i \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{I}_j \\ \mathbf{s}_j^{\top} \end{bmatrix}$$
. Then, it is easy to see  $\overline{\mathsf{sk}}_{i+1} = \begin{bmatrix} \overline{\mathsf{sk}}_i \\ \mathbf{s}_{i+1}^{\top} \cdot \overline{\mathsf{sk}}_i \end{bmatrix}$ , hence 
$$\overline{\mathsf{sk}}_{i+1} = \begin{bmatrix} \overline{\mathsf{sk}}_i \\ \mathbf{s}_{i+1}^{\top} \cdot \overline{\mathsf{sk}}_i \end{bmatrix}. \tag{1}$$

The core intuition of the circuit approach is based on this relation. Assume that  $\mu$  belongs to the idx-th party. Our goal is to generate the RLWE encryptions of

{7}------------------------------------------------

 $g_j\mu$  and  $g_j\mu\cdot \overline{s}_i$  under secret  $\overline{\mathsf{sk}}$ , for  $1\leq i\leq n$  and  $0\leq j< d$ . To allow this, we first compute all the encryptions of  $g_js_i$  under the key  $\mathsf{sk}_{\mathsf{idx}-1}$  for all  $1\leq i<\mathsf{idx}$ , iteratively. Computing  $g_j\cdot \overline{s}_1$  is straightforward, as  $\overline{s}_1=s_{1,0}$ . Now, suppose that we are given encryptions of  $g_j\overline{s}_1,g_j\overline{s}_2,\ldots,g_j\overline{s}_i$  for some  $1\leq i<\mathsf{idx}$  under the key  $\mathsf{sk}_i$ . Recall that  $\overline{s}_{i+1}=\sum_{k=0}^i \overline{s}_k s_{i+1,k}$  from Eq. (1). Therefore, we perform i+1 vector gadget multiplications between the RLWE encryption of  $g_j\cdot \overline{s}_k$  under  $\mathsf{sk}_i$  and the cLEV encryption of  $s_{i+1,k}$  for  $0\leq k\leq i$  under  $s_{i+1}$  to obtain encryptions of  $g_j\overline{s}_ks_{i+1,k}$  under the secret  $\mathsf{sk}_{i+1}$ , then the summation of them is essentially the RLWE encryption of  $g_j\overline{s}_{i+1}$  under  $\mathsf{sk}_{i+1}$ . The mathematical induction implies that we can generate the RLWE encryptions of  $g_j\overline{s}_i$  for  $1\leq i<\mathsf{idx}$  under the secret  $\mathsf{sk}_{\mathsf{idx}-1}$ .

Next, we compute the encryptions of  $g_j \mu \overline{s}_i$  for  $1 \leq i \leq idx$  analogously, using the cLEV encryptions of  $\mu$  and  $\mu s_{\mathsf{idx},j}$  for  $0 \leq j < idx$ . Specifically, if i < idx, we can multiply the cLEV encryption of  $\mu$  to the encryptions of  $g_j \overline{s}_i$ , and if i = idx, compute  $\overline{s}_{\mathsf{idx}} = \sum_{k=0}^{\mathsf{idx}} \overline{s}_k s_{\mathsf{idx},k}$ .

Finally, given the RLWE encryptions of  $g_j \mu \overline{s}_i$  for all  $1 \leq i \leq \text{idx}$  under secret  $\mathsf{sk}_{\mathsf{idx}}$ , we can compute the encryptions of  $g_j \mu \overline{s}_i$  under secret  $\mathsf{sk}_n$  easily by inductively performing homomorphic evaluation of the equation  $g_j \mu \overline{s}_{i+1} = \sum_{k=0}^i g_j \mu \overline{s}_k \cdot s_{i+1,k}$ . In total, we perform  $n \cdot n(n+1)/2 = O(n^3)$  vector gadget products to obtain an RGSW encryption of some message  $\mu$ .

Formalizing Our Multi-key RGSW. We formally describe our novel multi-key RGSW scheme, algorithm by algorithm, in the multi-key syntax. We first describe the key generation and the encryption algorithm, along with a cipher-text shrinking method for the cLEV encryption. Suppose that we are given a cLEV encryption  $\mathbf{C} = (c_{i,j})_{0 \le i < dK, 0 \le j \le K} \in R_Q^{dK \times (K+1)}$  of  $\mu$  under the secret  $\mathbf{t} = (t_i)_{0 \le i < K} \in R_Q^{K \times 1}$ . Then, for  $\mathbf{C}' = (c_{i,j})_{0 \le i < dK', 0 \le j \le K'} \in R_Q^{dK' \times (K'+1)}$  and  $\mathbf{t}' = (t_i)_{0 \le i < K'}$  for some  $1 \le K' < K$ , it is easy to see that

$$\mathbf{C}' \cdot \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{I}_{K'} \\ \mathbf{t}'^{\top} \end{bmatrix} \approx \mu \mathbf{g} \otimes \mathbf{I}_{K'},$$

hence making  $\mathbf{C}'$  a cLEV encryption of dimension K' under the secret  $\mathbf{t}'$ . Now, recall that our multi-key RGSW scheme requires the parties' secrets to have different dimensions. Therefore, in a direct instantiation of our scheme, the key generation and encryption algorithms depend on the index of the ciphertext and, hence, are inconsistent among the parties. We also describe the evaluation algorithm. When there are multiple evaluators, their joint keys may differ as they can choose different multiplication orders. To allow computation between the multi-key RGSW ciphertexts under different joint keys, we propose an efficient key-switching key generation algorithm. Given

Universal Key Generation. Although RGSW is theoretically useful due to its structural design, which does not require any evaluation keys other than the ciphertexts themselves, its performance makes it difficult to use as a main cryptographic tool in real-world applications. Therefore, we discuss how to apply our construction of the multi-key RGSW scheme to the best-performing FHE schemes, including CKKS [20], BFV [9,28], TFHE [21], Carousel [33] and LMKCDEY [41], thereby allowing us to compute with those schemes in the plain model.

The main challenge for MKFHE schemes lies in the evaluation key generation. Especially for the CKKS or BFV schemes, the generation of the relinearization and automorphism keys is the most crucial part.

For the joint secret  $\overline{\mathsf{sk}} = (1, \overline{s}_1, \dots, \overline{s}_n) \in R^{(n+1)\times 1}$ , the automorphism key for some automorphism  $\Psi$  is given by a stack of the encryptions of  $g_i \cdot \Psi(\overline{s}_j)$  under  $\overline{\mathsf{sk}}$  for  $0 \le i < d$  and  $1 \le j \le n$ . To generate this, we use the Eq. (1) again: we iteratively compute  $\Psi(\overline{s}_{i+1}) = \sum_{k=1}^{i} \Psi(\overline{s}_k) \Psi(s_{i+1,i-k})$  for  $1 \le i \le n$ , given the cLEV encryptions of  $\Psi(s_{i,j})$  for all  $1 \le i \le n$  and  $0 \le j < i$ . On the other hand, the relinearization key can be seen as RGSW encryptions of  $\overline{s}_i$ 's under the secret  $\overline{\mathsf{sk}}$ , for  $1 \le i \le n$ . An easy way to achieve this is to generate all the RGSW encryptions of  $s_{i,j}$  under  $\overline{\mathsf{sk}}$ , and use the same core formula  $\overline{s}_{i+1} = \sum_{k=0}^{i} \overline{s}_k s_{i+1,k}$  as the ciphertext extension to inductively generate the RGSW encryptions of  $\overline{s}_i$  for  $1 \le i \le n$ .

In contrast, the main challenge for the AP-like MKFHE schemes, namely TFHE [21], Carousel [33] and LMKCDEY [41], lies in the blind rotation key generation. Suppose that each coefficient of  $\overline{\mathbf{s}}_{k} = (1, \overline{s}_{1}, \dots, \overline{s}_{n})$  is contained in an interval [-B, B] for some integer bound B > 0. Then, the blind rotation key is given by the RGSW encryptions of binary values that indicate if a coefficient of  $\overline{s}_{i}$  is a certain value inside [-B, B]. In other words, for  $\overline{s}_{i,j}$ , j-th coefficient of  $\overline{s}_{i}$ , we generate the RGSW encryptions of  $\delta(\overline{s}_{i,j} + B), \dots, \delta(\overline{s}_{i,j} - B)$  where  $\delta$  denotes the Dirac delta function. We observe that these encryptions can be generated iteratively from the 'indicator form' of the coefficients of each party's secret.

# 2.2 Multi-key FHE

A multi-key fully homomorphic encryption (MKFHE) is an encryption scheme that can evaluate an arbitrary circuit over encrypted data. We follow the definition by Lopez-Alt et al. [44], where the algorithms are dependent on a parameter K, the number of distinct secret keys that the scheme can deal with.

**Definition 1 (Multi-key Fully Homomorphic Encryption).** A multi-key fully homomorphic encryption (MKFHE) scheme is a family of probabilistic polynomial-time (PPT) algorithms  $\{\mathcal{E}^{(K)} = (\text{Setup}, \text{KeyGen}, \text{Enc}, \text{Eval}, \text{Dec})\}_{n>0}$ , defined as follows.

- $pp \leftarrow Setup(1^{\lambda}, 1^{K})$ : On input a security parameter  $\lambda$  and the number of parties K, returns a public parameter pp.
- (sk, ek, pk) ← KeyGen(pp): On input a public parameter pp, returns a secret key, (public) evaluation key, and public key pair (sk, ek, pk).
- $\bullet$  ct  $\leftarrow$  Enc(pk; m) : On input a public key pk and a message m, returns a ciphertext ct.
- $\operatorname{ct} \leftarrow \operatorname{Eval}(\mathcal{C}, \{(\operatorname{ek}_i, \operatorname{pk}_i, \operatorname{ct}_i)\}_{i \in [\ell]}) : On \ input \ the \ circuit \ \mathcal{C} \ and \ \ell \ tuples \ (\operatorname{ek}_i, \operatorname{pk}_i, \operatorname{ct}_i)$  where each consists of an  $(\operatorname{public})$  evaluation  $\operatorname{key} \operatorname{ek}_i$ , a  $\operatorname{public} \ \operatorname{key} \operatorname{pk}_i$  and a  $\operatorname{ciphertext} \operatorname{ct}_i$ ,  $\operatorname{returns} \ a \ \operatorname{ciphertext} \operatorname{ct}_i$ .
- $\mu \leftarrow \text{Dec}(\{\mathsf{sk}_i\}_{i \in [K]}; \mathsf{ct}) : On \ input \ secret \ keys \ (\mathsf{sk}_i)_{i \in [K]} \ and \ a \ ciphertext \ \mathsf{ct}, \ returns \ a \ message \ m.$

An MKFHE scheme needs to satisfy correctness and compactness. Formally, the following properties hold for all integers K > 0, circuits C, all sequences of K key tuples  $\{(\mathsf{ek}_i',\mathsf{pk}_i',\mathsf{sk}_i')\}_{i\in[K]}$  each of which is in the support of  $\mathsf{KeyGen}(1^\lambda)$ , all sequences of  $\ell$  key tuples  $\{(\mathsf{ek}_i,\mathsf{pk}_i,\mathsf{sk}_i)\}_{i\in[\ell]}$  each of which is in  $\{(\mathsf{ek}_i',\mathsf{pk}_i',\mathsf{sk}_i')\}_{i\in[K]}$ , and all messages  $(m_i)_{i\in[\ell]}$  and ciphertexts  $(\mathsf{ct}_i)_{i\in[\ell]}$  such that  $\mathsf{ct}_i$  is in the support of  $\mathsf{Enc}(\mathsf{pk}_i';m_i)$ .

- $Correctness: For \operatorname{ct} \leftarrow \operatorname{Eval}(\mathcal{C}, \{(\operatorname{pk}_i, \operatorname{ct}_i)\}_{i \in [K]}), \operatorname{Dec}(\{\operatorname{sk}_i\}_{i \in [K]}; \operatorname{ct}) = \mathcal{C}(m_1, \dots, m_K).$
- Compactness: For ct  $\leftarrow \text{Eval}(\mathcal{C}, \{(\mathsf{pk}_i, \mathsf{ct}_i)\}_{i \in [K]})$ , there exists a bivariate polynomial P such that the size of ct is bounded by  $P(\lambda, K)$ , hence independent from the depth of the evaluated circuit  $\mathcal{C}$ .

#### 2.3 Ring Learning With Errors

We describe the ring variants of the decisional Learning With Errors (LWE) problem, namely the Ring-LWE (RLWE) and Module-LWE (MLWE) problems. The RLWE [\[56](#page-33-11)[,45\]](#page-33-12) and MLWE [\[40\]](#page-32-10) problems are defined over the ideal lattice, especially over the ring of integers of a cyclotomic field, below.

Definition 2 (Decisional RLWE). Let Q be positive integers, N be a powerof-two, and ψ be an error distribution over R := Z[X]/(X<sup>N</sup> + 1). The Ring Learning with Errors (RLWE) distribution with parameters N, Q, ψ is defined as follows: sample a \$ ←− R<sup>Q</sup> and e ← ψ, and output (b, a) where b = −as + e (mod Q) for the secret s ∈ R. For a key distribution χ over R, the decisional RLWE problem, denoted by RLWE(N, Q, ψ, χ) calls for distinguishing the RLWE distribution for a secret s ← χ from the uniform distribution over R<sup>2</sup> Q.

Definition 3 (Decisional MLWE). Let k, Q be positive integers, N be a powerof-two, and ψ be an error distribution over R := Z[X]/(X<sup>N</sup> + 1). The Module Learning With Errors (MLWE) distribution with parameters k, N, Q, ψ is defined as follows: sample a \$ ←− R 1×k <sup>Q</sup> and e ← ψ, and output (b, a) ∈ R 1×(k+1) Q where b = −⟨a, s⟩ + e (mod Q) for the secret s ∈ R<sup>k</sup> . For a key distribution χ over R, the decisional MLWE problem, denoted by MLWE(k, N, Q, ψ, χ), calls for distinguishing the MLWE distribution for a secret s ← χ k from the uniform distribution over R 1×(k+1) <sup>Q</sup> .

We also remark that the RLWE problem is simply an extreme case of the MLWE problem, with k = 1.

In many works [\[52](#page-33-10)[,12](#page-30-7)[,5,](#page-30-8)[6\]](#page-30-9), a variant of RLWE, called the shared-A RLWE, that considers multiple (R)LWE samples with shared (public) randomness, was studied for better efficiency. We describe the shared-A RLWE as follows.

Definition 4 (Decisional shared-A RLWE). Let k, Q be positive integers, N be a power-of-two, and ψ be an error distribution over R := Z[X]/(X<sup>N</sup> + 1). The shared-A Ring Learning With Errors (shared-A RLWE) distribution with parameters k, N, Q, ψ is defined as follows: sample c<sup>0</sup> \$ ←− R<sup>Q</sup> and e<sup>i</sup> ← ψ for 1 ≤ i ≤ k, and output (c0, c1, . . . , ck) ∈ R 1×(k+1) <sup>Q</sup> where c<sup>i</sup> = −c0·si+e<sup>i</sup> (mod Q) for 1 ≤ i ≤ k, for the secret s = (s1, . . . , sk) ∈ R<sup>k</sup> . For a key distribution χ over R, the decisional shared-A RLWE problem, denoted by sARLWE(k, N, Q, ψ, χ), calls for distinguishing the shared-A RLWE distribution for a secret s ← χ k from the uniform distribution over R 1×(k+1) <sup>Q</sup> .

Based on these hardness problems, we can define the MLWE and shared-A encryption schemes.[6](#page-10-0) An MLWE encryption of a plaintext µ ∈ R<sup>Q</sup> under the

# 2.4 Gadget Decomposition and GSW

The gadget decomposition technique is a widely used noise management technique in lattice-based homomorphic cryptography. Let  $R := \mathbb{Z}[X]/(X^N+1)$  for some power-of-two N. Roughly speaking, the gadget decomposition is an injective function  $h: R_Q \to R^d$  which takes an arbitrary ring element modulo Q as an input and returns a small vector over R such that the input can be recovered with a dot product between the output vector and some vector  $\mathbf{g} \in R^d$ . Here, the vector  $\mathbf{g} \in \mathbb{Z}^d$  is called the  $gadget\ vector$ , and parameter d > 0 is called the  $gadget\ dimension$ . Let us formally describe the gadget decomposition below.

**Definition 5 (Gadget Decomposition).** Let Q and d be positive integers. Then, an injective function  $h: R_Q \to R^d$  is called the gadget decomposition with respect to the gadget vector  $\mathbf{g} \in \mathbb{Z}^d$  if it satisfies the following two conditions, for all  $a \in R_Q$  and some B > 0.

$$- \|h(a)\|_{\infty} \le B.$$
  
-  $\langle h(a), \mathbf{g} \rangle = a \pmod{Q}.$ 

Typically, we choose the gadget decomposition h and the gadget vector  $\mathbf{g}$  so that the bound  $B \approx Q^{1/d}$ . In the rest of the draft, we abuse the notation of the gadget decomposition h for the vector as well; in other words,  $h((a_1, \ldots, a_k)) = (h(a_1)^\top \mid \cdots \mid h(a_k)^\top)^\top$ . Similarly, this notation can be extended to the pointwise decomposition of matrices as well.

Building upon the gadget decomposition, we also describe the ring variant of the Gentry-Sahai-Waters (GSW) cryptosystem, referred to as RGSW. Let us first describe the encryption and decryption algorithm. Below, we fix the gadget decomposition  $h: R_Q \to R^d$  and the corresponding gadget vector  $\mathbf{g} \in \mathbb{Z}^d$ , and the noise distribution  $\psi$ .

•RGSW.Enc( $\mathbf{s}; \mu$ ): Given the secret  $\mathbf{s} \in R^k$  and the plaintext  $\mu$ , sample  $\mathbf{A} \leftarrow R_Q^{d(k+1) \times k}$  and  $\mathbf{e} \leftarrow \psi^{d(k+1)}$ . Output  $\overline{\mathbf{C}} = [\mathbf{b} \mid \mathbf{A}] \in R_Q^{d(k+1) \times (k+1)}$  where

$$\mathbf{b} = -\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{s} + \mu \cdot \mathbf{g} \otimes \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ \mathbf{s} \end{bmatrix} + \mathbf{e} \pmod{Q}.$$

{12}------------------------------------------------

•RGSW.Dec(s; C) : Given the secret s ∈ R<sup>k</sup> and the RGSW encryption C ∈ R d(k+1)×(k+1) <sup>Q</sup> , find and output µ that minimizes

$$\mathbf{e} := \overline{\mathbf{C}} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ \mathbf{s} \end{bmatrix} - \mu \cdot \mathbf{g} \otimes \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ \mathbf{s} \end{bmatrix} \pmod{Q}.$$

The RGSW cryptosystem offers two multiplicative operations in addition to the additive and subtractive operations that are naturally supported by the linearity of RLWE, called the internal product [\[29\]](#page-31-5) and external product [\[21\]](#page-31-8). Roughly speaking, the internal product takes two RGSW ciphertexts and outputs an RGSW ciphertext encrypting the multiplication of the messages of the input ciphertexts. On the other hand, the external product takes MLWE and RGSW ciphertexts for each and outputs an MLWE ciphertext encrypting the multiplication of the messages of the input ciphertexts. We describe them formally below.

Definition 6 (Internal Product). Given RGSW ciphertexts C1, C<sup>2</sup> ∈ R d(k+1)×(k+1) <sup>Q</sup> , the internal product ⊡ : R d(k+1)×(k+1) <sup>Q</sup> × R d(k+1)×(k+1) <sup>Q</sup> → R d(k+1)×(k+1) <sup>Q</sup> is defined as follows:

$$\overline{\mathbf{C}}_1 \boxdot \overline{\mathbf{C}}_2 = h(\overline{\mathbf{C}}_1)^{\top} \cdot \overline{\mathbf{C}}_2 \pmod{Q}.$$

Definition 7 (External Product). Given an MLWE ciphertext c ∈ R 1×(k+1) Q and an RGSW ciphertext C ∈ R d(k+1)×(k+1) <sup>Q</sup> , the external product <sup>⊠</sup> : <sup>R</sup> 1×(k+1) <sup>Q</sup> × R d(k+1)×(k+1) <sup>Q</sup> → R 1×(k+1) <sup>Q</sup> is defined as follows:

$$\mathbf{c} \boxtimes \overline{\mathbf{C}} = h(\mathbf{c})^{\top} \cdot \overline{\mathbf{C}} \pmod{Q}.$$

We roughly show the correctness of the internal and external products. Let s be the secret key of the ciphertexts, and m, µ1, µ<sup>2</sup> be the underlying plaintexts of c, C1, C2, respectively. Then,

$$\overline{\mathbf{C}}_1 \boxdot \overline{\mathbf{C}}_2 \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ \mathbf{s} \end{bmatrix} \approx h(\overline{\mathbf{C}}_1)^\top \cdot \mu_2 \mathbf{g} \otimes \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ \mathbf{s} \end{bmatrix} = \mu_2 \cdot \overline{\mathbf{C}}_1 \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ \mathbf{s} \end{bmatrix} = \mu_1 \mu_2 \cdot \mathbf{g} \otimes \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ \mathbf{s} \end{bmatrix} \pmod{Q}.$$

Therefore, C<sup>1</sup> ⊡ C<sup>2</sup> is a valid RGSW encryption of the message µ1µ2. On the other hand,

$$\mathbf{c} \boxdot \overline{\mathbf{C}}_1 \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ \mathbf{s} \end{bmatrix} \approx h(\mathbf{c})^\top \cdot \mu_1 \mathbf{g} \otimes \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ \mathbf{s} \end{bmatrix} = \mu_1 \cdot \mathbf{c} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ \mathbf{s} \end{bmatrix} = \mu_1 m \pmod{Q}.$$

Hence, c ⊡ C<sup>1</sup> is an MLWE encryption of the message µ1m.

# <span id="page-14-1"></span>3.2 Multi-key RGSW

The multi-key multiplication via the vector gadget product allows us to evaluate arbitrary multivariate polynomials between different parties. This directly comes from the fact that the RLWE cryptosystem naturally supports ciphertext addition. To be specific, given a multivariate polynomial, we can simply compute each term with cLEV encryptions of each party's message and the vector gadget product, and then perform ciphertext addition. Let n be the number of participants, and there exists a multivariate polynomial that is evaluated during the computation. Then, each term of the polynomial can be written as a multiplication of some coefficient and private data from a subset of participants.

Therefore, each party only needs to output the cLEV encryptions of their message and use the vector gadget product to compute each term. While this gives us a functionality of computing arbitrary multivariate polynomials, it still suffers from a few downsides. Firstly, the number of input ciphertexts grows exponentially as the polynomial degree increases. Recall that each party gets to multiply only once per term, making the parties output all the monomials required in the computation. Secondly, it does not support circuit evaluation, which is generally much cheaper than direct computation.

To overcome these issues, we construct a new multi-key variant of the RGSW scheme [29] on top of the cLEV cryptosystem and the vector gadget product. Let  $\overline{\mathsf{sk}} = (\overline{s}_0, \overline{s}_1, \dots, \overline{s}_n)$  be the joint secret for n parties, obtained from the gadget vector products. Recall that the RGSW encryption of a plaintext  $\mu \in R$  under this joint secret is composed with the MLWE encryptions of  $g_j \mu \overline{s}_i$  for  $0 \le i \le n$  and  $0 \le j < d$ , and  $s_i$ 's are multivariate polynomials of each party's secret key components.

{15}------------------------------------------------

Therefore, we can generate each row of the RGSW encryption with the vector gadget multiplication and ciphertext addition. To allow this, we require the cLEV encryptions of each party's secret key components, and the cLEV encryption of  $\mu$  and  $\mu$  multiplied by the secret key components of the encrypting party <sup>7</sup>. Let us first investigate the joint key structure of our multi-key scheme. Let  $\mathbf{s}_i = (s_{i,0}, \ldots, s_{i,i-1}) \in R^i$  be the secret of which the cLEV ciphertexts of i-th party are encrypted under, and  $\overline{\mathbf{sk}}_i \in R^{i+1}$  be the secret of the resulting RLWE after vector gadget multiplication by the cLEV ciphertext of i-th party. Then, Eq. (3) implies that

<span id="page-15-2"></span>
$$\overline{\mathsf{sk}}_{i} = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{I}_{i} \\ \mathbf{s}_{i}^{\top} \end{bmatrix} \cdot \overline{\mathsf{sk}}_{i-1} = \begin{bmatrix} \overline{\mathsf{sk}}_{i-1} \\ \mathbf{s}_{i}^{\top} \cdot \overline{\mathsf{sk}}_{i-1} \end{bmatrix}$$
(4)

for all i > 1, where  $\overline{\mathsf{sk}}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{I}_1 \\ \mathbf{s}_1^{\top} \end{bmatrix} = (1, s_{1,1})$ . Therefore, for  $\overline{\mathsf{sk}} = \overline{\mathsf{sk}}_n = (\overline{s}_0 = 1, \overline{s}_1, \dots, \overline{s}_n)$ , we have the recurrence relation

<span id="page-15-1"></span>
$$\overline{s}_i = \sum_{j=1}^i \overline{s}_{j-1} s_{i,j}. \tag{5}$$

It is easy to see that the number of terms of a multivariate polynomial  $\bar{s}_i$  is the sum of the numbers of the terms of each  $\bar{s}_0, \dots \bar{s}_{i-1}$ . This is essentially a geometric sequence with a common ratio of two, making the number of terms of  $\bar{s}_i$  grow exponentially,  $2^{i-1}$  terms to be precise. Therefore, a naïve evaluation of  $g_j \mu \bar{s}_i$  requires  $2^{i-1}$  vector gadget multiplications, making the RGSW extension inefficient with the time complexity exponential to the number of the parties n. Instead, we leverage the recurrence relation to reduce the time complexity for the RGSW extension from  $O(2^n)$  to  $O(n^2)$ .

The main idea is to generate the encryption of  $g_j \mu \overline{s}_i$  from the encryptions of  $g_j \mu \overline{s}_0, \dots g_j \mu \overline{s}_{i-1}$ . Since  $g_j \mu \overline{s}_i = \sum_{k=1}^i g_j \mu \overline{s}_{k-1} \cdot s_{i,k}$ , we can iteratively generate the encryptions of  $g_j \mu \overline{s}_k$ 's and utilize the vector gadget encryption to multiply  $s_{i,k}$ . We provide the full algorithm in Algorithm 1, that generates an RGSW encryption of  $\mu$ , which is a secret data from the idx-th party, under the joint key  $\overline{sk}$ . In the algorithm,  $\mathsf{cLEV}_{\mathbf{s}_i}(\mu)_{j+1}$  denotes the j+1-th row of the  $\mathsf{cLEV}$  encryption  $\mathsf{cLEV}_{\mathbf{s}_i}(\mu)$ .

#### 3.3 Discussion

Compact GSW (cGSW). Recall Algorithm 1, and observe that the RGSW extension algorithm requires not only a cLEV encryption of the message  $\mu$ , but also cLEV encryptions of  $\mu \cdot s_{\mathsf{idx},j}$ 's for  $0 \leq j < \mathsf{idx}$ . We remark that this resembles the structure of the RGSW encryption, which requires all the encryptions  $g_j \mu \overline{s}_i$  for all  $1 \leq i \leq n$ , not only the encryptions of  $g_j \mu$ . Therefore, for convenience, we define the compact GSW (cGSW) encryption as follows.

Full Homomorphism. As homomorphic operations incur noise growth, the bootstrapping operation is required to refresh the ciphertext and consequently obtain full homomorphism. Roughly speaking, the bootstrapping takes a ciphertext and the bootstrapping key, and homomorphically computes the decryption circuit. It is known that the bootstrapping for GSW only requires a polynomial overhead (consuming only one level) with the branching program-based approach [\[14,](#page-30-10)[3,](#page-29-2)[30](#page-31-10)[,27\]](#page-31-11). We remark that this approach can be adopted to our scheme with each party disclosing encryptions of each bit of their secret key, since essentially the decryption circuit of our scheme can be written in a branching program. Therefore, as long as the ciphertext modulus can tolerate the decryption circuit (with some margin for homomorphic computation and plaintext), our scheme is fully homomorphic[8](#page-17-0) .

Role of CRS. While the majority of the existing MKFHE schemes are based on the CRS model, there are a few works that consider multi-key schemes in the plain model. Among them, the construction of [\[44\]](#page-32-1) stands out with its noninteractive key generation and encryption algorithm. Their construction is based on the NTRU assumption, and later it was extended to the NGS encryption scheme [\[8\]](#page-30-11), concurrently in [\[59\]](#page-33-5) and [\[50\]](#page-33-6) to accelerate the TFHE bootstrapping in the multi-key setting. In these works, the joint key after multiplication has a multiplicative structure, and the noise grows exponentially with respect to the number of parties. In contrast, the multi-key schemes that assume the CRS model have a linear joint key structure after multiplication and also show polynomial noise growth with respect to the number of parties.

We note that our scheme also has a multiplicative joint key structure and exhibits exponential noise growth, as shown in Eq. [\(1\)](#page-6-0) and Lemma [3.](#page-18-0) Based on this observation, we introduce a conjecture on the relation between the CRS model and the key structure.

Conjecture 1. The usage of Common Reference/Random String (CRS) in the MKFHE allows us to have a linear joint key structure and a polynomial noise growth.

# <span id="page-19-0"></span>4 MKFHE in the plain model

In this section, we formalize our multi-key RGSW scheme and discuss how it can be used as a baseline solution for a two-round multi-party computation (MPC) protocol.

# 4.1 Building Blocks

In this section, we introduce some important building blocks required to realize the traditional syntax of the multi-key homomorphic encryption scheme with our multi-key RGSW scheme. Specifically, we devise a ciphertext shrinking method for the cLEV encryption, a key-switching key generation algorithm for our RGSW scheme, and finally formalize the evaluation algorithm with our multi-key RGSW scheme.

Ciphertext Shrinking. Note that our multi-key scheme from Section 3.2 requires each party to know the exact order in which its ciphertexts are multiplied beforehand since the sizes of the input cLEV ciphertexts differ with respect to the multiplication order. However, this contradicts the definition of MKFHE, as the key generation algorithm only takes the public parameters, not the index of the party. We can handle this issue by having each party output the cLEV encryptions of the maximum size and shrink the size of the cLEV encryption upon receiving them. Let us elaborate in detail. Recall that the cLEV encryption of  $\mu \in R$  under the secret  $\mathbf{s} = (s_1, \ldots, s_n) \in R^n$  with respect to the gadget vector  $\mathbf{g} \in \mathbb{Z}^d$  and the corresponding decomposition function  $h: R_Q \to R^d$  is given by

$$\mathbf{C} = (c_{i,j})_{1 \leq i \leq nd, 1 \leq j \leq n+1} \in R_Q^{nd \times (n+1)}, \text{ such that } \mathbf{C} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{I}_n \\ \mathbf{s}^\top \end{bmatrix} \approx \mu \cdot \mathbf{g} \otimes \mathbf{I}_n \pmod{Q}.$$

From this relation, we can easily show that

$$\mathbf{C}' = \begin{bmatrix} c_{1,1} & \dots & c_{1,k} & c_{1,n+1} \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots \\ c_{kd,1} & \dots & c_{kd,k} & c_{kd,n+1} \end{bmatrix} \in R_Q^{kd \times (k+1)}$$

is a cLEV encryption of  $\mu$  under the secret  $\mathbf{s}' = (s_1, \dots, s_k)$  for any  $1 \le k \le n$ , since

$$\mathbf{C}' \cdot \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{I}_k \\ \mathbf{s'}^{\top} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} c_{1,1} & \dots & c_{1,k} & c_{1,n+1} \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots \\ c_{kd,1} & \dots & c_{kd,k} & c_{kd,n+1} \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & \dots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \dots & 1 \\ s_1 & s_2 & \dots & s_k \end{bmatrix} \approx \mu \cdot \mathbf{g} \otimes \mathbf{I}_k \pmod{Q}.$$

We note that the ciphertext shrinking can be directly applied to the cGSW encryption as well.

**Key-Switching Key Generation.** In real-world protocols using homomorphic encryption schemes with multiple parties, there can be more than two evaluators, where each evaluates its circuit and then potentially evaluates another circuit on their outputs. However, their choices of indices for parties may differ in the asymmetric setting, making the joint secret of each evaluator completely different. We remark that these secrets cannot be interchanged with a simple rearrangement, unlike the prior works in the CRS model. This raises the need for key-switching keys between these ciphertexts.

Suppose that we want to switch the joint secret from  $\overline{\mathsf{sk}} = (\overline{s}_0 = 1, \overline{s}_1, \dots, \overline{s}_n)$  to  $\overline{\mathsf{sk}}' = (\overline{s}_0' = 1, \overline{s}_1', \dots, \overline{s}_n')$ . Then, we require the key-switching key  $\mathsf{ksk}_{\overline{\mathsf{sk}} \to \overline{\mathsf{sk}}'} \in R_Q^{nd \times (n+1)}$  from  $\overline{\mathsf{sk}}$  to  $\overline{\mathsf{sk}}'$ , which satisfies the following relation.

$$\mathsf{ksk}_{\overline{\mathsf{sk}} \to \overline{\mathsf{sk}}'} \cdot \overline{\mathsf{sk}}' \approx \mathbf{g} \otimes \begin{bmatrix} \overline{s}_1 \\ \vdots \\ \overline{s}_n \end{bmatrix} \pmod{Q}$$

Given an MLWE ciphertext  $\mathbf{c} = (c_0, \dots, c_n) \in R_Q^{n+1}$ , its key can be switched from  $\overline{\mathsf{sk}}$  to  $\overline{\mathsf{sk}}'$  as follows:

$$\mathbf{c}' = (c_0, 0, \dots, 0) + \left[ h(c_1)^\top \dots h(c_n)^\top \right] \cdot \mathsf{ksk}_{\overline{\mathsf{sk}} \to \overline{\mathsf{sk}}'} \pmod{Q}.$$

It is easy to show that this algorithm is valid since

$$\mathbf{c}' \cdot \overline{\mathsf{sk}}' \approx c_0 + \left[ h(c_1)^\top \dots h(c_n)^\top \right] \cdot \mathbf{g} \otimes \begin{bmatrix} \overline{s}_1 \\ \vdots \\ \overline{s}_n \end{bmatrix} = c_0 + \sum_{i=1}^n c_i s_i = \mathbf{c} \cdot \overline{\mathsf{sk}} \pmod{Q}.$$

Now, observe that the key-switching key  $\mathsf{ksk}_{\overline{\mathsf{sk}} \to \overline{\mathsf{sk}}'}$  from  $\overline{\mathsf{sk}}$  to  $\overline{\mathsf{sk}}'$  is essentially a stack of MLWE encryptions of  $g_j \overline{s}_i$  for  $1 \leq i \leq n, \ 0 \leq j < d$  under  $\overline{\mathsf{sk}}'$ . Naïvely, this can be generated from the cLEV encryptions of entries of each party's secret, and  $g_j \overline{s}_i$  is a multivariate polynomial of them. A more efficient (and conceptually simpler) way to achieve this is to utilize the internal product.

Roughly speaking, we first generate the RGSW encryptions of the entries of each party's secret under  $\overline{\mathsf{sk}}'$ , and then utilize the internal product to generate MLWE encryptions of  $\overline{s}_i$  from them. With this approach, we can use a similar iterative method for RGSW generation. We describe the key-switching key generation algorithm in Algorithm 2. In the algorithm description,  $\mathbf{s}_i = (s_{i,0},\ldots,s_{i,n-1}) \in R^n$  denotes the secret of the *i*-th party. We also consider two arrays idx and idx' of length n that hold the indices corresponding to the secret  $\overline{\mathsf{sk}}$  and  $\overline{\mathsf{sk}}'$ , respectively. In other words,

We also show the correctness and compactness and address the security of our MKFHE scheme below.

Correctness. By Lemma 3, each party's cGSW encryptions can be switched to the multi-key RGSW ciphertexts that encrypt the same underlying message. Therefore, due to the correctness of the circuit evaluation from the RGSW scheme, the correctness of our scheme holds.

**Compactness.** The output RGSW ciphertext from Algorithm 1 has a size of dn(n+1)N. In the typical RLWE setting, we set  $\log Q, N \in O(\lambda)$  for the security parameter  $\lambda$ . Since  $d = O(\log Q)$ , our ciphertext has an asymptotic size of  $O(n^2\lambda^2)$ , which is a bivariate polynomial of n and  $\lambda$ . Since the RGSW scheme preserves the size after the circuit evaluation, our scheme also satisfies the compactness from the definition of MKFHE.

**Security.** The security of our scheme relies on the shared-A RLWE hardness problem and the circular security assumption.

Achieving Stronger Security. We also discuss how to achieve stronger security for our MKFHE. As discussed in [44,22], a semi-honest MKFHE scheme can be transformed into a semi-malicious scheme by proving the well-formedness of the input encryptions and evaluation keys of each party. These can be achieved with non-interactive zero-knowledge proofs (NIZKs), without any additional communication rounds. It is worth noting that there are some RLWE-specific NIZKs with concrete performance for *n*-out-of-*n* threshold FHE schemes [15,31], which can be applied to our scheme as well, thereby achieving stronger security.

#### <span id="page-24-0"></span>4.3 Two-round Semi-Malicious Secure MPC

Finally, we provide a brief overview of how to build a two-round semi-malicious MPC protocol based on the transformation from [\[4\]](#page-29-0).

One-round decryption. As described in prior works [\[44,](#page-32-1)[38,](#page-32-12)[39,](#page-32-3)[4\]](#page-29-0), the distributed decryption process can be instantiated with another MPC protocol, including multi-party (threshold) FHE or a garbled circuit. However, the use of a general MPC would substantially increase the round complexity of the decryption protocol. Therefore, we leverage the transformation from Ananth et al. [\[4\]](#page-29-0) to achieve one-round decryption for our MKFHE scheme. In [\[4\]](#page-29-0), the authors built an MKFHE scheme in the plain model with one-round decryption from three ingredients, as we describe in Theorem [1.](#page-24-1)

- 2. Multi-key FHE in the plain model but with arbitrary round decryption,
- 3. Two-round semi-malicious oblivious transfer in the plain model

there exists multi-key FHE in the plain model with one-round decryption.

Briefly speaking, they evaluate the 'complex' decryption circuit of an MKFHE scheme in the plain model with arbitrary round decryption, using a non-compact multi-key homomorphic encryption scheme made from the OT and an MKFHE scheme with trusted setup (this is referred to as MHE in [\[4\]](#page-29-0)). Therefore, this construction can be naturally extended to our MKFHE scheme by realizing the decryption algorithm of our MKFHE scheme with this MHE. Note that the authors provided a concrete instantiation of MHE solely based on the hardness of the (R)LWE problem, from existing LWE-based MKFHE schemes including [\[48](#page-33-4)[,51](#page-33-8)[,16,](#page-30-0)[39](#page-32-3)[,17\]](#page-30-1) and LWE-based OT protocols such as [\[10,](#page-30-2)[7,](#page-30-3)[47](#page-33-13)[,43,](#page-32-13)[60,](#page-34-1)[61\]](#page-34-2). Two-round MPC protocol. Based on the one-round decryption, a two-round MPC directly follows from the construction of our MKFHE scheme. We adopt a classical server-(multiple) clients model, where a server computes a function value on behalf of all clients. In the first round, each party outputs the evaluation key, public key, and encryptions with a proof that these materials are wellformed by using a non-interactive zero-knowledge proof (NIZK) system. Then, the server verifies the correctness of the inputs and proceeds to evaluate the circuit if it is correct. Subsequently, in the second round, the evaluation results can be decrypted using the one-round decryption discussed above, with NIZK proving that the decryption share from each party is honestly generated.

For the proof that our two-round MPC is secure against semi-malicious adversaries, we refer to Theorem 8.1 of [\[4\]](#page-29-0). Since semi-malicious secure MKFHE implies semi-malicious MPC [\[48\]](#page-33-4), it suffices to show that our MKFHE with oneround decryption is secure against semi-malicious adversaries. # <span id="page-25-0"></span>5 Multi-key FHE Key Generation

In this section, we explain how our multi-key RGSW scheme can be used as a baseline for the construction of a multi-key variant of the existing RLWE-based FHE schemes, such as BFV [9,28], CKKS [20], TFHE [21], LMKCDEY [41], and Carousel [33]. The main difficulty in extending a single-key scheme to a multi-key scheme lies in the evaluation key generation. This is mainly because a single-key shared-A ciphertext can be easily converted into a multi-key ciphertext with simple zero padding, as discussed in Section 3.2, whereas the evaluation keys are essentially encryptions of some multivariate polynomial of each party's secret. Therefore, in this section, we only discuss how to generate the evaluation keys for a variety of homomorphic encryption schemes.

#### 5.1 BFV/CKKS

We consider the key generation for multi-key BFV and CKKS scheme<sup>11</sup>. These two schemes have the same evaluation key structure, although they have different functionalities; one supports finite field arithmetic, whereas the other supports fixed-point arithmetic. Roughly speaking, their evaluation key consists of two types of key-switching keys, namely the automorphism key and the relinearization key. Below, we describe how to generate them one by one.

**Automorphism Keys** The automorphism key with respect to the automorphism  $\Psi$  is given by a key-switching key from  $\Psi(\overline{\mathsf{sk}}) = (\Psi(s_0) = 1, \Psi(s_1), \dots, \Psi(s_n))$  to  $\overline{\mathsf{sk}}$ . Given an MLWE encryption  $\mathbf{c} = (c_0, \dots, c_n) \in R_Q^{n+1}$  of the plaintext m under the secret  $\overline{\mathsf{sk}}$ , i.e.,  $\mathbf{c} \cdot \overline{\mathsf{sk}} = \sum_{i=0}^n c_i \overline{s}_i \approx m \pmod{Q}$ , it is easy to see that  $\Psi(\mathbf{c}) = (\Psi(c_0), \dots, \Psi(c_n)) \in R_Q^{n+1}$  is an encryption of  $\Psi(m)$  under the secret  $\Psi(\overline{\mathsf{sk}})$  since

$$\Psi(\mathbf{c}) \cdot \Psi(\overline{\mathsf{sk}}) = \sum_{i=0}^{n} \Psi(c_i) \cdot \Psi(\overline{s}_i) \approx \Psi(m) \pmod{Q}.$$

Therefore, we can perform the homomorphic automorphism operation by simply switching the secret key of  $\Psi(\mathbf{c})$  from  $\Psi(\overline{\mathsf{sk}})$  to  $\overline{\mathsf{sk}}$ .

To generate the key-switching key, we utilize a similar induction from Algorithm 1. Note that the secret key  $\Psi(\overline{\mathsf{sk}})$  can be iteratively generated with the recurrence relation Eq. (5) in the same manner as  $\overline{\mathsf{sk}}$ . **Relinarization Key** The relinearization key is a special form of key-switching key that is necessary to perform homomorphic multiplication with the BFV or CKKS scheme. Let us briefly explain. Suppose that we are given two MLWE encryptions  $\mathbf{c} = (c_0, \ldots, c_n) \in R_Q^{n+1}$  and  $\mathbf{c}' = (c'_0, \ldots, c'_n) \in R_Q^{n+1}$  of plaintexts m and m' under the secret  $\overline{\mathsf{sk}}$ , i.e.,  $\mathbf{c} \cdot \mathsf{sk} = \sum_{i=0}^n c_i \overline{s}_i \approx m \pmod{Q}$  and  $\mathbf{c}' \cdot \mathsf{sk} = \sum_{i=0}^n c'_i \overline{s}_i \approx m' \pmod{Q}$ . Then, it is easy to see that the tensor  $\mathbf{c} \otimes \mathbf{c}'$  of  $\mathbf{c}$  and  $\mathbf{c}'$  is an MLWE encryption of  $m \cdot m'$  under the tensored key  $\overline{\mathsf{sk}} \otimes \overline{\mathsf{sk}}$ , since

$$(\mathbf{c} \otimes \mathbf{c}') \cdot (\overline{\mathsf{sk}} \otimes \overline{\mathsf{sk}}) = (\mathbf{c} \cdot \overline{\mathsf{sk}}) \cdot (\mathbf{c}' \cdot \overline{\mathsf{sk}}) \approx m \cdot m' \pmod{Q}^{12}.$$

Therefore, the homomorphic multiplication can be performed by switching the secret of the ciphertext  $\mathbf{c} \otimes \mathbf{c}'$  from the tensored key  $\overline{\mathsf{sk}} \otimes \overline{\mathsf{sk}}$  to the original key  $\overline{\mathsf{sk}}$ . In the prior multi-key schemes [17,37], this key-switching was done with n external products, given the RGSW encryptions of  $\overline{s}_1, \ldots, \overline{s}_n$ . Specifically, for two input ciphertexts  $\mathbf{c} = (c_0, \ldots, c_n) \in R_Q^{n+1}$  and  $\mathbf{c}' = (c'_0, \ldots, c'_n) \in R_Q^{n+1}$ , (roughly) we compute the following<sup>13</sup>.

$$c_0 \cdot \mathbf{c}' + \sum_{i=1}^n (c_i \cdot \mathbf{c}') \boxdot \mathsf{RGSW}(\overline{s}_i) \pmod{Q}.$$

Following this approach, we only need to generate the encryptions of RGSW encryptions of  $\bar{s}_i$ ,  $1 \leq i \leq n$ . # <span id="page-28-0"></span>6 Implementation

To show the correctness and to demonstrate the concrete performance of our MKFHE scheme, we provide a proof-of-concept implementation of it. We implemented our scheme on top of HIENAA.jl [\[32\]](#page-32-8) library, written in Julia. The source code is available at [https://github.com/SNUCP/crsless.](https://github.com/SNUCP/crsless)

Our recommended parameter sets allow the parties to evaluate circuits with a depth of at most two. We fix the Hamming weight of the secret keys by H = 192, and choose the ring degree N and the bit-length of modulus log Q to achieve 128-bit security. The lattice-estimator [\[2\]](#page-29-3) was used to measure the security of our parameter sets. In the experiments, we used the Residue Number System (RNS) gadget decomposition technique. Roughly speaking, the ciphertext modulus Q is a product of d primes Q1, . . . , Q<sup>d</sup> of the size log Q/d, and the gadget decomposition function outputs the ring element modulo Q<sup>i</sup> for all 1 ≤ i ≤ d. Therefore, B<sup>h</sup> naturally becomes max(Q1, . . . , Qd) ≈ Q<sup>1</sup>/d. We choose the discrete Gaussian distribution with σ = 3.2 as the noise distribution, which has the probabilistic bound of 3.2 × 13.11 ≈ 42 with an overwhelming probability of 1 − 2 <sup>−</sup><sup>128</sup>. By substituting these values into the noise formula for RGSW generation Lemma [3,](#page-18-0) we can guarantee that the decryption failure probability with our parameters is smaller than 2<sup>−</sup><sup>128</sup> .

#### <span id="page-35-0"></span>Proofs A

#### Proof of Lemma 1 $\mathbf{A.1}$

*Proof.* Now we show the correctness of our RGSW extension algorithm from Algorithm 1, using mathematical induction. In the following, we let  $sk_0 = (1)$  for convenience. Firstly, we claim that  $acc_{dk+j}^i$  is an encryption of  $\overline{s}_k g_j$  if i < idxand an encryption of  $\mu \overline{s}_k g_j$  if  $i \geq idx$ , under the secret  $\overline{sk}_i$  for any  $0 \leq j < d$ and  $0 \le k \le i$ . Let us first prove for the cases where i < idx. This is trivially true for i=0, since  $\overline{s}_0=1$  and  $\operatorname{acc}_i^0=(g_j)$  (which trivially decrypts to  $g_j$ ) for  $0 \le j < d$  by the definition. Now, assume that the claim holds for some  $i = \iota$ where  $0 \le \iota < \text{idx} - 1$ , i.e.,  $\text{acc}_{dk+j}^{\iota}$  is an MLWE encryption of  $\overline{s}_k g_j$  under the secret  $\overline{\mathsf{sk}}_{\iota}$  for all  $0 \leq k < \iota$ . Then, in the  $\iota + 1$ , j-th iterations of the outer loop, we do the following two things:

1. 
$$\operatorname{acc}_{dk+j}^{\iota+1} \leftarrow (\operatorname{acc}_{dk+j}^{\iota} \mid 0) \text{ for } 0 \leq k \leq \iota$$
  
2.  $\operatorname{acc}_{d(\iota+1)+j}^{\iota+1} \leftarrow \sum_{k=0}^{\iota} \operatorname{acc}_{dk+j}^{\iota} \boxtimes_{v} \operatorname{cLEV}_{\mathbf{s}_{\iota+1}}(s_{\iota+1,k})$ 

Thanks to the recurrence relation on the secret keys  $\overline{\mathsf{sk}}_{\iota}$  and  $\overline{\mathsf{sk}}_{\iota+1}$  from Eq. (4), we have

<span id="page-35-1"></span>
$$\operatorname{acc}_{dk+j}^{\iota+1} \cdot \overline{\mathsf{sk}}_{\iota+1} = \left[\operatorname{acc}_{dk+j}^{\iota} \mid 0\right] \cdot \overline{\mathsf{sk}}_{\iota+1} \\
= \left[\operatorname{acc}_{dk+j}^{\iota} \mid 0\right] \cdot \left[\begin{array}{c} \overline{\mathsf{sk}}_{\iota} \\ \mathbf{s}_{\iota+1}^{\top} \cdot \overline{\mathsf{sk}}_{\iota} \end{array}\right] \\
= \operatorname{acc}_{dk+j}^{\iota} \cdot \overline{\mathsf{sk}}_{\iota} \approx \overline{s}_{k} g_{j} \pmod{Q} \tag{8}$$

for any  $0 \le j < d$  and  $0 \le k \le \iota$ . On the other hand, we also have

<span id="page-35-2"></span>
$$\operatorname{acc}_{d(\iota+1)+j}^{\iota+1} \cdot \overline{\operatorname{sk}}_{\iota+1} = \sum_{k=0}^{\iota} \operatorname{acc}_{dk+j}^{\iota} \boxtimes_{v} \operatorname{cLEV}_{\mathbf{s}_{\iota+1}}(s_{\iota+1,k}) \cdot \overline{\operatorname{sk}}_{\iota+1}$$
