# <span id="page-0-0"></span>Three-Move Blind Signatures in Pairing-Free Groups

Yanbo Chen<sup>∗</sup>

March 25, 2026

### Abstract

We propose the first blind signature scheme that simultaneously achieves the following properties:

- It uses a pairing-free group and random oracles in a black-box manner;
- It provably achieves concurrent security based on standard assumptions (DDH) without the algebraic group model (AGM);
- It requires only three moves.

Moreover, the public key, signature, and communication of our scheme all consist of only a constant number of group/field elements.

Prior to our work, black-box, three-move pairing-free schemes were only known in the AGM. A recent line of work proposed and optimized schemes without the AGM, but they all require at least four moves.

# 1 Introduction

Blind signature schemes [\[Cha82\]](#page-25-0) are interactive protocols for joint signature generation between a signer holding the secret key and a user holding the message. The main security properties are onemore unforgeability and blindness. One-more unforgeability guarantees that a malicious user cannot produce more valid signatures than the number of completed signing sessions. Blindness ensures that the user's message is hidden from the signer. When the signer later sees the message-signature pair, it cannot link the pair to the corresponding invocation of the protocol. Blind signatures have found many applications, such as in e-cash [\[Cha82,](#page-25-0) [CFN90,](#page-25-1) [OO92\]](#page-27-0), anonymous credentials [\[Bra94,](#page-25-2) [CL01\]](#page-26-0), and e-voting [\[Cha88,](#page-25-3) [FOO92\]](#page-26-1).

Blind signatures in pairing-free groups. This work focuses on blind signatures in pairing-free groups. Such groups offer significant advantages in terms of computational efficiency and broad compatibility with existing cryptographic libraries.

Pairing-free blind signatures have been studied for over three decades. Early constructions, such as blind Okamoto-Schnorr signatures [\[Oka94\]](#page-27-1), have only been proved under limited concurrency [\[PS00,](#page-27-2) [HKL19\]](#page-26-2) in the random oracle model [\[BR93\]](#page-25-4). The blind Schnorr signature scheme [\[CP93\]](#page-26-3) also achieves only bounded concurrency, and its security proof [\[FPS20,](#page-26-4) [KLX22\]](#page-26-5) additionally assumes the algebraic group model (AGM) [\[FKL18\]](#page-26-6). Bounded concurrency is an inherent limitation, as efficient concurrent attacks on these schemes are known [\[BLL](#page-25-5)<sup>+</sup>21].

An exception is Abe's blind signature scheme [\[Abe01\]](#page-24-0), which is proved secure with unbounded concurrency in the AGM [\[KLX22\]](#page-26-5). Recent work [\[TZ22,](#page-27-3) [CKM](#page-25-6)<sup>+</sup>23] proposed improved constructions, also in the AGM. More recently, some proposals achieve non-AGM, unbounded-concurrency security [\[CATZ24,](#page-25-7) [KRW24,](#page-27-4) [KR25,](#page-27-5) [BHKR25,](#page-24-1) [RR25,](#page-27-6) [KLR25\]](#page-26-7).

We focus on schemes that make black-box use of groups and random oracles. With non-black-box techniques [Fis06], one can obtain round-optimal (i.e. two-move) pairing-free blind signatures. However, such constructions are significantly less efficient than black-box ones. Non-black-box use of hash functions also calls into question the security when the underlying scheme relies on the random oracle model.

Performance gap between AGM and non-AGM schemes. The most efficient AGM-based construction [CKM<sup>+</sup>23] has a signature/communication size of three/six group/field elements, and the protocol takes only three moves. According to a recent work by Dietz, Kastner, and Tessaro [DKT26], three moves is optimal for fully black-box pairing-free constructions. While slightly less efficient, the aforementioned series of non-AGM constructions has largely closed these gaps: the most efficient one [KRW24] has a signature size of six elements and constant communication. However, none of them has bridged the gap in the round complexity! Specifically, all the non-AGM constructions require at least four moves.

With preprocessing, a three-move blind signature scheme achieving unbounded concurrent security allows the signer to pre-generate and send the first message to the user. This reduces the online phase to a single round-trip (1-RTT). In contrast, four-move schemes require 2-RTT during their online execution. In practical applications such as e-cash, the round complexity of blind signatures directly dictates the user-perceived latency.

### 1.1 Contribution

We construct a blind signature scheme making black-box use of a pairing-free group and random oracles that, for the first time, achieves non-AGM, unbounded concurrent security with only three moves. Signatures, parameters, public keys, and communication all consist of a constant number of group/field elements. The security is based on the standard DDH assumption. ## 1.2 Our Techniques

Online-offline paradigm. At a high level, we apply the online-offline paradigm [EGM96] to reduce the round complexity of blind signature schemes. Forget about blindness and consider a

{2}------------------------------------------------

<span id="page-2-0"></span>four-move interactive signing protocol, where in the first move the user sends the message  $\mu$  to the signer in the clear. Our idea is to let the signer generate a public key pk of a one-time signature scheme, and initiate the four-move protocol to sign pk. Consequently, the first move of the protocol can be omitted. The actual message  $\mu$  is sent to the signer in the second move, and the signer generates a one-time signature  $\sigma_{\rm ot}$  on  $\mu$ . The final signature (pk,  $\sigma_{\rm ot}$ ,  $\sigma_{\rm is}$ ) consists of pk, the one-time signature  $\sigma_{\rm ot}$  on  $\mu$ , and the signature  $\sigma_{\rm is}$  on pk generated by the protocol.

**Blindness.** Things become much more complicated when considering blindness. First, pk should not be included in the signature in the clear, as it allows the signer to link the signature with its signing session. Next, if we do not include pk in the signature, verification of  $\sigma_{is}$  and  $\sigma_{ot}$  also becomes a problem.

As a solution, we base our three-move construction on a special four-move protocol, which we call an encrypted-message blind signature (EMBS) scheme. The protocol generates a signature that verifies on an encryption of the message. We let the user blind the one-time key pk and signature  $\sigma_{ot}$  into  $p\bar{k}$  and  $\bar{\sigma}_{ot}$ . Meanwhile, the EMBS protocol generates  $\sigma_{is}$  that verifies on an encryption Enc(pk) of the public key. Furthermore, the user generates a non-interactive proof of knowledge  $\pi$  of the blinding factors, connecting  $p\bar{k}$  and Enc(pk). The signature consists of  $(Enc(pk), \sigma_{is}, p\bar{k}, \bar{\sigma}_{ot}, \pi)$ .

For security, given a forgery in this form, the reduction either obtains a signature  $\sigma_{is}$  on a new encrypted message, or uses a previously seen encrypted pk. In the latter case, the reduction extracts the blinding factors from  $\pi$  and unblinds  $\bar{\sigma}_{ot}$  to break the one-time security.

Instantiation. We can instantiate the one-time signature scheme with the one-time version of Schnorr signatures [BS07]. For the EMBS scheme, we observe that a variant of the tightly secure blind signature scheme BHKR [BHKR25] suffices for our purpose. The key point is that BHKR is based on a structure-preserving signature and inherits this property to some extent. In particular, the protocol directly signs the original message, unlike other pairing-free constructions which sign hashed messages.

**Additional details.** For a constant signature size, we rely on rewinding to extract the proof of knowledge. Consequently, we actually use a two-time signature scheme, which supports two signing queries across the rewound executions.

We can achieve security under the one-more discrete logarithm assumption based on the two-time version of Schnorr signatures. To further eliminate the use of interactive assumptions, we actually use the two-time version of the Okamoto signature scheme [Oka93], which is secure under the discrete logarithm assumption.

### 1.3 Concurrent Work

Concurrent to our work, Chairattana-Apirom, Reichle, Tessaro [CART26] solve the same question: they also construct a fully black-box three-move pairing-free blind signature scheme under DDH without the AGM. They also achieve signature size and communication consisting of constant numbers of elements.

Their construction combines the Okamoto-Schnorr blind signature [Oka93] with an algebraic MAC. In our understanding, the underlying idea is similar to ours. The role of the Okamoto-Schnorr blind signature is conceptually (though not identically) similar to a partially one-time signature, where the nonce is used as the one-time key. They use an algebraic MAC to authenticate the one-time key, rather than an encrypted-message blind signature as in our construction.

Compared to our work, their construction achieves a more compact instantiation (37-element signatures and 31-element communication for computational blindness). They also achieve extensions such as statistical blindness, partial blindness, and strong unforgeability, while we focus only on the core question.

<span id="page-3-1"></span>We believe that the different technical path taken in our work has independent value and is meaningful for future work, for example for improving efficiency and instantiation based on different algebraic assumptions. Our construction idea stems from the online-offline paradigm of [EGM96] and uses two-time signatures as a building block. While their use of Okamoto-Schnorr blind signature gives a more efficient instantiation, our use of two-time signatures may provide insight for different instantiations.

## <span id="page-3-0"></span>2 Preliminaries

### Notations.

For any integer n, we use [n] to denote  $\{0, \ldots, n-1\}$  and  $[n]^+$  to denote  $\{1, \ldots, n\}$ . For a randomized algorithm  $A, y := A(x; \rho)$  denotes the output of A on input x with predetermined randomness  $\rho$ ;  $y \leftarrow A(x; \rho)$  denotes the same but with  $\rho$  freshly sampled.

We consider concrete security in this work. Cryptographic schemes are not parameterized by a security parameter. Throughout this work, we define all the schemes with respect to a fixed group  $\mathbb{G}$  with prime order p and generator G. The security parameter is implicitly used to choose a group at a sufficient security level.

Throughout this paper, we adopt additive notation for all group operations. For a group element  $G \in \mathbb{G}$  and a scalar  $x \in \mathbb{Z}_p$ , we denote scalar multiplication as xG, placing the scalar on the left. However, for a matrix  $\mathbf{M} \in \mathbb{G}^{m \times n}$  and a column vector  $\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{Z}_p^n$ , we write the product as  $\mathbf{M}\mathbf{v}$ , placing the scalar vector on the right.

**Assumptions.** We recall DDH and DL in Section A.1.

### Public-Key Encryption.

We will use the following extension of the ElGamal encryption scheme PKE = (KGen, Enc, Dec) over  $(\mathbb{G}, p, G)$  with message space  $\mathbb{G}^l$ . The scheme consists of the following algorithms:

- KGen()  $\to$  (ek, dk): The key generation algorithm KGen samples the decryption key dk :=  $(d_1, \ldots, d_l) \leftarrow \mathbb{Z}_p^l$  and computes the encryption key ek :=  $(1, d_1, \ldots, d_l) \cdot G \in \mathbb{G}^{l+1}$ .
- Enc(ek,  $\mathbf{M}; \rho$ )  $\to$  ct: Given the encryption key ek and a message  $\mathbf{M} \in \mathbb{G}^l$ , the encryption algorithm Enc samples  $\rho \leftarrow \mathbb{Z}_p$  and computes the ciphertext ct :=  $(O, \mathbf{M}) + \rho \cdot \mathsf{ek} \in \mathbb{G}^{l+1}$ .
- Dec(dk, ct)  $\to$  M: Given the decryption key dk and a ciphertext ct =  $(C_0, C_1, \dots, C_l)$ , the decryption algorithm Dec computes the plaintext as  $(C_1, \dots, C_l) dk \cdot C_0$ .

A public-key encryption scheme is rerandomizable if there exists an algorithm Rerand that, given the encryption key ek and a ciphertext ct, outputs a fresh-looking encryption ct' of the same message. The above scheme is rerandomizable: Rerand(ek, ct) samples  $r \leftarrow \mathbb{Z}_p$  and outputs ct' := ct +  $r \cdot$  ek.

The scheme is parameterized by the message length l. Different parts of the construction may use different values of l. To encrypt a shorter message  $\mathbf{M} \in \mathbb{G}^{l'}$  with  $l' \leq l$ , we use the first l' + 1 components of  $\mathsf{ek}$  and write  $\mathsf{Enc}(\mathsf{ek}, \mathbf{M}; \rho)$  by slight abuse of notation.

In the CPA security game  $\mathsf{CPA}^b$ , the adversary A is given an encryption key ek. Then A outputs two messages  $(\mathbf{M}_0, \mathbf{M}_1)$  and receives  $\mathsf{ct} \leftarrow \mathsf{Enc}(\mathsf{ek}, \mathbf{M}_b)$ . We say the scheme is  $(\tau, \varepsilon)$ -CPA secure if for any  $\tau$ -time A,  $|\mathsf{Pr}[\mathsf{CPA}^0(\mathsf{A}) = 1] - \mathsf{Pr}[\mathsf{CPA}^1(\mathsf{A}) = 1]| \leq \varepsilon$ . By the self-reduction of DDH, PKE is  $(\tau, \varepsilon_{\mathsf{ddh}})$ -CPA secure if DDH is  $(\tau', \varepsilon_{\mathsf{ddh}})$ -hard for  $\tau' \approx \tau$ .

We describe a  $\Sigma$ -protocol for  $\mathcal{R}_{lin}$  over  $(\mathbb{G}, p, G)$  in Section A.2.

## 2.1 Three-Move Blind Signatures

We define three-move blind signature schemes below. The signing protocol consists of four algorithms.  $\mathsf{Sign}_1$ ,  $\mathsf{User}_1$ ,  $\mathsf{Sign}_2$  generate the protocol messages for the three moves, respectively. The last algorithm  $\mathsf{User}_2$  generates the final signature.

**Definition 2** (Three-Move Blind Signature Schemes). A three-move blind signature scheme BS consists of the following algorithms:

- $\mathsf{Setup}() \to \mathsf{pp}$ : The setup algorithm  $\mathsf{Setup}$  outputs global parameters  $\mathsf{pp}$ . All algorithms related to  $\mathsf{BS}$  implicitly take  $\mathsf{pp}$  as input.
- $\mathsf{KGen}() \to (\mathsf{pk}, \mathsf{sk})$ : The key generation algorithm  $\mathsf{KGen}$  outputs a public key  $\mathsf{pk}$  and a secret key  $\mathsf{sk}$ .
- The interaction between the signer and the user consists of the following algorithms:
  - $\mathsf{Sign}_1(\mathsf{sk}) \to (\mathsf{sst}, \mathsf{smsg}_1)$ : On input a secret key  $\mathsf{sk}$ , the first-round signing algorithm  $\mathsf{Sign}_1$  outputs a signer state  $\mathsf{sst}$  and a first-round signer message  $\mathsf{smsg}_1$ .
  - $\mathsf{User}_1(\mathsf{pk}, \mu, \mathsf{smsg}_1) \to (\mathsf{ust}, \mathsf{umsg})$ : On inputs a public key  $\mathsf{pk}$ , a message  $\mu$ , and a first-round signer message  $\mathsf{smsg}_1$ , the first-round user algorithm  $\mathsf{User}_1$  outputs a user state  $\mathsf{ust}$  and a user message  $\mathsf{umsg}$ .
  - $Sign_2(sk, sst, umsg) \rightarrow smsg_2$ : On inputs a secret key sk, a signer state sst, and a user message umsg, the second-round signing algorithm  $Sign_2$  outputs a second-round signer message  $smsg_2$ .
  - $\mathsf{User}_2(\mathsf{pk}, \mu, \mathsf{ust}, \mathsf{smsg}_2) \to \sigma$ : On inputs a public key  $\mathsf{pk}$ , a message  $\mu$ , a user state  $\mathsf{ust}$ , and a second-round signer message  $\mathsf{smsg}_2$ , the second-round user algorithm  $\mathsf{User}_2$  outputs a signature  $\sigma$ .
- Vf(pk,  $\mu$ ,  $\sigma$ )  $\to$  0/1: On inputs a public key pk, a message  $\mu$ , and a signature  $\sigma$ , the deterministic verification algorithm Vf outputs 0 or 1.

We say BS is correct if for every  $pp \in Setup()$ , every  $(pk, sk) \in KGen()$ , and every message  $\mu$ , for signature  $\sigma$  returned by the following procedure, we have  $Pr[Vf(pk, \mu, \sigma) = 1] = 1$ .

# 3 Encrypted-Message Blind Signatures

In this section, we define four-move encrypted-message blind signature (EMBS) schemes as our main building block. An EMBS scheme is defined over a group ( $\mathbb{G}, p, G$ ) and associated with a PKE scheme PKE = (KGen, Enc, Dec) with message space  $\mathbb{G}^l$ . An encryption key ek is known to both the signer and the user. The signing protocol generates a signature that, instead of the message  $\mathbf{M}$ , verifies on an encryption of the message  $\mathbf{ct} := \mathsf{Enc}(\mathsf{ek}, \mathbf{M}; \rho)$ . We require that  $\rho$  is generated by the user together with the third-move protocol message. We also restrict that the first move is simply sending the message to the signer in the clear. Hence, we only need four algorithms to specify the protocol:  $\mathsf{Sign}_1$  and  $\mathsf{Sign}_2$  generate the signer's messages for the second and fourth moves, respectively;  $\mathsf{User}_1$  generates the user's message for the third move as well as the encryption randomness  $\rho$ ; and  $\mathsf{User}_2$ 

{6}------------------------------------------------

outputs the final signature. One can also view the protocol as three-move, with the message M publicly known by both parties in advance.

**Definition 5** (Four-Move Encrypted-Message Blind Signature Schemes). Let  $(\mathbb{G}, p, G)$  be a description of a prime-order cyclic group and PKE = (KGen, Enc, Dec) be a PKE scheme with message space  $\mathbb{G}^l$  and randomness space  $\mathcal{R}$ . A four-move encrypted-message blind signature scheme EMBS over  $(\mathbb{G}, p, G)$  associated with PKE consists of the following algorithms.

- $\mathsf{Setup}() \to \mathsf{pp}$ : The setup algorithm  $\mathsf{Setup}$  outputs global parameters  $\mathsf{pp}$ . All algorithms related to  $\mathsf{EMBS}$  implicitly take  $\mathsf{pp}$  as input.
- $\mathsf{KGen}() \to (\mathsf{pk}, \mathsf{sk})$ : The key generation algorithm  $\mathsf{KGen}$  outputs a public key  $\mathsf{pk}$  and a secret key  $\mathsf{sk}$ .
- The interaction between the signer and the user consists of the following algorithms:

Blindness of EMBS schemes is notably different from that for blind signature schemes, since the message  $\mathbf{M}$  is now known to the signer. We define encrypted-message blindness as a joint property of an EMBS scheme and the associated PKE scheme. To set up the security game, a PKE key pair (ek, dk) is generated, and ek is given to A. The two-stage adversary A first (possibly maliciously) generates a public key pk. Compared to the blindness for blind signature schemes, here A does not need to choose two messages in advance. Then A can open two arbitrarily interleaved signing sessions with the user oracles USER<sub>1</sub>, USER<sub>2</sub>, with a message  $\mathbf{M}$  as the input of USER<sub>1</sub>. When both sessions are completed, the adversary is given two ciphertext-signature pairs (Enc(ek,  $\mathbf{M}_0$ ;  $\rho_0$ ),  $\sigma_0$ ) and (Enc(ek,  $\mathbf{M}_1$ ;  $\rho_1$ ),  $\sigma_1$ ) that are swapped with probability 1/2, where the encryption randomness  $\rho_j$  and signature  $\sigma_j$  are generated by session j. The goal of A is to guess whether the two pairs are swapped.

**Definition 6** (Encrypted-Message Blindness). We say a four-move EMBS scheme EMBS is  $(\tau, \varepsilon)$ -encrypted-message blind if for every  $\tau$ -time algorithm A,  $\Pr[\mathsf{EMBLIND_{EMBS}}(\mathsf{A}) = 1] \leq 1/2 + \varepsilon$ , where the security game EMBLIND is defined in Fig. 3.

We define the unforgeability against two types of attacks, the distinct-message attack (DMA) and the extended-random-message attack (xRMA) [ACD+16]. The distinct-message attack allows A to choose the message M to sign, with each message signed at most once. In the random-message attack, the message M is instead uniformly chosen by oracle Sign<sub>1</sub>. The extended-random-message attack provides some extra information about M. Specifically, in this work we let the extra information be the discrete logarithms of M. It is easy to see that UF-DMA implies UF-xRMA under an additive loss of  $O(q_s^2)/p^l$ . UF-xRMA security is sufficient for our construction of three-move blind signature scheme, while we will give an instantiation of EMBS with UF-DMA security.

**Definition 7** (UF-DMA, UF-xRMA). We say a four-move EMBS scheme EMBS is  $(\tau, \varepsilon)$ -unforgeable under distinct-message (resp. extended-random-message) attack (UF-DMA (resp. Given a linear combination of the challenge elements  $\sum_{j \in [q_s]^+} (\beta_{R_j} R_j + \beta_{S_j} S_j + \beta_{X_j} X_j)$ , with the combination coefficients  $\{(\beta_{R_j}, \beta_{S_j}, \beta_{X_j})\}_{j \in [q_s]^+}$  (implicitly) as the inputs, PreImg returns  $\sum_{j \in [q_s]^+} (\beta_{R_j}(r_{1,j}, r_{2,j}) + \beta_{S_j}(s_{1,j}, s_{2,j}) + \beta_{X_j}(x_{1,j}, x_{2,j})$ . W randomly chooses the random coin tosses for A using its own coin tosses. It runs A and simulates  $G_1$  as follows:
- Setup: W sets the public parameter H to be its input of the same name. It executes the remaining part of the setup phase faithfully, namely generating the encryption key ek and the EMBS key pair (pk, sk). We recall that ek is generated with a known corresponding decryption key dk.
- Sign<sub>1</sub>: Upon the j-th Sign<sub>1</sub> query, W sets  $R_j, S_j, X_j$  to be its inputs of the same name. It executes EMBS.Sign<sub>1</sub>( $(R_j, S_j, X_j)$ , ek, sk) faithfully, to obtain (smsg'<sub>1</sub>, sst'). W returns (smsg'<sub>1</sub>,  $R_j, S_j, X_j$ ).
- SIGN<sub>2</sub>: On inputs  $(j, \mathsf{umsg})$ , W parses  $\mathsf{umsg}$  into  $(\mathsf{umsg}', b_j, c_j)$  and retrieves  $\mathsf{sst}'$  from the signer state of session j. W runs  $\mathsf{EMBS.Sign}_2((R_j, S_j, X_j), \mathsf{ek}, \mathsf{sk}, \mathsf{sst}', \mathsf{umsg}')$  faithfully to obtain  $\mathsf{smsg}_2'$ . It queries  $\mathsf{PREIMG}(R_j + b_j S_j + c_j X_j)$  to obtain  $(z_{1,j}, z_{2,j}) := (r_{1,j}, r_{2,j}) + b_j(s_{1,j}, s_{2,j}) + c_j(x_{1,j}, x_{2,j})$ . Finally, W returns  $(\mathsf{smsg}_2', z_{1,j}, z_{2,j})$ .
- $\mathsf{H}_{\pi}$ : During the game,  $\mathsf{W}$  answers each distinct  $\mathsf{H}_{\pi}$  query with  $h_1,\ldots,h_q$  in order.  $\mathsf{W}$  verifies whether  $\mathsf{A}$  wins  $\mathsf{G}_1$ , i.e.,  $\mathsf{A}$  returns k+1 valid signatures  $\{\sigma_i^*\}_{i\in[k+1]^+}$  on distinct  $\{\mu_i^*\}_{i\in[k+1]^+}$ . For  $i\in[k+1]^+$ , parse  $\sigma_i^*$  into  $(\sigma_i'^*,\mathbf{C}_i^*,\mathbf{D}_i^*,M_i^{\dagger*},\bar{R}_i^*,\bar{S}_i^*,\bar{X}_i^*,\pi_i^*,\bar{z}_{1,i}^*,\bar{z}_{2,i}^*)$ , and let  $(R_i^*,S_i^*,X_i^*)=\mathsf{Dec}(\mathsf{dk},\mathbf{C}_i^*)$ .  $\mathsf{W}$  also verifies whether Case 2 holds, i.e., for every  $i\in[k+1]^+$ , there exists  $j\in[q_s]^+$  such that session j is complete, and  $(R_i^*,S_i^*,X_i^*)=(R_j,S_j,X_j)$ .

If A wins and Case 2 holds, then we let W identify the special forgery  $\mu^*$ ,  $(\sigma'^*, \mathbf{C}^*, \mathbf{D}^*, M^{\dagger *}, \bar{R}^*, \bar{S}^*, \bar{X}^*, \pi^*, \bar{z}_1^*, \bar{z}_2^*)$ . It returns I such that  $h_I$  was assigned to the  $\mathsf{H}_{\pi}$  query corresponding to  $\pi^*$ , namely  $\mathsf{H}_{\pi}(\mathbf{C}^*, \mathbf{D}^*, M^{\dagger *}, \bar{R}^*, \bar{S}^*, \bar{X}^*, \mathsf{cm}^*)$  where  $(\mathsf{cm}^*, \mathsf{rs}^*) = \pi^*$ . W sets the auxiliary output (the Y in Lemma 8) to be the whole history of the game, including all variables used in the game and all interactions with A. If A does not win or Case 2 does not hold, then W returns I = 0.

We will apply the forking lemma (Lemma 8) to W', the combination of W and the PREIMG oracle considered as a single algorithm. Specifically, W' takes  $\{(r_{1,j},r_{2,j},s_{1,j},s_{2,j},x_{1,j},x_{2,j})\}_{j\in[q_s]^+}$  and  $h_1,\ldots,h_q$  as inputs. Then W' uses  $\{(r_{1,j},r_{2,j},s_{1,j},s_{2,j},x_{1,j},x_{2,j})\}_{j\in[q_s]^+}$  to compute the inputs  $\{(R_j,S_j,X_j)\}_{j\in[q_s]^+}$  for W. It runs W on inputs  $\{(R_j,S_j,X_j)\}_{j\in[q_s]^+}$  and  $h_1,\ldots,h_q$  and answers the PREIMG queries from W. At the end, W' forwards the output of W.

**Rewinding.** Now, we describe how our reduction B, who plays the AOMP[ $3q_s$ ] game, rewinds A. B uses W as a subroutine, which takes black-box access of A. It takes H and the challenge elements  $\{(R_j, S_j, X_j)\}_{j \in [q_s]^+}$  as inputs and has access to the PREIMG oracle. B runs fork as defined in Lemma 8 on inputs  $\{(r_{1,j}, r_{2,j}, s_{1,j}, s_{2,j}, x_{1,j}, x_{2,j})\}_{j \in [q_s]^+}$ , the secret preimages in the AOMP game which are unknown to B. Inside W', B implements the W-part by running W as a subroutine, while it performs the other part, i.e., computing inputs for W and answering PREIMG queries, using its own inputs and PREIMG oracle.

In the following, we describe the whole procedure how B runs fork<sup>W'</sup>. Under certain conditions, B does not have to complete the whole procedure to win the  $AOMP[3q_s]$  game.<sup>1</sup> We will come to this part later.

To be specific, B does the following:

- Forward Preims queries throughout the procedure: Recall that each Preims query is a linear combination of the challenge elements. We identify each query using the vector  $\vec{\beta}$  of the combination coefficients. Suppose that B has queried its own Preims oracle with  $\vec{\beta}_1, \ldots, \vec{\beta}_l$  and received the answers  $\mathbf{y}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{y}_l$ . When W makes a Preims query with vector  $\vec{\beta}$ , B checks if  $\vec{\beta}$  is in the span of  $\{\vec{\beta}_1, \ldots, \vec{\beta}_l\}$ . If it is, then B can express  $\vec{\beta}$  as a linear combination  $\sum_{i \in [l]^+} \theta_i \vec{\beta}_i$  and answers W's Preims query with  $\sum_{i \in [l]^+} \theta_i \mathbf{y}_i$ . If  $\vec{\beta}$  is not in the span, then B queries  $\vec{\beta}$  to its own Preims oracle and forwards the answer to W. In this way, B ensures that it only makes linearly independent queries to its Preims oracle.
- First execution of W: B runs W for the first time. B forwards its own inputs H,  $\{(R_j, S_j, X_j)\}_{j \in [q_s]^+}$  as the corresponding inputs of W with the same variable names. B uniformly samples the inputs  $h_1, \ldots, h_q \leftarrow \mathbb{Z}_p$  of W. In addition, B uniformly samples the random coin tosses for W. B answers W's Preims queries in the aforementioned way. At the end, if W returns I = 0, then B aborts. If  $I \geq 1$ , then B runs W for the second time.
- Second execution of W: B runs W for the second time. It still forwards its own inputs H,  $\{(R_j, S_j, X_j)\}_{j \in [q_s]^+}$  as the corresponding inputs of W with the same variable names. This time, B provides W with inputs  $h_1, \ldots, h_{I-1}, h'_I, \ldots, h'_q$ , where  $h_1, \ldots, h_{I-1}$  are the same as the first execution while  $h'_1, \ldots, h'_q \leftarrow \mathbb{Z}_p$  are resampled uniformly. It sets the random coin tosses to be the same as in the first time. Consequently, A (inside W) also uses the same coin tosses as in the first execution. B answers W's PREIMG queries in the aforementioned way. At the end, if W returns I' = I, and it holds that  $h'_I \neq h_I$ , then this rewinding procedure succeeds. Otherwise, B aborts. Note that B makes at most  $q_s$  queries to its PREIMG oracle for each execution of W. In total, B makes at most  $2q_s$  queries for this rewinding procedure.

The crucial query. We define the *crucial query* to be the first H query in the second execution of W satisfying the following conditions:

- It is in the form of  $\mathsf{H}(\mathbf{C}^*, \mathbf{D}^*, M^{\dagger *}, \bar{R}^*, \bar{S}^*, \bar{X}^*, \pi, \mu);$
- $\mathsf{NIV}^{\mathsf{H}_{\pi}(\mathbf{C}^*, \mathbf{D}^*, M^{\dagger *}, \bar{R}^*, \bar{S}^*, \bar{X}^*, \cdot)}(\mathsf{stm}, \pi) = 1$ , where  $\mathsf{stm} = \mathsf{FormatS}(\mathbf{C}^*, \mathbf{D}^*, M^{\dagger *}, \bar{R}^*, \bar{S}^*, \bar{X}^*)$ ; and
- $H_{\pi}(\mathbf{C}^*, \mathbf{D}^*, M^{\dagger *}, \bar{R}^*, \bar{S}^*, \bar{X}^*, \mathsf{cm})$  is assigned with  $h_I'$  (i.e., it is the *I*-th distinct  $H_{\pi}$  query, also the rewinding point), where we parse  $\pi$  into  $(\mathsf{cm}, \mathsf{rs})$ .

Here,  $(\mathbf{C}^*, \mathbf{D}^*, M^{\dagger *}, \bar{R}^*, \bar{S}^*, \bar{X}^*)$  and  $\pi^*$  are those from the special forgery in the first execution of W. Recall that when we modified the security game, we have made  $\mathsf{G}_1$  verify  $\pi$  for each H query.

**Solve AOMP.** We are ready to show how B wins the AOMP[ $3q_s$ ] game. B can solve for a sufficient solution  $\{(r_{1,j}^*, r_{2,j}^*, s_{1,j}^*, s_{2,j}^*, x_{1,j}^*, x_{2,j}^*)\}_{j \in [q_s]^+}$  if it can collect  $3q_s$  equations in the form of

$$y_1G + y_2H = \sum_{j \in [q_s]^+} (\beta_{R_j} R_j + \beta_{S_j} S_j + \beta_{X_j} X_j),$$

such that the coefficient vectors for the right-hand side of these equations are linearly independent. From each Preims query, B can obtain one such equation. In particular, to execute the rewinding procedure, each Preims query gives an equation in the form of

$$z_{1,j}G + z_{2,j}H = R_j + b_jS_j + c_jX_j,$$

where  $(\beta_{R_j}, \beta_{S_j}, \beta_{X_j}) \neq (0,0,0)$  only holds for one j. We only need to show that, at some point during the rewinding procedure, B can extract one equation that is linearly independent of those it has obtained from the PREIMG queries. Then B terminates the rewinding procedure and uses all its remaining PREIMG queries to obtain more independent equations. Eventually, it obtains one more equation than the number of its PREIMG queries, that is  $3q_s$  equations, and can solve for a solution.

Let  $j^*$  be the unique completed session such that  $Dec(dk, \mathbf{C}^*) = (R_{j^*}, S_{j^*}, X_{j^*})$ , i.e.,  $j^* = j(i^*)$  where  $i^*$  is the index of the special forgery. Depending on the moment when the crucial query occurs, there are two cases:

- Case (i): The crucial query occurs before session  $j^*$  is completed in the second execution of W.
- Case (ii): The crucial query occurs after session  $j^*$  is completed in the second execution of W.

Extract one more equation: Case (i). In Case (i), B runs the extraction once the crucial query occurs. It obtains  $\delta_{z_1}, \delta_{z_2}, \delta_s, \delta_x, \alpha_b, \alpha_c$  such that Eqs. (9) to (12) hold, where Eq. (12) states that  $\alpha_b \neq 0$  and  $\alpha_c \neq 0$ . B computes

$$b^* := \bar{b}^*/\alpha_b, \quad c^* := \bar{c}^*/\alpha_c, \quad z_1^* := \bar{z}_1^* - \delta_{z_1} - b^*\delta_s - c^*\delta_x, \quad z_2^* := \bar{z}_2^* - \delta_{z_2}, \tag{13}$$

where  $(\bar{b}^*, \bar{c}^*) = \mathsf{H}(\mathbf{C}^*, \mathbf{D}^*, M^{\dagger *}, \bar{R}^*, \bar{S}^*, \bar{X}^*, \pi^*, \mu^*)$  in the first execution of W. The validity of the special forgery in the first execution guarantees that

<span id="page-17-1"></span>
$$\bar{z}_1^* G + \bar{z}_2^* H = \bar{R}^* + \bar{b}^* \bar{S}^* + \bar{c}^* \bar{X}^*. \tag{14}$$

Combining Eqs. (9) to (11), (13) and (14), we have

$$z_1^*G + z_2^*H = R_{j^*} + b^*S_{j^*} + c^*X_{j^*}. (15)$$

This is the new equation that B extracts.

Since  $(\alpha_b G, \alpha_c G) = \text{Dec}(dk, \mathbf{D}^*)$  (implied by Eq. (6)), and by the definition of  $\mathsf{F}_{bc}$ , we have  $(b^*, c^*) = \mathsf{F}_{bc}(\mathbf{C}^*, \mathbf{D}^*, M^{\dagger *}, \bar{R}^*, \bar{S}^*, \bar{X}^*, \pi^*, \mu^*)$ . Recall that the special forgery is defined such that  $\mathsf{F}_{bc}(\mathbf{C}^*, \mathbf{D}^*, M^{\dagger *}, \bar{R}^*, \bar{S}^*, \bar{X}^*, \pi^*, \mu^*) \neq (b_{j^*}, c_{j^*})$ , and hence  $(b^*, c^*) \neq (b_{j^*}, c_{j^*})$ . As Case (i) holds, the only PREIMG query that B has made about  $(R_{j^*}, S_{j^*}, X_{j^*})$  is to complete session  $j^*$ , obtaining  $(z_{1,j^*}, z_{2,j^*})$  satisfying

Let  $\mu^{**}$ ,  $(\sigma'^{**}, \mathbf{C}^{**}, \mathbf{D}^{**}, M^{\dagger **}, \bar{R}^{**}, \bar{S}^{**}, \bar{X}^{**}, \pi^{**}, \bar{z}_1^{**}, \bar{z}_2^{**})$  be the special forgery in the second execution of W, where

As Case (ii) holds, B has completed session  $j^*$  in both executions of W. If  $(b_{j^*}, c_{j^*}) = (b'_{j^*}, c'_{j^*})$ , then B has only made one PREIMG query about  $(R_{j^*}, S_{j^*}, X_{j^*})$ . By the definition of special forgery (in the second execution), we have  $(b^{**}, c^{**}) = \mathsf{F}_{bc}(\mathbf{C}^{**}, \mathbf{D}^{**}, M^{\dagger **}, \bar{R}^{**}, \bar{S}^{**}, \bar{X}^{**}, \pi^{**}, \mu^{**}) \neq (b'_{j^*}, c'_{j^*})$ . We need to be careful about the case that  $(b_{j^*}, c_{j^*}) \neq (b'_{j^*}, c'_{j^*})$ . In this case, B has made two PREIMG queries about  $(R_{j^*}, S_{j^*}, X_{j^*})$  and obtained two linearly independent equations

<span id="page-18-3"></span>

We bound the probability that, conversely,  $(1, b^{**}, c^{**}) \in \mathsf{Span}\{(1, b_{j^*}, c_{j^*}), (1, b'_{j^*}, c'_{j^*})\}$ . Recall the following facts:

- $(b^{**}, c^{**}) = \mathsf{F}_{bc}(\mathbf{C}^{**}, \mathbf{D}^{**}, M^{\dagger **}, \bar{R}^{**}, \bar{S}^{**}, \bar{X}^{**}, \pi^{**}, \mu^{**}).$
- The value of  $\mathsf{F}_{bc}(\mathbf{C}^{**}, \mathbf{D}^{**}, M^{\dagger **}, \bar{R}^{**}, \bar{S}^{**}, \bar{X}^{**}, \pi^{**}, \mu^{**})$  is uniformly distributed over  $\mathbb{Z}_p^{*2}$  upon the query  $\mathsf{H}(\mathbf{C}^{**}, \mathbf{D}^{**}, M^{\dagger **}, \bar{R}^{**}, \bar{S}^{**}, \bar{X}^{**}, \pi^{**}, \mu^{**})$ .
- $\mathsf{H}(\mathbf{C}^{**},\mathbf{D}^{**},M^{\dagger **},\bar{R}^{**},\bar{S}^{**},\bar{X}^{**},\pi^{**},\mu^{**})$  is a crucial query.
- In Case (ii), the first crucial query occurs after  $(b'_{j^*}, c'_{j^*})$  has been fixed.

Since  $(b_{j^*}, c_{j^*}) \neq (b'_{j^*}, c'_{j^*})$ , the two vectors  $(1, b_{j^*}, c_{j^*})$  and  $(1, b'_{j^*}, c'_{j^*})$  span a 2-dimensional subspace of  $\mathbb{Z}_p^3$ . For a uniformly random  $(b^{**}, c^{**}) \in \mathbb{Z}_p^{*2}$ , the vector  $(1, b^{**}, c^{**})$  lies in this subspace with probability at most 1/(p-1). By the union bound over all possible crucial query positions, the probability that  $(1, b^{**}, c^{**}) \in \text{Span}\{(1, b_{j^*}, c_{j^*}), (1, b'_{j^*}, c'_{j^*})\}$  is at most q/(p-1). Thus, in Case (ii) B successfully obtains one more equation except with probability at most q/(p-1).

Let us put the previous bounds together. B wins the AOMP[ $3q_s$ ] game with probability at least  $\varepsilon_1(\varepsilon_1/q-1/p)-q/(p-1)$ . Recall that  $\varepsilon_1 \geq \varepsilon_{bs,2} - O(q^2)/p$ . Consequently, B wins with probability at least  $\varepsilon_{bs,2}^2/q - O(q)/p$ . Combining with Lemma 5 completes the proof.

## 5.1 Blindable $\Sigma$ -Protocols

We define blindable  $\Sigma$ -protocols following [KR25].

**Definition 8** (Blindable  $\Sigma$ -protocols). A blindable  $\Sigma$ -protocol for relation  $\mathcal{R} = \mathcal{X} \times \mathcal{W}$  with challenge set  $\mathcal{C}$ , randomness set  $\Omega$ , and blind randomness set  $\bar{\Omega}$  consists of the following algorithms:

{19}------------------------------------------------

- Com(stm, wit;  $\rho$ )  $\rightarrow$  cm: On inputs a statement stm and a witness wit, Com outputs a commitment cm.
- Resp(wit, ch,  $\rho$ )  $\rightarrow$  rs: On inputs a witness wit, a challenge ch, and randomness  $\rho$ , Resp outputs a response rs.
- Vf(stm, cm, ch, rs)  $\rightarrow 0/1$ : On inputs a statement stm, a commitment cm, a challenge ch, and a response rs, Vf outputs 0 or 1.
- BCom(stm, cm;  $\bar{\rho}$ )  $\rightarrow$  cm: On inputs a statement stm and a commitment cm, BCom outputs a commitment cm.
- BChal(ch,  $\bar{\rho}$ )  $\to$  ch: On inputs a challenge ch and blind randomness  $\bar{\rho}$ , BChal outputs a challenge ch.
- BChal<sup>-1</sup>( $\bar{ch}, \bar{\rho}$ )  $\rightarrow$  ch: On inputs a challenge  $\bar{ch}$  and blind randomness  $\bar{\rho}$ , BChal<sup>-1</sup> outputs a challenge ch.
- BResp(rs,  $\bar{\rho}$ )  $\to$  rs: On inputs a response rs and blind randomness  $\bar{\rho}$ , BResp outputs a response rs.

We require that (Com, Resp, Vf) is a (normal)  $\Sigma$ -protocol for  $\mathcal{R}$  with challenge set  $\mathcal{C}$  and randomness set  $\Omega$ , for which we require correctness, SHVZK, and special soundness. Moreover, we require a blindable  $\Sigma$ -protocol to be correct, blindable, and translatable, as defined below.

**Definition 9** (Correctness). A blindable  $\Sigma$ -protocol  $\Sigma$  is correct if the following holds:

- (Perfect) correctness: For every  $(\mathsf{stm}, \mathsf{wit}) \in \mathcal{R}, \, \rho \in \Omega, \, \text{and } \mathsf{ch} \in \mathcal{C}, \, \text{if we let } \mathsf{cm} := \mathsf{Com}(\mathsf{stm}, \mathsf{wit}; \rho), \, \mathsf{rs} := \mathsf{Resp}(\mathsf{wit}, \mathsf{ch}, \rho), \, \mathsf{then it must hold that } \mathsf{Vf}(\mathsf{stm}, \mathsf{cm}, \mathsf{ch}, \mathsf{rs}) = 1.$
- (Perfect) blind correctness: For every (stm, wit)  $\in \mathcal{R}$ , (cm, ch, rs) such that Vf(stm, cm, ch, rs) = 1, and  $\bar{\rho} \in \bar{\Omega}$ , if we let  $c\bar{m} := \mathsf{BCom}(\mathsf{stm}, \mathsf{cm}; \bar{\rho})$ ,  $c\bar{h} := \mathsf{BChal}(\mathsf{ch}, \bar{\rho})$ , and  $\bar{\mathsf{rs}} := \mathsf{BResp}(\mathsf{rs}, \bar{\rho})$ , then it must hold that Vf(stm,  $c\bar{m}$ ,  $c\bar{h}$ ,  $r\bar{\mathsf{s}}$ ) = 1.
- Bijectivity of BChal: For every  $\bar{\rho} \in \bar{\Omega}$ , BChal $(\cdot, \bar{\rho})$  is a permutation over  $\mathcal{C}$  with BChal $^{-1}(\cdot, \bar{\rho})$  as its inverse.

**Definition 10** ((Transcript) blindability). A blindable  $\Sigma$ -protocol  $\Sigma$  is (perfectly) blindable if for every (stm, wit)  $\in \mathcal{R}$ , (cm, ch, rs) such that Vf(stm, cm, ch, rs) = 1, the following two procedures have identically distributed outputs:

- Blind the given transcript: Sample  $\bar{\rho} \leftarrow \bar{\Omega}$ ; Compute  $\bar{\mathsf{cm}} := \mathsf{BCom}(\mathsf{stm}, \mathsf{cm}; \bar{\rho}), \bar{\mathsf{ch}} := \mathsf{BChal}(\mathsf{ch}, \bar{\rho}),$  and  $\bar{\mathsf{rs}} := \mathsf{BResp}(\mathsf{rs}, \bar{\rho})$ ; Output  $(\bar{\mathsf{cm}}, \bar{\mathsf{ch}}, \bar{\mathsf{rs}})$ .

## <span id="page-21-1"></span><span id="page-21-0"></span>5.3 Dual-Mode Commitment Schemes

We will use the following dual-mode commitment scheme CM over  $(\mathbb{G}, p, G)$  with message space  $\mathbb{G}^l$ . CM (implicitly) takes a common reference string  $\operatorname{crs} \in \mathbb{G}^{(l+1)\times(l+1)}$  as an input. Given a message  $\mathbf{M} \in \mathbb{G}^l$  and randomness  $\vec{\eta} \leftarrow \mathbb{Z}_p^{l+1}$ , CM.Com $(\mathbf{M}; \vec{\eta})$  outputs the commitment as  $\operatorname{cm} := \operatorname{crs} \cdot \vec{\eta} + \binom{O}{\mathbf{M}} \in \mathbb{G}^{l+1}$ . The scheme is parameterized by the message length l. Different parts of the construction may use different values of l and correspondingly sized  $\operatorname{crs}$ .

CM has the following properties:

- Hiding mode: If crs is rank-(l+1), then CM is in the hiding mode. In the hiding mode, for any message  $\mathbf{M}$ , CM.Com( $\mathbf{M}$ ) is uniformly distributed over  $\mathbb{G}^{l+1}$  (with  $\vec{\eta}$  uniformly chosen from  $\mathbb{Z}_p^{l+1}$ ).
- Binding (extractable) mode: If crs is rank-1, then for any cm  $\in \mathbb{G}^{l+1}$ , there exists a unique message  $\mathbf{M}$  such that cm is a possible commitment to  $\mathbf{M}$ . Moreover, given  $\mathsf{td} = (d_1, \ldots, d_l)$  such that

$$\mathsf{crs} = \left(\begin{array}{c} \mathbf{A} \ d_1 \mathbf{A} \ \vdots \ d_l \mathbf{A} \end{array}\right)$$

for a row vector  $\mathbf{A} \in \mathbb{G}^{l+1}$ , the committed message can be efficiently extracted from cm.

• Distributions of crs: A uniformly random rank-(l+1) crs (hiding mode) is of statistical distance O(1/p) from the uniform distribution over  $\mathbb{G}^{(l+1)\times(l+1)}$ .

  5: \mathsf{wit}^\dagger := \mathsf{FormatW}^\dagger(\mathsf{sk}, \{\zeta_i\}_{i \in [3]})
  6: \pi \leftarrow \mathsf{NIP}^{\mathsf{H}_{\pi}(\mathsf{pk}, \{\mathsf{ct}_i\}_{i \in [3]}, \cdot)}(\mathsf{stm}^{\dagger}, \mathsf{wit}^{\dagger})

### • $\mathcal{S}_{\mathsf{ct}_0}$ OR $\mathcal{S}_{\mathsf{dh}}$ :

-  $S_{\text{ct}_0}$  states that  $\text{ct}_0$  encrypts  $\mathbf{X} + w\mathbf{M}$ , where  $\mathbf{X}$  is the message committed in cmx, and wG is the message committed in cmw. Formally, it states the existence of  $\vec{\eta}_x$ ,  $\zeta_0$ ,  $\vec{\eta}_w$ , w such that

$$\mathsf{ek}_{\mathsf{RE},0} \cdot \zeta_0 - \mathsf{crs} \cdot \vec{\eta}_x = \mathsf{ct}_0 - \mathsf{cmx} - w\mathbf{C}_0$$

and

$$\operatorname{crs}' \cdot \vec{\eta}_w = \operatorname{cmw} - (O, wG).$$

-  $S_{dh}$  states that (G, H, U, V) is a DH-tuple. Formally, it states the existence of u such that (U, V) = u(G, H).

## • $\mathcal{S}_{\mathsf{ct}_2}$ OR $\mathcal{S}_{=}$ :

-  $\mathcal{S}_{\mathsf{ct}_2}$  states that  $\mathsf{ct}_2$  encrypts the message committed in cmy. Formally, it states the existence

{23}------------------------------------------------

of  $\vec{\eta}_y$ ,  $\zeta_2$  such that

$$\mathsf{ek}_{\mathsf{RE},2} \cdot \zeta_2 - \mathsf{crs}' \cdot \vec{\eta}_y = \mathsf{ct}_2 - \mathsf{cmy}.$$

-  $S_{=}$  states that  $\mathsf{ct}_0$  and  $\mathsf{ct}_1$  encrypt the same message. Formally, it states the existence of  $\zeta_0, \zeta_1$  such that

$$\operatorname{\mathsf{ek}}_{\mathsf{RF}\,0}\cdot\zeta_0-\operatorname{\mathsf{ek}}_{\mathsf{RF}\,1}\cdot\zeta_1=\operatorname{\mathsf{ct}}_0-\operatorname{\mathsf{ct}}_1.$$

The signer uses the witness for  $S_{ct_0}$  and  $S_{=}$  to perform  $\Sigma$ . We assume helper algorithms FormatS and FormatW that compute and format the statement and witness into the form of the  $\mathcal{R}_{cr,l}$  relation. We omit their specific descriptions. The signer sends  $\{ct_i\}_{i\in[3]}$  and  $cm_{\Sigma}$ .

For the third move, the user samples  $\rho \leftarrow \mathbb{Z}_p$  and generates  $\mathbf{C} := \mathsf{Enc}(\mathsf{ek}, \mathbf{M}; \rho)$ . It rerandomizes  $\{\mathsf{ct}_i\}_{i \in [3]}$  to  $\{\mathsf{ct}_i'\}_{i \in [3]}$  using rerandomization factors  $r_0, r_1, r_2 \leftarrow \mathbb{Z}_p$ . The user translates the statement stm set up by the signer to the statement stm', where  $\mathbf{C}_0$  and  $\{\mathsf{ct}_i'\}_{i \in [3]}$  are replaced with  $\mathbf{C}$  and  $\{\mathsf{ct}_i'\}_{i \in [3]}$ . We assume a helper algorithm FormatV that generates the value v satisfying the syntax of TStm and TResp from  $\rho, r_0, r_1, r_2$ .

Under the original statement stm, the user blinds  $\mathsf{cm}_{\Sigma}$  into  $\mathsf{cm}_{\Sigma}$  using BCom with randomness  $\bar{\rho}$ . It computes  $\mathsf{ch} := \mathsf{H}(\mathsf{pk}, \mathbf{C}, \{\mathsf{ct}_i'\}_{i \in [3]}, \mathsf{cm}_{\Sigma})$ . Then the user unblinds the challenge using BChal<sup>-1</sup> to get  $\mathsf{ch}$ . It sends  $\mathsf{ch}$  and also fixes the encryption randomness  $\rho$ .

For the fourth move, the signer responds to ch with rs. Then it generates a non-interactive proof  $\pi$  using NIP which proves that  $\mathsf{ct}_0$ ,  $\mathsf{ct}_1$ ,  $\mathsf{ct}_2$  encrypt the message committed in cmx, the message committed in cmx, and O, respectively. Formally, it proves the existence of  $\vec{\eta}_x$ ,  $\zeta_0$ ,  $\zeta_1$ ,  $\zeta_2$  such that

$$\begin{split} \mathsf{ek}_{\mathsf{RE},0} \cdot \zeta_0 - \mathsf{crs} \cdot \vec{\eta}_x &= \mathsf{ct}_0 - \mathsf{cmx}, \\ \mathsf{ek}_{\mathsf{RE},1} \cdot \zeta_1 - \mathsf{crs} \cdot \vec{\eta}_x &= \mathsf{ct}_1 - \mathsf{cmx}, \\ \mathsf{ek}_{\mathsf{RE},2} \cdot \zeta_2 &= \mathsf{ct}_2. \end{split}$$

We assume helper algorithms FormatS<sup>†</sup> and FormatW<sup>†</sup> for this statement. The signer sends  $(rs, \pi)$ . Finally, the user verifies the non-interactive proof. If it verifies, then the user blinds rs into rs under stm, and then translates into rs under stm. The signature consists of  $(\{ct_i'\}_{i\in[3]}, cm_{\Sigma}, rs')$ .

**Verification.** To verify the signature, one computes  $c\bar{h} := H(pk, C, \{ct'_i\}_{i \in [3]}, c\bar{m}_{\Sigma})$  and runs  $\Sigma.Vf(stm', c\bar{m}_{\Sigma}, c\bar{h}, r\bar{s}')$ . The correctness follows from the properties of  $\Sigma$ .

### Optimization and Efficiency.

It is well-known that for  $\mathcal{R}_{\text{lin}}$  and  $\mathcal{R}_{\text{or},l}$ , we can let the non-interactive proof and the EMBS signature contain (ch, rs) instead of (cm, rs). Also, we can let cmy,  $\vec{\eta}_y$ , and ct<sub>2</sub> consist of two elements. With this optimization, the signature size and communication of EMBS are  $10\mathbb{G} + 17\mathbb{Z}_p$  and  $24\mathbb{G} + 25\mathbb{Z}_p$ , respectively.

Also note that, for both our EMBS and BS[EMBS], the public parameters are uniformly distributed, so they can be generated by a hash function without any trusted setup.

## <span id="page-24-5"></span>6.3 UF-DMA

Theorem 7 (UF-DMA). If DDH is  $(\tau', \varepsilon_{\text{ddh}})$ -hard over  $(\mathbb{G}, p, G)$  then EMBS is  $(\tau, \varepsilon_{\text{embs}})$ -UF-DMA, where essentially  $\tau' \approx \tau$ ,  $q = O(q_s + q_h)$ , and

$$\varepsilon_{\text{embs}} \le O(q_s \log q_s)(\varepsilon_{\text{ddh}} + \frac{q}{p}) + \frac{O(q^2)}{p}.$$

We defer the proof to Section D. The proof closely follows [BHKR25]. We sketch the main idea here. For simplicity, assume that A always returns  $\mathbf{C}^*$  that encrypts  $\mathbf{M}^* \notin \{\mathbf{M}_j\}_{j \in [q_s]^+}$ . We introduce an additional forgery check: we require  $\mathsf{pt}_0^* \in \mathcal{P}$ , where  $\mathsf{pt}_0^* = \mathsf{RE.Dec}(\mathsf{dk}_{\mathsf{RE},0},\mathsf{ct}_0^*)$ , and  $\mathcal{P}$  is defined as  $\{\mathbf{X}\}_{j \in [q_s]^+} = \{\mathbf{x}G\}$ . This does not decrease the winning probability much.

We also consider a "dual game". In the original game, we have w := 0. In the dual game, we set w := 1. The signing oracle generates  $\mathsf{ct}_0$ ,  $\mathsf{ct}_1$  as encryptions of  $\mathbf{X} + \mathbf{M}$  instead of  $\mathbf{X}$ . The definition of  $\mathcal{P}$  is modified to  $\{\mathbf{X} + \mathbf{M}_j\}_{j \in [q_s]^+}$ .

We note that in the dual game, forgery is unconditionally hard. Since  $\mathcal{S}_{dh}$  is false, A has to make  $\mathcal{S}_{ct_0}$  true, i.e., let  $\mathsf{pt}_0^* = \mathbf{X} + \mathbf{M}^*$ . The forgery check  $\mathsf{pt}_0^* \in \mathcal{P}$  requires  $\mathbf{M}^* \in \{\mathbf{M}_j\}_{j \in [q_s]^+}$ , which contradicts the assumption  $\mathbf{M}^* \notin \{\mathbf{M}_j\}_{j \in [q_s]^+}$ .

The heaviest part of the proof is to show that the winning conditions of the original game and the dual game are close. The key step is to change what  $ct_0$  and  $ct_1$  encrypt in each session. The proof follows the adaptive partitioning technique [Hof17, AHN<sup>+</sup>17, BHKR25], which performs a partitioning loop. In each step, we break the statement  $S_{=}$  for half of the sessions while still requiring it on the forgery. The double key structure (encrypting the same message under  $ek_{RE,0}$  and  $ek_{RE,1}$ ) allows us to change the plaintext  $pt_0$ ,  $pt_1$  one by one while still being able to perform the forgery check about  $\mathcal{P}$ . After sufficiently many steps, the plaintext used in each session becomes independently random. The same game hops also apply to the dual game, so we can relate their winning probabilities.

Definition 13 (Decisional Diffie-Hellman DDH). We say that DDH is (τ, ε)-hard over group (G, p, G) if for every τ -time algorithm A,

$$|\Pr[\mathsf{A}(H,aG,aH)=1:H\leftarrow\mathbb{G},a\leftarrow\mathbb{Z}_p]-\Pr[\mathsf{A}(H,aG,bH)=1:H\leftarrow\mathbb{G},a,b\leftarrow\mathbb{Z}_p]|\leq\varepsilon.$$

Definition 14 (Discrete logarithm DL). We say that DL is (τ, ε)-hard over group (G, p, G) if for every τ -time algorithm A,

$$\Pr[\mathsf{A}(aG) = a : a \leftarrow \mathbb{Z}_p] \le \varepsilon.$$

We also present the SHVZK simulator Sim and the special-soundness extractor Ext. It is straightforward to prove that the protocol satisfies correctness, SHVZK, and special soundness. We also describe the non-interactive prover and verifier algorithms NIP, NIV defined with respect to a hash function H. They are obtained by applying the Fiat-Shamir transformation [FS87] to the  $\Sigma$ -protocol (in fact, we just describe the generic transformation).

- G<sub>2</sub>: Program fresh H values in User<sub>1</sub>. Since  $\mathsf{RE.Dec}(\mathsf{dk}_{\mathsf{RE},2},\mathsf{ct}^*_2) \neq \beta G = yG$  (due to the change in  $\mathsf{G}_{8.k.3}$ ), and now CM is in the binding mode, the statement  $\mathcal{S}_{\mathsf{ct}_2}$  is false for the forgery. Thus, by the soundness of  $\Sigma$ , the statement  $\mathcal{S}_{=}$  needs to be true except with probability  $q_h/p$ . Consequently, we have  $\mathsf{pt}^*_0 = \mathsf{pt}^*_1$ , and changing the forgery check does not change the result. In conclusion, we have  $\varepsilon_{8.k.7} \geq \varepsilon_{8.k.6} - q_h/p$ .

 $\mathsf{G}_{8.k.8}$ : Generate crs in hiding mode. This game samples crs as a uniformly rank-4 matrix, which switches CM to the hiding mode. We have  $\varepsilon_{8.k.8} \ge \varepsilon_{8.k.7} - 3\varepsilon_{\mathrm{ddh}} - O(1)/p$ .

 $G_{8.k.9}$ : Change  $\mathbf{Z}_{k[1],j}$  if  $\beta \neq j[k+1]$ . In this game, Sign<sub>1</sub> sets  $\mathbf{Z}_{k[1],j}$  by the following:

$$\mathbf{Z}_{k[1],j} := \begin{cases} \mathsf{F}_k(j|_k), & \beta = j[k+1] \\ \mathsf{F}'_k(j|_k), & \beta \neq j[k+1] \end{cases}, \quad j \in \mathcal{J}.$$

Namely, the game changes  $\mathbf{Z}_{k[1],j}$  for  $j \in \mathcal{J}$  with  $\beta \neq j[k+1]$ , using  $\mathsf{F}'_k$  rather than  $\mathsf{F}_k$ .

Note that by the change in  $G_{8.k.4}$ ,  $\beta \neq j[k+1]$  means that the signing oracles use the witness of statement  $\mathcal{S}_{\mathsf{ct}_2}$ , so they can perform correctly with  $\mathcal{S}_{=}$  being false. Also note that the forgery check about  $\mathcal{P}$  is on  $\mathsf{pt}^*_{\overline{k[1]}} = \mathsf{Dec}(\mathsf{dk}_{\mathsf{RE},\overline{k[1]}},\mathsf{ct}^*_{\overline{k[1]}})$  (by  $\mathsf{G}_{8.k.7}$ ), and  $\mathsf{dk}_{\mathsf{RE},k[1]}$  is never referenced. Therefore, by the mCPA security of RE, we have  $\varepsilon_{8.k.9} \geq \varepsilon_{8.k.8} - 3\varepsilon_{\mathsf{ddh}}$ .

 $\mathsf{G}_{8.k.10}$ : Use witness for  $\mathcal{S}_{=}$  if  $\beta \neq j[k+1]$ . By  $\mathsf{G}_{8.k.5}$ ,  $\mathsf{G}_{8.k.9}$ , both  $\mathsf{Z}_{0,j}$  and  $\mathsf{Z}_{1,j}$  are set to be  $\mathsf{F}'_k(j|_k)$  if  $\beta \neq j[k+1], j \in \mathcal{J}$ , so the statement  $\mathcal{S}_{=}$  becomes true again. We let the game use the witness for  $\mathcal{S}_{=}$  to perform  $\Sigma$ . By the perfect SHVZK, we have  $\varepsilon_{8.k.10} = \varepsilon_{8.k.9}$ .

 $G_{8.k.11}$ : Rewrite  $Z_{0,j}$ ,  $Z_{1,j}$  as  $F_{k+1}(j|_{k+1})$ . Note that for  $j \in \mathcal{J}$ ,  $Z_{0,j}$  and  $Z_{1,j}$  are set to be

$$\begin{cases} \mathsf{F}_k(j|_k), & j[k+1] = \beta \\ \mathsf{F}'_k(j|_k), & j[k+1] = \bar{\beta} \end{cases}$$

This can be rewritten as  $\mathsf{F}_{k+1}(j|_{k+1})$ , where  $\mathsf{F}_{k+1}$  is a random function on (k+1)-bit inputs taking values in  $\mathbb{G}^3$ . We have  $\varepsilon_{8.k.11} = \varepsilon_{8.k.10}$ .

 $G_{8.k.12}$ : Change the definition of  $\mathcal{P}$ . In the preceding game,  $\mathcal{P}$  is defined as

$$\mathcal{P} = \{\mathsf{F}_{k}(j|_{k}) + w\mathsf{M}_{j}\}_{j \in \mathcal{J}}$$

$$= \underbrace{\{\mathsf{F}_{k}(j|_{k}) + w\mathsf{M}_{j} \mid j \in \mathcal{J}, j[k+1] = \beta\}}_{=:\mathcal{S}_{\mathrm{both}}} \cup \underbrace{\{\mathsf{F}_{k}(j|_{k}) + w\mathsf{M}_{j} \mid j \in \mathcal{J}, j[k+1] = \bar{\beta}]\}}_{=:\mathcal{S}_{k.11}}$$

In this game,  $\mathcal{P}$  is defined as

$$\mathcal{P} = \{ \mathsf{F}_{k+1}(j|_{k+1}) + w\mathsf{M}_{j} \}_{j \in \mathcal{J}}$$

$$= \underbrace{\{ \mathsf{F}_{k}(j|_{k}) + w\mathsf{M}_{j} \mid j \in \mathcal{J}, j[k+1] = \beta \}}_{=\mathcal{S}_{\text{both}}} \cup \underbrace{\{ \mathsf{F}'_{k}(j|_{k}) + w\mathsf{M}_{j} \mid j \in \mathcal{J}, j[k+1] = \bar{\beta} ] \}}_{=:\mathcal{S}_{k.12}}.$$

We define an intermediate game  $G_{8.k.11'}$  where  $\mathcal{P}$  is defined to be  $\mathcal{S}_{\text{both}}$ .

 $\mathsf{G}_{8.k.11'}$  differs from the preceding game only if A returns a forgery with  $\mathsf{pt}^*_{\overline{k}[1]} \in \mathcal{S}_{k.11} \setminus \mathcal{S}_{\mathrm{both}}$ , which the preceding game accepts while  $\mathsf{G}'$  does not. Observe that

$$S_{k.11} \setminus S_{both} = \{ \mathsf{F}_k(j|_k) + w\mathsf{M}_j \mid j \in \mathcal{J}, \ j[k+1] = \bar{\beta}, \quad \forall j' \in \mathcal{J} : j'|_{k+1} \neq (\beta, j|_k) \}$$
$$= \{ \mathsf{F}_k(s) + w\mathsf{M}_j \mid (\bar{\beta}, s) \in \mathcal{J}_{k+1}, (\beta, s) \notin \mathcal{J}_{k+1} \},$$

where  $\mathcal{J}_{k+1}$  is the set of k+1-bit prefixes of all strings in  $\mathcal{J}$ . Note that for any k-bit s,  $\mathsf{F}_k(s)$  is only referenced in session  $j \in \mathcal{J}$  such that the (k+1)-bit prefix of j is  $(\beta, s)$ . Thus, the  $\mathsf{F}_k$  values in  $\mathcal{S}_{k,11} \setminus \mathcal{S}_{\text{both}}$  are never referenced and perfectly hidden from  $\mathsf{A}$ . We thus have  $\varepsilon_{8,k,11'} \geq \varepsilon_{8,k,11} - q_s/p$ .  $\mathsf{G}_{8,k,12}$  accepts more values of  $\mathsf{pt}^*_{\overline{k}[1]}$  than  $\mathsf{G}_{8,k,11'}$ , so  $\varepsilon_{8,k,12} \geq \varepsilon_{8,k,11'}$ . Therefore, we have  $\varepsilon_{8,k,12} \geq \varepsilon_{8,k,11} - q_s/p$ .

 $G_{8.k.13}$ : **Do not abort if** RE.Dec(dk<sub>RE,2</sub>, ct<sub>2</sub>\*)  $\neq \beta G$ . In this game, the aborting condition RE.Dec(dk<sub>RE,2</sub>, ct<sub>2</sub>\*)  $\neq \beta G$  is removed. Thanks to  $G_{8.k.10}$ , the signing oracles use witness for  $S_{=}$  no matter  $j[k+1] = \beta$  or not. Thanks to  $G_{8.k.11}$ , the assignment of  $\mathbf{Z}_{0,j}$  and  $\mathbf{Z}_{1,j}$  does not need to check whether  $j[k+1] = \beta$ . Therefore, the game does not reference  $\beta$  except setting y. Since CM is in the hiding mode,  $\beta$  is perfectly hidden. Therefore, we have  $\varepsilon_{8.k.13} = 2\varepsilon_{8.k.12}$ .

 $G_{8.k.14}$ : Set  $z_{2,j} := 0$ . This game sets  $z_{2,j} := 0$  for every  $j \in \mathcal{J}$ . Since the signing oracles use the witness for  $\mathcal{S}_{=}$ , the randomness for  $\mathsf{ct}_{2,j}$  is not referenced. By the mCPA security of RE, we have  $\varepsilon_{8.k.14} \ge \varepsilon_{8.k.13} - 3\varepsilon_{\mathrm{ddh}}$ .

 $\mathsf{G}_{8.k.15}$ : **Do not sample**  $\beta$ . This game does not sample  $\beta$  and sets y := 0. Since CM is in the hiding mode, we have  $\varepsilon_{8.k.15} = \varepsilon_{8.k.14}$ .

**End of the partitioning loop.** After the partitioning loop, we have  $\mathbf{Z}_{0,j} = \mathbf{Z}_{1,j} = \mathsf{F}(j)$  for  $j \in \mathcal{J}$  for a random function  $\mathsf{F}$  taking values in  $\mathbb{G}^3$ .  $\mathcal{P}$  is defined as  $\{\mathsf{F}(j) + w\mathsf{M}_j\}_{j\in\mathcal{J}}$ . The forgery check about  $\mathcal{P}$  is on  $\mathsf{pt}_0^*$ .

The intermediate game. Now, we have arrived at the intermediate game  $G_I$ . In session  $j^*$ ,  $\mathsf{ct}_{0,j^*}$  and  $\mathsf{ct}_{1,j^*}$  encrypt O. We have  $\mathbf{Z}_{0,j} = \mathbf{Z}_{1,j} = \mathsf{F}(j)$  for  $j \in \mathcal{J}$  for a random function  $\mathsf{F}$ .  $\mathcal{P}$  is defined as  $\{\mathsf{F}(j)\}_{j\in\mathcal{J}}$ . The forgery check about  $\mathcal{P}$  is on  $\mathsf{pt}_0^*$ . Equivalently, in this game,  $\mathsf{ct}_{0,j}$  and  $\mathsf{ct}_{1,j}$  encrypt a uniformly random plaintext  $\mathsf{pt}_j$  for each  $j \in \mathcal{J}$ , and  $\mathcal{P}$  is defined as  $\{\mathsf{pt}_j\}_{j\in\mathcal{J}}$ .

The same sequence of game hops can be applied to  $\mathsf{G}_5^*$ . In session  $j^*$ ,  $\mathsf{ct}_{0,j^*}$  and  $\mathsf{ct}_{1,j^*}$  encrypt O. That will end up with a game where  $\mathbf{Z}_{0,j} = \mathbf{Z}_{1,j} = \mathsf{F}(j)$  for  $j \in \mathcal{J}$  for a random function  $\mathsf{F}$ .  $\mathcal{P}$  is defined as  $\{\mathsf{F}(j) + \mathbf{M}_j\}_{j \in \mathcal{J}}$ . Note that  $\mathsf{ct}_{0,j}$  and  $\mathsf{ct}_{1,j}$  also encrypt a uniformly random plaintext  $\mathsf{pt}_j$  in this game, and  $\mathcal{P}$  also equals  $\{\mathsf{pt}_j\}_{j \in \mathcal{J}}$ . We have bridged  $\mathsf{G}_5$  and  $\mathsf{G}_5^*$  using this intermediate game. Putting everything together, we have

$$\varepsilon_5 - \varepsilon_5^* \le O(\log q_s)(\varepsilon_{\rm ddh} + q/p).$$
