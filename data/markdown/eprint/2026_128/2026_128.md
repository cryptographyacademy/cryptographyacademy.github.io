## The Impossibility of Post-Quantum Public Indifferentiability for Merkle-Damgård

#### Akinori Hosoyamada

NTT Social Informatics Laboratories, Tokyo, Japan NTT Research Center for Theoretical Quantum Information, Atsugi, Japan akinori.hosoyamada@ntt.com

Abstract. The Merkle-Damgård construction (in its strengthened form used as in SHA-2) is not classically indifferentiable from a Variable-Input-Length (VIL) random oracle because of the length-extension attack. Nevertheless, Dodis, Ristenpart, and Shrimpton showed that Merkle-Damgård is publicly indifferentiable, a weaker notion that still justifies replacing a VIL random oracle by Merkle-Damgård in many security proofs when all inputs to a random oracle are public (e.g., Fiat-Shamir and full-domain-hash signatures).

In this paper, we show that this replacement fails in the post-quantum setting: (Strengthened) Merkle-Damgård is not publicly indifferentiable from a VIL random oracle against quantum distinguishers with superposition access to the underlying primitive (while construction queries remain classical), even if the compression function is ideally random. We first formalize post-quantum public indifferentiability so that the corresponding composition theorem extends to the quantum random oracle model. We also introduce a post-quantum version of sequential indifferentiability, an even weaker notion. We then prove that (strengthened) Merkle-Damgård satisfies neither notion by showing that an explicit quantum distinguisher achieves non-negligible advantage against any efficient simulator, using Zhandry's compressed-oracle technique. We thus obtain an explicit, conjecture-free separation between an indifferentiabilitystyle notion in the classical setting and its post-quantum analogue.

Keywords: symmetric cryptography · Merkle-Damgård · post-quantum security · public indifferentiability · sequential indifferentiability.

## 1 Introduction

Security proofs for cryptosystems often rely on idealized models such as the Random Oracle Model (ROM) [2]. In practice, however, random oracles are instantiated by concrete hash functions with nontrivial internal structure—for example, the Merkle-Damgård construction [10, 25] underlying SHA-2 [27]. It is non-trivial whether ROM proofs remain sound once the random oracle is replaced with a structured construction. Re-proving security for each cryptosystem under each specific hash structure is costly, so a systematic justification is desirable.

{1}------------------------------------------------

This issue can be addressed using the notion of indifferentiability, which was introduced by Maurer et al [24]. If a construction is indifferentiable from a random oracle, then the composition theorem [24, 29] allows it to safely replace the random oracle in security proofs, as long as the security notion can be defined via a single-stage game. For instance, the sponge construction [4, 5] is indifferentiable from a Variable-Input-Length (VIL) random oracle, which justifies the use of SHA-3 [28] in many cryptosystems proven secure in the ROM.

By contrast, the Merkle-Damgård construction most widely used in practice namely, the strengthened variant that pads a message by simply appending a length-encoding at its end, as in SHA-2—is not indifferentiable from a VIL random oracle due to the length-extension attack [9]. It can be made indifferentiable if some modifications are made (e.g., prefix-free encoding is used) or input lengths are fixed [9]. However, widely deployed standards such as SHA-2 and RIPEMD-160 (standardized by ISO/IEC [18] and used in Bitcoin [26]) are not designed or specified with such modifications in mind and are typically used as-is. Moreover, many applications hash variable-length inputs—for example, in signature schemes.

Nevertheless, many cryptosystems appear to remain secure in practice even when the (strengthened) Merkle-Damgård is used in place of a VIL random oracle. To bridge this gap, Dodis et al. [12] introduced a weaker notion called *public indifferentiability*. Roughly speaking, when a cryptosystem only hashes public values, public indifferentiability suffices (via an appropriate composition theorem) to preserve many security proofs in the ROM. Prominent examples of such cryptosystems include Fiat-Shamir signatures [15] and Full Domain Hash (FDH) signatures [2]. Dodis et al. proved that the Merkle-Damgård construction satisfies public indifferentiability, thereby ensuring the security of these schemes even when a VIL random oracle is instantiated by Merkle-Damgård.

(Post-)Quantum Security. Nowadays, post-quantum security is crucial in the analysis of practical cryptographic systems; hash functions are no exception. In the Quantum Random Oracle Model (QROM) [6], an adversary is a quantum algorithm that may query the random oracle in superposition. When analyzing a structured hash construction built from a smaller primitive (e.g., the compression function in Merkle-Damgård or the permutation in sponge), it is natural to model that adversaries have oracle access to that primitive. In the post-quantum setting, we allow these primitive queries to be in quantum superposition.

In this model, the sponge construction has been proven indifferentiable against quantum adversaries [1]. Merkle-Damgård is also quantum indifferentiable from a random oracle *if a modification is made so a prefix-free encoding is used, or input lengths are fixed* [34, 1]. However, since the most practically common, strengthened Merkle-Damgård (which is used in various standards [18, 27] including SHA-2 and typically deployed without such modifications) is not indifferentiable from a VIL random oracle classically, it cannot be quantum indifferentiable either.

While public indifferentiability is known to hold for the (strengthened) Merkle-Damgård classically, whether it extends to the (post-)quantum setting has re

mained open. This question is important not only theoretically but also practically. For example, many papers (e.g., [6, 33, 30, 19, 14, 20, 13, 31, 16, 21]) prove the post-quantum security of Fiat-Shamir or FDH signatures in the (VIL) QROM. If Merkle-Damgård were post-quantum public indifferentiable from a VIL random oracle, then a broader set of practical candidates (including SHA-2) could instantiate quantum random oracles in those schemes, while automatically preserving the soundness of the security proofs.

Motivated by this, we investigate the following question:

*Does the (strengthened) Merkle-Damgård construction satisfy public indifferentiability in the (post-)quantum setting?*

#### 1.1 Our Contributions

We answer this question in the negative: the (strengthened) Merkle-Damgård is *not* post-quantum publicly indifferentiable from a variable-input-length (VIL) random oracle, even when the compression function is an ideal random function. The details are as follows.

#### Formalizing Post-Quantum Public and Sequential Indifferentiability.

Although public indifferentiability is well understood classically, we are not aware of any prior work that formalizes a post-quantum analogue that considers quantum adversaries with superposition oracle access. Thus, we first formalize postquantum public indifferentiability in a setting where a distinguisher *D* may make quantum superposition queries to the underlying primitive (and hence simulators are fully quantum), while *D* makes only classical queries to the construction oracle. This formulation preserves the composition theorem for typical QROM proofs for classical cryptosystems, which assume honest parties are classical. Consequently, any hash construction satisfying our notion can soundly instantiate a quantum random oracle in many post-quantum security proofs when all hashed inputs are public (e.g., those for Fiat-Shamir and FDH signatures).

We also formalize a post-quantum analogue of sequential indifferentiability [23], which is a notion even weaker than public indifferentiability, by naturally restricting the class of distinguishers for post-quantum public indifferentiability.

Impossibility for (Strengthened) Merkle-Damgård to Achieve Post-Quantum Public/Sequential Indifferentiability. We then prove that the (strengthened) Merkle-Damgård construction does not satisfy post-quantum public indifferentiability nor post-quantum sequential indifferentiability, even if the underlying compression function is ideally random. More precisely, we explicitly construct an efficient distinguisher *D* that breaks the post-quantum sequential indifferentiability, and thereby post-quantum public indifferentiability, of the (strengthened) Merkle-Damgård.

The distinguisher *D* runs one of two subroutines, *D*<sup>0</sup> and *D*1, at random. Roughly, *D*<sup>0</sup> succeeds if a simulator *S* "records" (i.e., makes non-negligible quantum correlation with) the query input by *D*<sup>0</sup> to *S* in superposition during the

Implications. Our attack does not immediately break real-world schemes that instantiate random oracles via the (strengthened) Merkle-Damgård. Such schemes may remain secure under tailored analyses. What fails is the generic substitution guarantee afforded by classical public indifferentiability: Even when only public values are hashed, the (strengthened) Merkle-Damgård cannot be treated unconditionally as a VIL random oracle in the post-quantum setting. Accordingly, instantiating a quantum random oracle with Merkle-Damgård calls for extra care (e.g., use the prefix-free padding) and/or scheme-specific proofs.

#### 1.2 Related Works

Quantum indifferentiability has been studied in a number of works. Carstens et al. [8] argued that, under the conjecture that quantum indifferentiability requires classical indifferentiability with stateless simulators, the sponge and Feistel constructions are not quantum indifferentiable. They further showed that this conjecture holds for *perfect* quantum indifferentiability (i.e., zero distinguishing advantage) assuming an information-theoretic conjecture. Subsequently, however, the sponge construction was proven quantum indifferentiable [1]. Zhandry [35] showed the existence of a construction which is built from an ideal cipher and quantum indifferentiable from an arbitrary-size random oracle, and that generic groups are quantum (reset) indifferentiable from ideal ciphers. In later work, Zhandry [36] discussed indifferentiability from a random unitary operator and showed that the pseudo-random unitary construction of [22] is not indifferentiable. Carolan et al. proved that one-round sponge is (quantum) indifferentiable even if precomputation is allowed for adversaries [7]. Benedikt and Fischlin investigated, mainly from an attack perspective, how the order of hashing different types of inputs (e.g., public keys and messages) affects the post-quantum security of Fiat-Shamir schemes when using the Merkle-Damgård or sponge construction [3]. In [3], they also mentioned that it is unknown whether the public indifferentiability of Merkle-Damgård carries over to the (post-)quantum setting.

## 2 Preliminaries

For a bit string x, its length is denoted by |x|. The empty string has length 0. For an integer  $\ell \geq 1$ ,  $(\{0,1\}^{\ell})^+$  denotes the set of bit strings whose length is a positive multiple of  $\ell$ , and  $\{0,1\}^{\leq \ell}$  denotes the set of bit strings of length at most  $\ell$ . We identify  $\{0,1\}^m \times \{0,1\}^n$  with  $\{0,1\}^{m+n}$  via concatenation:  $(w,z) \in \{0,1\}^m \times \{0,1\}^n$  corresponds to  $w||z \in \{0,1\}^{m+n}$ . We also view  $\{0,1\}^n$  as an n-dimensional vector space over  $\mathbb{F}_2$ , where addition is bitwise XOR.

We write  $y \leftarrow \mathcal{A}(x)$  for the event that a (classical or quantum) algorithm  $\mathcal{A}$ , on input x, outputs the classical bit string y. A classical or quantum algorithm is efficient if it runs in time polynomial in the security parameter. For a vector  $|\phi\rangle$  in a Hilbert space (resp., a linear operator L on a Hilbert space),  $||\phi\rangle||$  (resp.,  $||L||_{\text{op}}$ ) denotes its Euclidean norm (resp., operator norm). If  $||\phi\rangle - |\psi\rangle|| \leq \varepsilon$  (resp.,  $||L - L'||_{\text{op}} \leq \varepsilon$ ), then we say that  $|\phi\rangle$  is  $\varepsilon$ -close to  $|\psi\rangle$  (resp., L is  $\varepsilon$ -close to L'), and we write  $|\phi\rangle \stackrel{\varepsilon}{\approx} |\psi\rangle$  (resp.,  $L \stackrel{\varepsilon}{\approx} L'$ ).

For a fixed-input-length (FIL) function  $g:\{0,1\}^m \to \{0,1\}^n$ , we model its quantum oracle as the unitary  $O_g$  defined by

$$O_g |x\rangle_X |y\rangle_Y = |x\rangle_X |y \oplus g(x)\rangle_Y,$$

where X and Y are an m-qubit register and an n-qubit register, respectively. The quantum oracle of a VIL function  $f:\{0,1\}^{\leq \ell} \to \{0,1\}^n$  is defined analogously, assuming that register X has sufficiently many qubits and that the input length is implicitly encoded in x.

To simplify notation, we occasionally permute tensor factors without further comment. For example, a state  $|\psi\rangle = \sum_{x,y,z} \alpha_{xyz} |x\rangle_X |y\rangle_Y |z\rangle_Z$  may also be written as  $\sum_{x,y,z} \alpha_{xyz} |y\rangle_Y |x\rangle_X |z\rangle_Z$  or  $\sum_{x,y,z} \alpha_{xyz} |z\rangle_Z |y\rangle_Y |x\rangle_X$ . Similarly, when the identity operator on the remaining registers is clear from context, we may write a linear operator L in place of  $L \otimes I$  or  $I \otimes L$ .

We sometimes denote the state defined in Eq. (i) by  $|(i)\rangle$ . This shorthand is used to simplify long derivations and surrounding text. If Eq. (i) has the form  $|\psi\rangle \stackrel{\varepsilon}{\approx} \cdots \stackrel{\varepsilon'}{\approx} |\phi\rangle$  (or  $|\psi\rangle = \cdots = |\phi\rangle$ ), then  $|(i)\rangle$  refers to the explicit ket vector  $|\phi\rangle$  appearing in that equation (typically the rightmost ket expression in that equation). We use this shorthand only when the intended vector expression is unambiguous.

#### 2.1 Compressed Oracle Technique

Recording quantum queries to an oracle is generally difficult due to the nocloning theorem. For quantum random oracles, however, Zhandry's compressedoracle technique [34] partially overcomes this difficulty.

Let RO be a (VIL) random oracle with domain  $\mathcal{X} := \{0,1\}^{\leq \ell}$  and range  $\{0,1\}^n$ . The quantum random oracle is primarily modeled by (1) sampling a function  $f: \mathcal{X} \to \{0,1\}^n$  uniformly at random and then (2) providing oracle access to the unitary  $O_f: |x\rangle_X |y\rangle_Y \mapsto |x\rangle_X |y \oplus f(x)\rangle_Y$ . As oracles perfectly indistinguishable from this, the *standard oracle* and the *compressed oracle* are defined.

**Standard Oracle.** The standard oracle is obtained by purifying the randomness of the choice of f into a uniform superposition. Specifically:

- 1. The oracle sets its initial state as  $|\operatorname{Init}_{\mathsf{StO}}\rangle_F := \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}} \sum_{f \in \mathsf{Funcs}} |f\rangle_F$ , where Funcs is the set of all functions from  $\mathcal{X}$  to  $\{0,1\}^n$ ,  $N := |\mathsf{Funcs}|$ , and F is a register to store the truth table of  $f \in \mathsf{Funcs}$ .
- 2. Queries are responded by applying the unitary operator  $\mathsf{StO}: |x\rangle_X |y\rangle_Y |f\rangle_F \mapsto |x\rangle_X |y\oplus f(x)\rangle_Y |f\rangle_F.$

The standard oracle is perfectly indistinguishable from the original quantum random oracle.

Fix an arbitrary ordering of the elements of  $\mathcal{X}$ , and denote the *i*-th element by  $x^{(i)}$ . We represent the truth table of f as the  $n|\mathcal{X}|$ -bit string  $f(x^{(0)})||f(x^{(1)})|| \cdots ||f(x^{(|\mathcal{X}|-1)})|$  and view register F as consisting of subregisters F[x] that store f(x) for  $x \in \mathcal{X}$ . In particular,  $|f\rangle_F = \bigotimes_{x \in \mathcal{X}} |f(x)\rangle_{F[x]}$ .

**Database.** A core concept in the compressed oracle technique is the *database*. Consider partial functions  $f: \mathcal{X} \to \{0,1\}^n \cup \{\bot\}$ , where  $f(x) = \bot$  indicates that the value at x is undefined. The truth table of such a partial function can be identified with the set of pairs  $\{(x, f(x)) : x \in \mathcal{X}, f(x) \neq \bot\}$ .

Conversely, any set  $D := \{(x_1, y_1), \dots, (x_t, y_t)\} \subset \mathcal{X} \times \{0, 1\}^n$  with distinct  $x_i$ 's can be identified with the partial function  $f_D$  such that  $f_D(x_i) = y_i$  for  $i = 1, \dots, t$  and  $f_D(x) = \bot$  for  $x \in \mathcal{X} \setminus \{x_1, \dots, x_t\}$ . We call such a set D a database. The empty database is denoted by  $\varnothing$ .

We use partial functions and databases interchangeably: for a database D, we also write  $D(x_i) = y_i$  and  $D(x) = \bot$  for  $x \notin \{x_1, \ldots, x_t\}$ . For any database D and any  $(x,y) \in \mathcal{X} \times \{0,1\}^n$ , let  $D[x \mapsto y]$  be the database such that  $D[x \mapsto y](x) = y$  and  $D[x \mapsto y](x') = D(x')$  for  $x' \neq x$ . Similarly,  $D[x_1 \mapsto y_1, x_2 \mapsto y_2]$  denotes the database that assigns  $x_i \mapsto y_i$  for  $i \in \{1,2\}$  and agrees with D elsewhere.

**Compressed Oracle.** From now on, we extend the register F so that it can store databases (i.e., partial functions) as well as fully defined functions. Equivalently, for each x, the Hilbert space of F[x] is spanned by  $\{|y\rangle:y\in\{0,1\}^n\cup\{\bot\}\}$ . We also extend StO so that StO  $|x\rangle_X\,|y\rangle_Y\,|D\rangle_F=|x\rangle_X\,|y\rangle_Y\,|D\rangle_F$  if  $D(x)=\bot$ .

For  $y \in \{0,1\}^n$ , let  $|\hat{y}\rangle := \sum_{w \in \{0,1\}^n} \frac{(-1)^{w \cdot y}}{2^{n/2}} |w\rangle$ , where  $w \cdot y$  denotes the dot product over  $\mathbb{F}_2$ . Define the unitary  $\mathsf{Comp}_{F[x]}$  by  $\mathsf{Comp}_{F[x]} := |\bot\rangle \langle \hat{0}|_{F[x]} + |\hat{0}\rangle \langle \bot|_{F[x]} + \sum_{y \in \{0,1\}^n \setminus \{0^n\}} |\hat{y}\rangle \langle \hat{y}|_{F[x]}$ , and (with a slight abuse of notation) set  $\mathsf{Comp}_F := \bigotimes_{x \in \mathcal{X}} \mathsf{Comp}_{F[x]}$ . Note that  $\mathsf{Comp}_{F[x]}^{\dagger} = \mathsf{Comp}_{F[x]}$  and hence  $\mathsf{Comp}_F^{\dagger} = \mathsf{Comp}_F$ .

**Definition 1 (Compressed Oracle).** The compressed oracle initializes register F in the state  $|\varnothing\rangle_F$  and answers quantum queries by applying the unitary

$$cO_{XYF} := Comp_F \cdot StO_{XYF} \cdot Comp_F. \tag{4}$$

This oracle is perfectly indistinguishable from the standard oracle (and thus from the original quantum random oracle), since  $\mathsf{Comp}_F \, |\varnothing\rangle_F = |\mathsf{Init}_{\mathsf{StO}}\rangle_F$  and  $\mathsf{Comp}_F$  acts only on the oracle register F.

Let  $L_{XF} := \sum_{x \in \mathcal{X}} |x\rangle \langle x|_X \otimes \mathsf{Comp}_{F[x]}$ . As shown in [1], we also have

$$cO_{XYF} = L_{XF} \cdot StO_{XYF} \cdot L_{XF}. \tag{5}$$

The following lemma is an immediate consequence of (5).

**Lemma 1.** For any x, y and any database D, every database that appears in the superposition  $cO(|x\rangle_X |y\rangle_Y |D\rangle_F$  has size at most |D|+1. In particular, after an adversary makes q queries to the compressed oracle, the oracle register F is supported on databases of size at most q.

The following lemma follows by a straightforward calculation from (5).

**Lemma 2.** If  $D(x) = \bot$ , then  $\operatorname{cO} |x\rangle_X |y\rangle_Y |D\rangle_F$  is equal to

$$\begin{split} \sum_{\alpha \in \{0,1\}^n} \frac{1}{2^{n/2}} \, |x\rangle_X \, |y \oplus \alpha\rangle_Y \, |D[x \mapsto \alpha]\rangle_F + \frac{1}{2^{n/2}} \, |x\rangle_X \, |\hat{0}\rangle_Y \, |D\rangle_F \\ - \sum_{\beta \in \{0,1\}^n} \frac{1}{2^n} \, |x\rangle_X \, |\hat{0}\rangle_Y \, |D[x \mapsto \beta]\rangle_F \, . \end{split}$$

#### 3 Classical Indifferentiability Notions

This section reviews three indifferentiability-style notions: (original) indifferentiability [24], public indifferentiability [12], and sequential indifferentiability [23]. We also summarize their relationships in Section 3.4.

In what follows, prim denotes an idealized primitive (e.g., an ideally random compression function for a Merkle-Damgård hash, or a random permutation for a sponge function), and C[prim] denotes a construction built from prim (e.g., a Merkle-Damgård or sponge hash). Moreover, R denotes an ideally random object that has the same input/output interface as C[prim] (e.g., a VIL random oracle).

{8}------------------------------------------------

#### 3.1 Indifferentiability

Indifferentiability of C[prim] from R is defined via indistinguishability between a real world and an ideal world equipped with a simulator. In the real world, a distinguisher  $\mathcal{D}$  is given oracle access to (C[prim], prim). In the ideal world,  $\mathcal{D}$  is given oracle access to  $(R, \mathcal{S}[R])$ , where the simulator  $\mathcal{S}$  has oracle access to R. Intuitively, by querying R,  $\mathcal{S}$  tries to mimic the behavior of prim consistently with R. The construction C[prim] is said to be indifferentiable from R if there exists an efficient  $\mathcal{S}$  such that the distinguishing advantage is negligible for any efficient  $\mathcal{D}$ . The formal definition is as follows.

**Definition 2 (Indifferentiability).** The construction C[prim] is indifferentiable from R if there exists an efficient simulator S such that, for any efficient distinguisher D, the indifferentiability advantage

$$\mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{indiff}}_{\mathsf{R},\mathsf{C}[\mathsf{prim}],\mathcal{S}}\left(\mathcal{D}\right) := \left| \Pr \left[ 1 \leftarrow \mathcal{D} \big[ \mathsf{C}[\mathsf{prim}], \mathsf{prim} \big] \right] - \Pr \left[ 1 \leftarrow \mathcal{D} \big[ \mathsf{R}, \mathcal{S}[\mathsf{R}] \big] \right] \right| \quad (6)$$

is negligibly small.

We call the oracle of C[prim] in the real world (resp., R in the ideal world) the construction oracle, and the oracle of prim (resp., the oracle interface of S[R] given to D) the primitive oracle. Accordingly, we refer to queries to C[prim] or R as construction queries, and those to prim or S[R] as primitive queries.

Composition Theorem. Informally, indifferentiability means that C[prim] behaves as random as R, so that R can be safely replaced with C[prim] in various cryptosystems. That is, if a cryptosystem  $\Pi[R]$  is secure (in the model where an ideally random R is available to all parties) and C[prim] is indifferentiable from R, then  $\Pi[C[prim]]$  is also secure (in the model where an ideally random prim is available to all parties).

More precisely, suppose that the security of  $\Pi[R]$  is defined by a single-stage game G. Here, G interacts with R and a single adversarial algorithm  $\mathcal{B}$  (which is given oracle access to R), and finally returns a bit b (denoted as  $b \leftarrow G[R, \mathcal{B}[R]]$ ). $$\Pr\left[1 \leftarrow G\left[\mathsf{C}[\mathsf{prim}], \mathcal{A}[\mathsf{prim}]\right]\right] \leq \Pr\left[1 \leftarrow G\left[\mathsf{R}, \mathcal{B}[\mathsf{R}]\right]\right] + \mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{indiff}}_{\mathsf{R}, \mathsf{C}[\mathsf{prim}], \mathcal{S}}\left(\mathcal{D}\right), \quad (7)$$

where  $\mathcal{D}$  is efficient if  $\mathcal{A}$  is (resp.,  $\mathcal{B}$  is efficient if  $\mathcal{A}$  and  $\mathcal{S}$  are), and  $\mathcal{D}$  does not depend on  $\mathcal{S}$ .

At first, the theorem was believed to be true for any cryptosystem [24], but later found true only if G is single-stage [29].

A notable example of indifferentiable constructions is the sponge hash [5], where prim is a public invertible random permutation and R is a (variable input/output length) random oracle. If the security of a cryptosystem  $\Pi[R]$  is defined with a single-stage game and proven in the ROM (where R is a VIL random oracle), then it remains secure even if R is replaced with the sponge hash (in the model that the underlying public permutation is ideally random).

Meanwhile, the (strengthened) Merkle-Damgård construction is not indifferentiable from a VIL random oracle because of the length extension attack [9]. In fact, if some modifications are allowed (e.g., by using a prefix-free padding instead of Eq. (2)) or input lengths are fixed [9], Merkle-Damgård can also be made indifferentiable. However, various standards including SHA-2 [27] and RIPEMD-160 [18] are not specified with such modifications, and inputs to hash functions are variable-length in many applications including some signatures.

#### 3.2 Public Indifferentiability

In public indifferentiability, the key difference from original indifferentiability is that the simulator is additionally allowed to see the entire set of query-response pairs for the ideal construction oracle R. More specifically, we assume that the simulator can access not only R but also a "reveal" oracle  $\mathsf{rvl}_\mathsf{R}$ , which takes no input and returns the set of all queries made so far to R together with their responses. The formal definition is as follows.

**Definition 3 (Public indifferentiability).** The construction C[prim] is public indifferentiable from R if there exists an efficient simulator S such that, for any efficient distinguisher D, the public indifferentiability advantage

$$\mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{pub-indiff}}_{\mathsf{R},\mathsf{C}[\mathsf{prim}],\mathcal{S}}\left(\mathcal{D}\right) := \left| \Pr\left[ 1 \leftarrow \mathcal{D}\big[\mathsf{C}[\mathsf{prim}],\mathsf{prim}\big] \right] - \Pr\left[ 1 \leftarrow \mathcal{D}\big[\mathsf{R},\mathcal{S}[\mathsf{R},\mathsf{rvl}_\mathsf{R}]\big] \right] \right|$$

is negligibly small. Here,  $\mathsf{rvl}_\mathsf{R}$  is the oracle that takes no input and returns the set of the queries to  $\mathsf{R}$  made so far by  $\mathcal D$  or  $\mathcal S$  (together with the corresponding responses).

Dodis et al. proved that Merkle-Damgård is publicly indifferentiable from a VIL random oracle [12] when prim is an ideally random compression function<sup>4</sup>.

Composition Theorem. A composition theorem analogous to Theorem 1 also holds for public indifferentiability. Suppose that the security of a cryptosystem  $\Pi[R]$  is defined by a single-stage game G as before, and assume additionally that the set of query-response pairs for R is visible to adversaries (i.e., the reveal oracle  $\mathsf{rvl}_R$  is available). If  $\Pi[R]$  is secure (in the model that the oracles of an ideally random R and  $\mathsf{rvl}_R$  are available to all the parties) and  $\mathsf{C}[\mathsf{prim}]$  is public indifferentiable from R, then  $\Pi[\mathsf{C}[\mathsf{prim}]]$  is also secure (in the model that the oracle of an ideally random  $\mathsf{prim}$  is available to all the parties). #### 3.3 Sequential Indifferentiability

Sequential indifferentiability [23] is defined in the same way as the usual indifferentiability, except that the distinguisher  $\mathcal{D}$  is restricted to make construction queries only after it finishes all primitive queries. Equivalently, once  $\mathcal{D}$  makes a construction query, it may no longer make primitive queries.

**Definition 4 (Sequential Indifferentiability).** The construction C[prim] is sequentially indifferentiable from R if there exists an efficient simulator S such that, for any efficient distinguisher D that makes construction queries only after finishing primitive queries, the sequential indifferentiability advantage<sup>5</sup>

$$\mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{seq\text{-}indiff}}_{\mathsf{R},\mathsf{C}[\mathsf{prim}],\mathcal{S}}\left(\mathcal{D}\right) := \left| \Pr \left[ 1 \leftarrow \mathcal{D} \big[ \mathsf{C}[\mathsf{prim}], \mathsf{prim} \big] \right] - \Pr \left[ 1 \leftarrow \mathcal{D} \big[ \mathsf{R}, \mathcal{S}[\mathsf{R}] \big] \right] \right| \quad (8)$$

is negligibly small.

#### 3.4 Relationships among the Notions

The following sequence of implications immediately follows from the definitions:

Indifferentiability *⇒* Public indifferentiability *⇒* Sequential indifferentiability*.*

Moreover, Mandal et al. [23] showed that sequential indifferentiability implies public indifferentiability (and thus the two notions are equivalent) if the construction oracles C[prim] and R are stateless and deterministic<sup>6</sup> .

On the Order of Quantifiers. In this paper, indifferentiability-related notions are defined in their strong form: A construction is considered secure only if there exists a universal simulator *S* such that the distinguishing advantage is small for any distinguisher *D* (i.e., *∃ S ∀ D*).

Meanwhile, one can also consider a weaker variant that allows the simulator to depend on the distinguisher. That is, a construction is defined as secure if, for every *D*, there exists a suitable simulator *S<sup>D</sup>* (and *S<sup>D</sup>* may change depending on *D*), corresponding to the quantifier order *∀ D ∃ SD*.

The original definition by Maurer et al. [24] follows the weaker quantifier order (i.e., the latter version). We adopt the stronger one to remain consistent with the works that introduced public indifferentiability [12] and sequential indifferentiability [23]. Sometimes, whether a universal simulator exists really affects the (im)possibility of achieving a given indifferentiability-related notion [35]. However, all the results of this paper hold regardless of the order of the quantifiers for *S* and *D* (i.e., regardless of whether *∃ S ∀ D* or *∀ D ∃ SD*).

## 4 Post-Quantum Public/Sequential Indifferentiability

A fully quantum analogue of (original) indifferentiability is conceptually simple: one lets all parties be quantum and allows superposition queries to all oracles. Such notions have been studied extensively in [8, 35, 7, 36, 1].

In contrast, defining a quantum analogue of public indifferentiability is not quite straightforward. Classically, the reveal oracle exposes the set of queries made so far to R together with their responses. However, recording quantum queries is, in general, highly non-trivial due to the no-cloning theorem. While Zhandry's compressed-oracle technique (or related tools) may help in specific settings, formalizing a fully quantum reveal interface would require sophisticated additional machinery.

#### 4.1 Post-Quantum Public Indifferentiability

Trying to formalize a post-quantum analogue of public indifferentiability like above, we naturally arrive at the following observations<sup>7</sup>.

- Distinguishers must be allowed superposition queries to the primitive oracle (prim in the real world, or a simulator in the ideal world). To enable the simulator to effectively simulate the quantum oracle for prim, the simulator should also be allowed to make superposition queries to R.
- Distinguishers' queries to the construction oracle (R or C[prim]) can be kept classical: In composition theorems, such construction queries correspond to oracle calls to R made by the security game (e.g., queries to a random oracle made in a signing oracle), which are classical under our assumptions.

The next issue to consider is how to define the reveal oracle in the post-quantum setting: In the classical definition, the reveal oracle returns the full set of construction queries together with the corresponding responses. In our setting, since the simulator may perform quantum queries, how to efficiently record its own queries is unclear. Meanwhile, the distinguisher's construction queries are classical and can be recorded straightforwardly. We therefore let the reveal oracle expose only the set of the distinguisher's construction queries and the corresponding responses. #### 4.2 Post-Quantum Sequential Indifferentiability

By restricting the distinguisher's query order in post-quantum public indifferentiability, we obtain a post-quantum analogue of sequential indifferentiability. Specifically, we consider the following setup:

- As in the classical notion, the distinguisher queries the construction oracle only after finishing all primitive queries<sup>9</sup>,
- primitive queries may be in quantum superposition, whereas construction queries remain classical, and
- the simulator may make quantum superposition queries to the ideally random construction oracle.

**Definition 6 (Post-Quantum Sequential indifferentiability).** The construction C[prim] is post-quantum sequentially indifferentiable from R if there exists an efficient simulator S such that, for any efficient distinguisher D that makes construction queries only after finishing primitive queries, the advantage

$$\mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{pqseq\text{-}indiff}}_{\mathsf{R},\mathsf{C}[\mathsf{prim}],\mathcal{S}}(\mathcal{D}) := \left| \Pr\left[ 1 \leftarrow \mathcal{D} \big[ \mathsf{C}[\mathsf{prim}], \mathsf{prim} \big] \right] - \Pr\left[ 1 \leftarrow \mathcal{D} \big[ \mathsf{R},\mathcal{S}[\mathsf{R}] \big] \right] \right|$$

is negligible. Here, both S and D are quantum algorithms. S makes quantum superposition queries to R. The distinguisher's construction queries are classical, while its primitive queries may be in quantum superposition (in particular, S has to simulate the quantum oracle of prim).

As in the classical case, post-quantum public indifferentiability implies postquantum sequential indifferentiability. For stateless and deterministic constructions, the converse might also hold as in [23]. Still, proving it in our post-quantum setting would require substantial additional analysis and is beyond our scope.

## 6 Proof of Theorem 4

This section provides a proof of Theorem 4. Firstly, it is straightforward to check that both  $\mathcal{D}_0$  and  $\mathcal{D}_1$  always output 0 in the real world. Therefore,

$$\Pr_{h} \left[ 1 \leftarrow \mathcal{D} \left[ \mathsf{MD}[h], h \right] \right] = \sum_{r \in \{0, 1\}} \frac{1}{2} \Pr_{h} \left[ 1 \leftarrow \mathcal{D}_{r} \left[ \mathsf{MD}[h], h \right] \right] = 0. \tag{9}$$

Let  $p := \Pr_{\mathsf{RO}} \left[ 1 \leftarrow \mathcal{D}\left[\mathsf{RO}, \mathcal{S}[\mathsf{RO}]\right] \right]$  and  $p_r := \Pr_{\mathsf{RO}} \left[ 1 \leftarrow \mathcal{D}_r\left[\mathsf{RO}, \mathcal{S}[\mathsf{RO}]\right] \right]$  for  $r \in \{0, 1\}$ . Then,

$$\begin{aligned} \mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{pqseq\text{-}indiff}}_{\mathsf{RO},\mathsf{MD}[h],\mathcal{S}}(\mathcal{D}) &= \left| \Pr_{\mathsf{RO}} \left[ 1 \leftarrow \mathcal{D} \left[ \mathsf{RO}, \mathcal{S}[\mathsf{RO}] \right] \right] - \Pr_{h} \left[ 1 \leftarrow \mathcal{D} \left[ \mathsf{MD}[h], h \right] \right] \right| \\ &= p = \frac{p_0 + p_1}{2} \end{aligned}$$

follows from Eq. (9) and the definition of  $\mathcal{D}$ .

Below, we will show that p is non-negligible for any efficient simulator S. Without loss of generality, we assume that S does not perform intermediate measurements.

When  $\mathcal{D}_1$  runs relative to RO and  $\mathcal{S}[RO]$  in the ideal world, define

$$p_1^{\mathsf{chk1}} := \Pr_{\mathsf{RO}} \left[ \mathsf{flag}_1 = 1 \text{ in Step 7 of } \mathcal{D}_1 \right], \quad p_1^{\mathsf{chk2}} := \Pr_{\mathsf{RO}} \left[ \mathsf{flag}_2 = 1 \text{ in Step 7 of } \mathcal{D}_1 \right],$$

$$p_1^{c \neq \gamma} := \Pr_{\mathsf{RO}} \left[ c \neq \gamma \text{ in Step 7 of } \mathcal{D}_1 \right].$$

Our technical core is the proposition below, which roughly says that  $p_1$  becomes non-negligible if  $p_0$ ,  $p_1^{\mathsf{chk1}}$ ,  $p_1^{\mathsf{chk2}}$ , and  $p_1^{c\neq\gamma}$  are negligible.

**Proposition 1.** Suppose that  $p_0, p_1^{\mathsf{chk1}}, p_1^{\mathsf{chk2}}$ , and  $p_1^{c \neq \gamma}$  are upper bounded by a positive value  $\varepsilon \leq 2^{-20}$ . ## A Proof of Theorem 3

Proof (of Theorem 3). Distinguishers for public indifferentiability are given a construction oracle and a primitive oracle, which we denote by  $\mathcal{O}_{c}$  and  $\mathcal{O}_{p}$  (i.e.,  $(\mathcal{O}_{c}, \mathcal{O}_{p})$  is either (C[prim], prim) or (R,  $\mathcal{S}[R, \text{rvl}_{R}])$ ). Define  $\mathcal{D}$  as the distinguisher that runs the game G with  $\mathcal{A}$ , relaying the construction queries and primitive queries to  $\mathcal{O}_{c}$  and  $\mathcal{O}_{p}$  given to itself. That is,  $\mathcal{D}[\mathcal{O}_{c}, \mathcal{O}_{p}] := G[\mathcal{O}_{c}, \mathcal{A}[\mathcal{O}_{p}]]$ .

In addition, let  $\mathcal{B}$  be an adversary (given oracle access to R and  $\mathsf{rvl}_\mathsf{R}^{(G)}$ ) that runs  $\mathcal{A}$  and returns the output of  $\mathcal{A}$  as its own output, emulating the prim oracle by  $\mathcal{S}[\mathsf{R},\mathsf{rvl}_\mathsf{R}^{(G)}]$ , i.e.,  $\mathcal{B}\big[\mathsf{R},\mathsf{rvl}_\mathsf{R}^{(G)}\big] := \mathcal{A}\big[\mathcal{S}[\mathsf{R},\mathsf{rvl}_\mathsf{R}^{(G)}]\big]$ .

By the definition of  $\mathcal{D}$  and  $\mathcal{B}$ ,

$$\Pr\left[1 \leftarrow G\big[\mathsf{C}[\mathsf{prim}], \mathcal{A}[\mathsf{prim}]\big]\right] = \Pr\left[1 \leftarrow \mathcal{D}\big[\mathsf{C}[\mathsf{prim}], \mathsf{prim}]\right]$$

and

$$\Pr\left[1 \leftarrow G\left[\mathsf{R}, \mathcal{B}[\mathsf{R}, \mathsf{rvl}_\mathsf{R}^{(G)}]\right]\right] = \Pr\left[1 \leftarrow G\left[\mathsf{R}, \mathcal{A}\left[\mathcal{S}[\mathsf{R}, \mathsf{rvl}_\mathsf{R}^{(G)}]\right]\right]\right] = \Pr\left[1 \leftarrow \mathcal{D}\left[\mathsf{R}, \mathcal{S}[\mathsf{R}, \mathsf{rvl}_\mathsf{R}^{(G)}]\right]\right]$$

hold. In addition, the reveal interface  $\mathsf{rvl}_\mathsf{R}^{(G)}$  of the game G matches the reveal oracle in considering post-quantum public indifferentiability advantage of the above  $\mathcal{D}$  with  $\mathcal{S}$  because, in the ideal world, the queries to  $\mathsf{RO}$  by  $\mathcal{D}$  correspond to the queries to  $\mathsf{RO}$  generated in G other than those by an adversary.

Hence, the inequality

$$\Pr\left[1 \leftarrow G\left[\mathsf{C}[\mathsf{prim}], \mathcal{A}[\mathsf{prim}]\right]\right] \leq \Pr\left[1 \leftarrow G\left[\mathsf{R}, \mathcal{B}[\mathsf{R}, \mathsf{rvl}_\mathsf{R}^{(G)}]\right]\right] + \mathsf{Adv}_{\mathsf{R}, \mathsf{C}[\mathsf{prim}], \mathcal{S}}^{\mathsf{pqpub-indiff}}(\mathcal{D})$$

follows.

The claims about the efficiency also follow from the definition of  $\mathcal{D}$  and  $\mathcal{B}$ .  $\square$
