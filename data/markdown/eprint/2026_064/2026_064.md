# Breaking the KAZ Suite: Practical Key Recovery Attacks on MySEAL 2.0's Post-Quantum Candidates

Zhuo Huang<sup>1</sup> , Chongrong Li<sup>1</sup> , Yu Yu<sup>1</sup> , and Xiaogang Zhou<sup>2</sup>

<sup>1</sup> Shanghai Jiao Tong University <sup>2</sup> China Telecom Quantum Information Technology Group Co., Ltd

Abstract. We present practical attacks that completely break all four cryptographic schemes submitted to Malaysia's MySEAL 2.0 standardization initiative: the KAZ-KA key agreement scheme, the KAZ-KEM key encapsulation mechanism, the KAZ-SIGN v1.6.4, and KAZ-SIGN v2.0 digital signature schemes. KAZ-KA, KAZ-KEM, and KAZ-SIGN v2.0 operate over Z<sup>N</sup> where N is a primorial, the product of consecutive small primes. This design choice makes the group order φ(N) extremely smooth, enabling efficient attacks. For KAZ-KA and KAZ-KEM, we recover the private key by enumerating candidates modulo each small prime factor and solving discrete logarithms in small groups. For KAZ-SIGN v2.0, we exploit the linear structure of signatures to formulate a hidden number problem instance, which we solve using lattice reduction with only two signatures. For KAZ-SIGN v1.6.4, we demonstrate universal signature forgery attacks using only the public key by exploiting its verification algorithm, without requiring the private key. All attacks are implemented and executed in under one second on a standard consumer laptop (a MacBook) across all suggested security levels (128, 192, and 256 bits). These results conclusively prove that the analyzed schemes are fundamentally insecure and unsuitable for any deployment or migration.

# 1 Introduction

The National Trusted Cryptographic Algorithm List (MySEAL) is an initiative by CyberSecurity Malaysia designed to establish a sovereign standard for cryptographic modules within the nation [\[12](#page-24-0)[,13\]](#page-24-1). It serves to ensure that the cryptographic tools used for national security are robust and locally validated [\[12\]](#page-24-0).

The initiative is divided into two primary categories:

- 1. AKSA (Algoritma Kriptografi Sedia Ada): Focuses on "Existing Cryptographic Algorithms" that are internationally recognized and already in use [\[12](#page-24-0)[,13\]](#page-24-1), such as AES, Camellia, PRESENT, Skinny, ChaCha20, SHA2/SHA3, PHOTON, BLS, ECDSA, HMAC, ASCON-AEAD.
- 2. AKBA (Anugerah Kripto Bestari Award): Targets "New Cryptographic Algorithms", encouraging the development of indigenous or novel candidates to be vetted for future standardization [\[12](#page-24-0)[,13\]](#page-24-1).

{1}------------------------------------------------

The recent MySEAL 2.0 initiative was announced in March 2024, with a submission deadline set for March 7, 2025. Following this deadline, four candidate algorithms were submitted and have now entered the evaluation phase, commencing with the AKBA MySEAL 2.1 cycle in Q3 2025. These candidates are focused on asymmetric and post-quantum primitives [11] (with a demo provided at [26]):

- 1. KAZ-KA v1.0 [6]: A key agreement scheme that enables two parties to establish a shared secret over an insecure channel. The scheme operates over  $\mathbb{Z}_{N_j}$  where  $N_j$  is the product of j consecutive odd primes  $3, 5, 7, \ldots, p_{j+1}$ . The public key consists of two elements  $A_1, A_2 \in \mathbb{Z}_{N_j}^*$  computed as  $A_1 \equiv g_1^{a_1} \cdot g_2^{2a_2}$  (mod  $N_j$ ) and  $A_2 \equiv g_1^{a_2} \cdot g_2^{a_1}$  (mod  $N_j$ ), where  $a_1, a_2$  are private keys. The designers claim security based on the Double Discrete Logarithm Problem (DDLP) and the Hidden Number Problem (HNP).
- 2. KAZ-KEM v1.0 [7]: A key encapsulation mechanism sharing the same mathematical structure as KAZ-KA. It uses the primorial modulus  $N_j = \prod_{i=2}^{j+1} p_i$  and relies on the same underlying hardness assumptions. The encapsulation produces a ciphertext from which the shared key can be derived using the recipient's private key.
- 3. KAZ-SIGN v2.0 [5]: A redesigned digital signature scheme that returns to the primorial-based structure. Like KAZ-KA and KAZ-KEM, it operates over  $\mathbb{Z}_{N_j}$  with  $N_j$  being the product of consecutive primes. However, unlike KAZ-KA and KAZ-KEM where the public key provides two equations in two unknown secrets, the public key of KAZ-SIGN v2.0 yields only a single equation. The scheme claims security from the DDLP and the HNP.
- 4. KAZ-SIGN v1.6.4 [3]: A digital signature scheme based on an entirely different mathematical foundation. Unlike the other three schemes, KAZ-SIGN v1.6.4 relies on the Modular Reduction Problem rather than the discrete logarithm structure. The signature verification involves checking a congruence relation  $V_1 \equiv \alpha \pmod{G_1\rho}$  where  $G_1$  is a large prime and  $\rho$  is derived from the message hash.

These algorithms are to be deployed in critical areas, including database encryption, next-generation firewalls/VPNs, and mobile radio networks, as companies actively seek broader domestic and international ecosystem partnerships [21,19].

All four schemes are candidates in this cycle. KAZ-KA v1.0, KAZ-KEM v1.0, and KAZ-SIGN v2.0 share a common mathematical foundation: they operate over  $\mathbb{Z}_{N_j}$  where  $N_j = \prod_{i=2}^{j+1} p_i = 3 \cdot 5 \cdot 7 \cdots p_{j+1}$  is the product of j consecutive odd primes. The recommended parameters use j = 65, 96, 122 for 128-, 192-, and 256-bit security levels respectively. KAZ-SIGN v1.6.4 is fundamentally different, with details deferred to Section 5.

We also note that KAZ-SIGN v2.0 is distinct in nature from the NIST PQC Standardization Round 1 candidate KAZ-SIGN [4], which was broken by Bernstein via signature forgery attacks [8]. The NIST submission KAZ-SIGN used a "Second Order DLP" structure of the form  $g^{R^{\alpha}} \equiv V \pmod{N}$ , whereas KAZ-SIGN v2.0 employs the dual-generator structure  $V = g_1^s \cdot g_2^t \pmod{N_j}$ . Our work

focuses on key recovery for three schemes and public-key forgery for KAZ-SIGN v1.6.4.

In this paper, we show that all four schemes are insecure. For KAZ-KA, KAZ-KEM, and KAZ-SIGN v2.0, the root cause lies in the choice of the modulus  $N_j$ . Since  $N_j$  is a primorial, the group order  $\varphi(N_j) = \prod_{i=2}^{j+1} (p_i - 1)$  factors completely into small primes. This extreme smoothness enables efficient application of the Pohlig-Hellman algorithm to solve discrete logarithms in the multiplicative group  $\mathbb{Z}_{N_j}^*$ .

For KAZ-KA and KAZ-KEM, we present direct key recovery attacks. Given only the public key, we can recover the private key by solving discrete logarithms modulo each prime factor of  $N_j$  and combining the results via the Chinese Remainder Theorem (CRT). These attacks complete within seconds on a standard laptop.

For KAZ-SIGN v2.0, a direct application of Pohlig–Hellman to the public key faces a practical obstacle: the algorithm requires storing discrete logarithm tables for each prime factor, which leads to prohibitive memory consumption at the recommended security levels. Instead, we develop a more practical lattice-based attack that exploits the HNP structure inherent in the signature scheme. By collecting only two valid signatures, we construct a lattice whose shortest vector reveals the signing key. This attack bypasses the memory bottleneck while maintaining computational efficiency. For KAZ-SIGN v1.6.4, we do not recover the signing key, but we can forge signatures from the public key.

Section 3 reviews the mathematical background including the discrete logarithm problem, the Pohlig–Hellman algorithm, and lattice-based techniques for the HNP. Section 4 describes the KAZ schemes and their parameters. Section 5 presents our attacks on KAZ-KA, KAZ-KEM, KAZ-SIGN v1.6.4, and KAZ-SIGN v2.0. Section 6 concludes the paper.

# 2 Related Work

#### 2.1 DLP in practice

Discrete logarithm computations have repeatedly exhibited gaps between conjectured theoretical hardness and deployed parameters. For finite-field Diffie–Hellman (DH) key exchange and DSA, the most acute operational risk arises when many servers reuse the same small prime modulus. The Logjam line of work [1,27] showed that after a one-time number field sieve precomputation for a widely used 512-bit prime, individual TLS/DH handshakes using that modulus can be solved efficiently enough to enable practical man-in-the-middle attacks at scale against legacy deployments. Beyond actively exploited settings, the public record for prime-field DLP computations—most notably the 768-bit prime-field discrete logarithm computation reported by Kleinjung et al. [17]—provides a pragmatic benchmark for parameter sizes plausibly within reach of well-resourced adversaries.

#### 4 Huang et al.

By contrast, elliptic-curve discrete logarithms on standard (non-pairing-friendly) curves still have no known comparable subexponential attacks; real-world breaks more often trace to poor curve selection or parameterization, such as curves susceptible to MOV [20] or Frey-Rück reductions that embed the problem back into a finite-field DLP. Consequently, standardized cryptographic algorithms and libraries emphasize the use of carefully selected base curves and strict validation to avoid such structural pitfalls.

Our work differs from the Logjam-style NFS attacks on large primes as follows: the MySEAL candidates instantiate their groups over a primorial modulus whose totient factors into small primes. This extreme smoothness makes the classical Pohlig-Hellman strategy the natural baseline, turning what appears to be a large-modulus DLP into many small-subgroup instances that can be solved and recombined efficiently.

#### 2.2 HNP in practice

HNP provides a complementary lens: it models settings where an attacker observes noisy, truncated, or otherwise approximate modular relations involving a fixed secret, and reduces key recovery to lattice problems such as approximate shortest- or closest-vector instances. Boneh and Venkatesan [9] formalized this leakage-to-lattice pipeline while studying the bit security of Diffie-Hellman secrets, showing that sufficiently many imperfect observations can enable recovery of the hidden value. In real-world DSA and ECDSA deployments, this perspective explains how biased, repeated, or partially leaked per-signature nonces can lead to full private-key compromise without any advance in generic DLP algorithms. Howgrave-Graham and Smart [14] demonstrated that if a short window of consecutive nonce bits leaks (e.g., via side channels or partial state disclosure), then collecting enough signatures yields hidden-number instances that can be solved via LLL-based lattice reduction to recover the long-term signing key. Nguyen and Shparlinski [22] subsequently proved polynomial-time key recovery under mild randomness assumptions once a threshold number of signatures is available, strengthening earlier heuristic attacks with provable guarantees.

These ideas have repeatedly materialized in practice. The Minerva work [15] identified commodity smart cards and software stacks whose ECDSA scalar multiplication leaked only the nonce bit-length; despite this weak signal, the leakage still fits the HNP template, and lattice decoding enables recovery of 256-bit curve keys given sufficiently many signatures. Breitner and Heninger [10] further demonstrated a high-impact pipeline by collecting biased or repeated ECDSA signatures from cryptocurrencies and Internet-wide scans, and then automatically recovering large numbers of production private keys via HNP-style lattice attacks. Accordingly, modern engineering guidance emphasizes hardened nonce generation: either ensure strong, bias-free randomness with robust side-channel protections, or adopt deterministic nonce derivation (e.g., RFC 6979 [24]) to reduce exposure when entropy sources or hardware isolation are uncertain.

The MySEAL candidates introduce a designer-defined DDLP over  $\mathbb{Z}_{N_j}^*$  with a primorial modulus, yielding algebraic structure in a group of high smoothness.

{4}------------------------------------------------

While DDLP is syntactically distinct from classical DLP or standard HNP formulations, our attacks follow a related methodological theme: we exploit parameter structure to reduce the problem into small-modulus components and recombine partial information to recover secrets efficiently. In particular, for KAZ-SIGN v2.0 we show that the signing equations themselves induce an HNP-like lattice instance, enabling practical key recovery without assuming any external leakage model.

### <span id="page-4-0"></span>3 Preliminaries

#### 3.1 Notation

We write  $\mathbb{Z}$  for the ring of integers and  $\mathbb{R}$  for the field of real numbers. For a positive integer N, let

$$\mathbb{Z}_N := \mathbb{Z}/N\mathbb{Z},$$

denote the residue class ring modulo N. An element  $a \in \mathbb{Z}_N$  is a *unit* (i.e., invertible) if there exists  $b \in \mathbb{Z}_N$  such that  $ab \equiv 1 \pmod{N}$ . The set of all units forms a multiplicative group

$$\mathbb{Z}_N^* := \{ a \in \mathbb{Z}_N : \gcd(a, N) = 1 \},$$

called the *group of units* of  $\mathbb{Z}_N$ . In particular, when p is prime,  $\mathbb{Z}_p$  is a field (canonically identified with  $\mathbb{F}_p$ ), and  $\mathbb{Z}_p^* = \mathbb{F}_p^{\times}$  is the multiplicative group of the field. For an integer  $x \geq 1$ , we write  $\text{len}(x) := \lfloor \log_2 x \rfloor + 1$  for its bit-length.

Euler's totient function. The Euler totient function  $\varphi(N)$  denotes the number of units modulo N, i.e.,  $|\mathbb{Z}_N^*| = \varphi(N)$ . In particular, if p is prime and  $k \geq 1$ , then the units modulo  $p^k$  are exactly those residues not divisible by p, so  $\varphi(p^k) = p^k - p^{k-1} = p^{k-1}(p-1)$ . Combining these two properties yields the standard formula for computing  $\varphi(N)$  from the prime factorization of N. Let  $N = \prod_{i=1}^t p_i^{e_i}$  be the prime-power decomposition of N (distinct primes  $p_i$  and integers  $e_i \geq 1$ ). Then

$$\varphi(N) = \prod_{i=1}^{t} \varphi(p_i^{e_i}) = \prod_{i=1}^{t} p_i^{e_i - 1} (p_i - 1).$$

Cyclic Groups. A group G is cyclic if it is generated by a single element  $g \in G$ , i.e.,

$$G = \langle g \rangle := \{ g^k : k \in \mathbb{Z} \}.$$

For finite G, the *order* of g, denoted  $\operatorname{ord}(g)$ , is the least positive integer t such that  $g^t = e$ . If  $G = \langle g \rangle$  and |G| = n, then  $\operatorname{ord}(g) = n$  and every element of G can be written uniquely as  $g^k$  with some  $k \in \{0, 1, \ldots, n-1\}$ . The group  $\mathbb{F}_p^{\times}$  is cyclic of order p-1; a generator is often called a *primitive element*.

{5}------------------------------------------------

Discrete logarithm. Let  $G = \langle g \rangle$  be a finite cyclic group of order n. For  $x \in G$ , the discrete logarithm of x to base g is the integer

$$\log_q(x) \in \mathbb{Z}_n,$$

satisfying  $g^{\log_g(x)} = x$ . When the base g is clear from context, we may simply write  $\log(x)$ .

Chinese Remainder Theorem (CRT). Let  $N_1, \ldots, N_k$  be pairwise coprime positive integers and set  $N = \prod_{i=1}^k N_i$ . The Chinese Remainder Theorem states that the natural reduction map

$$\mathbb{Z}_N \longrightarrow \mathbb{Z}_{N_1} \times \cdots \times \mathbb{Z}_{N_k}$$
  
 $x \mod N \longmapsto (x \mod N_1, \dots, x \mod N_k),$ 

is a ring isomorphism. Restricting to units yields a group isomorphism

$$\mathbb{Z}_N^* \cong \mathbb{Z}_{N_1}^* \times \cdots \times \mathbb{Z}_{N_k}^*$$
.

Equivalently, for any residues  $a_i \in \mathbb{Z}_{N_i}$  there exists a unique  $x \in \mathbb{Z}_N$  such that  $x \equiv a_i \pmod{N_i}$  for all i. Specifically, let  $M_i := N/N_i$ , and since  $\gcd(M_i, N_i) = 1$ , let  $t_i := M_i^{-1} \mod N_i$ . Then the unique x can be computed efficiently as

$$x \equiv \sum_{i=1}^{k} a_i \cdot M_i \cdot t_i \mod N.$$

We therefore use the notation  $CRT([a_1, \ldots, a_k], [N_1, \ldots, N_k])$  to denote this unique solution modulo N produced by the above construction.

Vectors and norms. Bold letters such as  $\mathbf{v} = (v_1, \dots, v_n)$  denote vectors. We write  $\|\mathbf{v}\|$  for the Euclidean norm,

$$\|\mathbf{v}\| := \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n v_i^2}.$$

#### 3.2 Double Discrete Logarithm Problem

As defined in the KAZ-KA/KAZ-KEM/KAZ-SIGN v2.0 specifications, let N be a composite modulus and let  $g_1, g_2 \in \mathbb{Z}_N^*$  be chosen so that neither  $\log_{g_1}(g_2)$  nor  $\log_{g_2}(g_1)$  exists modulo N. Let  $\operatorname{ord}(g_i)$  denote the order of  $g_i$  in  $\mathbb{Z}_N^*$ . Given

$$A \equiv g_1^s g_2^t \pmod{N},$$

### 3.3 Modular Reduction Problem (MRP)

As defined in the KAZ-SIGN v1.6.4 specification, let N be a composite modulus, let n be the bit-length of N, and let  $p_k$  be a factor of N. Choose  $\alpha$  with  $2^{n-1} < \alpha < N$  and compute

$$A \equiv \alpha \pmod{p_k}$$
.

The Modular Reduction Problem is, given  $(A, N, p_k)$ , to recover  $\alpha$ . This is the stated hardness assumption for KAZ-SIGN v1.6.4. [3] The specification likewise mentions HNP as an auxiliary layer; we treat MRP as the primary assumption and analyze its concrete instantiation in Section 5. The authors describe MRP as not well explored and suggest that it may be a potential quantum-resistant hard problem underpinning a post-quantum signature candidate. [3]

# 3.4 Primorials and Smooth Numbers

A positive integer n is called B-smooth if all prime factors of n are at most B. The primorial  $p_k\#$  is defined as the product of the first k primes. In the KAZ schemes, the modulus is defined as  $N_j = \prod_{i=2}^{j+1} p_i = 3 \cdot 5 \cdot 7 \cdots p_{j+1}$ , the product of j consecutive odd primes.

For such a primorial  $N_j$ , the Euler totient function is

$$\varphi(N_j) = \prod_{i=2}^{j+1} (p_i - 1).$$

Since each  $p_i - 1$  is even and factors into small primes,  $\varphi(N_j)$  is extremely smooth. This property is critical to our attacks.

#### 3.5 Discrete Logarithm Problem

The Discrete Logarithm Problem (DLP) in a cyclic group  $G = \langle g \rangle$  of order n is defined as follows: given g and  $h \in G$ , find x such that  $g^x = h$ . The difficulty of DLP depends heavily on the structure of n.

The Pohlig-Hellman algorithm [23] exploits the factorization of the group order to reduce DLP to smaller subproblems. Let  $n = \prod_{i=1}^k q_i^{e_i}$  be the prime factorization of the group order. The algorithm proceeds as follows:

- 1. For each prime power  $q_i^{e_i}$  dividing n, compute the discrete logarithm modulo  $q_i^{e_i}$  by working in the subgroup of order  $q_i^{e_i}$ .
- 2. Combine the results using the CRT to obtain  $x \mod n$ .

The complexity of the Pohlig-Hellman algorithm is  $O\left(\sum_{i=1}^k e_i(\log n + \sqrt{q_i})\right)$ . When n is B-smooth, all  $q_i \leq B$ , making the algorithm efficient.

#### 3.6 Lattices

A lattice  $\mathcal{L}$  is a discrete additive subgroup of  $\mathbb{R}^n$ . Given a basis  $\mathbf{B} = \{\mathbf{b}_1, \dots, \mathbf{b}_n\}$ , the lattice is

$$\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{B}) = \left\{ \sum_{i=1}^{n} z_i \mathbf{b}_i : z_i \in \mathbb{Z} \right\}.$$

The Shortest Vector Problem (SVP) asks to find a nonzero vector in  $\mathcal{L}$  of minimal Euclidean norm. The LLL algorithm [18] finds an approximate shortest vector in polynomial time. For a lattice of dimension n, the LLL algorithm outputs a vector  $\mathbf{v}$  satisfying  $\|\mathbf{v}\| \leq 2^{(n-1)/2}\lambda_1(\mathcal{L})$ , where  $\lambda_1(\mathcal{L})$  denotes the length of the shortest nonzero vector within the lattice  $\mathcal{L}$ . For random lattices, it is standard to use the Gaussian heuristic to estimate the scale of  $\lambda_1(\mathcal{L})$  via  $\mathrm{gh}(\mathcal{L}) \approx (\det(\mathcal{L}))^{1/n} \sqrt{n/(2\pi e)}$  [2]. Here  $\det(\mathcal{L})$  denotes the lattice determinant and n its dimension.

### 3.7 Hidden Number Problem

The Hidden Number Problem (HNP), introduced by Boneh and Venkatesan [9], asks to recover a hidden number  $\alpha$  given partial information about  $\alpha \cdot t_i \mod p$  for known random values  $t_i$ . Formally, given d samples  $(t_i, u_i)$  where  $u_i$  approximates  $\alpha \cdot t_i \mod p$  with  $|\alpha \cdot t_i - u_i| \mod p | < B$  for some bound B, the goal is to recover  $\alpha$ .

HNP can be formulated as a Closest Vector Problem (CVP). Consider the lattice  $\mathcal{L}$  spanned by the rows of

$$\mathbf{B} = \begin{pmatrix} p & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & p & \cdots & 0 & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & p & 0 \\ t_1 & t_2 & \cdots & t_d & 1 \end{pmatrix}.$$

The target vector is  $\mathbf{t} = (u_1, u_2, \dots, u_d, 0)$ . The lattice vector closest to  $\mathbf{t}$  reveals  $\alpha$  since  $(\alpha t_1 \mod p, \dots, \alpha t_d \mod p, \alpha)$  lies in  $\mathcal{L}$ .

In practice, we reduce CVP to SVP using Kannan's embedding [16]. We construct the extended lattice  $\mathcal{L}'$  spanned by the rows of

$$\mathbf{B}' = \begin{pmatrix} p & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & p & \cdots & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & p & 0 & 0 \\ t_1 & t_2 & \cdots & t_d & 1 & 0 \\ u_1 & u_2 & \cdots & u_d & 0 & K \end{pmatrix},$$

where K is a scaling factor. The target short vector is  $(e_1, e_2, \ldots, e_d, -\alpha, K)$ , where  $e_i = u_i - \alpha t_i \mod p$  are small. Applying LLL to **B'** recovers this short vector, from which  $\alpha$  can be extracted.

We now describe the four KAZ schemes referenced in this paper. The first three schemes share a common structure: they operate over  $\mathbb{Z}_{N_j}$  where  $N_j = \prod_{i=2}^{j+1} p_i$ is the product of j consecutive odd primes, and use two selected primes  $g_1, g_2 \in$  $\mathbb{Z}_{N_i}^*$ . KAZ-KA v1.0. The protocol consists of three procedures. Algorithm 1 generates keys by sampling  $a_1 \in \mathbb{Z}_{o_1}$  and  $a_2 \in \mathbb{Z}_{o_2}$ , then publishing  $A_1$  and  $A_2$ . Algorithm 2 uses fresh  $b_1$  and  $b_2$  with a slightly different structure to form the partial key  $B_1$  and  $B_2$  for exchange. #### <span id="page-14-4"></span>Key Recovery Attack on KAZ-KA and KAZ-KEM 5.1

Pohlig-Hellman is effective when the group order is smooth because it reduces a DLP in a group of order n to DLPs in subgroups of order  $q_i^{e_i}$  and recombines the solutions via the CRT. When all  $q_i$  are small, these subproblems are cheap, and the overall DLP becomes practical.

Our key recovery attack on KAZ-KA and KAZ-KEM recovers the private key  $(a_1, a_2)$  from the public key  $(A_1, A_2)$  in polynomial time. The attack exploits the fact that  $N_j$  is a primorial, allowing us to work modulo each small prime factor independently.

The recommended parameters use j = 65, 96, 122 consecutive odd primes for 128-, 192-, and 256-bit security levels respectively. The modulus  $N_j=3$  $5 \cdot 7 \cdot \cdots p_{j+1}$  has the largest prime factor  $p_{j+1} = 317, 509, 677$  for these security levels.

We start from the public key equations:

$$A_2 \equiv g_1^{a_2} g_2^{a_1} \pmod{N_i}. \tag{2}$$

Since  $N_j = \prod_{i=2}^{j+1} p_i$ , by the CRT we can work independently modulo each prime factor  $p_i$ . For each  $p_i$ , equations (1) and (2) reduce to:

$$A_1 \equiv g_1^{a_1} g_2^{2a_2} \pmod{p_i},$$

$$A_2 \equiv g_1^{a_2} g_2^{a_1} \pmod{p_i}.$$

Step 1: Enumerate and Solve DLP. For each prime p dividing  $N_j$ , let  $\omega_1$  $\operatorname{ord}_p(g_1)$  and  $\omega_2 = \operatorname{ord}_p(g_2)$  be the multiplicative orders of  $g_1$  and  $g_2$  in  $\mathbb{Z}_p^*$ . For each candidate value  $u \in [0, \omega_1)$  representing a guess for  $a_1 \mod \omega_1$ , we compute:

$$T_1 \equiv A_1 \cdot g_1^{-u} \equiv g_2^{2a_2} \pmod{p}.$$

We then solve for  $\log_{g_2} T_1$  in  $\mathbb{Z}_p^*$  to obtain  $2a_2 \mod \omega_2$ , and subsequently  $a_2 \mod$  $\omega_2$  (handling the factor of 2 appropriately).

For each candidate pair (u, v) where  $u = a_1 \mod \omega_1$  and  $v = a_2 \mod \omega_2$ , we verify consistency with equation (4) by checking:

$$A_2 \cdot g_2^{-u} \equiv g_1^v \pmod{p}.$$

Valid candidates that satisfy both equations are retained.

Step 2: Combine via CRT. Let  $L_p = \text{lcm}(\omega_1, \omega_2)$  for each prime p. For each prime, we obtain a set of candidate solutions  $(a_1 \mod L_p, a_2 \mod L_p)$ . We use the CRT to merge solutions across all primes. The final candidates are verified against the original public key  $(A_1, A_2)$  to recover the correct private key  $(a_1, a_2)$ .

We can estimate the time complexity per prime factor. The enumeration step tries  $\omega_1 \leq p-1$  candidates for  $a_1 \mod \omega_1$ . For each candidate we solve  $\log_{g_2} T_1$  in  $\mathbb{Z}_p^*$  using baby-step giant-step [25], which costs  $O(\sqrt{\omega_2})$  time and space with  $\omega_2 \leq p-1$ . Since  $\omega_1, \omega_2 \leq p-1$ , the per-prime complexity is  $O(\omega_1 \cdot \sqrt{\omega_2}) = O(p \cdot \sqrt{p}) = O(p^{3/2})$ . Combining candidates across primes via the CRT adds only linear overhead relative to the number of candidates.

The total complexity over all j primes is:

$$\sum_{i=2}^{j+1} O(p_i^{3/2}) = O\left(\sum_{i=2}^{j+1} p_i^{3/2}\right).$$

By the prime number theorem,  $p_i \approx i \ln i$ , so the sum is dominated by the largest terms and is approximately  $O(j \cdot p_{j+1}^{3/2})$ .

In practice, the per-prime DLPs are tiny. Each p is at most a few hundred, so we precompute discrete logarithm tables:

$$dlog_1 : g_1^k \mod p \mapsto k, \quad \text{for } k \in [0, \omega_1),$$
  
 $dlog_2 : g_2^k \mod p \mapsto k, \quad \text{for } k \in [0, \omega_2).$ 

Each table requires O(p) time and space, which is negligible at these sizes. With precomputed tables, each DLP lookup takes O(1) time, reducing the per-prime complexity to O(p) and the total complexity to  $O(j \cdot p_{j+1})$ . The tables are computed once per prime and reused across all key instances, so the online cost is dominated by simple modular checks.

# 5.2 Lattice Attack on KAZ-SIGN v2.0

For KAZ-SIGN v2.0, the public key  $V \equiv g_1^s g_2^t \pmod{N_j}$  provides only a single equation in the two unknown secrets (s,t). A direct application of the table-based approach from Section 5.1 would require enumerating all possible values of one secret, leading to exponential complexity. Instead, we develop a lattice-based attack that exploits the algebraic structure of the signature scheme, requiring only two signatures. The two signatures can be on different messages, since the hash values h are public and computable.

We begin by recalling the signature equations, which make explicit how the short ephemeral values  $(e_1, e_2)$  interact with the long-term secrets (s, t). A signature  $\sigma = (S_1, S_2, S_3)$  on message m with hash h = H(m) satisfies:

<span id="page-17-0"></span>
$$S_1 \equiv g_1^{e_1} g_2^{e_2} \pmod{N_i},\tag{5}$$

$$S_2 \equiv (h - s \cdot S_1) \cdot e_1^{-1} \pmod{\varphi(N_i)},\tag{6}$$

$$S_3 \equiv h - t \cdot S_1 - e_2 \cdot S_2 \pmod{\varphi(N_i)},\tag{7}$$

where  $(e_1, e_2)$  are ephemeral values chosen during signing.

Rearranging equation (6):

<span id="page-17-2"></span><span id="page-17-1"></span>
$$s \cdot S_1 + e_1 \cdot S_2 \equiv h \pmod{\varphi(N_i)}. \tag{8}$$

Similarly, from equation (7):

<span id="page-17-4"></span>
$$t \cdot S_1 + e_2 \cdot S_2 \equiv h - S_3 \pmod{\varphi(N_i)}. \tag{9}$$

To expose the hidden-number structure, we normalize each equation because  $S_2^{(i)}$  may not be invertible modulo  $\varphi(N_j)$ . We remove the non-invertible factor of  $S_2^{(i)}$  by letting  $g^{(i)} = \gcd(S_2^{(i)}, \varphi(N_j))$  and defining  $\varphi_i = \varphi(N_j)/g^{(i)}$  and  $\tilde{S}_2^{(i)} = S_2^{(i)}/g^{(i)}$ . Then  $\tilde{S}_2^{(i)}$  is invertible modulo  $\varphi_i$ , and dividing equation (8) by  $S_2^{(i)}$  yields:

<span id="page-17-3"></span>
$$e_1^{(i)} \equiv b_i - s \cdot a_i \pmod{\varphi_i},$$
 (10)

where  $a_i = S_1^{(i)} \cdot (\tilde{S}_2^{(i)})^{-1} \mod \varphi_i$  and  $b_i = h_i \cdot (\tilde{S}_2^{(i)})^{-1} \mod \varphi_i$ .

With two signatures we obtain two congruences in the same secret s with small offsets  $e_1^{(i)}$ . Concretely, equation (10) gives:

$$e_1^{(1)} \equiv b_1 - s \cdot a_1 \pmod{\varphi_1},$$
  
 $e_1^{(2)} \equiv b_2 - s \cdot a_2 \pmod{\varphi_2}.$

# 5.3 Signature Forgery Attack on KAZ-SIGN v1.6.4

We analyze the public-key relations implied by Algorithms [10–](#page-11-1)[12,](#page-12-1) and show that a forger can construct a valid signature (S1, S2) for any message m without access to any prior signatures. The signer uses S<sup>2</sup> to iterate h = H(m) + S<sup>2</sup> until the range and exclusion filters pass. In our forgery, we search S<sup>2</sup> over 16-bit values and compute h = H(m) + S2. For each S<sup>2</sup> we compute a candidate S<sup>1</sup> from the public congruences and then check the remaining filters.

Algorithm [15](#page-21-0) summarizes the forgery procedure. The verifier checks the main congruence RS<sup>1</sup> ≡ R<sup>T</sup> (mod G0) with T ≡ V φ(Q) 1 · 2h φ(qQ) (mod G1). Since ord<sup>G</sup><sup>0</sup> (R) = G1, this is equivalent to

$$S_1 \equiv T \pmod{G_1}$$
.

Filter (v) enforces QS<sup>1</sup> ≡ 2V<sup>2</sup> (mod qQ), and the public key relation V<sup>2</sup> ≡ Q·SK (mod qQ) makes SK mod q public, yielding

$$S_1 \equiv 2 \cdot SK \pmod{q}$$
.

Finally, the additional congruence on G1<sup>A</sup> gives

$$S_1 \equiv V_1^{\varphi(Q)} \cdot 2h^{\varphi(qQ)} \pmod{G_{1A}}.$$

Combining these constraints via CRT yields a unique residue class

$$S_1 \equiv s_1 \pmod{\operatorname{lcm}(G_1, q, G_{1A})}.$$

Because G1<sup>A</sup> = G1ρ/A with q and ρ distinct primes, the modulus simplifies to

$$lcm(G_1, q, G_{1A}) = G_1 q \rho.$$

Write S<sup>1</sup> = s<sup>1</sup> + k · G1qρ for some integer k. The range filters (i)–(ii) require S<sup>1</sup> ∈ [2<sup>L</sup>G1qQρ−<sup>1</sup> , G1qQρ − 1], so k must satisfy

$$\left\lceil \frac{2^{L_{G_1 qQ\rho} - 1} - s_1}{G_1 q\rho} \right\rceil \le k \le \left\lfloor \frac{G_1 qQ\rho - 1 - s_1}{G_1 q\rho} \right\rfloor.$$

Thus, k ranges over a contiguous interval on the order of Q, and once s<sup>1</sup> is fixed, there are roughly Q admissible candidates for S1.

$$Y_1 \equiv V_1^{\varphi(Q)} \cdot 2h^{\varphi(qQ)} \pmod{G_1 Q \rho},$$
  
$$Y_2 \equiv V_1^{\varphi(Q)} \cdot 2h^{\varphi(qQ)} \pmod{G_1 \rho}.$$

# <span id="page-22-0"></span>6 Conclusion

We demonstrate practical attacks against four cryptographic schemes submitted to the MySEAL 2.0 initiative: KAZ-KA v1.0, KAZ-KEM v1.0, KAZ-SIGN 

{23}------------------------------------------------

v1.6.4, and KAZ-SIGN v2.0. We recover private keys for KAZ-KA, KAZ-KEM, and KAZ-SIGN v2.0, and we can perform universal signature forgeries for KAZ-SIGN v1.6.4 from the public key. Our attacks complete in under one second for all recommended security levels, namely 128, 192, and 256 bits.

The fundamental vulnerability of KAZ-KA v1.0, KAZ-KEM v1.0, and KAZ-SIGN v2.0 lies in the choice of the modulus  $N_j$ . By defining  $N_j = \prod_{i=2}^{j+1} p_i$  as the product of consecutive small primes, the designers inadvertently make the group order  $\varphi(N_j) = \prod_{i=2}^{j+1} (p_i - 1)$  extremely smooth. This smoothness has two critical consequences:

- 1. For KAZ-KA and KAZ-KEM, working modulo each small prime  $p_i$  reduces the DLP to groups of order at most  $p_i 1$ . The two public key equations provide sufficient constraints to recover the two private key components via enumeration and table lookup.
- 2. For KAZ-SIGN v2.0, while the single public key equation is insufficient for direct key recovery, the signature scheme leaks linear relations involving the signing key and small ephemeral values. The size disparity between the ephemeral values ( $\approx k$  bits) and  $\varphi(N_j)$  ( $\approx 3k$  bits) creates an HNP instance solvable via lattice reduction.
- 3. For KAZ-SIGN v1.6.4, the verification structure enables public-key signature forgery without recovering the private key.

We conclude that KAZ-KA v1.0, KAZ-KEM v1.0, KAZ-SIGN v1.6.4, and KAZ-SIGN v2.0 are insecure and should not be used. Any future designs based on similar principles must avoid smooth-order groups by using moduli with large prime factors. For KAZ-SIGN v2.0, the signature equations expose an HNP instance because the nonce values are much smaller than  $\varphi(N_j)$ , enabling lattice-based key recovery. For KAZ-SIGN v1.6.4, the verification structure leaves a large solution space that enables public-key signature forgery without recovering the private key.
