# On the Power of Polynomial Preprocessing: Proving Computations in Sublinear Time, and More

<span id="page-0-0"></span>Matteo Campanelli<sup>1</sup> , Mario Carrillo<sup>2</sup> , Ignacio Cascudo<sup>3</sup> , Dario Fiore<sup>3</sup> , Danilo Francati<sup>4</sup> , and Rosario Gennaro<sup>5</sup>

> Offchain Labs Universitat Rovira i Virgili 3 IMDEA Software Institute Royal Holloway University of London The City College of New York

Abstract. Cryptographic proof systems enable a verifier to be convinced of a computation's correctness without re-executing it; common efficiency requirements include both succinct proofs and fast verification. In this work we put forth the general study of cryptographic proof systems with sublinear proving time (after a preprocessing).

Prior work has achieved sublinear proving only for limited computational settings (e.g., vector commitments and lookup arguments), relying on specific assumptions or through non-black-box use of cryptographic primitives. In this work we lift many of these limitations through the systematic study of a specific object: polynomial commitments (PC) with sublinear proving time, a choice motivated by the crucial role that PC play in the design of efficient cryptographic schemes.

We show a simple construction of a PC with sublinear prover based on any vector commitment scheme (VC) and any preprocessing technique for fast polynomial evaluation. We prove that this PC satisfies evaluation binding, which is the standard security notion for PC, and show how to expand our construction to achieve the stronger notion of knowledge soundness (extractability).

Next, we study applications of PCs with sublinear prover. The first one is to construct index-efficient SNARKs, that are SNARKs where the prover is sublinear, after preprocessing, in the size of the index (i.e., the NP-relation describing the proven statement). We propose a method to transform a class of standard Polynomial Interactive Oracle Proofs (PIOPs) into index-efficient PIOPs, obtaining two results that advance the state of the art: the first lookup argument for unstructured tables in which the prover is sublinear in the size of the table, while making only black-box use of a VC and thus allowing instantiations from generic assumptions such as collision-resistant hash functions, and the first transparent SNARK for circuits where the prover is sublinear in the number of addition gates. Finally, our last application is a transformation that builds UC-secure SNARKs from simulation-extractable ones, with an approximately linear overhead in proving time (as opposed to quadratic in prior work).

## <span id="page-3-0"></span>1 Introduction

Cryptographic proof systems allow a weak machine V to be persuaded by a (potentially malicious) prover P that a computation C produces a specific output on some input x. Efficient proofs typically require both low communication complexity (e.g., that the "certificate" of the computation be short) and verification efficiency. In particular, we are usually interested in the verifier working sublinearly in the complexity T of the computation C. [6](#page-3-1) This efficiency profile is possible, naturally, only after the verifier has performed (once and for all) a preprocessing stage of the computation C, of which it keeps a short digest (e.g., a verification key).

In this work, we tackle the following research direction:

Is it possible to build proof systems where the prover can also run in time sublinear in T (after a preprocessing)?

This seemingly paradoxical property already appears in the literature, albeit with some limitations. For example, in vector commitments (VCs) [\[CF13\]](#page-34-1), which can be viewed as succinct arguments that a certain v is the i-th value of a committed vector, one can precompute the opening proofs for all positions in such a way that, at proving time, only the correct proofs needs to be fetched. Some schemes even support a nontrivial form of this functionality to generate succinct proofs for arbitrary subsets of positions [\[BBF19,](#page-33-1)[TAB](#page-35-0)+20[,CFG](#page-34-2)+20]. However, VCs are succinct arguments with a limited expressivity because their statements are the positions, i.e., integers from a polynomial-size set. In lookup arguments, which allow proving that each entry of a committed vector v is in a large committed table t, it is a desirable feature that the proving time be sublinear in |t|. However, in the state of the art, this efficiency profile is only achieved by constructions that make non-black box use of cryptographic schemes (e.g., due to encoding Merkle trees in a circuit [\[BFR](#page-33-2)+13] or to recursion [\[BC23\]](#page-33-3)), by pairing-based schemes [\[ZBK](#page-35-1)+22[,ZGK](#page-36-2)+22[,EFG22,](#page-34-3)[CFF](#page-34-4)+24[,CFG25\]](#page-34-5), or in the special case of structured tables [\[STW24\]](#page-35-2). The same limitations of lookup arguments apply to existing SNARKs for RAM computation with space complexity S and time complexity T = o(S), in which one wishes the proving time to be sublinear in S.

This state of affairs shows that more is to be understood about the feasibility of succinct arguments with sub-linear prover. In particular, a desirable goal is to achieve sublinear proving for relatively general functionalities, with constructions that make black-box use of cryptographic primitives and can be instantiated from a variety of assumptions.

In this work, we achieve this goal by focusing on a specific type of computation: proving evaluations of committed polynomials. Polynomial commitment schemes (PC) [\[KZG10\]](#page-35-3) are a cryptographic primitive that allows a prover to commit to a polynomial f with a string cm (commitment) and subsequently produce a proof of the correctness π<sup>x</sup> of its evaluation y = f(x) at any point x. Consider the case of a polynomial f with m variables of degree less than d in each variable, which can be described by a vector of |f| = d <sup>m</sup> coefficients. The crucial property of polynomial commitments is succinctness: both the commitment string and the evaluation proofs must be "short", i.e., sublinear in |f| (and ideally, also the verification time should be sublinear in |f|).

PCs have already proved to have a central role in the design of SNARKs, and have many other applications (e.g., verifiable secret sharing). For this reason, we believe that the study of PCs with sublinear proving time can be a stepping stone towards the achievement of sublinear

<span id="page-3-1"></span><sup>6</sup> For example, if C is a circuit of size T = n <sup>2</sup> where n = |x|, we would like verification to require—at most—subquadratic time. In the setting of arguments of knowledge the verifier may even be required to work sublinearly in n, and instead of reading x explicitly the verifier is simply convinced that P knows such x.

proving for more general computations, and a valuable tool with applications beyond the specifics of cryptographic proofs.

Research in PC has been focused on improving the efficiency of the verifier, whereas it is widely accepted that the prover must run at least linearly in the size |f| = d <sup>m</sup> of the m-variate polynomial. In fact, this linear-time behavior appears unavoidable in order to, at the very least, compute the output y = f(x).

In this work, we challenge this state of affairs by investigating the design and the applications of polynomial commitments with a sublinear prover. In such a scheme, at commitment time the prover also performs a preprocessing of the polynomial f which results in some auxiliary information aux. Later, when asked to prove an evaluation of f at an input x, the prover can use aux to quickly produce the evaluation y = f(x) and its proof in sublinear time. Preprocessing schemes to quickly evaluate polynomials have been known in the complexity literature for some time now, e.g. the work by Kedlaya and Umans [\[KU11](#page-35-4)[,KU08\]](#page-35-5) (KU in short) that we use in this paper. Our challenge was to make sure we could quickly compute the proofs too, and achieve provable and meaningful notions of security for the resulting PC. In contrast to vector commitments or lookup arguments, where one could think of computing a proof for each position in the preprocessing stage, the challenge in PC is that the number of evaluation points is exponential, that is |Rm| for a polynomial defined over a ring R.

## <span id="page-4-0"></span>1.1 Our Results

Constructing PCs with sublinear prover: Towards finding constructions of PCs with sublinear proving time, we observe that the recent work of Ateniese et al. [\[ABC](#page-32-0)+24] proposes a novel cryptographic scheme, dubbed verifiable data structure for univariate polynomial evaluations, which can also be seen as a PC for univariate polynomials satisfying a weaker security notion—weak evaluation binding—which states that an adversary should not be able to produce valid proofs for a false evaluation y ̸= f(x) and with respect to honestly generated commitments to f. Their construction achieves sublinear proving time by combining the preprocessing-based fast evaluation techniques for univariate polynomials of Kedlaya and Umans [\[KU11,](#page-35-4)[KU08\]](#page-35-5) together with a vector commitment.

In our work, we formalize a construction of PC with sublinear proving time by generalizing the construction of [\[ABC](#page-32-0)+24] to the case of multivariate polynomials (this is crucial for some of our applications) and by proving that it satisfies the standard security notion of PC, evaluation binding. The latter guarantees that it is hard to generate two (possibly malicious) proofs that are accepted with respect to the same (possibly malicious) commitment, the same point evaluation point x, but claiming distinct outputs y ̸= y ′ . Since evaluation binding may be insufficient in some applications, we also show how to extend the PC construction to achieve stronger security, specifically knowledge soundness, albeit this comes at the price of employing a SNARK.

This PC scheme has fast openings, meaning that opening proofs can be computed in sublinear time in the description of the polynomial f. In particular, for a univariate polynomial f of degree at most d − 1 (i.e., |f| = d), this means proving in time poly(log d), whereas, for an m-variate polynomial f of degree at most d − 1 in each variable (i.e., |f| = d <sup>m</sup>), it means proving in time poly(d, m).

See Section [1.2](#page-5-0) for an informal description of the technical challenges involved in formalizing the construction in [\[ABC](#page-32-0)+24] as a PC and extending it to the multivariate case.

Index-Efficient SNARKs: We present an application of PCs with sublinear prover to construct SNARKs where the prover can be sublinear, after preprocessing, in the size of the NP relation – what is usually referred to as the "index". We call this property index-efficiency. To build index-efficient SNARKs, we refine the popular framework to build SNARKs from polynomial interactive oracle proofs (PIOPs) and PCs [\[CHM](#page-34-6)+20[,BFS20\]](#page-33-4) showing how index-efficient SNARKs can be constructed black-box from index-efficient PIOPs and PCs with sublinear prover. Next, to instantiate this framework, our main technical contribution is a method to transform a class of standard PIOPs into index-efficient PIOPs. Interestingly, the combination of all our results yields two novel applications.

- Application 1: new general lookup arguments with o |T| prover : we present the first lookup argument for unstructured tables in which the prover is sublinear in the size of the table T, while making only black-box use of a VC and thus allowing instantiations from generic assumptions such as collision-resistant hash functions. As mentioned earlier in the paper, prior lookup arguments with sublinear provers make non-black-box use of cryptographic schemes [\[BFR](#page-33-2)+13[,BC23\]](#page-33-3), rely on pairings [\[ZBK](#page-35-1)+22[,ZGK](#page-36-2)+22[,EFG22,](#page-34-3)[CFF](#page-34-4)+24], or can only deal with structured tables [\[STW24\]](#page-35-2).
- Application 2: transparent SNARKs for circuits with o #gates(+) prover : Our second application is the first universal SNARK for circuits (with transparent setup) in which the prover is sublinear in the number of addition gates. This also gives us the first SNARK of this kind with a transparent setup. Prior SNARKs in which the prover running time does not depend on the number of addition gates are either circuit-specific [\[GGPR13,](#page-34-7)[Gro16\]](#page-35-6) or, if universal, ([\[GKM](#page-34-8)+18]) do rely on structured reference strings that need a trusted setup.

Witness-Succinct UC-SNARKs: We present an application of PCs with sublinear prover, like the ones we constructed above, to build a compiler from simulation-extractable SNARKs to UCsecure SNARKs, in which the prover is quasilinear in the size of the witness. We design our compiler by extending the one of Ganesh et al. [\[GKO](#page-34-0)+23]. However, while the compiler of [\[GKO](#page-34-0)+23] yields schemes with quadratic proving time, ours achieves approximately linear (i.e. O(n 1+ϵ ) proving time.

## <span id="page-5-0"></span>1.2 Overview of our techniques

PCs with fast opening. Kedlaya and Umans (KU) [\[KU11](#page-35-4)[,KU08\]](#page-35-5) show how a univariate polynomial f with d coefficients can be preprocessed and stored as a data structure DS of O(d 1+ϵ ) elements. Following this step, it is possible to evaluate y = f(x) by simply querying DS on poly(log d) points (see Theorem [3](#page-16-1) for the precise statement). In [\[ABC](#page-32-0)+24], Ateniese et al. show ho to make the KU-based data structure verifiable by using a vector commitment (VC) on top of it. The idea is to use the VC to commit to the data structure DS encoding the preprocessing of the polynomial f. At evaluation time, to prove y = f(x), the prover opens the k = poly(log d) values required to evaluate f at x, and the verifiers run the KU evaluation algorithm themselves using those k points and check a VC opening for each of them. If the VC has (poly)logarithmic opening proofs, then the entire opening proof for our PC is sublinear in |f|, and so is the running time of the verifier.

In this paper, we extend this PC construction to work for m-variate polynomials of degree d, relying on the KU's preprocessing result for multivariate polynomials. The complexity of KU's technique in this case has been explored in [\[LMW23\]](#page-35-7), showing that one evaluation can be computed with poly(d, m) queries to the data structure DS obtained after preprocessing, and that the size of DS is d <sup>m</sup> · poly(d, m) · O(mm) (see Theorem [2\)](#page-15-1). Observe that the query complexity poly(d, m) is sublinear in the size of the multivariate polynomial |f| = d <sup>m</sup>. In our PC scheme, as in [\[ABC](#page-32-0)+24], we use a VC to commit to DS and to open the poly(d, m) values needed to compute the evaluation. We highlight that, in the multivariate case, DS is not of polynomial size in |f| for every choice of d and m (a necessary requirement for having a polynomial-time PC). This is because the size of the data structure depends on the factor  $m^m$ , as described above. To avoid this issue, we show a selection of parameters that yield a PC with polynomial-time preprocessing time and space. We provide more details in Section 3, where we focus in particular on going beyond constant arity m. Concretely, we show the following consequence of Theorem 2 and our PC construction:

Corollary 2 (Informal). Assuming collision resistance hash functions, there exists a PC scheme for polynomials in  $\mathbb{Z}_q[X_1,\ldots,X_m],\ q\approx 2^{\lambda}$ , with the following running times

- For univariate polynomials (m = 1) of degree at most  $d \in \mathsf{poly}(\lambda)$ , the committing time is  $\mathsf{poly}(\lambda, \log d) \cdot O(d^{1+\epsilon})$  and the opening time is  $\mathsf{poly}(\lambda, \log d)$  for every positive constant  $\epsilon > 0$ .
- For multivariate polynomials f where  $\deg_1(f), \ldots, \deg_m(f) < d$ ,  $|f| := d^m = \operatorname{poly}(\lambda)$  and where  $d = \log^{3/\epsilon}(|f|)$  (which implies  $m = \frac{\epsilon}{3}\log(|f|)/(\log\log(|f|))$ ) for an (arbitrary) constant  $\epsilon > 0$ , we have committing time  $\operatorname{poly}(\lambda, m, d) \cdot O(d^{m(1+\epsilon)})$  and opening time  $\operatorname{poly}(\lambda, m, d)$ .

The result above does not directly cover the relevant case, for many applications, of multilinear m-variate polynomials (d=2 and  $|f|=2^m=\mathsf{poly}(\lambda)$ ); however, we will show how it can still be used in a concrete application in such a setting, namely in our construction of index-efficient PIOPs.

One of our contributions with respect to this construction of PC with sublinear prover is to prove that it satisfies *evaluation binding*, the standard security notion for PCs, which informally says that for any committed string cm (even a maliciously generated one), it is infeasible to produce valid openings for the same evaluation point x and two different outputs. In our PC construction, this security notion is inherited by the *position binding* security property of the underlying VC, which guarantees that given a committed vector DS it is infeasible to find an index i and two different valid openings of DS at i (see Theorem 4).

Stronger notions of security for PC can be defined as follows: strong evaluation binding [LM19,CFT22] says that, for any sequence of valid opening proofs for evaluations  $y_i = f(x_i)$ , there must exist a (univariate or multivariate depending on the polynomial space) polynomial f of degree d such that  $f(x_i) = y_i$ . Even stronger is knowledge soundness which says that such polynomial can be extracted from such proofs.

We first show (Appendix B) that our combination of KU with VC, does *not* satisfy strong evaluation binding. Intuitively the reason is that there are many more possible tables DS than polynomials of degree d and therefore simply committing to a table does not readily identify a *single* polynomial.<sup>7</sup> We then discuss (Appendix C) possible ways to achieve strong evaluation binding and/or knowledge soundness by augmenting the commitment phase with a SNARK to guarantee that the DS committed by the VC is indeed the result of a correctly executed preprocessing on the polynomial f.

**Index-Efficient SNARKs.** Our first step towards constructing index-efficient SNARKs is an adaptation of the popular framework for building SNARKs from PIOPs and PCs [CHM $^+$ 20,BFS20]. In brief, in a PIOP a computation F, also known as the "index", is "arithmetized" and preprocessed into a small set of polynomials to which the verifier has oracle access. Next, prover and verifier interact: the prover sends oracle polynomials and the verifier sends random challenges. Finally, the

<span id="page-6-0"></span><sup>&</sup>lt;sup>7</sup> Think about a large data structure such that a part encodes evaluations of f and the second part encodes evaluations to f' where  $f \neq f'$ .

verifier can query, on a small set of points, all the polynomials sent by the prover as well as the index polynomials. The compilation of the idealized PIOP into a succinct argument consists into replacing the oracles by commitments, while the queries of the verifier are replaced by having the prover evaluate the polynomials and prove these evaluations with the PC scheme.

To achieve index-efficiency, we adapt this compiler with three main changes. (i) We assume that the PIOP is index-efficient, namely the PIOP prover is sublinear in the size of the index. (ii) We commit to the index polynomials using a PC scheme with sublinear prover. (iii) For every query of the PIOP verifier to the index polynomials, we let the prover use fast-evaluation techniques to compute the result and the sub-linear proof generation to compute the evaluation proof.

Equipped with this framework and with our PC construction, then we turn our attention to constructing index-efficient PIOPs. To take advantage of existing literature, we tackle the problem by proposing a transformation from a class of standard PIOPs to index-efficient PIOPs. Roughly speaking, our transformation applies to PIOPs in which the prover algorithm can be implemented using a small number of evaluations of the index polynomials. While in the original PIOP each of these evaluations must be fully computed by the prover (in at least linear time), in our case we exploit preprocessing techniques and replace them by fast evaluations, e.g., using [KU08]. This step is clearly natural. However, the main technical obstacle is that PIOPs normally work with multilinear polynomials (which is the most popular case in the literature) but the known results for preprocessing schemes only capture either univariate polynomials or multivariate polynomials with a very specific choice of parameters m and d, which in particular requires a degree larger than 1 to achieve efficiency.

For this reason, we additionally make the assumption that our starting class of standard PIOPs satisfy the property that, when we replace each multilinear polynomial produced by the indexer algorithm by a polynomial of degree d>1 that agrees with it when evaluated on the boolean hypercube, the PIOP is still correct and knowledge sound. This assumption seems to be a natural property satisfied by most PIOP constructions, e.g., [Set20,CBBZ23,STW24]. See Theorem 6 for the concrete statement of these requirements.

Then, the idea of the transformation (Construction 2) is that we can use a "change of basis" technique as follows. We encode a multilinear n-variate polynomial  $\tilde{f}$  with a degree-d m-variate polynomial  $\hat{f}$  where m < n and  $(d+1)^m = 2^n$ . We show how to construct a degree-d polynomial f in n variables such that: (a) f agrees with  $\tilde{f}$  on the boolean hypercube; (b) an evaluation  $f(\mathbf{z})$  can be efficiently obtained from an evaluation  $\hat{f}(G(\mathbf{z}))$ , where G is a linear function. These two properties yield the desired outcome. By (a) and our assumptions on the PIOP we can replace  $\tilde{f}$  by f in the PIOP while preserving correctness and soundness (albeit with a larger loss  $d/|\mathbb{F}|$ ).

By property (b) we get fast evaluations since we have freedom to choose the parameters m and d of  $\hat{f}$  in a suitable way to allow fast evaluation techniques.

We refer to Section 5.2 for more details.

Table-Efficient Lookup Arguments. Our application to table-efficient lookup arguments derives by observing that (a slight generalization of) the GeneralizedLasso protocol from [STW24] is a PIOP that satisfies all the conditions needed for our transformation. In particular, in this protocol the indexer produces a multilinear polynomial encoding of the (possibly unstructured) table. This observation, in combination with all the results described earlier yields the first table-efficient lookup argument for unstructured tables that makes only black-box use of a VC and thus allows instantiations from generic assumptions such as collision-resistant hash functions.

Index-Efficient SNARKs for Circuits. The main ingredient that yields our application to index-efficient SNARKs for circuits, where the prover is sublinear in the number of addition gates, is an index-efficient PIOP for linear checks. In this PIOP, after a preprocessing of the "index" matrix M of dimension N × N, one can prove that M · z = v by running in time Npolylog(N), that is sublinear in N<sup>2</sup> . We obtain this index-efficient PIOP for linear checks by applying our transformation to a PIOP based on Thaler's matrix multiplication protocol [\[Tha13\]](#page-35-10) where, we show, it is possible to implement the prover using O(N log N) evaluation calls of the multilinear polynomial M˜ (·) interpolating M. Then we can replace each of these calls with a fast evaluation that costs polylog(N). Finally, we obtain the index-efficient SNARK for circuits by using the indexefficient PIOP for linear checks to build an index-efficient PIOP for R1CS where the prover is sublinear in the number of nonzero entries of the index matrices[8](#page-8-0) , and then applying our indexefficient compiler that uses PCs with fast openings.

Efficient SE-to-UC SNARK compiler The Universal Composability (UC) framework is considered the "gold-standard" of security for cryptographic protocols. It ensures that protocols remain secure even when composed with others in arbitrary environments, which is a crucial property for SNARKs since they are almost always used in the context of larger protocols. However, a fundamental challenge arises when trying to prove UC-security for SNARKs: the "knowledge extractor" has to be "straight-line" (non-rewinding) and black-box (i.e., having only access to the I/O of the adversary). Prior work has tackled this problem but commonly it suffers from losing succinctness or being restricted to arguments with a very specific structure[9](#page-8-1) .

Our starting point instead is the generic compiler by Ganesh et al. [\[GKO](#page-34-0)+23] which builds a UCsecure witness-succinct[10](#page-8-2) SNARK generically from any simulation-extractable one. Their approach can be informally described as follows

- The witness w is represented as a polynomial f<sup>w</sup> via a redundant randomized encoding, which is committed with a PC cm<sup>w</sup> = PC.Com(fw);
- The SE-SNARK is used to prove that w is a valid witness and that cm<sup>w</sup> is a valid PC commitment to the correct randomized polynomial for w;
- r evaluations of f<sup>w</sup> are provided to the verifier together with their evaluation proofs with respect to f<sup>w</sup>

In the last step r is chosen small enough to guarantee succinctness of the final proof. Additionally the r evaluations must satisfy a particular property meant to enforce a "proof of work" by the prover (generalizing Fischlin's approach [\[Fis05\]](#page-34-10)). This step requires the prover to actually evaluate f<sup>w</sup> at a linear number of points (to find the r ones that satisfy the property – only this small fraction of them is revealed), and from this high number of queries, the extractor (which sees all the queries the prover makes, not just the r revealed ones) can reconstruct the witness. No knowledge about w is leaked due to two facts: (i) w is encoded via a randomized polynomial, and r is small enough;

(ii) the PC they use (KZG [\[KZG10\]](#page-35-3)) has the "evaluation-hiding" property that an opening proof for a particular point reveals no information about the value of the polynomial at other points.

This approach however blows up the complexity of the prover: evaluating f<sup>w</sup> at a linear number of points, without preprocessing, yields a quadratic total running time. A naive application of our sublinear opening PC does not work because the KU technique does not guarantee that our PC is evaluation-hiding (see Section [6\)](#page-27-0). Therefore, we modify the [\[GKO](#page-34-0)+23] compiler in two major ways:

- 1. To achieve hiding, we use a different hybrid encoding for the witness. We first encrypt it ct = Enc(sk, w) using any symmetric encryption scheme. We then encode sk with a randomized polynomial g, while the ciphertext ct is instead encoded deterministically as a polynomial f (e.g. by parsing it as coefficients over a field)
- 2. We use two different PCs to commit to our hybrid encoding of the witness. The randomized polynomial g is committed via KZG [\[KZG10\]](#page-35-3), while f is committed using our KU-based PC with fast opening.

Note that our scheme is approximately linear for the Prover since the openings of f take sublinear time with our PC (note that the degree of g is independent of the size of w so those openings do not count), with the additional cost for the prover of O(n 1+ϵ ) for preprocessing. Zero-Knowledge is preserved because (as in [\[GKO](#page-34-0)+23]) the randomization of the encoding of sk means that no knowledge is leaked about sk even after r evaluations are revealed, due also to the fact that sk is committed with an evaluation-hiding PC. The r evaluations may leak knowledge of the value ct (since that is encoded deterministically, and the PC is not evaluation-hiding), but these amount to encryptions of w under sk; therefore, they hide w since no further knowledge is leaked on sk.

The reader can find an expanded version of our technical overview on our UC-SNARK applications at the beginning of Section [6](#page-27-0).

## <span id="page-9-0"></span>1.3 Related Work

As we discussed earlier, the KU polynomial preprocessing technique was introduced as a tool in the design of cryptographic protocols by Lin et al. who built a Doubly Efficient Private Information Retrival (DEPIR) scheme [\[LMW23\]](#page-35-7). A Private Information Retrieval (PIR) scheme, is a protocol between a Server holding a database DB and a Client who queries DB on an index i. At the end of the protocol the Client learns DB(i) while the Server has no information on what index was queried. The crucial property of PIR is that communication between the parties be sublinear in N = |DB| (otherwise the solution is trivial by having the Server send the entire DB to the Client). Before [\[LMW23\]](#page-35-7) it was widely believed that any PIR scheme required the Server to work in Ω(N) steps, since if the Server did not read every item in the database, he would get information about i. Surprisingly [\[LMW23\]](#page-35-7) shows that by preprocessing DB into a data structure DSDB using KU it is possible to answer a query by only looking at a O(log<sup>c</sup> N) entries in DSDB yielding a "doubly efficient" PIR for both Server and Client.

Ateniese et al. [\[ABC](#page-32-0)+24] showed that the KU (univariate) technique can be used to improve the efficiency of proofs of storage. Assume that a storage provider needs to prove to a client that it is storing a file F. One approach is to encode F as a univariate polynomial f. Then, by querying the polynomial at a random point, the client can be assured that the file is still stored (indeed, the file can theoretically be retrieved by querying a sufficient number of points to allow interpolation of f). However, this approach requires the provider to read the entire file every time in order to evaluate f. By using KU, the file is encoded into a larger data structure DS, which allows the provider to answer in sublinear time.

**Discussion and Future Work** On practical instantiations: We acknowledge that our work is unlikely to be *immediately* practical due to the large constants in the specific preprocessing scheme [KU11] that we use. Given the many practical applications that motivated this work, and that we show can be built from PC with fast opening, it is an important research question to investigate different approaches for polynomial preprocessing, with the goal of getting schemes that are more practically feasible.

More functionalities with sublinear proving? We showcase our framework for general relations provable in sublinear time, index-efficient PIOPs, by obtaining a new construction of table-efficient lookup arguments and index-efficient SNARK for circuits. Yet interesting open questions remain: Can we build index-efficient PIOPs for other relations that would be surprising to prove in sublinear time (and from relatively simple tools)? For what kinds of relations is it even conceivable to achieve sublinear proving?

More properties/applications for commitments with fast openings? To go beyond evaluation binding, our PC construction must be augmented with a SNARK. Is that inherent, or can strong binding/extractability for PC with fast opening be achieved with simpler tools/weaker assumptions? What about hiding (which as we point out, does not readily hold for our scheme)? Can we build such a commitment directly for multilinear polynomials, rather than go through the workaround we use for index-efficient PIOPs? What are other settings, beyond the one in this work—UC-NIZK compiler—where amortizing an expensive preprocessing yields a better efficiency overall?

For a finite set S, we write  $a \leftarrow S$  to mean that a is sampled uniformly at random from S. ## <span id="page-11-0"></span>2.1 Polynomial Commitment

A polynomial commitment scheme allows a prover to commit to a (possibly multivariate) polynomial f ∈ R[X1, . . . , Xm], producing a commitment value cm, and then compute proofs π<sup>x</sup> demonstrating that y is the correct evaluation of the committed f at an input x ∈ Rm.

Definition 1 (Polynomial Commitment [\[KZG10\]](#page-35-3)). A polynomial commitment scheme PC for multivariate polynomials over a ring R is defined by the following algorithms:

- Setup(1<sup>λ</sup> , m, d) → ck: On input the security parameter λ, the number of variables m, and a bound d for the degree of each variable, the setup algorithm outputs a public commitment key ck, which implicitly defines the message space of the commitment scheme Pm,d = {f ∈ R[X1, . . . , Xm] | deg<sup>i</sup> (f) < d, ∀i ∈ [m]}.
- Com(ck, f; r) → (cm, aux): On input the commitment key ck, a polynomial f ∈ Pm,d, and (possibly) a randomness r, the commit algorithm outputs a commitment cm and an auxiliary information aux.
- Open(ck, x, aux) → πx: On input the commitment key ck, a point x ∈ Rm, and auxiliary information aux, the open algorithm outputs a proof πx.
- Ver(ck, cm, x, y, πx) → b: On input the commitment key ck, the commitment cm, x ∈ Rm, y ∈ R, and a proof πx, the verification algorithm outputs b ∈ {0, 1}.

Correctness ensures that honestly generated proofs always pass verification. See Appendix [A.1](#page-36-1) for the formal definition. Furthermore, we are interested in (fully) succinct schemes, i.e., the sizes of commitments and proofs, as well as the running time of Ver, are sublinear in the size of the polynomial.

<span id="page-11-1"></span>Definition 2 (Succinctness of PC). We say that a polynomial commitment scheme PC is succinct if the size of the commitments produced by Com, the proofs produced by Open and the running time of Ver are sublinear (e.g., polylogarithmic) in the size of the polynomial.

A polynomial commitment scheme must satisfy at least one of the following security properties, which we define in order from weaker to stronger:

- Binding. It is computationally infeasible to find two different polynomials f ̸= f ′ (and two randomnesses r and r ′ that produce the same commitment cm.
- Weak evaluation binding. It is computationally infeasible to produce a verifying proof π<sup>x</sup> for an incorrect evaluation y ̸= f(x) when the commitment cm of f (required for verification) is honestly computed.
- Evaluation binding. It extends the guarantees of weak evaluation binding even in the presence of a maliciously computed commitment cm.

Strong evaluation binding. It guarantees that, for any maliciously chosen sequence of verifying proofs  $\pi_i$  for evaluations  $y_i$  and points  $x_i$ , there must exist a polynomial  $f \in \mathcal{P}_{m,d}$  (where  $\mathcal{P}_{m,d}$  is the supported domain of the polynomial commitment scheme) such that  $f(x_i) = y_i$ .

**Knowledge soundness.** This last property says that it exists of an efficient non-black-box extractor able to extract the committed polynomial f if the corresponding adversary is able to provide verifying proofs.

Below, we provide the formal definition of evaluation binding. We refer the reader to Appendix A.1 for the remaining ones.

**Definition 3 (Evaluation Binding of PC).** A polynomial commitment scheme PC satisfies evaluation binding if for any PPT adversary A, the following holds:

<span id="page-12-4"></span><span id="page-12-2"></span>
$$\Pr\left[ \begin{array}{c} \mathsf{Ver}(\mathsf{ck},\mathsf{cm},x,y,\pi_x) = 1 \land \\ \mathsf{Ver}(\mathsf{ck},\mathsf{cm},x,y',\pi_x') = 1 \land \ y \neq y' \ \middle| \ \mathsf{ck} \leftarrow \mathsf{Setup}(1^\lambda,m,d) \\ (\mathsf{cm},x,y,\pi_x,y',\pi_x') \leftarrow \mathcal{A}(\mathsf{ck}) \end{array} \right] \in \mathsf{negl}(\lambda).$$

The following theorem relates the above five security definitions. Such relations are standard and we refer the reader to [CFT22,BCFL23] for more details.

**Theorem 1.** Let PC be a correct polynomial commitment scheme. Then,

- 1. If PC is weak evaluation binding then PC is also binding.
- 2. If PC is evaluation binding then PC is weak evaluation binding.
- 3. If PC is strong evaluation binding then PC is evaluation binding.
- 4. If PC satisfies non-black-box (resp. black-box) knowledge soundness then PC is strong evaluation binding.

#### <span id="page-12-0"></span>2.2 Vector Commitments

A vector commitment (VC) scheme permits to commit on vector of messages and later open individual positions. We make use of the standard definition of VC composed of algorithms (Setup, Com, Open, Ver), satisfying correctness and position binding (see Appendix A.2 for more details.).

<span id="page-12-3"></span>We are interested in succinct vector commitments schemes, meaning that the sizes of commitments and proofs, as well as the running time of Ver, are sublinear in the size of the vector.

**Definition 4 (Succinctness of VC).** We say that VC is succinct if the size of the commitments produced by Com, the proofs produced by Open and the running time of Ver are  $poly(\lambda, \log n)$  where n is the size of the vector.

In addition to the above, we formalize the notion of vector commitments in which the opening algorithm is "fast", i.e., polylogarithmic after a one-time preprocessing which happens at commitment time. To model that the preprocessing cost of the commitment can be amortized, our definition requires Com to run in quasilinear time.

<span id="page-12-1"></span>**Definition 5** (VC with Fast Opening). We say that a vector commitment scheme VC has fast openings if the running time  $T_{Com}(\lambda, n)$  of Com is  $poly(\lambda, \log n) \cdot n$ , and the running time  $T_{Open}(\lambda, n)$  of Open is  $poly(\lambda, \log n)$ .

A natural instantiation of VC satisfying position binding, succinctness, and fast openings, is Merkle tree [Mer88], which can be built from collusion resistant hash functions (CHRF). Other instantiations are discussed in Appendix A.2.

#### <span id="page-13-0"></span>2.3 SNARKs for Indexed Relations

We recall the definition of succinct non-interactive arguments of knowledge (SNARKs) for indexed relations.

<span id="page-13-4"></span>**Definition 6 (SNARK).** A SNARK for an indexed relation  $\mathcal{R}$  is defined by the following algorithms:

Setup( $1^{\lambda}$ )  $\rightarrow$  srs: On input the security parameter  $\lambda$ , the randomized setup algorithm returns a structured reference srs.

Index(srs, i)  $\rightarrow$  (pk<sub>i</sub>, vk<sub>i</sub>) On input the structured references srs and an index i, the deterministic indexing algorithm outputs a proving key pk<sub>i</sub> and a verification key vk<sub>i</sub>.

Prove( $pk_i, x, w$ )  $\to \pi$  On input the proving key  $pk_i$  (for index i), a statement-witness pair (x, w) such that  $(i, x, w) \in \mathcal{R}$ , the randomized proving algorithm outputs a proof  $\pi$ .

 $\mathsf{Ver}(\mathsf{vk}_{i}, x, \pi) \to b$  On input the verification key  $\mathsf{vk}_{i}$  (for index i), a statement x, and proof  $\pi$ , the deterministic verification algorithm outputs a bit  $b \in \{0, 1\}$ .

<span id="page-13-5"></span>**Definition 7 (Correctness of SNARK).** A SNARK for an indexed relation  $\mathcal{R}$  is correct if for any  $(i, x, w) \in \mathcal{R}$  we have:

$$\Pr\left[ \mathsf{Ver}(\mathsf{vk}_{\underline{i}}, \underline{\mathbb{x}}, \pi) = 1 \left| \begin{matrix} (\mathsf{pk}_{\underline{i}}, \mathsf{vk}_{\underline{i}}) \leftarrow \mathsf{Index}(\mathsf{Setup}(1^{\lambda}), \underline{i}) \\ \pi \leftarrow \mathsf{Prove}(\mathsf{pk}_{\underline{i}}, \underline{\mathbb{x}}, \underline{\mathbb{w}}) \end{matrix} \right| = 1.$$

<span id="page-13-7"></span>**Definition 8 (Succinctness of SNARK).** A SNARK is succinct if Prove outputs proofs of size  $poly(\lambda, \log |w|)$ , the running time of Prove is  $poly(\lambda, |x|, |w|)$ , and the running time of Ver is  $poly(\lambda, |x|, \log |w|)$ .

<span id="page-13-6"></span>**Definition 9 (Knowledge soundness of SNARK).** A SNARK for an indexed relation  $\mathcal{R}$  satisfies knowledge soundness if for any PPT adversary  $\mathcal{A}$ , there exists a polynomial-time extractor  $\mathsf{Ext}_{\mathcal{A}}$  (running on the same input of  $\mathcal{A}$ , including its random coins) such that:

$$\Pr \begin{bmatrix} \mathsf{Ver}(\mathsf{vk}_{\dot{\mathbb{i}}}, \mathbb{x}, \pi) = 1 \\ \wedge \ (\dot{\mathbb{i}}, \mathbb{x}, \mathbb{w}) \notin \mathcal{R} \\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{vmatrix} \mathsf{srs} \leftarrow \mathsf{Setup}(1^{\lambda}) \\ ((\dot{\mathbb{i}}, \mathbb{x}, \pi), \mathbb{w}) \leftarrow (\mathcal{A}(\mathsf{srs}), \mathsf{Ext}_{\mathcal{A}}(\mathsf{srs})), \\ ((\mathsf{pk}_{\dot{\mathbb{i}}}, \mathsf{vk}_{\dot{\mathbb{i}}}) \leftarrow \mathsf{Index}(\mathsf{srs}, \mathsf{vk}, \dot{\mathbb{i}}) \\ \end{bmatrix} \in \mathsf{negl}(\lambda).$$

The above definition assumes a non-black-box extractor. Sometimes we make us of the *black-box knowledge soundness* flavour, which is identical to the above except that the extractor Ext is universal.

## <span id="page-13-1"></span>3 Polynomial Preprocessing Schemes

In this section, we define a building block called the polynomial preprocessing scheme (PP). Intuitively, a PP scheme abstracts algorithmic methods (such as [KU08,KU11]) that enable the fast evaluation of a polynomial  $f \in R[X_1, ..., X_m]$  at any given point  $x \in R^m$ , after performing a one-time preprocessing.<sup>12</sup> The main objective of a PP is to achieve an evaluation time complexity that is significantly lower (e.g., sublinear) than the natural polynomial evaluation, which requires reading the entire description of f, whose size is  $O(d^m \cdot \log |R|)$ .

<span id="page-13-3"></span><span id="page-13-2"></span>Alternatively, a PP scheme can be seen as the algorithmic process of building a data structure for evaluating polynomials.

- Definition 10 (Polynomial Preprocessing Scheme). A polynomial preprocessing scheme PP is defined by the following algorithms:
- Params(R, m, d) → pp: On input the description of a ring R, the number of variables m ∈ N, and the degree d ∈ N, the randomized parameter generation algorithm outputs the parameters pp that can be used for the execution of the following algorithms on m-variate polynomials of individual degree at most d. We assume the public parameters pp includes an upper bound S on the size of the data structures produced by the preprocessing algorithm below.
- PreProcess(pp, f) → DS: On input a m-variate polynomial f ∈ R[X1, . . . , Xm] of individual degree at most d, the randomized preprocess algorithm outputs a data structure DS = (v1, . . . , vS) (where S is the data structure upper bound contained in p). We model DS as a vector in M<sup>S</sup> for a message space M.
- Lookup(pp, x) → (i<sup>j</sup> )j∈[k] : On input the public parameters pp and a point x ∈ Rm, the deterministic lookup algorithm outputs k indices i1, . . . , i<sup>k</sup> ∈ [S].
- Reconstruct(pp, x, v1, ..., vk) → y: On input the public parameters pp, an evaluation point x ∈ Rm, and k values v1, . . . , v<sup>k</sup> ∈ M, the deterministic reconstruct algorithm outputs a value y ∈ R.

Looking ahead, we are interested into efficient PP schemes which access only a "small" subset (vi<sup>k</sup> )i∈[k] of the data structure DS (i.e., k ≪ |D|). To accomplish this, it is necessary to work in the RAM model of computation to efficiently retrieve (vi<sup>k</sup> )i∈[k] from DS.

Correctness says that the reconstruction algorithm computes f(x) when (v1, . . . , vk) = (DSi<sup>1</sup> , . . . , DSi<sup>k</sup> ), for DS the data structure computed by the preprocess algorithm and (i1, . . . , ik) the indices outputted by the lookup algorithm.

<span id="page-14-1"></span>Definition 11 (Correctness of PP). A polynomial preprocessing scheme PP is correct for a ring R if for any integers m, d ∈ N, any polynomial f ∈ R[X1, . . . , Xm] such that deg<sup>1</sup> (f), . . . , degm(f) < d, and for any point x ∈ Rm, we have Reconstruct(pp, x, vi<sup>1</sup> , . . . , vi<sup>k</sup> ) = f(x) where pp ← Params(R, m, d), (vi)i∈[S] ← PreProcess(pp, f), and (i1, . . . , ik) ← Lookup(pp, x).

The property below capture the main benefit of using a PP scheme, i.e., enabling fast polynomial evaluation (compared to reading the entire polynomial) at the price of having a one-time preprocessing. We observe that the definition below allows PreProcess to be "slow" and produce data structures of size larger than |f| = d <sup>m</sup>. This is because, to enable fast evaluation, such schemes (e.g., [\[KU11,](#page-35-4)[KU08\]](#page-35-5)) encode additional and redundant information into DS.

<span id="page-14-2"></span>Definition 12 (Efficiency of PP). A polynomial preprocessing scheme PP satisfies efficiency for R if, for every d, m ∈ N, for every polynomial f ∈ R[X1, . . . , Xm] such that deg<sup>1</sup> (f), . . . , degm(f) < d, we have:

- 1. the running time of both Lookup and Reconstruct is poly(log |R|, log d) (resp. poly(log |R|, d, m)) for the univariate case m = 1 (resp. multivariate case m > 1).[13](#page-14-0)
- 2. the running time of PreProcess and the size S of the data structure DS that it produces are poly(log |R|, dm) (ideally slightly superlinear in d <sup>m</sup>).

Remark 1 (Comparison with [\[ABC](#page-32-0)+24]). In [\[ABC](#page-32-0)+24], Ateniese et al. propose an abstraction with the same intent as ours: abstracting any algorithmic method of preprocessing polynomials.

<span id="page-14-0"></span><sup>13</sup> We explicate a different bound (i.e., poly(log |R|, log d)) for univariate polynomial since applying the multivariate one (for m = 1) would result in having a meaningless efficiency definition since |f| ∈ poly(log |R|, d).

Despite both abstractions ([\[ABC](#page-32-0)+24] and ours) having the same final objective, there are crucial differences.

The abstraction by Ateniese et al., named the data structure for univariate polynomial evaluation,[14](#page-15-2) is composed of two algorithms: (i) GenData, which computes the data structure given the polynomial to be evaluated, and (ii) Eval, which outputs f(x) given the entire data structure and the point x. This abstraction does not explicitly highlight that these algorithms leverage the RAM model to achieve efficiency. In particular, Eval hides inside the fact that a subset of the large data structure (provided as input) is retrieved to compute the final output y. For this reason, [\[ABC](#page-32-0)+24] formalizes, alongside the abstraction, the notion of localized RAM computation, which captures the idea that if a RAM computation outputs y by reading a subset of a large input (e.g., a data structure), then there exists a method (the localized RAM computation) to compute the same output using only the subset accessed by the original computation. In other words, the original computation depends only on a subset of the large input.

In this regard, our abstraction is simpler and makes this aspect more explicit. In a nutshell, Definition [10](#page-13-3) decouples the RAM algorithm Eval of [\[ABC](#page-32-0)+24] into two separate ones: Lookup entitled to identify the subset (determined by the indexes (i1, . . . , ik)) of the data structure DS = (v1, . . . , vS) that must be accessed to compute the correct output, and Reconstruct which computes the actual output y given the subset (vi<sup>1</sup> , . . . , vi<sup>k</sup> ) ⊆ DS. We emphasize that our abstraction captures PP schemes such that indexes computed by Lookup depend only on the point x, which is indeed the case for the data structures in [\[KU11,](#page-35-4)[KU08\]](#page-35-5).

## <span id="page-15-0"></span>3.1 Kedlaya-Umans technique as PP scheme

In [\[KU08\]](#page-35-5), Kedlaya and Umans describe an elegant and efficient method for the fast evaluation of polynomials with preprocessing. Their method (which is the one we focus on in this work) is perfectly captured by our PP abstraction. For the sake of our results, we focus on the case of the ring of integers Zq, as also summarized in [\[LMW23](#page-35-7)[,LMW22,](#page-35-13) Theorem A.4].

<span id="page-15-1"></span>Theorem 2 (Kedlaya-Umans PP scheme [\[LMW22,](#page-35-13) Theorem A.4]). Let q ∈ N. There exists an efficient polynomial preprocessing scheme for the ring R = Z<sup>q</sup> and for m-variate polynomials f ∈ R[X1, . . . , Xm] of individual degree deg<sup>1</sup> (f), . . . , degm(f) < d.

In more details, the efficiency of KU polynomial preprocessing scheme is defined as follows:

- 1. the running time of both Lookup and Reconstruct is poly(log q, d, m);
- 2. the running time of PreProcess and the size S of the data structure DS that it produces are d <sup>m</sup> · poly(log q, m, d) · O(m(log m + log d + log log q))m.

In the remaining part of this work, we refer to the aforementioned PP scheme as KU-based PP.

When focusing on polynomial-time algorithms (as in this work), we can easily observe that parameters matter when considering Theorem [2.](#page-15-1) Indeed, preprocessing some poly(λ)-size polynomials using the KU-based PP scheme of Theorem [2](#page-15-1) can cause the preprocessing algorithm PreProcess (and the corresponding size S of the output data structure) to be exponential in the security parameter λ. For instance, PreProcess runs in exponential time if we consider: (i) poly(λ)-variate polynomials (due to the O(mm) term present in Theorem [2\)](#page-15-1), or (ii) O(log λ)-variate polynomials of individual degree d ∈ poly(λ) (due to the d <sup>m</sup> term in Theorem [2\)](#page-15-1).

<span id="page-15-2"></span><sup>14</sup> The term "univariate" comes from the fact that [\[ABC](#page-32-0)<sup>+</sup>24]'s abstraction only covers univariate polynomials over Zp. In contrast, we consider m-variate polynomials over arbitrary rings R.

Unfortunately, even the case of multilinear m-variate polynomials, where  $2^m = N = \mathsf{poly}(\lambda)$ , implies PreProcess to run in superpolynomial time due to  $O(m^m) = O(N^{\log N})$ . This is the use case arising in most applications of polynomial commitments to SNARKs where multilinear polynomials are used to encode N-long vectors via interpolation and thus we cannot apply the KU preprocessing technique in a straightforward way to this important use case.

For this reason, in the following corollary we show a selection of parameters to instantiate Theorem 2 in such a way that the preprocessing (resp. evaluation) time is slightly *superlinear* (resp. *polylogarithmic*) in the size of the polynomial. These will be the parameters of interest in this work.

<span id="page-16-4"></span>Corollary 1 (KU-based PP scheme for multivariate polynomials). Let  $\lambda \in \mathbb{N}$  be a security parameter, and let q, d, N such that  $q \approx 2^{\lambda}$ ,  $N = d^m \in \mathsf{poly}(\lambda)$ , and  $d = \log^{3/\epsilon}(N)$  for some constant  $\epsilon > 0$ . Then, there exists a (KU-based) PP scheme for m-variate polynomials  $f \in \mathbb{Z}_q[X_1, \ldots, X_m]$  of individual degree  $\mathsf{deg}_1(f), \ldots, \mathsf{deg}_m(f) < d$  achieving the following efficiency property:

- 1. the running time of both Lookup and Reconstruct is poly(log q, d, m);
- 2. the running time of PreProcess and the size S of the data structure DS that it produces are  $O(N^{1+\epsilon}) \cdot \operatorname{poly}(\log q, \log N)$ .

*Proof.* Observe that our parameters' choice gives  $m = \frac{\epsilon}{3} \log(N)/(\log \log N)$ . Hence, the proof follows by leveraging Theorem 2 by using the following bounds (similar to [LMW23,LMW22]):<sup>15</sup>  $m^m \leq (\log N)^{\frac{\epsilon}{3} \log(N)/(\log \log N)} = N^{\epsilon/3}$ , and  $O(\log m + \log d + \log \lambda)^m \leq O(\log N)^m \leq O(1)^m (\log N)^m \leq O(N^{\epsilon/3})N^{\epsilon/3}$ .

As observed, the parameters in Theorem 2 do not result in an efficient PP scheme for univariate polynomials. Specifically, when m=1, the evaluation time becomes polynomial in the degree d, which is at least as large (or potentially larger) than the size of the polynomial itself. However, for the univariate case, we can obtain polylogarithmic efficiency by encoding the degree d univariate polynomial into a multivariate one. This is essentially the result shown in [KU11, Theorem 5.1], which we report below.

<span id="page-16-1"></span>Theorem 3 (KU-based PP scheme for univariate polynomials [KU11, Theorem 5.1]). Let  $\lambda \in \mathbb{N}$  be a security parameter, and let q, d such that  $q \approx 2^{\lambda}$ ,  $d \in \text{poly}(\lambda)$ . Then, there exists a (KU-based) PP scheme for univariate polynomials  $f \in \mathbb{Z}_q[X]$  of degree  $\deg(f) < d$  achieving the following efficiency property:

- 1. the running time of both Lookup and Reconstruct is poly(log q, log d);
- 2. the running time of PreProcess and the size S of the data structure DS that it produces are  $O(d^{1+\epsilon}) \cdot \operatorname{poly}(\log q, \log d)$ .

#### <span id="page-16-0"></span>4 Polynomial Commitments with Fast Opening

We show how to construct polynomial commitment schemes which achieve a sublinear opening time. Below, we formally define this efficiency notion, dubbed *fast openings*, which is reminiscent to that of VC (see Definition 5) but adapted to the polynomial setting.

<span id="page-16-3"></span>**Definition 13** (PC with Fast Opening). We say that a polynomial commitment scheme PC for multivariate (resp. univariate) polynomials over a ring R has fast openings if the running time

<span id="page-16-2"></span> $<sup>\</sup>overline{}^{15}$  Contrarily to [LMW23,LMW22], we do not need d to be a prime number.

TCom(λ, d, m, |R|) of Com is poly(λ, log |R|, dm) (resp. poly(λ, log |R|, d)), and the running time TOpen(λ, d, m, |R|) of Open is poly(λ, log |R|, d, m) (resp. poly(λ, log |R|, log d)), where the parameters m (number of variables) and d (maximum individual degree) are chosen on setup.[16](#page-17-1)

We highlight that achieving fast openings in PC is much more challenging than in the vector commitment setting. Given a committed vector of polynomial size, there are only a polynomial number of indices that can be opened. Thus, fast opening can be achieved by preprocessing all those proofs and leveraging the RAM model to retrieve each of these proofs when needed. However, in PC, the number of possible proofs corresponds to the cardinality of the point space R<sup>m</sup> of the target polynomials f ∈ R[X1, . . . , Xm]. For some choices of parameters (e.g., m ∈ ω(log(λ))) the cardinality of R<sup>m</sup> is exponential in λ. Hence, simply preprocessing all proofs is not feasible and would result in exponential memory usage. This is essentially the reason why research focuses on building PP schemes, i.e., efficient algorithms for polynomial evaluation at all points (such as [\[KU08,](#page-35-5)[KU11\]](#page-35-4)).[17](#page-17-2)

## <span id="page-17-0"></span>4.1 Evaluation Binding (VC-based) Polynomial Commitment with Fast Opening

Our main construction, dubbed PCVC, is obtained by combining a PP scheme (Definition [10\)](#page-13-3) with a VC that enjoys fast opening time (Definition [5\)](#page-12-1). The basic idea of the construction PCVC is that the commitment algorithm runs the preprocessing algorithm of the PP scheme and uses the VC to commit to the blocks of resulting data structure DS. Next, in order to prove an evaluation in a point x, one can give to the verifier the blocks of DS at the k indices identified by PP.Lookup for the point x, along with opening proofs for those indices. The verifier can check this proof and runs the PP.Reconstruct algorithm to recover f(x). The prove that this construction PCVC satisfies evaluation binding, yet it is provably not strong evaluation binding. Therefore, in Section [4.2](#page-19-0) we show how to build PCs with sublinear opening time that achieve stronger security.

We highlight that this technique is used in [\[ABC](#page-32-0)+24] to build a verifiable data structure for polynomial evaluation. Also, [\[ABC](#page-32-0)+24] mentions in the introduction the possibility of viewing their data structure as a polynomial commitment. However, our objective is to carefully analyze and provide a formal treatment of this statement in the setting of polynomial commitments, aiming at clarifying the extent to which security is guaranteed.

<span id="page-17-3"></span>Construction 1 Let PP be a PP scheme (Definition [10\)](#page-13-3) and VC be a vector commitment (Definition [22\)](#page-37-3) scheme for a message space {0, 1} ∗ . We build a PC scheme PCVC for multivariate polynomials over a ring R as follows:

Setup(1<sup>λ</sup> , m, d) → ck: On input the security parameter λ, the number of variables m, and a bound d for the degree of each variable, the setup algorithm computes pp ← PP.Params(R, m, d) and ckVC ← VC.Setup(1<sup>λ</sup> , S), where S is the upper bound on the size of the data structure included in pp (see Definition [10\)](#page-13-3). Finally, it outputs ck = (pp, ckVC).

Com(ck, f; r) → (cm, aux): On input the commitment key ck = (pp, ckVC), a polynomial f ∈ Pm,d, and a randomness r, the commit algorithm computes DS ← PP.PreProcess(pp, f) and (cmDS, auxDS) ← VC.Com(ckVC, DS; r) (recall that DS is a vector, i.e., DS = (v1, . . . , vS)). Finally, it outputs cm = cmDS and aux = (auxDS, DS).

<span id="page-17-2"></span><sup>17</sup> While considering PC with fast openings, a crucial requirement is the balance between memory usage (to store the precomputed data) and running time of Open.

- Open(ck, x, aux)  $\to \pi_x$ : On input the commitment key ck = (pp, ck<sub>VC</sub>), a point  $x \in R^m$ , and auxiliary information aux = (aux<sub>DS</sub>, DS) where DS =  $(v_1, \ldots, v_S)$ , the open algorithm retrieves the indexes  $(i_1, \ldots, i_k) \leftarrow \text{PP.Lookup}(pp, x)$  and compute an opening proof  $\pi_j \leftarrow \text{VC.Open}(ck_{VC}, i_j, aux_{DS})$  for every  $j \in [k]$ . Finally, it outputs  $\pi_x = (v_{i_j}, \pi_j)_{j \in [k]}$ .
- $\begin{array}{l} \operatorname{Ver}(\operatorname{ck},\operatorname{cm},x,y,\pi_x) \to b \text{:} \quad On \ \, input \ \, the \ \, commitment \ \, key \ \, \operatorname{ck} = (\operatorname{pp},\operatorname{ck}_{\operatorname{VC}}), \ \, the \ \, commitment \ \, \operatorname{cm} = (\operatorname{cm}_{\operatorname{DS}}, \ \, x \in R^m, \ \, an \ \, evaluation \ \, y \in R, \ \, and \ \, a \ \, proof \ \, \pi_x = (v_j,\pi_j)_{j\in[k]}, \ \, the \ \, verification \ \, algorithm \\ outputs \ \, b \in \{0,1\} \ \, equals \ \, to \ \, \bigwedge_{j\in[k]}\operatorname{VC.Ver}(\operatorname{ck}_{\operatorname{VC}},\operatorname{cm}_{\operatorname{DS}},i_j,v_j,\pi_j) \stackrel{?}{=} 1 \ \, \wedge \ \, y \stackrel{?}{=} \operatorname{PP.Reconstruct}(\operatorname{pp}, x,v_1,\ldots,v_k). \\ \end{array}$

<span id="page-18-0"></span>Below, we state the properties achieved by our  $PC_{VC}$  of Construction 1 and we defer the formal proof to Appendix E.1.

**Theorem 4.** Let PP, VC, and PC<sub>VC</sub> as defined in Construction 1.

- 1. If both PP and VC satisfy correctness (Definitions 11 and 23), then PC<sub>VC</sub> satisfies correctness (Definition 17).
- 2. If VC satisfies position binding (Definition 24), then PC<sub>VC</sub> satisfies evaluation binding (Definition 3).
- 3. If PP satisfies efficiency (Definition 12) and VC satisfies succinctness (Definition 4), then PC<sub>VC</sub> satisfies succinctness (Definition 2).
- 4. If PP satisfies efficiency (Definition 12) and VC satisfies fast openings (Definition 5), then PC<sub>VC</sub> satisfies fast openings (Definition 13).

Instantiations. One can obtain several instantiations of  $PC_{VC}$  by using the KU-based PP schemes for either multivariate (Corollary 1) or univariate (Theorem 3) polynomials, and a VC with fast openings such as Merkle trees [Mer88], KZG [KZG10] or [CFG<sup>+</sup>20]. The following corollary provides an example of instantiation considering  $R = \mathbb{Z}_q$  and using Merkle trees [Mer88]. For the multivariate case, we focus on particular choices of parameters d and m that yield a polynomial-time instantiation of  $PC_{VC}$ .

<span id="page-18-1"></span>Corollary 2 (Polynomial-time instantiations of  $PC_{VC}$ ). Assuming collision resistance hash functions, there exists a PC scheme  $PC_{VC}$  for multivariate (resp. univariate) polynomials over the ring  $R = \mathbb{Z}_q$  for  $q \approx 2^{\lambda}$  that satisfies correctness, succinctness, evaluation binding, and fast opening where the running times of  $PC_{VC}$ . Com and  $PC_{VC}$ . Open are defined as follows:

- For univariate polynomials of degree at most  $d \in \mathsf{poly}(\lambda)$ , we have  $\mathsf{T}_{\mathsf{PC.Com}} = \mathsf{poly}(\lambda, \log d) \cdot O(d^{1+\epsilon})$  and  $\mathsf{T}_{\mathsf{PC.Open}} = \mathsf{poly}(\lambda, \log d)$  for every positive constant  $\epsilon > 0$ .
- For m-variate polynomials such that  $\deg_1(f),\ldots,\deg_m(f)< d$ ,  $|f|:=d^m=\operatorname{poly}(\lambda)$ , and  $d=\log^{3/\epsilon}(|f|)$  (which in turn implies  $m=\frac{\epsilon}{3}\log(|f|)/(\log\log|f|)$ ), we have  $\mathsf{T}_{\mathsf{PC.Com}}=\operatorname{poly}(\lambda,m,d)\cdot O(d^{m(1+\epsilon)})$  and  $\mathsf{T}_{\mathsf{PC.Open}}=\operatorname{poly}(\lambda,m,d)$  for every positive constant  $\epsilon>0$ . Observe that for our choices of d and m, we have that  $|f|=d^m\in\operatorname{poly}(\lambda)$ .

<span id="page-18-2"></span>We are interested only in m-variate polynomials of polynomial size  $d^m \in \mathsf{poly}(\lambda)$ . Hence, it is fundamental to balance out d and m (as defined here and in Corollary 1) to enforce  $d^m \in \mathsf{poly}(\lambda)$  and  $m^m \in \mathsf{poly}(\lambda)$  (so that pre-processing can run in polynomial time) while obtaining non-trivial choices of d and m (i.e., choices which are different from just fixing O(1) degree or O(1) variables).

Lack of Strong Evaluation Binding. Theorem [4](#page-18-0) states the evaluation binding property of PCVC. A natural question is whether the same construction achieves stronger binding notions, such as strong evaluation binding or knowledge soundness. In Appendix [B,](#page-37-1) we give a negative answer to this question, showing that PCVC is not strongly evaluation binding. In a nutshell, the core functioning of PCVC relies on the usage of data structures DS, whose space is larger than that of the polynomial space Pm,d. This allows an adversary to commit to an inconsistent data structure (with one block sampled at random) whose resulting evaluations (output of PP.Reconstruct) can be interpolated only from polynomials of larger degrees (lying outside Pm,d). We provide more details in Appendix [B.](#page-37-1)

## <span id="page-19-0"></span>4.2 Beyond Evaluation Binding

In Appendix [C,](#page-40-0) we show that deploying a SNARK on top of Construction [1](#page-17-3) is sufficient to construct a knowledge-sound polynomial commitment scheme (recall that knowledge soundness implies strong evaluation binding; see Theorem [1\)](#page-12-4).[19](#page-19-2)

In a nutshell, during the execution of Com, our approach is to compute a commitment cm = cmDS (where cmDS is computed as defined in Construction [1\)](#page-17-3). In addition, we include into the auxiliary information a proof of correctness π demonstrating that DS has been computed by running PP.PreProcess. This can be accomplished by using a SNARK for the following relation:

<span id="page-19-4"></span>
$$\mathcal{R}_{\mathsf{VC},\mathsf{PP}} = \{ ((\mathsf{pp},\mathsf{ck},\mathsf{cm}),(f,r)) \mid (\mathsf{cm},\cdot) = \mathsf{VC}.\mathsf{Com}(\mathsf{ck},\mathsf{PP}.\mathsf{PreProcess}(\mathsf{pp},f);r) \} \tag{1}$$

Each time an opening proof is computed, we include π into it. Then, when verifying an opening proof, we simply perform all steps defined in Construction [1](#page-17-3) and, in addition, verify the correctness of the commitment by checking π. Observe that the resulting PC still achieves the succinctness and fast opening properties (the latter thanks to the succinctness property of SNARKs). We refer the reader to Appendix [C](#page-40-0) for more details.

Engineering the (SNARK) relation through algebraic approaches. The aforementioned approach requires a SNARK supporting RVC,PP, which, in turn, relies on the entire computation of PP.PreProcess(pp, f). By opening the box, we observe that the PP scheme of Kedlaya and Umans [\[KU08,](#page-35-5)[KU11\]](#page-35-4) has an algebraic structure. For example, the data structure DS (output by PP.PreProcess) consists of multiple small tables, each containing all evaluations of a multivariate polynomial f<sup>i</sup> (derived by manipulating the original one) modulo a small prime p<sup>i</sup> . Due to the algebraic nature of this PP scheme, we envision the possibility of engineering and simplifying the SNARK relation to achieve a faster PC. However, this direction is outside the scope of this paper, and we leave it as a possible direction for future work.

## <span id="page-19-1"></span>5 Index-Efficient SNARKs

We give applications of polynomial preprocessing schemes and PCs with fast opening to construct index-efficient SNARKs, i.e., preprocessing SNARKs in which the prover runs in time which is sub-linear in the size of the index.[20](#page-19-3)

<span id="page-19-2"></span><sup>19</sup> The resulting PC achieves non-black-box or black-box knowledge soundness depending on the flavor of knowledge soundness of the underlying SNARK.

<span id="page-19-3"></span><sup>20</sup> We remark that our notion of index-efficiency is different from [\[CHM](#page-34-6)<sup>+</sup>20] that uses the term "index-efficient" for an argument to indicate that the running time of the prover is polynomial in the size of the index, but independent of the size of the universal relation and the structured reference string.

**Definition 14 (Index-Efficient SNARKs).** We say that a preprocessing SNARK (Definition 6) for an indexed  $\mathcal{R}$  is index-efficient if the running time of the prover is polylogarithmic in the size of the index, i.e.,  $poly(\lambda, \log |i|, |\mathbf{x}|, |\mathbf{w}|)$ .

To construct index-efficient SNARKs, we proceed as follows. First, we formalize the notion of index-efficient PIOPs, which are a class of PIOPs in which the prover's running time depends polylogarithmically on the size of the index. Second, we show how to use index-efficient PIOPs along with PCs with fast openings in order to construct an index-efficient SNARK. Third, in Section 5.2 we study the feasibility of instantiating the above result, namely the existence of index-efficient PIOPs. Our result is a technique to transform a class of (non-index-efficient) PIOPs into index-efficient ones. Finally, in Section 5.3 we show how the combination of the three results above can be used to obtain new table-efficient lookup arguments.

## <span id="page-20-0"></span>5.1 Index-Efficient PIOPs and Applications to Index-Efficient SNARKs

We start by recalling the definition of polynomial interactive oracle proofs (PIOP) for indexed relations.

<span id="page-20-1"></span>**Definition 15 (Polynomial IOPs).** Let R be a ring and R be an indexed relation. A polynomial IOP for R is defined by a tuple  $(\mathcal{I}, \mathcal{P}, \mathcal{V})$  such that:

**Offline phase:**  $\mathcal{I}(i)$  is a deterministic algorithm that on input the index i outputs i oracle polynomials  $p_1^i, \ldots, p_i^i$ .

Online phase: the prover  $\mathcal{P}$  and the verifier  $\mathcal{V}$  run an interactive protocol, compactly denoted by  $\langle \mathcal{P}(i, x, w), \mathcal{V}^{\mathcal{I}(i)}(x) \rangle$ , where in each round the prover  $\mathcal{P}$  sends oracle polynomials and/or ring elements, and the verifier  $\mathcal{V}$  sends random challenges. Each oracle [p] specifies the number of variables m and the degree in each variable. The verifier can query all the oracles, the ones sent by the prover and the ones generated by  $\mathcal{I}$ , at arbitrary points  $\mathbf{r} \in R^m$ . At the end of the execution,  $\mathcal{V}$  accepts or rejects.

**Correctness:** For any honest execution of  $\langle \mathcal{P}(i, x, w), \mathcal{V}^{\mathcal{I}(i)}(x) \rangle$  on  $(i, x; w) \in \mathcal{R}$ , the verifier accepts.

δ-Soundness: A PIOP is δ-sound if for any unbounded adversary  $\mathcal{A}$  and any  $(i, x) \notin \mathcal{L}(\mathcal{R})$ , we have  $\Pr\left[\langle \mathcal{A}(i, x), \mathcal{V}^{\mathcal{I}(i)}(x) \rangle = 1\right] \leq \delta$ .

δ-Knowledge Soundness: A PIOP is δ-knowledge-sound if there exists a PPT oracle machine Ext (which can query all the oracle polynomials at arbitrary points) such that for any adversary A and any (i, x), the following probability holds:

$$\Pr\left[\langle \mathcal{A}(i, x), \mathcal{V}^{\mathcal{I}(i)}(x)\rangle = 1 \ \land \ (i, x; w) \notin \mathcal{R} \ | w \leftarrow \mathsf{Ext}^{\mathcal{A}}(i, x) \right] \leq \delta.$$

We measure the efficiency of a PIOP according to the following metrics: the running times  $T_{\mathcal{P}}$  and  $T_{\mathcal{V}}$  of prover and verifier, the number of rounds r, the query complexity q (i.e., number of queries performed by the verifier), the number of oracles sent by the prover o, and the size s of the proof oracles (i.e., the sum of length of all the polynomials sent by the prover).

Next we define a class of PIOPs in which the prover's running time depends polylogarithmically on the size of the index. This is however possible after a preprocessing of the index performed by the indexer. More formally:

**Definition 16 (Index-Efficient PIOPs).** A PIOP for an indexed relation  $\mathcal{R}$  is called index-efficient if it satisfies the following conditions:

- The indexer  $\mathcal{I}(i)$  outputs oracle polynomials  $p_1^i, \ldots, p_i^i$  and a preprocessed index  $\mathsf{hk}_i$ .
- The prover  $\mathcal{P}(\mathsf{hk}_i, x, w)$  works as in a standard PIOP except that it takes as input  $\mathsf{hk}_i$  instead of the plain index i.
- **Efficiency:** the running time of the prover  $T_{\mathcal{P}}$  is  $poly(\lambda, \log |i|, |x|, |w|)$ .

Compiling Index-Efficient PIOPs into Index-Efficient SNARKs. We show that given an index-efficient PIOP and a PC with fast openings it is possible to construct an index-efficient SNARK.

Theorem 5 (Index-Efficient Compilation of PIOPs). Let  $\Pi$  be a PIOP for a ring R and an indexed relation  $\mathcal{R}$ , let PP be a polynomial preprocessing scheme, and let PC and subPC be two polynomial commitment schemes for polynomials with coefficients in R. If  $\Pi$  has  $negl(\lambda)$ -knowledge soundness, PC is knowledge-sound (Definition 21) and subPC is weak evaluation binding (Definition 19) then there exists a public-coin interactive argument  $\Pi$  for the indexed relation  $\mathcal{R}$ . Furthermore, if  $\Pi$  is index-efficient and subPC has fast openings, then  $\Pi$  is index-efficient.

The construction  $\Pi$  is obtained via a slight variant of the usual PIOP compilation appeared in previous work [CHM<sup>+</sup>20,BFS20]. The main difference is that the polynomials produced by the PIOP indexer are committed using the PC scheme subPC with fast opening; this occurs in the preprocessing phase of the argument system. Later, for every PIOP verifier's query to such polynomials, the prover uses the PP scheme to fast-evaluate the polynomial and the PC's fast opening to create a proof. Since this compiler is similar to previous work, we give an informal presentation emphasizing the main differences in more detail.

 $\Pi$ . Setup runs the setup algorithms of both PC and subPC and store in srs their commitment keys. Given an index i,  $\Pi$ . Index(srs, i) calls the PIOP indexer  $(p_1^i, \ldots, p_i^i, hk_i) \leftarrow \mathcal{I}(i)$  and then, for each polynomial  $p_i^i$  it runs the PP preprocessing,  $DS_i \leftarrow PP$ . PreProcess $(p_i^i)$ , commits to it using subPC, and stores all this preprocessed information in  $pk_i$  and the commitments in  $vk_i$ .

The prover  $\Pi.\mathsf{Prove}(\mathsf{pk}_i, \mathtt{x}, \mathtt{w})$  runs the PIOP prover  $\mathcal{P}(\mathsf{hk}_i, \mathtt{x}, \mathtt{w})$  until the end of the interactive protocol with  $\Pi.\mathcal{V}$  by sending to the verifier, in each round, commitments to the polynomials generated by  $\Pi.\mathcal{P}$  computed using PC. Finally, for every query of  $\mathcal{V}$  to a polynomial the prover of  $\Pi$  evaluates the polynomial and produces an evaluation for it. In particular, for polynomials produced by  $\mathcal{P}$  this is done using standard evaluation and by using PC for the evaluation proofs; this is the same as in previous compilers. For polynomials produced by  $\mathcal{I}$ , the evaluations are performed using the PP scheme and the proofs are generated using subPC.Open algorithm.

The verifier  $\Pi$ .Ver runs the PIOP verifier  $\Pi$ . $\mathcal{V}$  and checks the validity of all the evaluations and the associated proofs using PC or subPC.

Compared to previous work, the main difference in the proof of knowledge soundness of  $\Pi$  is that our compiler uses two different polynomial commitments. The crucial observation is that we commit the polynomials produced by the PIOP indexer  $\mathcal{I}$  as part of the  $\Pi$ -Index algorithm<sup>21</sup> and thus any incorrectly proven evaluation of these polynomials (which can be checked without extraction) can be reduced to the weak evaluation binding property of subPC.

<span id="page-21-0"></span><sup>&</sup>lt;sup>21</sup> In the real world, this algorithm is supposed to be executed by the verifier during preprocessing; in the knowledge-soundness experiment it is executed by the challenger.

The efficiency of the resulting argument depends on the efficiency of  $\Pi$ , PP, PC, and subPC as follows. For  $\Pi$ , let  $T_{\Pi,\mathcal{P}}$  and  $T_{\Pi,\mathcal{V}}$  be its prover and verifier running times, and let  $r, q_{\mathcal{I}}, q_{\mathcal{P}}, o, s$  be, respectively, its round complexity, number of verifier's queries to  $\mathcal{I}$ 's and  $\mathcal{P}$ 's oracles respectively, number of prover's oracles, and total size of the prover's oracles. For PC, let  $T_{PC.Com}$ ,  $T_{PC.Open}$ ,  $T_{PC.Ver}$  be the running times of the corresponding algorithms, and let |cm| and  $|\pi_{PC}|$  be the size of a commitment and a proof respectively, and consider analogous notations for subPC complexity. Then the argument  $\Pi$  enjoys the following efficiency:

- Proving time is  $T_{\Pi,P} + T_{PC.Com} \cdot s + (T_{subPC.Open} + T_{PP.Lookup} + T_{PP.Reconstruct}) \cdot q_{\mathcal{I}} + T_{PC.Open} \cdot q_{\mathcal{P}}$
- Verifier time is  $T_{\Pi,\mathcal{V}} + T_{subPC,Ver} \cdot q_{\mathcal{I}} + T_{PC,Ver} \cdot q_{\mathcal{P}}$
- Proof size is  $|\mathsf{cm}| \cdot \mathsf{o} + |\pi_{\mathsf{PC}}| \cdot \mathsf{q}_{\mathcal{P}}$

One can see that the index-efficiency of  $\Pi$ 's prover follows by the facts that the following terms are all polylogarithmic in  $|\dot{\mathbf{i}}|$ :  $T_{\Pi.\mathcal{P}}$ , and thus s and  $q_{\mathcal{P}}$ , due to the index-efficiency of  $\Pi$ ,  $q_{\mathcal{I}}$  due to the succinctness of the PIOP,  $T_{\text{subPC.Open}}$  due to the fast opening property of subPC,  $T_{\text{PP.Lookup}} + T_{\text{PP.Reconstruct}}$  due to the efficiency of PP, and  $T_{\text{PC.Open}}$  since it is a function of the size of the polynomials produced by  $\Pi.\mathcal{P}$  which is polylogarithmic in  $|\dot{\mathbf{i}}|$  due to the index-efficiency of  $\Pi$ .

#### <span id="page-22-0"></span>5.2 Our Construction of Index-Efficient PIOPs

In the following theorem we show how to transform a class of (non-index-efficient) PIOPs into index-efficient ones. The main technical obstacle in this transformation is that we want to transform PIOPs that work with multilinear polynomials (which is the most popular case in the literature) but the known results for preprocessing schemes only capture either univariate polynomials or multivariate polynomials with very specific configuration in terms of number of variables and degree. Our contribution is a technique that solves this issue by encoding the multilinear polynomial with a low-degree multivariate polynomial for which we can use a PP scheme.

<span id="page-22-1"></span>Theorem 6 (Index-Efficient PIOPs from PP schemes). Let PP be a polynomial preprocessing scheme for m-variate polynomials of degree  $\leq d$  such that  $(d+1)^m = 2^n$  and d is at most poly(n). Let  $\Pi$  be a PIOP for a finite field  $\mathbb{F} = \mathbb{Z}_q$  for a prime  $q \approx 2^{\lambda}$  and for an indexed relation  $\mathcal{R}$ , where:

- 1.  $\Pi.\mathcal{I}$  produces multilinear polynomials  $\tilde{p}_1^{i}, \ldots, \tilde{p}_i^{i} \in \mathbb{Z}_q[X_1, \ldots, X_n]$ .
- There is an implementation of  $\Pi$  such that:
- <span id="page-22-2"></span>2. it remains correct if one replaces each indexer's multilinear polynomial  $\tilde{p}_j^i$  with a polynomial  $p_j^i$  of higher degree d > 1 that agrees on the boolean hypercube, i.e.,  $\forall \mathbf{b} \in \{0, 1\}^n : \tilde{p}_j^i(\mathbf{b}) = p_j^i(\mathbf{b});$
- 3. the prover's algorithm has only oracle access to the indexer's polynomials and its running time is  $T_{\Pi.\mathcal{P}} = T_{\mathbb{F}} + T_{i}$ , where  $T_{\mathbb{F}}$  is the number of field operations and  $T_{i}$  the number of oracle calls to the indexer's polynomials;
- 4. the prover's time  $T_{\mathbb{F}}$ ,  $T_i$  and the remaining efficiency measures of  $\Pi$  depend only polynomially on the degree d and the number of variables n of the indexer's polynomials (namely, we can express them as functions  $T_{\mathbb{F}}(d,n), T_i(d,n), r(d,n), q(d,n), o(d,n), s(d,n),$  ignoring for simplicity their dependence on  $|\mathbf{x}|, |\mathbf{w}|$ );
- 5. it achieves  $\delta(d,n)$ -knowledge-soundness with  $\delta(d,n) = \text{negl}(\lambda)$  whenever  $d = \text{poly}(\lambda)$ . Then, there exists an index-efficient PIOP  $\hat{\Pi}$  for the indexed relation  $\mathcal{R}$ .

We now provide the required preliminaries on Lagrange interpolation before describing our index-efficient PIOP construction  $\hat{\Pi}$  (see Construction 2) stated in Theorem 6 above. Then, we provide the corresponding proof.

Preliminaries on Lagrange interpolation. For any  $\mathbf{b} \in \{0,1\}^n$ , let  $\tilde{eq}(\mathbf{X}; \mathbf{b}) = \prod_{i=1}^n (X_i b_i + (1 - X_i)(1 - b_i))$  be the multilinear polynomial in  $\mathbb{F}[\mathbf{X}]$  such that, for every  $\mathbf{x} \in \{0,1\}^n$ ,  $\tilde{eq}(\mathbf{x}; \mathbf{b}) = 1$  if  $\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ , and is 0 otherwise. The set  $\{\tilde{eq}(\mathbf{X}; \mathbf{b}) : \mathbf{b} \in \{0,1\}^n\}$  is the set of Lagrange basis polynomials for interpolation domain  $\{0,1\}^n$ . Here we shortly call it a  $\{0,1\}^n$ -basis.

Given a set  $\mathbb{H} \subset \mathbb{F}$  of size d+1, we define the m-variate polynomials  $\{L(\mathbf{Y}; \mathbf{h}) : \mathbf{h} \in \mathbb{H}^m\}$  of individual degree  $\leq d$  such that, for every  $\mathbf{y} \in \mathbb{H}^m$ ,  $L(\mathbf{y}; \mathbf{h}) = 1$  if  $\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{h}$ , and is 0 otherwise. These polynomials are the set of Lagrange basis polynomials for interpolation domain  $\mathbb{H}^m$ , or shortly an  $\mathbb{H}^m$ -basis.

For any  $h \in \mathbb{H}$ , let L'(X,h) be the h-th Lagrange univariate polynomial for interpolation domain  $\mathbb{H}$ , i.e.,  $L'(X;h) = \prod_{h' \in \mathbb{H}, h' \neq h} \frac{X-h'}{h-h'}$ , that is of degree  $d = |\mathbb{H}| - 1$ . Then, L is the product of the m Lagrange polynomials  $L(X_1, \ldots, X_m; h_1, \ldots, h_m) = \prod_{i=1}^m L'(X_i; h_i)$ .

For  $\mathbb{F} = \mathbb{Z}_q$ , let  $\mathbb{H} = \{0, 1, \dots, d\}$  and let  $m, n, \delta \in \mathbb{Z}$  such that  $(d+1)^m = 2^n$  and  $\delta \cdot m = n$  (i.e.  $\delta = \log_2(d+1)$ ). We define the linear map:

$$-\mathsf{G}:\mathbb{F}^n \to \mathbb{F}^m, \quad (x_1,\ldots,x_n) \mapsto \left(\sum_{\eta=1}^{\delta} x_{\delta(k-1)+\eta} \cdot 2^{\eta-1}\right)_{k=1}^m.^{22}$$

## <span id="page-24-0"></span>5.3 Application to Table-Efficient Lookup Arguments

As a novel application of our techniques in this section, we can instantiate Theorem 6 using the GeneralizedLasso PIOP in [STW24, Appendix F.2] which supports lookup relations over arbitrary tables. In a nutshell, this is a PIOP in which the indexer produces a multilinear polynomial  $\tilde{t} \in \mathbb{F}[X_1, \ldots, X_n]$  such that each  $\tilde{t}(\mathbf{b})$  encodes a distinct value of the lookup table, and then the prover can send a multilinear polynomial  $\tilde{a} \in \mathbb{F}[X_1, \ldots, X_{\mu}]$  and convince the verifier that  $\forall \mathbf{j} \in \{0, 1\}^{\mu}$ :  $\tilde{a}(\mathbf{j}) \in \{\tilde{t}(\mathbf{b}) : \mathbf{b} \in \{0, 1\}^n\}$ .

A crucial building block of the GeneralizedLasso PIOP is a PIOP for sparse-dense sumcheck in which the prover wants to convince the verifier that  $y = \sum_{\mathbf{b} \in \{0,1\}^n} \tilde{u}(\mathbf{b}) \cdot \tilde{t}(\mathbf{b})$  where  $\tilde{t}$  is the same multilinear polynomial produced by the indexer, and  $\tilde{u}$  is a multilinear n-variate polynomial (implicitly defined by other polynomials previously sent by the prover) that is sparse, namely the number of nonzero values in  $\{\tilde{u}(\mathbf{b}) : \mathbf{b} \in \{0,1\}^n\}$  is  $2^{\mu} \ll 2^n$ . ## <span id="page-25-0"></span>5.4 Application to Index-Efficient SNARKs for Circuits

In this section, we show how to use our Theorem [6](#page-22-1) to obtain another new result: an index-efficient PIOP for linear checks, namely to prove that v = M · z in time N · polylog(N) when the N × N matrix M is fixed and preprocessed and v, z are two vectors sent by the prover. This result is new because in existing PIOPs the prover complexity is at best O(max(||M||, N)), hence linear in the number of nonzero entries of the matrix ||M||, which is N<sup>2</sup> ) in the worst case.

Our new index-efficient PIOP for linear checks can be used to construct an index-efficient PIOP for (universal) rank-1 constraint systems (R1CS), which can be in turn used to build an index-efficient PIOP for circuit satisfiability. In R1CS, the universal relation is indexed by matrix tuples (A, B, C) and the prover needs to convince the verifier that ∃z : (A · z) ◦ (B · z) = (C · z). Several works show how to construct a PIOP for R1CS by combining a PIOP for linear checks (or "lincheck"), to prove  $\exists \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}, \mathbf{c}, \mathbf{z} : \mathbf{a} = \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{z}, \mathbf{b} = \mathbf{B} \cdot \mathbf{z}, \mathbf{c} = \mathbf{C} \cdot \mathbf{z}$ , with a PIOP for Hadamard products, to prove that  $\mathbf{a} \circ \mathbf{b} = \mathbf{c}$ . Therefore, to build an index-efficient PIOP for R1CS we can simply replace the lincheck PIOP in other constructions, e.g., [Set20], with our index-efficient one. This way, after preprocessing the matrices  $\mathbf{A}, \mathbf{B}, \mathbf{C}$ , the prover's running time does not depend on their density and thus it is index-efficient whenever the number of nonzero entries is  $\omega(N \operatorname{polylog}(N))$ . This is for example the case for circuits with many more addition gates than multiplication gates e.g.,  $O(N^2)$  addition gates and O(N) multiplication gates.

## <span id="page-27-0"></span>6 Witness-Succinct UC SNARKs with Nearly Optimal Proving Time

Here, we show how to obtain UC-SNARKs with approximately-linear proving time generically from PCs with fast opening. The reader is referred to the introduction for a discussion of the importance of proving SNARKs in the UC model and the associated challenges. All the polynomial commitments in this section are implicitly for the *univariate case* only.

Our starting point is  $[GKO^+23]$ , which proposes a general and elegant compiler which maps any simulation-extractable zkSNARK  $\Pi$  into an UC-secure one. In order to obtain straight-line extractability they adopt a Fischlin-style proof-of-work [Fis05]: the witness is extracted by observing the prover's queries to the random oracle (queries that are "forced on" the prover by the proof-of-work aspect of technique). A key step of their approach involves the prover sending a polynomial commitment, allegedly opening to (an encoding of) the witness.

Efficiency-preserving transformations. One of the advantages of the compiler in [GKO<sup>+</sup>23] is that it preserves the essential verification and communication complexity of the original zkSNARK  $\Pi$  Unfortunately, for proving time, the same compiler substantially changes the prover's efficiency profile: even if the original proving time of  $\Pi$  is O(n) (where n is the size of the witness), the resulting UC zkSNARK will run in time at least  $O(n^2)$ .

The overhead above stems from the requirement in [GKO<sup>+</sup>23] that the prover queries the random oracle multiple times during the proof-of-work stage. Recall that, as mentioned above, the prover holds a commitment  $\mathsf{cm}_f$  to a univariate polynomial f dependent on the witness. Each of the prover's query q to the random oracle is supposed to include, among other elements, a tuple  $(z, y, \pi)$  where  $\pi$  is an evaluation proof for  $\mathsf{cm}_f$  showing that f(z) = y. The prover performs  $\approx n$  of these invocations throughout the protocol. If at this step, we are applying a standard polynomial commitments, then each evaluation proof requires approximately  $O(\deg(f))$  steps.<sup>23</sup> Since it holds that  $\deg(f) \geq n$ , a traditional polynomial commitment will lead to a total quadratic proving time.

A first (flawed) approach. It seems that using our PC with sublinear opening time in the compiler in [GKO<sup>+</sup>23], would remove this quadratic complexity. At commitment time, the prover will spend  $O_{\lambda}(n^{1+\epsilon})$  time (see preliminaries) to generate the polynomial commitment and will then store the appropriate auxiliary information; for each of the  $\approx n$  queries to the RO the prover computes

an evaluation opening and each can be performed in O(polylog(n)) time with our construction in Section [4.](#page-16-0) Since the total proving time is ≈ n · polylog(n) ∈ Oλ(n 1+ϵ ), we obtain as a result an approximately-linear time prover (as long as the original zkSNARK prover runs in quasilinear-time). As an implication, we have the first compiler for general SNARKs with such property. Notice that, if the zkSNARK has polylogarithmic proof size and verification time, these are also preserved.

Unfortunately, the above approach is flawed because our PC is not directly applicable to the the compiler in [\[GKO](#page-34-0)+23] which requires the PC to satisfy some "special" properties which are not all satisfied by our PC.

Solving the additional challenges. The first special property is a soft form of non-malleability: it informally states that an efficient adversary should not be able to produce two different evaluation proofs for the same input point z for an honestly generated polynomial commitment ("unique proofs"). Our construction PCVC does already satisfy this property (see Theorem [7\)](#page-31-1).

The second required property is a form of hiding. In order to state it, we first recall the methodology followed in the compiler in [\[GKO](#page-34-0)+23], which can be thought of as an encode&commit approach where the prover computes cm<sup>f</sup> ← PC.Com(fw) where f<sup>w</sup> ← PES.Enc(w). That is, it first encodes the witness (a vector) into a polynomial—using a "polynomial encoding scheme" PES—to which it then commits using a polynomial commitment PC. The encoding algorithm is randomized and its role is to mask the witness, so that the latter is still hidden to the verifier even after seeing several—approximately λ—polynomial evaluation proofs. If this is the case we say that the polynomial commitment PC is "evaluation-hiding" with respect to the encoding scheme PES. The reason this notion relies on an additional object, the encoding scheme, is that it allows it to coexist with the requirement over unique proofs. In fact, the latter can be satisfied, at least seemingly, only by a PC with deterministic commitment and proving algorithms. This rules out some intuitive-butwrong approaches; for example, applying a ZK proof systems to achieve this hiding property is not an option.

Our construction in Section [4](#page-16-0) does not directly satisfy evaluation-hiding for any of the polynomial encoding schemes in literature we are aware of (we give additional details in Appendix [F\)](#page-57-1). The reason is that the underlying approach of Section [4](#page-16-0) might inherently have too much leakage.[24](#page-28-0) As a consequence, we need to develop a method that can be applied to the compiler from [\[GKO](#page-34-0)+23] despite the limitations of our constructions.

Our approach: "bootstrapping" evaluation-hiding. Recall that our main goal is to be able to produce evaluation proofs obtaining, at the same time, three requirements: (i) sublinear opening time; (ii) evaluation-hiding w.r.t. some encoding scheme; (iii) unique proofs. We will temporarily ignore the last property and focus on the tension between the first two. Our solution will turn out to be a hybrid approach that, given:

- a polynomial commitment subPC satisfying (i);
- a polynomial commitment hidPC that is evaluation hiding w.r.t. an encoding scheme hidPC (i.e., that together satisfy (ii));

obtains a polynomial commitment scheme (and a companion encoding scheme) allowing us to satisfy both (i) and (ii) at the same time. Our approach is relying on subPC to the heavy lifting

in terms of opening proofs, but at the same time it is relying completely on (hidPC, hidPES) for evaluation-hiding without having to pay in the total size of the witness (and thus losing property (i)).

In a nutshell, what we do is this. For encoding, we first sample a (symmetric) encryption key sk and encrypt the witness obtaining a ciphertext ct ← SKE.Enc(sk, w) (both sk and ct, vectors of scalars); we then build two polynomials f(X) := P <sup>i</sup> Xi−<sup>1</sup> ct<sup>i</sup> and g(X) := hidPES.Enc(sk). In order to commit to this encoding, we generate cm<sup>f</sup> ← subPC.Com(f) and cm<sup>g</sup> ← hidPC.Com(g). At opening time, we produce two proofs, one for each commitment. (Note: in exposition above, for simplicity, we "produce in output" two commitments and two encodings, whereas the framework in [\[GKO](#page-34-0)+23] expects just one of each. This is easy to fix—as we show in our formal treatment in Fig. [1](#page-30-4) —by what is essentially a concatenation of the coefficients and the commitments). In the rest of this section we refer to the resulting "hybrid" polynomial commitment and encoding scheme respectively as PChyb and PEShyb .

As for security, we are able to achieve property (i) because the (hiding) scheme with "slow" openings is operating on a very small polynomial (of degree roughly λ, the size of the encoding of the secret key); we are able to achieve property (ii) because the ciphertexts reveal nothing about the witness (by semantic security) and the openings from cm<sup>g</sup> reveal nothing about the secret key thanks to its evaluation-hiding property. It is straightforward to see that if both polynomial commitments have unique proofs so does the resulting one.

We see our approach as a technique of independent interest since it allows to transform any polynomial commitment scheme into one satisfying evaluation hiding by "piggybacking" on an some other already evaluation-hiding scheme, using it only over a limited portion of the polynomial (e.g., linear in λ). This can be useful, as in our setting, where we want to leverage efficiency properties of a polynomial commitment scheme that is otherwise "too leaky". The fact that our transformation also preserves unique proofs makes it essentially a bootstrapping technique for the [\[GKO](#page-34-0)+23] compiler (see also Appendix [F\)](#page-57-1).

## <span id="page-30-0"></span>6.1 Instantiating our Hybrid Construction

We provide the formal description of the "hybrid" polynomial commitment  $PC^{hyb}$  and the encoding scheme  $PES^{hyb}$  (based on hidPC and subPC, as described above) in Figure 1. Next, we discuss how to instantiate the building blocks in our hybrid approach, informally presented in the last paragraphs, and state some of its basic properties. The notions of unique proofs and evaluation hiding are provided in the appendix (Definition 30 and Definition 32), but the reader can find their informal description in the technical overview. We state only the formal lemmas (defining the properties required by underlying hidPC and subPC) which, in turn, will allow us to establish the final security of  $PC^{hyb}$  and  $PES^{hyb}$ . The formal proofs of the following two lemmas appears in Appendix E.2.

<span id="page-30-1"></span>**Lemma 1.** If hidPC and subPC are polynomial commitments satisfying completeness, (weak) evaluation binding and the unique proof property (Definition 30 in the appendix) then PC<sup>hyb</sup> (previous paragraphs; Fig. 1) satisfies the unique proof property.

<span id="page-30-2"></span>**Lemma 2.** If hidPC satisfies evaluation hiding (Definition 32) with respect to hidPES then  $PC^{hyb}$  in Fig. 1 satisfies evaluation hiding with respect to  $PES^{hyb}$ .

<span id="page-30-3"></span>We now show that our PC Construction 1 satisfies the unique proof property whenever the underlying vector commitment also has unique proofs (this notion is a straightforward counterpart of the one for polynomial commitments and is in Definition 34 in the appendix). Recall that unique proofs property is one of the necessary properties of hidPC and subPC as stated in Lemma 1. We refer the reader to Appendix E.3 for the formal proof.

Lemma 3. If the underlying VC satisfies position binding[25](#page-31-2) (Definition [24\)](#page-37-5) and unique proofs (Definition [34\)](#page-48-1) then the PCVC construction in Section [4](#page-16-0) is a polynomial commitment with unique proofs (Definition [30\)](#page-46-0).

The lemma above, together with the fact that Merkle Trees have computationally unique proofs (this follows directly from collision-resistance) gives us a candidate instantiation for our hybrid construction.

<span id="page-31-1"></span>Theorem 7. If there exist CRHF then there exists a polynomial commitment subPC satisfying evaluation binding and unique proofs; for a polynomial of degree d, it features Oλ(1)-sized commitments, Oλ(d 1+ϵ ) commitment time, Oλ(polylog(d)) opening time and evaluation proofs/verification of size/time Oλ(polylog(d)).

Instantiating PChyb and PEShyb . We will use Theorem [7](#page-31-1) in order to instantiate subPC in Fig. [1.](#page-30-4) All we are left with is to instantiate hidPC and hidPES, for which we will use some of the building blocks from the work in [\[GKO](#page-34-0)+23]. We will use KZG [\[KZG10\]](#page-35-3) as a PC. It features constant-size commitments, proofs and verification; it is secure under the standard Strong Diffie-Hellman (SDH) assumption [\[BB04\]](#page-33-11).

KZG was shown in [\[GKO](#page-34-0)+23] to be evaluation hiding with respect an encoding PEScoeff, which we pick as our choice for hidPES. This simple encoding essentially, transforms the input secret vector (in our case case the secret key sk) by parsing it as the vector of coefficients of a polynomial and concatenating ≈ λ random freshly sampled coefficients. Its resulting output polynomial is therefore of size O(poly(λ)). Full formal treatment of these building blocks is in Appendix [D.](#page-42-0)

Remark 2 (Efficiency of PChyb and PEShyb). The resulting efficiency of our hybrid construction (in all metrics) stems from the sum of the efficiency profiles of the its building blocks. Recall that at encoding time we perform an encryption of the witness and we produce an encoding (through hidPES) of sk. The polynomial commitment algorithms invoke commitment/opening/verification of each of the two underlying schemes. For our specific instantiations, from the above and Theorem [7,](#page-31-1) we can conclude that: i) encryption dominates the costs in our hybrid encoding scheme, and thus the latter runs in linear time, i.e. Oλ(|w|); the costs in the resulting polynomial commitment are dominated by those of subPC.

In the following theorem, whose proof is immediate from the observations and results in this section, we use the fact that the SDH assumption implies DLOG and the existence of CRHF (see Remark [3](#page-48-2) in Appendix [D\)](#page-42-0).

<span id="page-31-3"></span>Theorem 8. Under the SDH assumption ([\[BB04\]](#page-33-11)) there exist a polynomial commitment PChyb with the properties and efficiency in Theorem [7](#page-31-1) and an encoding scheme PEShyb with linear-time encoding/decoding such that PChyb is evaluation hiding with respect to PEShyb .

## <span id="page-31-0"></span>6.2 Our Main Result on Succinct UC-NIZKs

Before stating our own result, we first recall one regarding the compiler from [\[GKO](#page-34-0)+23]. The latter, on input a PC (with some extra properties) and a simulation-extractable NIZK, outputs a

<span id="page-31-2"></span><sup>25</sup> Although it is not necessary for our specific Merkle Tree instantiation, this hypothesis could be weakened by just assuming weak position binding (where the commitments is generated honestly).

compiled protocol ΠGKOPTT implementing a UC-NIZK (we refer the reader to Appendix [D.5](#page-50-0) for the full description of the scheme). The following theorem summarizes its efficiency properties.

<span id="page-32-2"></span>Theorem 9 (Implicit in [\[GKO](#page-34-0)+23[,BCC](#page-33-0)+24]). Consider the following primitives:

- NIZK be a simulation-extractable NIZK (Definition [28\)](#page-44-0), which, for a witness of size n, has: proof size <sup>O</sup>λ(ℓNIZK(n)), quasi-linear proving time (i.e., <sup>T</sup>NIZK.Prove <sup>=</sup> <sup>O</sup>eλ(n)),[26](#page-32-1), and verification time Oλ(ℓNIZK(n)).
- PC be a polynomial commitment having weak evaluation binding (Definition [19\)](#page-36-5), unique proofs (Definition [30\)](#page-46-0), commitments and evaluation proofs both of size Oλ(ℓPC(d)), commitment and proving time Oλ(TPC.Com(d)) and Oλ(TPC.Open(d)) respectively, and verification time Oλ(ℓPC(d)).
- PES = (Enc, Dec) be an encoding scheme (Definition [31\)](#page-46-2) s.t. PC is evaluation hiding (Definition [32\)](#page-46-1) with respect to PES and with linear encoding time.

Then, there exists a UC-NIZK (Definition [29\)](#page-45-1) with proof size O<sup>λ</sup> ℓNIZK(n) + ℓPC(n) , proving time Oeλ TPC.Com(n) + n · TPC.Open(n) , and verification time Oλ(ℓNIZK(n) + ℓPC(n)).

Our main technical result of this section is a a general theorem showing one can instantiate the compiler in [\[GKO](#page-34-0)+23] from any (reasonably efficient) simulation-extractable NIZK and obtain a UC-secure NIZK with approximately-linear time and polylog proof size and verification. The theorem follows by plugging in Theorem [9](#page-32-2) our "hybrid" PChyb and PChyb instantiated as described in this section.

Theorem 10. Under the Strong Diffie-Hellman assumption ([\[BB04\]](#page-33-11)), if there exists a simulationextractable NIZK NIZK with quasilinear proving-time and proof size and verification time Oλ(polylog(n)) (for a witness of size n), then there exists a UC-NIZK (Definition [29](#page-45-1) in Appendix [D\)](#page-42-0) with Oλ(polylog(n)) proof size and verification time and approximately-linear proving time.

When instantiated with the pairing-based simulation-extractable scheme from Groth and Maller [\[GM17\]](#page-34-12) whose security relies on the usual flavor of knowledge-of-exponent assumptions (see Appendix [D.4\)](#page-50-1) we obtain the following result.

Corollary 3. Under the Strong Diffie-Hellman assumption ([\[BB04\]](#page-33-11)), the Computational Polynomial Assumption and the XPKE Assumption ([\[GM17\]](#page-34-12)) there exists a UC-NIZK with Oλ(polylog(n)) proof size and verification and approximately-linear proving time.

## <span id="page-36-0"></span>A Further Preliminaries

#### <span id="page-36-1"></span>A.1 Definitions of Polynomial Commitments

<span id="page-36-3"></span>**Definition 17 (Correctness of PC).** A polynomial commitment scheme PC as defined above is correct if  $\forall \lambda, m, d \in \mathbb{N}$ , for any polynomial  $f \in \mathcal{P}_{m,d}$ , for any point  $x \in R^m$ , and for any randomness  $r \in \{0,1\}^*$ , the following holds:

$$\Pr\left[ \mathsf{Ver}(\mathsf{ck},\mathsf{cm},x,f(x),\pi_x) = 1 \, \left| \begin{matrix} \mathsf{ck} \leftarrow \mathsf{Setup}(1^\lambda,m,d) \\ (\mathsf{cm},\mathsf{aux}) \leftarrow \mathsf{Com}(\mathsf{ck},f;r) \\ \pi_x \leftarrow \mathsf{Open}(\mathsf{ck},x,\mathsf{aux}) \end{matrix} \right] = 1.$$

**Definition 18 (Binding of PC).** A polynomial commitment scheme PC satisfies commitment binding if for any PPT adversary A, the following holds:

$$\Pr\left[ \begin{array}{c} \mathsf{Com}(\mathsf{ck}, f; r) = \mathsf{Com}(\mathsf{ck}, f'; r') \\ \land f \neq f' \end{array} \middle| \begin{array}{c} \mathsf{ck} \leftarrow \mathsf{Setup}(1^\lambda, m, d) \\ (f, r, f', r') \leftarrow \mathcal{A}(\mathsf{ck}) \end{array} \right] \in \mathsf{negl}(\lambda).$$

<span id="page-36-5"></span>**Definition 19 (Weak Evaluation Binding of PC).** A polynomial commitment scheme PC satisfies weak evaluation binding if for any PPT adversary A, the following holds:

$$\Pr\left[ \begin{array}{c} \mathsf{Ver}(\mathsf{ck},\mathsf{cm},x,y,\pi_x) = 1 \\ \land y \neq f(x) \end{array} \middle| \begin{array}{c} \mathsf{ck} \leftarrow \mathsf{Setup}(1^\lambda,m,d) \\ (f,r,x,y,\pi_x) \leftarrow \mathcal{A}(\mathsf{ck}) \\ (\mathsf{cm},\mathsf{aux}) \leftarrow \mathsf{Com}(\mathsf{ck},f;r) \end{array} \right] \in \mathsf{negl}(\lambda).$$

<span id="page-36-7"></span>**Definition 20 (Strong Evaluation Binding of PC).** A polynomial commitment scheme PC satisfies strong evaluation binding if for any PPT adversary A, the following holds:

$$\Pr\left[ \begin{array}{l} \forall i \in [N], \ \mathsf{Ver}(\mathsf{ck}, \mathsf{cm}, x_i, y_i, \pi_i) = 1 \\ \land \not\exists f \in \mathcal{P}_{m,d}, \ \forall i \in [N], \ f(x_i) = y_i \end{array} \middle| \begin{array}{l} \mathsf{ck} \leftarrow \mathsf{Setup}(1^\lambda, m, d) \\ (\mathsf{cm}, \{x_i, y_i, \pi_i\}_{i \in [N]}) \leftarrow \mathcal{A}(\mathsf{ck}) \end{array} \right] \in \mathsf{negl}(\lambda).^{27}$$

<span id="page-36-4"></span>**Definition 21 (Non-black-box Knowledge Soundness of** PC). A polynomial commitment scheme PC satisfies non-black-box knowledge soundness if for any PPT adversary  $\mathcal{A}$  there exists a polynomial-time extractor  $\mathsf{Ext}_{\mathcal{A}}$  such that:

$$\Pr\left[ \begin{array}{c} \mathsf{Ver}(\mathsf{ck},\mathsf{cm},x,y,\pi_x) = 1 \land \\ ((\mathsf{cm},\cdot) \neq \mathsf{Com}(\mathsf{ck},f;r) \lor y \neq f(x)) \end{array} \middle| \begin{array}{c} \mathsf{ck} \leftarrow \mathsf{Setup}(1^\lambda,m,d) \\ (\mathsf{cm},x,y,\pi_x) \leftarrow \mathcal{A}(\mathsf{ck}) \\ (f,r) \leftarrow \mathsf{Ext}_{\mathcal{A}}(\mathsf{ck}) \end{array} \right] \in \mathsf{negl}(\lambda).$$

Sometimes we will refer to black-box knowledge soundness which is essentially identical to the above definition except that the extractor Ext is universal.

<span id="page-36-6"></span>The condition  $\nexists f \in \mathcal{P}_{m,d}$ ,  $\forall i \in [N]$ ,  $f(x_i) = y_i$  is equivalent to saying that there is no solution  $\mathbf{z}$  to the linear system  $\mathbf{M} \cdot \mathbf{z} = \mathbf{y}$ , where  $\mathbf{M}$  is the  $(N \times d^m)$ -size matrix whose row i contains all the monomials evaluated in the  $x_i$ 's,  $\mathbf{y}$  is the N-size vector consisting of the outputs  $y_i$ , and  $\mathbf{z}$  is the unknown for the  $d^m$ -size vector of coefficients of the polynomial f.

## <span id="page-37-0"></span>A.2 Definitions of Vector Commitments

<span id="page-37-3"></span>**Definition 22 (Vector Commitments** [CF13]). A vector commitment scheme VC for a message space  $\mathcal{M}$  is defined by the following algorithms:

Setup( $1^{\lambda}$ , n)  $\rightarrow$  ck: On input the security parameter  $\lambda$  and a bound n for the length of the committed vectors, the setup algorithm outputs a public commitment key ck.

 $\mathsf{Com}(\mathsf{ck}, \mathbf{v}; r) \to (\mathsf{cm}, \mathsf{aux})$ : On input the commitment key  $\mathsf{ck}$ , a vector  $\mathbf{v} \in \mathcal{M}^n$ , and (possibly) a randomness r, the commit algorithm outputs a commitment  $\mathsf{cm}$  and an auxiliary information  $\mathsf{aux}$ .

Open(ck, i, aux)  $\rightarrow \pi_i$ : On input the commitment key ck, an index  $i \in [n]$ , and auxiliary information aux, the open algorithm outputs a proof  $\pi_i$ .

Ver(ck, cm,  $i, y, \pi_i$ )  $\rightarrow$  b: On input the commitment key ck, the commitment cm, index  $i \in [n]$ , value  $y \in \mathcal{M}$ , and a proof  $\pi_i$ , the verification algorithm outputs a bit  $b \in \{0, 1\}$ .

<span id="page-37-4"></span>**Definition 23 (Correctness of VC).** A vector commitment scheme VC as defined above is correct if  $\forall \lambda, n \in \mathbb{N}$ , for any vector  $\mathbf{v} \in \mathcal{M}^n$ , for any randomness  $r \in \{0, 1\}^*$ , and  $\forall i \in [n]$ , the following holds:

$$\Pr\left[ \mathsf{Ver}(\mathsf{ck},\mathsf{cm},i,v_i,\pi_i) = 1 \, \middle| \! \begin{array}{c} \mathsf{ck} \leftarrow \mathsf{Setup}(1^\lambda,n) \\ (\mathsf{cm},\mathsf{aux}) \leftarrow \mathsf{Com}(\mathsf{ck},\mathbf{v};r) \\ \pi_i \leftarrow \mathsf{Open}(\mathsf{ck},i,\mathsf{aux}) \end{array} \right] = 1.$$

<span id="page-37-5"></span>**Definition 24 (Position Binding).** A vector commitment scheme VC satisfies position binding if for any PPT adversary A, the following holds:

$$\Pr \left[ \begin{array}{l} \operatorname{Ver}(\mathsf{ck},\mathsf{cm},i,y,\pi_i) = 1 \\ \wedge \operatorname{Ver}(\mathsf{ck},\mathsf{cm},i,y',\pi_i') = 1 \\ \wedge y \neq y' \end{array} \middle| \begin{array}{l} \mathsf{ck} \leftarrow \operatorname{Setup}(1^{\lambda},n) \\ (\mathsf{cm},i,y,\pi_i,y',\pi_i') \leftarrow \mathcal{A}(\mathsf{ck}) \end{array} \right] \in \mathsf{negl}(\lambda).$$

<span id="page-37-2"></span>**Instatiations of VC with fast openings.** We recall a few instantiations of VCs with fast opening:

- Merkle trees [Mer88] are position binding based on collision resistant hash functions, achieve  $O(\lambda \log n)$  succinctness, and have fast opening with  $\mathsf{T}_{\mathsf{Com}}(\lambda, n) = \mathsf{poly}(\lambda) \cdot n$  and  $\mathsf{T}_{\mathsf{Open}}(\lambda, n) = O(\lambda \log n)$ . The fast opening follows by storing all the nodes of the tree which can be accessed in constant time in the RAM model.
- VCs with quasilinear algorithms to compute openings for all positions. There are a variety of schemes where succinctness and  $\mathsf{T}_{\mathsf{Open}}(\lambda,n)$  are a fixed  $\mathsf{poly}(\lambda)$ , and where  $\mathsf{T}_{\mathsf{Com}}(\lambda,n) = \mathsf{poly}(\lambda) \cdot n \log n$  (e.g., for the KZG-based VC from pairings [KZG10]) or  $\mathsf{T}_{\mathsf{Com}}(\lambda,n) = \mathsf{poly}(\lambda) \cdot n \log^2 n$  (e.g., for schemes based on hidden-order groups like [CF13,BBF19,CFG<sup>+</sup>20]). In fact, Campanelli et al. [CFG<sup>+</sup>20] show that fast opening follows from any VC with incremental (dis)aggregation.

## <span id="page-37-1"></span>B Lack of Strong Evaluation Binding of PC<sub>VC</sub>

Theorem 4 states the evaluation binding property of PC<sub>VC</sub>, which is the standard security notion of polynomial commitments. One may wonder if our construction can satisfy one of the stronger

properties such as strong evaluation binding (Definition 20) or knowledge soundness (Definition 21). In the following theorem we show that, when the PP scheme of Construction 1 is instantiated with the KU-based scheme [KU08,KU11], then the resulting  $PC_{VC}$  is not strongly evaluation binding.

<span id="page-38-1"></span>**Theorem 11.** If the PP scheme of Construction 1 is instantiated with the PP scheme from Theorem 2, then the resulting  $PC_{VC}$  does not satisfy strong evaluation binding.

*Proof.* We first describe the general strategy to construct attacks, and then show a concrete instantiation.

The core observation at the base of the proof is that the space of the data structures is significantly larger than the space of the polynomials we preprocess, whereas, to answer a specific point query, we read only a small subset (k values) of the data structure DS. To win the strong evaluation binding experiment, it is sufficient to satisfy the following two conditions:

- <span id="page-38-0"></span>1. provide  $d^m$  valid proofs  $\{\pi_{x_i}\}_{i\in[d^m]}$  and evaluations  $\{y_i\}_{i\in[d^m]}$  w.r.t. a set of points  $\{x_i\}_{i\in[d^m]}$  such that the evaluations uniquely determine a polynomial  $f\in\mathcal{P}_{m,d}$  with  $f(x_i)=y_i$  for all  $i\in[d^m]$ . For simplicity we will set f=0, i.e. the set of points  $\{x_i\}_{i\in[d^m]}$  should be such that no nonzero polynomial from  $\mathcal{P}_{m,d}$  can evaluate to 0 in all points. Accordingly, we will set  $y_i=0$  for all  $i\in[d^m]$ .
- <span id="page-38-2"></span>2. provide an additional verifying proof  $\tilde{\pi}_x$  for a new point  $\tilde{x}$  whose claimed evaluation  $\tilde{y}$  is different from 0.

A sufficient condition to satisfy condition 1 above is that the set of points  $\{x_i\}_{i\in[d^m]}$  is of the form  $S^m$ , with  $S\subseteq\mathbb{Z}_q$  being of size d (indeed in these conditions we have a Lagrange basis of  $\mathcal{P}_{m,d}$  with respect to these set of points as shown in Section 5.2). When using the  $\mathsf{PC}_{\mathsf{VC}}$  of Theorem 11, condition 2 can be achieved by selecting a point  $\tilde{x}$  such that running  $\mathsf{PP.Lookup}(\mathsf{pp},\tilde{x})$  produces indices  $\tilde{i}_1,\ldots,\tilde{i}_k$  some of which are never queried by  $\mathsf{PP.Lookup}(\mathsf{pp},x_i)$  for all  $x_i\in S^m$ . Without loss of generality let  $\tilde{i}_1,\tilde{i}_2,\ldots,\tilde{i}_\ell$  be those previously unqueried indices. Given  $\mathsf{DS}\leftarrow\mathsf{PP.PreProcess}(pp,0)$  we need to be able to change some of  $v_{\tilde{i}_1},\ldots,v_{\tilde{i}_\ell}$  in  $\mathsf{DS}$  (without modifying the remaining  $v_{\tilde{i}_{\ell+1}},\ldots,v_{\tilde{i}_k}$ ) in such a way that  $\mathsf{PP.Reconstruct}(\mathsf{pp},\tilde{x},v_{\tilde{i}_1},\ldots,v_{\tilde{i}_k})$  outputs a value y different from 0.

Under the conditions above we have the following attack:

- Run DS  $\leftarrow$  PP.PreProcess(pp,0). Modify the values  $v_{\tilde{i}_1},\ldots,v_{\tilde{i}_\ell}$  in DS as above and commit to the resulting modified DS with PC<sub>VC</sub>.Com.
- Compute the proofs  $\pi_{x_i}$  for all  $x_i$  and  $\tilde{\pi}_x$  for  $\tilde{x}$  as in PP.Open.

Clearly the resulting openings will be correct, the proofs  $\pi_{x_i}$  will open to f = 0 and the proof  $\tilde{x}$  will open to a value y different from 0. Because 0 is the only polynomial consistent with the evaluations on all  $x_i$  being 0, there cannot be any polynomial f in  $\mathcal{P}_{m,d}$  simultaneously consistent with  $f(x_i) = 0$  for  $i \in [d^m]$  and  $f(\tilde{x}) = y$ , breaking strong binding.

We show an explicit choice of  $\{x_i\}_{i\in[d^m]}$  and  $\tilde{x}$  for large enough q. For this, we recall that we are using the 2-level recursion version of the Kedlaya-Umans preprocessing algorithm presented in [LMW22, Theorem A.4]). The data structure output by PP.PreProcess(pp, f) consists of subtables  $\mathcal{T}_{p,p'}$  indexed by pairs (p,p') running over a certain subset of pairs of primes where in particular it always holds p' < p. The primes p run over the set  $\mathcal{P}$  of all primes less than some bound that guarantees that  $\prod_{p\in\mathcal{P}} p > M$  where M is the maximum possible value of any f(x) (for all  $f \in \mathcal{P}_{m,d}$  and  $x \in \mathbb{Z}_q^m$ ) when both f and x are lifted to the integers (i.e. computed without reduction mod q)<sup>28</sup>. Concretely,  $M = d^m q^{m(d-1)+1}$ . While in [KU08,KU11,LMW22] the set  $\mathcal{P}$  is taken to be

<span id="page-38-3"></span> $<sup>\</sup>overline{^{28}}$  This is because the algorithm reconstruct f(x) over the integers, using the Chinese Remainder Theorem

all primes up to  $16 \log M$  so that it works uniformly for any choice of d, q, m, here we are going to assume that we use instead the minimal set such that  $\prod_{p \in \mathcal{P}} p > M$  (i.e., if  $p_{\max}$  denotes the largest prime in the set, then  $\prod_{p \in \mathcal{P} \setminus \{p_{\max}\}} < M$ ) since this is enough to apply the algorithm and simplifies the exposition. Of course, in particular we have  $p_{\max} \leq 16 \log M$ .

Each sub-table  $\mathcal{T}_{p,p'}$  of the data structure contains the evaluations of certain polynomial  $f_{p,p'}$  on all  $a \in \mathbb{Z}_{p'}^m$ ; the polynomial  $f_{p,p'}$  is obtained by first lifting f to  $\mathbb{Z}[X_1,\ldots,X_m]$ , then reducing modulo p and then repeating this operation on the resulting polynomial and p'. Note that, in particular for  $\mathsf{DS} \leftarrow \mathsf{PP.PreProcess}(pp,0)$ , all entries at all tables  $\mathcal{T}_{p,p'}$  are 0. When evaluating f on any  $x \in \mathbb{Z}_q^m$ , only one index is queried for each  $\mathcal{T}_{p,p'}$ , namely the one containing  $f_{p,p'}(a)$  such that a is obtained from x by first lifting to  $\mathbb{Z}^m$ , then reducing modulo p and repeating the operation again for p' (we abbreviate this by writing  $a = x \mod p \mod p'$ ).

Let us partition  $\mathcal{P}$  as  $\mathcal{P} = \mathcal{P}_{\text{small}} \cup \mathcal{P}_{\text{large}}$  such that: i) all primes in  $\mathcal{P}_{\text{small}}$  are smaller than those in  $\mathcal{P}_{\text{large}}$ ; ii)  $(\prod_{p \in \mathcal{P}_{\text{small}}} p) > p_{\text{max}}$ ; and iii)  $\mathcal{P}_{\text{small}}$  is minimal satisfying conditions i) and ii). Note  $\mathcal{P}_{\text{large}}$  is non-empty: indeed  $\mathcal{P}_{\text{small}} \subseteq \mathcal{P} \setminus \{p_{\text{max}}\}$  because the product of all primes in  $\mathcal{P} \setminus \{p_{\text{max}}\}$  is always larger than the next prime  $p_{\text{max}}$ , as otherwise we would reach a contradiction when considering the primality of  $(\prod_{p \in \mathcal{P} \setminus \{p_{\text{max}}\}} p) - 1$ . <sup>29</sup> We call  $P = \prod_{p \in \mathcal{P}_{\text{small}}} p$  and  $Q = \prod_{p \in \mathcal{P}_{\text{large}}} p$ . From the above we have  $P > p_{\text{max}}$  but also  $Q = PQ/P < PQ/p_{\text{max}} = \prod_{p \in \mathcal{P} \setminus \{p_{\text{max}}\}} < M$ .

Our attack now applies when  $q > d(p_{\text{max}})^2$ . This holds when q is large enough with respect to d and m, because we know  $d(p_{\text{max}})^2 \le 16^2 d(\log M)^2$ , but  $\log M = O(\log q)$ . Under these conditions, we have that q > dP. Indeed, if  $P > q/d > (p_{\text{max}})^2$  then we would contradict our choice that our partition is such that  $\mathcal{P}_{\text{small}}$  is minimal: we can remove the largest prime from  $\mathcal{P}_{\text{small}}$  and we would still have  $P > p_{\text{max}}$ .

From q > dP, we conclude there are at least d values s in  $\mathbb{Z}_q$  such that when lifted to  $\mathbb{Z}$ , we have  $s \mod P = 1$ . Let S be the set of these d values, let  $\{x_i\}_{i \in [d^m]} = S^m$  (which satisfies condition 1 as explained above) and  $\tilde{x} = (0, 0, \dots, 0)$ . Finally we will choose the evaluation  $\tilde{y}$  to be  $Q \mod q$ , which is different than 0 because Q is a product of primes smaller than q.

By construction, for every  $x_i$ , we have the following: at every  $\mathcal{T}_{p,p'}$  with  $p \in \mathcal{P}_{small}$ , PP.Lookup(pp,  $x_i$ ) queries the index containing  $f_{p,p'}(1,1,\ldots,1)$  because all the coordinates of every  $x_i$  are 1 modulo  $P = \prod_{p \in \mathcal{P}_{small}} p$ .

On the other hand, PP.Lookup(pp,  $\tilde{x}$ ) queries the index of every table  $\mathcal{T}_{p,p'}$  containing  $f_{p,p'}(0,0,\ldots,0)$ . In particular the indices queried by PP.Lookup(pp,  $\tilde{x}$ ) at every table  $\mathcal{T}_{p,p'}$  with p in  $\mathcal{P}_{\text{small}}$  have not been queried by any PP.Lookup(pp,  $x_i$ ).

We can therefore change the entries  $v_i$  containing  $f_{p,p'}(0,0,\ldots,0)$  at tables  $\mathcal{T}_{p,p'}$  for every prime p in  $\mathcal{P}_{\text{small}}$  to be instead  $v'_i = Q \mod p \mod p'$ . On the other hand, for p in  $\mathcal{P}_{\text{large}}$  we have  $Q = 0 \mod p$ , so the unmodified 0 values in tables  $\mathcal{T}_{p,p'}$  are already consistent with the claimed evaluation  $f(\tilde{x}) = Q \mod q$ .

In summary, after doing these changes and calling  $v'_{\tilde{i}_1},\ldots,v'_{\tilde{i}_k}$  the modified values at the queried indices for  $\tilde{x}$  we have PP.Reconstruct $(pp,\tilde{x},v'_{\tilde{i}_1},\ldots,v'_{\tilde{i}_k})=Q \mod q$  by correctness of Kedlaya-Umans (where we are also using that Q < M), while PP.Reconstruct outputs 0 when applied to  $x_i$  and the corresponding unmodified entry values, which are all 0. Therefore the attack above will work.

## <span id="page-40-0"></span>C Knowledge Sound (SNARK-based) Polynomial Commitment with Fast Opening

The following construction make use of a SNARK for the (non-indexed) relation defined in Equation (1). Despite our preliminaries focus on indexed relations, the syntax and properties of non-indexed relations can be easily derived from Definition 6.

<span id="page-40-1"></span>Construction 3 Let PP be a PP scheme (Definition 10) and VC be a vector commitment scheme (Definition 22) for a message space  $\{0,1\}^*$ . Also, let SNARK be a SNARK for the relation  $\mathcal{R}_{VC,PP}$  (Equation (1)) parametrized by a VC and PP scheme (e.g., as the ones of Construction 1). We build a PC scheme  $PC_{SNARK}$  for multivariate polynomials over a ring R as follows:

- Setup( $1^{\lambda}$ , m, d)  $\rightarrow$  ck: On input the security parameter  $\lambda$ , the number of variables m, and a bound d for the degree of each variable, the setup algorithm computes  $pp \leftarrow PP.Params(R, m, d)$ ,  $ck_{VC} \leftarrow VC.Setup(1^{\lambda}, S)$ , and  $srs \leftarrow SNARK.Setup(1^{\lambda}, \mathcal{R}_{VC,PP})$ , where S is the upper bound on the size of the data structure included in pp (see Definition 10). Finally, it outputs  $ck = (pp, ck_{VC}, srs)$ .
- Com(ck,  $f; r) \rightarrow$  (cm, aux): On input the commitment key ck = (pp, ck<sub>VC</sub>, srs), a polynomial  $f \in \mathcal{P}_{m,d}$ , and a randomness r, the commit algorithm computes DS  $\leftarrow$  PP.PreProcess(pp, f) and (cm<sub>DS</sub>, aux<sub>DS</sub>)  $\leftarrow$  VC.Com(ck<sub>VC</sub>, DS; r) (recall that DS is a vector, i.e., DS = ( $v_1, \ldots, v_S$ )). Finally, it outputs cm = cm<sub>DS</sub> and aux = (aux<sub>DS</sub>, DS,  $\pi_{DS}$ ) where  $\pi_{DS} \leftarrow$  SNARK.Prove(srs, (pp, ck<sub>VC</sub>, cm<sub>DS</sub>), (f, r)).

## <span id="page-42-1"></span>D.1 UC Framework

In this work, we use the *Universal Composability* (UC) framework [Can01] for security proofs. UC follows the simulation-based paradigm where the security of a protocol is defined with respect to an ideal world where a trusted party, the functionality  $\mathcal{F}$ , does the all of the computation. Informally, a protocol securely realizes  $\mathcal{F}$  in the real world if for any real world adversary there exist an equivalent ideal world adversary (the simulator), "equivalent" meaning that any outside observer (the environment) cannot distinguish between the real protocol execution and the ideal execution. UC's composition theorem ensures that one can safely compose protocols that have been proven UC-secure.

Global Random Oracle. More precisely, we use the generalized UC (GUC) framework [CDPW07] which allows to model global functionalities that are shared between different protocol instances. We consider a hybrid-model where parties have access to a (non-programmable) global random oracle  $\mathcal{G}_{RO}$  as introduced in [CJS14]. We follow the simplified description from [CDG<sup>+</sup>18]. The  $\mathcal{G}_{RO}$  functionality can be queried by any party and the ideal adversary with two commands: QUERY and OBSERVE. The environment can query  $\mathcal{G}_{RO}$  by spawning additional dummy parties outside the context of the current protocol execution.  $\mathcal{G}_{RO}$  answers all new QUERY command by lazy sampling from the domain and stores them locally in a list  $\mathcal{Q}$ . A repeated query requires a simple lookup in  $\mathcal{Q}$ . Some QUERY queries are marked "illegitimate" and can be observed via OBSERVE command. Next we explain which query counts as an illegitimate one. Each party is associated with its party identifier pid and a session identifier sid. When a party queries  $\mathcal{G}_{RO}$  with the command (QUERY, x), the query is parsed as (s, x') where s denotes the session identifier associated with the party. A

  - Look up v if there is a pair (x, v) for some  $v \in \{0, 1\}^{\ell(\lambda)}$  in the (initially empty) list  $\mathcal{Q}$  of past queries. Else, choose uniformly  $v \in \{0, 1\}^{\ell(\lambda)}$  and store the pair (x, v) in  $\mathcal{Q}$ .

## <span id="page-43-0"></span>D.2 (Succinct) Non Interactive Zero-Knowledge Proofs

Although we already defined SNARK for indexed relations in ??, in this section we provide an alternative syntax and formalism in order to stay as faithful as possible to the original treatment in  $[GKO^+23]$ ; we will also not be needing indexed relations.

A non-interactive proof system for relation  $\mathcal{R}$ , denoted by  $\Pi_{\mathcal{R}}$ , consists a tuple of algorithms (Setup,  $\mathcal{O}_{\mathsf{Setup}}$ ,  $\mathcal{P}$ ,  $\mathcal{V}$ ).

- pp  $\leftarrow$  Setup(1 $^{\lambda}$ ): Takes as input the security parameter  $\lambda$  and outputs public parameters pp. Once Setup is invoked we assume that all of the following algorithms take pp as an implicit input.
- out  $\leftarrow \mathcal{O}_{Setup}(in)$ : A stateful setup oracle that takes an input string in and outputs out.
- $-\pi \leftarrow \mathcal{P}^{\mathcal{O}_{\mathsf{Setup}}}(x, w)$ : Takes as input a statement x and witness w, and outputs a proof  $\pi$  if  $(x, w) \in \mathcal{R}$ .
- $-b \leftarrow \mathcal{V}^{\mathcal{O}_{\mathsf{Setup}}}(x,\pi)$ : Takes as input a statement x and proof  $\pi$ , and outputs a bit b, indicating "accept" or "reject".

We introduce the setup oracle  $\mathcal{O}_{\mathsf{Setup}}$  to the notation of NIZKs to capture the two typical setup assumptions in an abstract manner. That is, if a proof system is instantiated in the CRS model, then  $\mathcal{O}_{\mathsf{Setup}}$  internally generates crs upon receiving a query with any input for the first time, and keeps outputting the same crs regardless of the input. When instantiating the RO model,  $\mathcal{O}_{\mathsf{Setup}}$  is initialized with an empty query-response table and proceeds as follows. On receiving in  $\in \{0,1\}^*$ , if in has never been queried, sample uniform out  $\in \{0,1\}^{\ell(\lambda)}$ , store (in, out) in the table, and return out. Otherwise, look up the table to find out associated with in, and return out.

We define three basic security properties for  $\Pi_{\mathcal{R}}$  in the stand-alone setting.

**Definition 26 (Completeness).**  $\Pi_{\mathcal{R}}$  satisfies completeness if for every  $(x, w) \in \mathcal{R}$ , it holds that

$$\Pr\left[b = 1 \ : \ \mathsf{pp} \leftarrow \mathsf{Setup}(1^\lambda); \pi \leftarrow \mathcal{P}^{\mathcal{O}_{\mathsf{Setup}}}(x, w); b \leftarrow \mathcal{V}^{\mathcal{O}_{\mathsf{Setup}}}(x, \pi)\right] = 1.$$

We define zero-knowledge by following the syntax of [FKMV12,GOP+22]. A zero-knowledge simulator  $\mathcal{S}$  is defined as a stateful algorithm with initial state  $\mathsf{st} = \mathsf{pp}$  that operates in two modes. The first mode,  $(\mathsf{out}, \mathsf{st}') \leftarrow \mathcal{S}(1, \mathsf{st}, \mathsf{in})$  takes care of handling calls to the oracle  $\mathcal{O}_{\mathsf{Setup}}$  on input in. The second mode,  $(\pi, \mathsf{st}') \leftarrow \mathcal{S}(2, \mathsf{st}, x)$  simulates a proof for the input statement x. For convenience we define three "wrapper" oracles. These oracles are stateful and share the internal state  $\mathsf{st}$ , which initially contains an empty string.

- $-\mathcal{S}_1(\mathsf{in})$  to denote the oracle that returns the first output of  $\mathcal{S}(1,\mathsf{st},\mathsf{in})$ ;
- $-\mathcal{S}_2(x,w)$  that returns the first output of  $\mathcal{S}(2,\mathsf{st},x)$  if  $(x,w)\in\mathcal{R}$  and  $\perp$  otherwise;
- $-\mathcal{S}'_2(x)$  that returns the first output of  $\mathcal{S}(2,\mathsf{st},x)$ .

**Definition 27 ((Unbounded) Zero-Knowledge).** Consider a non-interactive proof system  $\Pi_{\mathcal{R}} = (\mathsf{Setup}, \mathcal{O}_{\mathsf{Setup}}, \mathcal{P}, \mathcal{V})$  for relation  $\mathcal{R}$ .  $\Pi_{\mathcal{R}}$  is unbounded non-interactive zero-knowledge (NIZK), if there exists a PPT simulator  $\mathcal{S}$  with wrapper oracles  $\mathcal{S}_1$  and  $\mathcal{S}_2$  such that for all PPT adversaries  $\mathcal{A}$  it holds that

## <span id="page-45-0"></span>D.3 Special Properties of Polynomial Commitments and Polynomial Encoding Schemes [\[GKO](#page-34-0)+23[,BCC](#page-33-0)+24]

In this section we follow [\[GKO](#page-34-0)+23[,BCC](#page-33-0)+24] and define a series of properties and notions that are ancillary to polynomial commitments. These include: unique proofs property, polynomial encoding scheme, evaluation hiding, non-extrapolation (which we will present only to show it is implied by evaluation hiding and thus justify why we do not include it among our hypothesis in Theorem 9). <span id="page-46-0"></span>**Definition 30 (Unique Proof (Polynomial Commitments)).** We say a polynomial commitment scheme has unique proofs if, for all PPT adversaries A,

$$\Pr \begin{bmatrix} \pi \neq \pi' & \mathsf{ck} \leftarrow \mathsf{Setup}(\lambda, d); \\ \wedge b = 1 & : & (c, z, y, \pi, \pi') \leftarrow \mathcal{A}(\mathsf{ck}); \\ \wedge b' = 1 & b \leftarrow \mathsf{Ver}(\mathsf{ck}, c, z, y, \pi); \\ b' \leftarrow \mathsf{Ver}(\mathsf{ck}, c, z, y, \pi') \end{bmatrix} \leq \mathsf{negl}(\lambda).$$

We adopt the definition of polynomial encoding scheme from [GKO<sup>+</sup>23] (using the variant formalization presented in [BCC<sup>+</sup>24]). At its essence, a polynomial encoding scheme takes a vector of field elements and outputs a corresponding (appropriately masked) polynomial.

<span id="page-46-2"></span>**Definition 31 (Polynomial Encoding Scheme).** *A* polynomial encoding scheme, *denoted by* = PES, *is a tuple of algorithms* (Enc, Dec)

- $-f \leftarrow \mathsf{Enc}(1^{\lambda}, \mathbf{w}, n, \ell; \boldsymbol{\rho})$ : Takes as inputs a security parameter,  $\mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{F}^n$ , dimension of the vector n > 0, evaluation bound  $\ell > 0$ , and randomness  $\boldsymbol{\rho} \in \mathbb{F}^{\ell}$ , and outputs a polynomial  $f \in \mathbb{F}_{< d}[X]$  where d is a function of n and  $\ell$ .
- $-\mathbf{w}' \leftarrow \mathsf{Dec}(1^{\lambda}, f, n, \ell)$ : Takes as inputs a security parameter,  $f \in \mathbb{F}_{< n+\ell}[X]$ , n > 0, and  $\ell > 0$ , and deterministically outputs  $\mathbf{w}' \in \mathbb{F}^n$ .

We say = PES is correct if  $\mathbf{w} = \mathsf{Dec}(1^\lambda, \mathsf{Enc}(1^\lambda, \mathbf{w}, n, \ell; \boldsymbol{\rho}), n, \ell)$  for any n > 0,  $\ell > 0$ ,  $\mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{F}^n$ , and  $\boldsymbol{\rho} \in \mathbb{F}^\ell$ . We define the stretch factor  $\mathsf{stretch}(\lambda, n, \ell)$  of the PES as the difference between the size of the encoding and the original size of the vector  $\mathbf{w}$ , i.e.,  $\mathsf{stretch}(\lambda, n, \ell)$  will always be equal to  $\mathsf{deg}(f) + 1 - n$ . We say that a polynomial encoding scheme is " $n \to_{\lambda} d$ " if, on input a security parameter  $\lambda$  and a string of size n, it outputs a polynomial of degree d.

We only consider polynomial encoding schemes where the size of the field domain is exponential in the security parameter, i.e.  $|\mathbb{F}| \in O(2^{\lambda})$ .

Let PCS = (Setup, Com, Open, Ver) be a polynomial commitment scheme in the random oracle model  $\mathcal{O}_{Setup}$  and PES = (Enc, Dec) be a polynomial encoding scheme. $$\begin{aligned} & \ell := \phi(\lambda, n, r); d := n + \operatorname{stretch}(\lambda, n, \ell); \\ & \operatorname{ck} \leftarrow \operatorname{Setup}(1^{\lambda}, d); \\ & \mathbb{F}^{n} \ni \mathbf{w} \leftarrow \mathcal{A}_{1}(\operatorname{ck}); \mathbf{z} \leftarrow \mathbb{F}^{r} \\ & \boldsymbol{\rho}_{w} \leftarrow \mathbb{F}^{\ell}; b \leftarrow \mathbb{F}^{\ell}; b \leftarrow \mathbb{F}^{r} \\ & \boldsymbol{\rho}_{w} \leftarrow \operatorname{Sinc}(1^{\lambda}, b \cdot \mathbf{w}, n, \ell; \boldsymbol{\rho}_{w}); \\ & c \leftarrow \operatorname{Com}(\operatorname{ck}, f); \\ & \boldsymbol{v} := f(\mathbf{z}); \\ & \boldsymbol{\pi} \leftarrow \operatorname{Open}(\operatorname{ck}, c, \mathbf{z}, \mathbf{y}, f); \\ & b' \leftarrow \mathcal{A}_{2}(c, \mathbf{y}, \boldsymbol{\pi}) \end{aligned}$$

where  $A_1, A_2$  share the internal states,  $\mathbf{y} := f(\mathbf{z})$  denotes setting  $y_i := f(z_i)$  for all  $i \in [|\mathbf{z}|]$ ,  $\pi \leftarrow \mathsf{Open}(\mathsf{ck}, c, \mathbf{z}, \mathbf{y}, f)$  denotes setting  $\pi_i \leftarrow \mathsf{Open}(\mathsf{ck}, c, z_i, y_i, f)$  for all  $i \in [|\mathbf{z}|]$ . If PCS is  $\phi$ -evaluation hiding with respect to PES for some  $\phi \in O_\lambda(n+r)$  then we simply say that "PCS is evaluation hiding with respect to PES".

<span id="page-47-0"></span>**Definition 33** ( $\phi$ -Non-Extrapolation). Let PCS = (Setup, Com, Open, Ver) be a polynomial commitment scheme in the random oracle model  $\mathcal{O}_{Setup}$  and PES = (Enc, Dec) be a polynomial encoding scheme. We say PCS supports  $\phi$ -non-extrapolation with respect to = PES if for all PPT adversaries  $\mathcal{A}$ , for all  $\lambda, n, r \in \mathbb{N}$ 

$$\ell := \phi(\lambda, n, r); d := n + \operatorname{stretch}(\lambda, n, \ell); \\ \operatorname{ck} \leftarrow \operatorname{Setup}(1^{\lambda}, d); \\ \operatorname{\mathbf{z}} \leftarrow \operatorname{\mathbb{F}}^{r}; \boldsymbol{\rho}_{w} \leftarrow \operatorname{\mathbb{F}}^{\ell}; \\ f \leftarrow \operatorname{Enc}(1^{\lambda}, 0^{n}, n, \ell; \boldsymbol{\rho}_{w}); \\ c \leftarrow \operatorname{Com}(\operatorname{ck}, f); \\ \operatorname{\mathbf{y}} := f(\operatorname{\mathbf{z}}); \\ \operatorname{\mathbf{\pi}} \leftarrow \operatorname{Open}(\operatorname{ck}, c, \operatorname{\mathbf{z}}, \operatorname{\mathbf{y}}, f); z^{*} \leftarrow \operatorname{\mathbb{F}} \\ (y^{*}, \pi^{*}) \leftarrow \mathcal{A}(\operatorname{ck}, c, \operatorname{\mathbf{z}}, \operatorname{\mathbf{y}}, \pi, z^{*}); \\ v \leftarrow \operatorname{Ver}(\operatorname{ck}, c, z^{*}, y^{*}, \pi^{*}) \end{bmatrix}$$

In its original presentation in [GKO<sup>+</sup>23], non-extrapolation property is described as something to be "proven separately". However, it is in fact implied by  $\phi$ -evaluation hiding in all scenarios of interest (as also hinted in [BCC<sup>+</sup>24]). An implication of this is that one can remove non-extrapolation from the requirements of the compiler in [GKO<sup>+</sup>23] (and therefore in Theorem 9). We formalize this intuition in the following theorem (whose proof is essentially the same as in [BCC<sup>+</sup>24] where it is used to prove a more specific statement).

**Theorem 13.** A polynomial commitment satisfying weak evaluation binding and  $\phi$ -evaluation hiding with respect to a polynomial encoding scheme also satisfies  $\phi$ -non-extrapolation with respect to the same encoding scheme.

*Proof.* Consider the following hybrids:

- Hyb<sub>0</sub>: this is the same as the game in Definition 33 where an all-zero vector of length n is encoded as a polynomial and we provide the adversary  $\mathcal{A} = (\mathcal{A}_1, \mathcal{A}_2)$  with up to r evaluation points and corresponding evaluation proofs.
- Hyb<sub>1</sub>: we now change part of the challenger's code. Instead of encoding an all-zero vector, we proceed by sampling a set of random evaluations and then using (in part) the evaluation points required by the adversary to interpolate the polynomial. More in detail:
  - we first sample d random evaluations  $y_i \leftarrow \mathbb{F}$ .
  - Let **z** be the sampled evaluation points and let **z**' a vector of unique points in **z**. Let  $r' := |\mathbf{z}'|$  and let n' := d r'.
  - Sample n' points  $\mathbf{z}''$  from  $\mathbb{F}^{n'}$ .
  - Interpolate f so that  $f(z_i') = y_i$  for  $i \in [r']$  and  $f(z_i'') = y_{j+r'}$  for  $j \in [n']$

• Compute commitments and evaluation proofs as before.

By applying  $\phi$ -evaluation hiding we can conclude that the two hybrids are indistinguishable and therefore the polynomial f looks random to  $\mathcal{A}$  after requesting r evaluations. Let us now consider  $(y^*, \pi^*)$ , the output of  $\mathcal{A}_2$  for  $z^* \leftarrow \mathbb{F}$ . By the previous observation, the probability that  $\Pr[y^* = f(z^*)]$  is negligible. If  $y^* \neq f(z^*)$  and  $\mathcal{A}$  wins it is then possible to break evaluation binding since we can produce two valid evaluation proofs for two distinct points for the same committed polynomial. This concludes the proof.

#### <span id="page-51-1"></span>E.1 Proof of Theorem 4

Correctness. The correctness of  $PC_{VC}$  follows from the correctness of PP and VC, respectively; this is straightforward by inspection.

**Evaluation Binding.** We proceed by contradiction. Assume an adversary  $\mathcal{A}$  that breaks the evaluation binding of  $\mathsf{PC}_{\mathsf{VC}}$  with non-negligible probability  $\epsilon$ . We demonstrate how to construct an adversary  $\mathcal{B}$  that breaks the position binding of  $\mathsf{VC}$  with the same probability.  $\mathcal{B}$  selects the public parameters  $\mathsf{pp} \leftarrow \mathsf{PP.Params}(R, m, d)$  of the PP scheme and receives from the challenger a  $\mathsf{VC}$  commitment key  $\mathsf{ck}_{\mathsf{VC}}$  corresponding to the length S in  $\mathsf{pp}$ . Then,  $\mathcal{B}$  runs  $\mathcal{A}$  on input  $\mathsf{ck} = (\mathsf{ck}_{\mathsf{VC}}, \mathsf{pp})$ , which, in turn, returns  $(\mathsf{cm}, x, y, \pi_x, y', \pi'_x)$  to  $\mathcal{B}$ , where:

$$\operatorname{Ver}(\operatorname{ck},\operatorname{cm},x,y,\pi_x)=1 \wedge \operatorname{Ver}(\operatorname{ck},\operatorname{cm},x,y',\pi_x')=1 \wedge y \neq y'.$$

Let  $\mathsf{cm} = \mathsf{cm}_{\mathsf{DS}}$ ,  $\pi_x = (v_1, \pi_1, \dots, v_k, \pi_k)$ , and  $\pi'_x = (v'_1, \pi'_1, \dots, v'_k, \pi'_k)$ . Then, the above equation can be rewritten as follows:

$$\bigwedge_{j \in [k]} \mathsf{VC.Ver}(\mathsf{ck}_{\mathsf{VC}}, \mathsf{cm}_{\mathsf{DS}}, i_j, v_j, \pi_j) = 1 \ \land \ y = \mathsf{PP.Reconstruct}(\mathsf{pp}, x, v_1, \dots, v_k)$$
 
$$\bigwedge_{j \in [k]} \mathsf{VC.Ver}(\mathsf{ck}_{\mathsf{VC}}, \mathsf{cm}_{\mathsf{DS}}, i_j, v_j', \pi_j') = 1 \ \land \ y' = \mathsf{PP.Reconstruct}(\mathsf{pp}, x, v_1', \dots, v_k')$$
 
$$\bigwedge_{j \in [k]} \mathsf{VC.Ver}(\mathsf{ck}_{\mathsf{VC}}, \mathsf{cm}_{\mathsf{DS}}, i_j, v_j', \pi_j') = 1 \ \land \ y' = \mathsf{PP.Reconstruct}(\mathsf{pp}, x, v_1', \dots, v_k')$$
 
$$\bigwedge_{j \in [k]} \mathsf{VC.Ver}(\mathsf{ck}_{\mathsf{VC}}, \mathsf{cm}_{\mathsf{DS}}, i_j, v_j', \pi_j') = 1 \ \land \ y' = \mathsf{PP.Reconstruct}(\mathsf{pp}, x, v_1', \dots, v_k')$$

where  $(i_1, \ldots, i_k) \leftarrow \mathsf{PP}.\mathsf{Lookup}(\mathsf{pp}, x)$ .

Consider the following two mutually exclusive cases:

 $-(v_1,...,v_k)=(v'_1,...,v'_k)$ . Then, we obtain

$$y = \mathsf{PP}.\mathsf{Reconstruct}(\mathsf{pp}, x, v_1, \dots, v_k) = \mathsf{PP}.\mathsf{Reconstruct}(\mathsf{pp}, x, v_1', \dots, v_k') = y'$$

which contradicts  $y \neq y'$  as defined above.

 $-(v_1,...,v_k) \neq (v_1',...,v_k')$ . Then, there exists some  $j \in [k]$  such that  $v_j \neq v_j'$  and VC.Ver(ck<sub>VC</sub>, cm<sub>DS</sub>,  $i_j, v_j, \pi_j$ ) = VC.Ver(ck<sub>VC</sub>, cm<sub>DS</sub>,  $i_j, v_j', \pi_j'$ ) = 1. In this case,  $\mathcal{B}$  returns (cm<sub>DS</sub>,  $i_j, v_j, \pi_j, v_j'$ ,  $\pi_j'$ ), which contradicts the position binding property of VC.

This concludes the proof.

**Succinctness.** We prove the succinctness of  $PC_{VC}$  for the multivariate case. The proof for univariate polynomials is analogous.

Regarding the commitment size, we observe that cm corresponds to  $cm_{DS}$ , i.e., the VC commitment over the vector DS (data structure) of size S. By combining the succinctness of VC and the efficiency of PP, we have:

<span id="page-52-0"></span>
$$S \in \mathsf{poly}(\log |R|, d^m) \text{ and } |\mathsf{cm}| = |\mathsf{cm}_{\mathsf{DS}}| \in \mathsf{poly}(\lambda \log |R|, \log(S)).$$
 (7)

The above implies that the size of cm in PC is  $poly(\lambda \log |R|, \log(d^m))$ , where  $d^m$  represents the size of the polynomial.

Regarding the opening proof size, we note that  $\pi_x$  consists of k opening proofs  $\{\pi_j\}_{j\in[k]}$  and k blocks  $\{v_j\}_{j\in[k]}$  of the data structure S, where k is the number of indices generated by PP.Lookup. By combining the succinctness of VC and the efficiency of PP, we obtain:

<span id="page-52-1"></span>
$$k, |v_i| \in \mathsf{poly}(\log |R|, d, m) \text{ and } |\pi_i| \in \mathsf{poly}(\lambda, \log |R|, \log(S)),$$
 (8)

where S is as defined in Equation (7), and the bounds on k and  $v_j$  are derived from the running time of both PP.Lookup and PP.Reconstruct (see Definition 12). By combining these observations, we conclude that  $|\pi_x| = k(|\pi_j| + |v_j|) \in \text{poly}(\lambda, \log |R|, d, m)$ .

Finally, the running time of Ver in PC<sub>VC</sub> can be decomposed as follows:

- The running time of both PP.Lookup and PP.Reconstruct, which is bounded by poly(log |R|, d, m), as PP satisfies efficiency.

- k times the running time of VC.Ver, where both are bounded by  $poly(\lambda, \log |R|, d, m)$ . This follows from the fact that  $k \in poly(\log |R|, d, m)$ , as defined in Equation (8), and that VC satisfies succinctness.

This concludes the proof.

**Fast Openings.** We prove the fast openings of  $PC_{VC}$  for the multivariate case. The proof for univariate polynomials is analogous.

The running time of Com in  $PC_{VC}$  can be decomposed as follows:

- The running time of PP.PreProcess, which is bounded by  $poly(log |R|, d^m)$  since PP is efficient.
- The running time of VC.Com. Since VC satisfies fast openings, its running time is bounded by  $\mathsf{poly}(\lambda, \log S) \cdot S$ , where S is the size of the data structure generated by PP.PreProcess. Due to the efficiency of PP, we get  $S \in \mathsf{poly}(\log |R|, d^m)$ .

By combining these observations, we conclude that Com runs in time  $poly(\lambda, \log |R|, d^m)$ . Finally, the running time of Open in  $PC_{VC}$  can be decomposed as follows:

- The running time of PP.Lookup, which is bounded by poly(log |R|, d, m) since PP is efficient.
- k times the running time of VC.Open. Since VC satisfies fast openings, the running time of VC.Open is bounded by  $poly(\lambda, \log S)$ . Given that PP is efficient, we have  $S \in poly(\log |R|, d^m)$  and  $k \in poly(\log |R|, d, m)$ , where the latter is bounded by the running time of PP.Lookup.

These bounds imply that Open runs in time  $poly(\lambda, \log |R|, d, m)$ .

## <span id="page-53-0"></span>E.2 Proof of Lemmas 1 and 2

**Proof of Lemma 1.** The proof is straightforward and we present only an informal outline. Recall that the unique proof property (informally) states that no efficient adversary can produce a polynomial commitment cm, a claimed evaluation output y and two different accepting proofs  $\pi$  and  $\pi'$  for cm and y. The proof follows by contradiction: if we assume that there exists such an adversary for PC<sup>hyb</sup> then we are able to break either the unique proof property or the evaluation binding for at least one of the two underlying schemes hidPC and subPC. More in detail: recall from Fig. 1 that a proof for PC<sup>hyb</sup> consists of a tuple  $(\pi_f, \pi_g, y_g)$ . Now assume an adversary that successfully provides two different tuples  $\pi := (\pi_f, \pi_g, y_g)$  and  $\pi' := (\pi'_f, \pi'_g, y'_g)$ . There are two possibilities: either we have that (i)  $y_g = y'_g$ , or (ii)  $y_g \neq y'_g$ . If (i) holds then we must have  $\pi_\omega \neq \pi'_\omega$  (with  $\omega \in \{f,g\}$ ) implying that we are able to produce two distinct proofs for the same point  $y_g$  (resp.  $y_f := y - y_g$ ) for the polynomial commitment hidPC (resp. subPC). If (ii) then  $\pi_g$  is a valid proof claiming evaluation output  $y'_g \neq y_g$ , contradicting the assumption on hidPC satisfying evaluation binding.

**Proof of Lemma 2.** Consider an adversary  $\mathcal{A}_{\phi}^{\text{hyb}} = (\mathcal{A}_{\phi,1}^{\text{hyb}}, \mathcal{A}_{\phi,2}^{\text{hyb}})$  against the  $\phi$ -evaluation game for PC<sup>hyb</sup> and parameters  $\lambda, n, r \in \mathbb{N}$  (all other parameters are determined by these; see caption of Fig. 7). We define a series of hybrids. The first hybrid  $\mathcal{H}_0$  (Fig. 7) corresponds to the  $\phi$ -hiding game where we encode the vector  $\mathbf{w}$  provided by the adversary. We fully expand the encoding step of the polynomial encoding scheme PES<sup>hyb</sup> since this is where the changes will occur between hybrids. The last hybrid  $\mathcal{H}_3$  (Fig. 7) corresponds to the same game as  $\mathcal{H}_0$  but where we encode the vector of all zeros instead of what is provided by the adversary.

- $-\mathcal{H}_0 \approx \mathcal{H}_1$ : the difference between these two games is that in  $\mathcal{H}_1$  has to do with how we define the polynomial g: instead of producing it as an encoding of sk, we instead encode a dummy string of all zeros. In order to show that  $\mathcal{H}_0 \not\approx \mathcal{H}_1$  we can build an adversary  $\mathcal{A}_{\phi}^{\text{hid}}$  (shown in Appendix E.2) against the  $\phi$ -hiding game. This adversary would emulate all the other parts of the execution (the ciphertexts, the commitment and the polynomial commitment proofs for f, etc.) and then use the output of  $\mathcal{A}_{\phi}^{\text{hyb}}$  to identify whether it has received an encoding of sk or  $\mathbf{0}$
- $-\mathcal{H}_1 \approx \mathcal{H}_2$ : the only difference between these two hybrids is what is actually encrypted in the output of PES<sup>hyb</sup>.Enc (w or 0). We can rely on semantic security to claim that the difference in the advantage of the adversary is negligible. We construct a reduction against semantic security (Definition 38) so that a noticeable difference in output between the two hybrids corresponds to a noticeable advantage against the semantic security experiment leading to a contradiction. Our reduction to semantic security (Definition 38) is straightforward and at the high level works as follows:
  - the target messages are  $(m_0 := \mathbf{w}, m_1 := \mathbf{0});$
  - the challenger generates a secret key and provides the adversary with an encryption  $\mathsf{ct}^*$  of  $m_b$  where  $b \leftarrow \$ \{0, 1\};$
  - the adversary constructs g exactly as done in both  $\mathcal{H}_1$  and  $\mathcal{H}_2$  (as an encoding of  $\mathbf{0}$ ), f as polyFromCoeffs( $\mathsf{ct}^{\star}$ ), it then proceeds following the rest of the pseudocode in  $\mathcal{H}_1$  (and hence  $\mathcal{H}_2$ ).
  - the adversary finally outputs b'.
- $-\mathcal{H}_2 \approx \mathcal{H}_3$ : here we restore the encoding of sk to generate g; we can argue exactly as we did to show  $\mathcal{H}_0 \approx \mathcal{H}_1$ .

Since we have shown that  $\mathcal{H}_0 \approx \mathcal{H}_3$ , we can immediately conclude that the advantage of any PPT adversary against  $\phi$ -hiding would be negligible.

## <span id="page-57-0"></span>E.3 Proof of Lemma 3

Consider an adversary against the unique proof property for polynomial commitments (Definition 30) and let  $\pi, \pi'$  be the two proofs that it generates. Denote by  $\mathbf{v}$  and  $\mathbf{v}'$  the values in  $\pi$  and  $\pi'$  respectively as by the notation in the verification algorithm in Section 4. Then there are two cases: (A)  $\mathbf{v} \neq \mathbf{v}'$ ; (B)  $\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{v}'$ . If case (A) occurs it is easy to see that one can break position binding. This is because the two verification executions will have the same set of indices returned by PP.Lookup(pp, x) and therefore there will be an index i for which the adversary is successfully proving two distinct values  $v_i \neq v_i'$ . Case (B) can directly be reduced to the unique proof property of the underlying vector commitment.

# <span id="page-57-1"></span>F Our "Hybrid" Construction and Other Instantiations of the [GKO<sup>+</sup>23] Compiler in Literature

Instantiations in [GKO<sup>+</sup>23] In [GKO<sup>+</sup>23], Ganesh et al. discuss simple forms of polynomial encodings (some described in Appendix D) which allows the KZG polynomial commitment [KZG10] to satisfy evaluation-hiding. One of their approaches is based on adding masking coefficients to the polynomial whose vector coefficients is  $\mathbf{w}$ . That is,  $\mathsf{Enc}(\mathbf{w})$  returns  $f(X) := \sum_{i=1}^n w_i X^{i-1} + \sum_{i=1}^{\ell} \rho_i X^{n+i-1}$  where the  $\rho_i$ -s are freshly sampled scalars and  $\ell$  is a parameter determining the level of "masking". They also propose a similar approach based on polynomial interpolations, that is, the encoding returns a polynomial obtained as follows: let  $\rho_1, \ldots, \rho_\ell$  be some random scalars; encoding then returns the polynomial obtained through interpolation (over a fixed domain) of the vector  $(\mathbf{w}||\rho_1, \ldots, \rho_\ell)$ . Ganesh et al. show that the KZG polynomial commitment [KZG10] is evaluation-hiding with respect to these two encoding schemes.

The encoding schemes above cannot provide hiding for our construction in Section 4, at least directly and with the analysis from [GKO<sup>+</sup>23]. The reason is that those techniques, when adding  $\ell$  random coefficients/evaluations, can provide security up to  $\ell-1$  evaluations seen by the adversary (the intuition behind the security of KZG when paired with them can roughly be reduced to this observation). However, our construction, at each opening provides several evaluations of polynomials directly related to  $\mathbf{w}$ ; recall, in fact that at each opening, we provide evaluations of polynomials corresponding to some type of CRT encoding of the original polynomial (each of these sub-polynomials is related to the original polynomials evaluated modulo p, where p is a relatively small prime). As a consequence, at each evaluation we are intuitively leaking significantly more than what we leak with an approach like the one based on KZG.

It is at least conceivable that a more sophisticated analysis could show that this type of encodings could yield evaluation hiding if one used an appropriate number of extra coefficient/evaluations. However, this may reveal to be quite complicated (at the very least, it eluded some initial attempts on our side). We leave this as an interesting open problem.

The recent work in  $[BCC^+24]$  also provides a PC and PES with similar properties. Their constructions cannot unfortunately be used in our setting: their PC verifier runs in linear time and, while their PES might be evaluation hiding when paired with our  $PC_{VC}$ , it would require an extremely complex analysis, similar to the one they need to do to show the compatibility of their PES with their Bulletproofs-based PC (in contrast, our hybrid approach is very simple to prove secure). For this reason we do not pursue this path.

Viceversa, our construction, cannot provide a simpler solution for their setting: since they require transparent constructions they cannot rely on KZG-like constructions as we do and thus need to bootstrap the [\[GKO](#page-34-0)+23] framework with new tools themselves.
