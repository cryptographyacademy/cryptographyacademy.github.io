# **Tailored Limb Counts, Faster Arithmetic: Improved TMVP Decompositions for Curve5453 and Curve6071**

Murat Cenk<sup>1</sup> , N. Gamze Orhon Kılıç<sup>2</sup> , Halil Kemal Taşkın<sup>3</sup> and Oğuz Yayla<sup>2</sup>

<sup>1</sup> Ripple Lab Inc, Canada 2 Institute of Applied Mathematics, Middle East Technical University, Türkiye <sup>3</sup> ASELSAN, Türkiye

**Abstract.** Curve5453 and Curve6071 are Montgomery curves over the primes 2 <sup>545</sup> − 3 and 2 <sup>607</sup> − 1, providing 271- and 302-bit classical security, respectively. Their TMVPbased field multiplication in 10-limb representation costs 77 multiplications. We reduce this to 60 for Curve5453 (22% fewer) using a 9-limb radix-2 <sup>61</sup> representation, and to 54 for Curve6071 (30% fewer) using a 12-limb radix-2 <sup>51</sup> representation with hierarchical block-level TMVP. Choosing the limb count to produce 3 × 3 Toeplitz blocks aligns the structure with the size-3 TMVP formula, computing each block product in 6 multiplications rather than 9. Portable C implementations benchmarked on ARM64 and x86-64 confirm speedups of up to 16% in field multiplication and 13% in scalar multiplication. On ARM64, Curve5453 reaches 90*.*6% of OpenSSL's assemblyoptimized NIST P-521 ECDH throughput with 12 additional bits of classical security, and Curve6071 delivers 302-bit classical security at 80*.*8% of P-521's throughput.

**Keywords:** TMVP · Field multiplication · Montgomery curves · Radix representations · Efficient implementation

## **1 Introduction**

Elliptic curve cryptography (ECC), introduced independently by Miller [\[Mil86\]](#page-9-0) and Koblitz [\[Kob87\]](#page-9-1), is widely deployed in TLS, SSH, and related protocols. Widely used curves such as Curve25519 [\[Ber06\]](#page-9-2) and NIST P-256 [\[Nat23\]](#page-10-0) target 128-bit classical security; however, the prospect of fault-tolerant quantum computers running Shor's algorithm [\[Sho94\]](#page-10-1) estimated to require roughly 2*,*124 logical qubits at this security level [\[HJN](#page-9-3)<sup>+</sup>20]—motivates higher security margins, particularly for hybrid key exchange protocols that pair classical ECC with post-quantum primitives. The SafeCurves project [\[BL25\]](#page-9-4) evaluates curves against criteria spanning parameter generation, ECDLP hardness, and implementationlevel security; yet among compliant curves, none beyond 260-bit security has demonstrated competitive performance.

Montgomery curves [\[Mon87,](#page-9-5) [CS18\]](#page-9-6) provide inherent constant-time scalar multiplication via the Montgomery ladder [\[BL17\]](#page-9-7), making them well suited for side-channel-resistant implementations. The dominant cost in ladder-based scalar multiplication is field multiplication, and for primes of the form *p* = 2*<sup>m</sup>* − *c*—Mersenne (*c* = 1) or Crandall primes [\[Cra92\]](#page-9-8)—the combined multiplication-and-reduction step can be cast as a Toeplitz matrix-vector product (TMVP). Ali and Cenk [\[AC18\]](#page-9-9) first exploited this structure for F2 <sup>521</sup>−1; Taşkın and Cenk [\[TC18,](#page-10-2) [TC20\]](#page-10-3) generalized the approach to Curve25519 and introduced the notion of *TMVP-friendly primes*, where the reduction constant admits efficient scaling.

{1}------------------------------------------------

Building on this line of work, Cenk et al. [COKTY25] proposed two Montgomery curves  $E_M: y^2 = x^3 + Ax^2 + x$  over TMVP-friendly primes with high classical security: Curve5453, over the Crandall prime  $p = 2^{545} - 3$  with A = 115794 and 271-bit security; and Curve6071, over the Mersenne prime  $p = 2^{607} - 1$  with A = 2720010 and 302-bit security—the highest among SafeCurves-compliant curves. Both curves have cofactor 4, coefficient B = 1, and are generated by a rigid, deterministic process; Curve6071 satisfies all eleven SafeCurves criteria, while Curve5453 meets all except completeness. Using 10-limb radix representations (radix- $2^{55}$  for  $\mathbb{F}_{5453}$ , radix- $2^{61}$  for  $\mathbb{F}_{6071}$ ), field multiplication was realized via recursive TMVP decomposition of the  $10 \times 10$  Toeplitz matrices at a cost of 77 multiplications, with variable-base scalar multiplication measured at 871,898 and 903,542 cycles on ARM64, respectively.

In this work, we investigate alternative limb representations for the fields in [COKTY25]. For  $\mathbb{F}_{5453}$ , a 9-limb radix-2<sup>61</sup> representation yields a 9 × 9 Toeplitz matrix that admits a  $3 \times 3$  block decomposition; applying the size-3 TMVP formula at the inner level reduces the cost to 60 multiplications. For  $\mathbb{F}_{6071}$ , a 12-limb radix-2<sup>51</sup> representation produces a  $12 \times 12$ Toeplitz structure supporting hierarchical  $3 \times 3$  block TMVP at both outer and inner levels, achieving 54 multiplications. The reduction from 77 to 60 multiplications for  $\mathbb{F}_{5453}$  (22%) arises because the 9-limb representation yields a  $3 \times 3$  block partition whose sub-matrices align directly with the size-3 TMVP formula, whereas the 10-limb baseline requires a less natural recursive  $2 \times (2+3)$  decomposition. For  $\mathbb{F}_{6071}$ , we systematically explore four strategies spanning 104 to 54 multiplications; the best exploits the  $12 = 3 \times 4$  factorization to apply a size-3 TMVP at the block level, reducing the number of  $4 \times 4$  sub-products from nine to six and achieving a 30% reduction. Beyond the algebraic savings, fewer limbs in the 9-limb case also reduce the per-operation cost of field additions and carry propagation throughout the Montgomery ladder, compounding the benefit at the scalar multiplication level. We implement all strategies in portable C and provide dual-platform benchmarks on ARM64 and x86-64—the first cross-platform evaluation for these curves—together with a comparative ECDH throughput analysis against OpenSSL.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section 2 recalls the TMVP formulas and introduces the block decomposition framework; Section 3 develops the alternative representations and multiplication strategies for both fields; Section 4 presents dual-platform benchmarks and a comparative ECDH throughput analysis; and Section 5 concludes.

## <span id="page-1-0"></span>2 Preliminaries and Notation

This section establishes the notation and algebraic tools used throughout the paper: radix representations of field elements, the Toeplitz structure of the resulting multiplication matrices, the TMVP formulas that reduce the cost of computing these products, and a block decomposition framework that organizes the computation at two levels.

### <span id="page-1-1"></span>2.1 Radix Representation

Let  $p = 2^m - c$  for a small integer c > 0, and let  $\alpha \in \mathbb{F}_p$ . A radix- $2^r$  representation splits  $\alpha$  into  $t = \lceil m/r \rceil$  limbs, each fitting in a single machine word:

$$\alpha = \sum_{i=0}^{t-1} \alpha_i \, 2^{\lceil ri \rceil}, \qquad 0 \le \alpha_i < 2^r. \tag{1}$$

Viewing  $\alpha$  as the polynomial  $\alpha(x) = \sum_i \alpha_i \, x^i$  evaluated at  $x = 2^r$ , the product  $\gamma = \alpha \cdot \beta \mod p$  corresponds to polynomial multiplication modulo  $x^t - d$ , where  $d = 2^{rt - m} \cdot c$ , reducing upper coefficients via  $\alpha_i \, x^{t+j} \equiv d \cdot \alpha_i \, x^j \pmod{x^t - d}$ .

{2}------------------------------------------------

## **2.2 Toeplitz Matrices**

A Toeplitz matrix is an *n* × *n* matrix with *Ti,j* = *ti*−*<sup>j</sup>* , determined by 2*n* − 1 parameters (*t*−(*n*−1)*, . . . , t*0*, . . . , tn*−1). The polynomial multiplication from Section [2.1](#page-1-1) yields precisely such a structure: a *t* × *t* Toeplitz matrix whose lower triangle contains the original limbs and whose upper triangle entries are scaled by *d*. This matrix admits a natural decomposition *M* = *L* + *d* · *U*, where *L* is the lower triangular part and *U* is the strictly upper triangular part; the products *Lβ* and *Uβ* are computed independently and combined as *γ* = *Lβ* + *d* · *Uβ*.

## **2.3 Toeplitz Matrix-Vector Product**

Computing *T* · *v* by the schoolbook method costs *n* <sup>2</sup> multiplications. The TMVP technique reduces this via Karatsuba-like decompositions that exploit the diagonal structure; we recall the formulas for sizes 2 and 3.

### **2.3.1 TMVP of Size 2.**

Given a 2 × 2 Toeplitz matrix with parameters (*α*0*, α*1*, α*2) and a vector (*β*0*, β*1) <sup>⊤</sup>, the product is computed as

<span id="page-2-1"></span>
$$\begin{bmatrix} \alpha_0 & \alpha_1 \\ \alpha_2 & \alpha_0 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} \beta_0 \\ \beta_1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} P_1 + P_2 \\ P_1 + P_3 \end{bmatrix}$$
 (2)

where *P*<sup>1</sup> = *α*0(*β*<sup>0</sup> + *β*1), *P*<sup>2</sup> = (*α*<sup>1</sup> − *α*0)*β*1, and *P*<sup>3</sup> = (*α*<sup>2</sup> − *α*0)*β*0. The cost is 3**M** + 3**A** + 1**A***<sup>d</sup>* [1](#page-2-0) , compared with 4**M** + 2**A***<sup>d</sup>* for the schoolbook method.

### **2.3.2 TMVP of Size 3.**

For a 3 × 3 Toeplitz matrix with parameters (*α*0*, α*1*, α*2*, α*3*, α*4) and a vector (*β*0*, β*1*, β*2) ⊤, the product is computed with six multiplications:

<span id="page-2-2"></span>
$$\begin{bmatrix} \alpha_{0} & \alpha_{1} & \alpha_{2} \\ \alpha_{3} & \alpha_{0} & \alpha_{1} \\ \alpha_{4} & \alpha_{3} & \alpha_{0} \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} \beta_{0} \\ \beta_{1} \\ \beta_{2} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} P_{3} + P_{4} + P_{6} \\ P_{2} - P_{4} + P_{5} \\ P_{1} - P_{2} - P_{3} \end{bmatrix}, \quad P_{1} = (\alpha_{4} + \alpha_{3} + \alpha_{0}) \cdot \beta_{0}, \\ P_{2} = \alpha_{3} \cdot (\beta_{0} - \beta_{1}), \\ P_{3} = \alpha_{0} \cdot (\beta_{0} - \beta_{2}), \\ P_{4} = \alpha_{1} \cdot (\beta_{1} - \beta_{2}), \\ P_{5} = (\alpha_{0} + \alpha_{1} + \alpha_{3}) \cdot \beta_{1}, \\ P_{6} = (\alpha_{0} + \alpha_{1} + \alpha_{2}) \cdot \beta_{2}. \end{cases}$$
(3)

The cost is 6**M** + 8**A** + 6**A***d*, compared with 9**M** + 6**A***<sup>d</sup>* for the schoolbook method. Applying [\(2\)](#page-2-1) at the block level with 2×2 sub-matrices yields a size-4 TMVP at 3×3 = 9**M**; recursive composition of [\(2\)](#page-2-1) and [\(3\)](#page-2-2) produces TMVPs of arbitrary size.

## <span id="page-2-3"></span>**2.4 Block Decomposition Framework**

When *t* = *nk* for positive integers *n* and *k*, the *t* × *t* Toeplitz matrix *M* partitions into an *n* × *n* block Toeplitz array of *k* × *k* Toeplitz sub-matrices, with the input vector split into sub-vectors V0*, . . . ,* V*n*−<sup>1</sup> of dimension *k*.

### <span id="page-2-4"></span>**2.4.1 The** *L* **+** *dU* **Decomposition.**

Under the block partition, *L* is block lower triangular and *U* is block upper triangular (with strictly upper triangular diagonal blocks), giving

$$\gamma = L \cdot \beta + d \cdot (U \cdot \beta). \tag{4}$$

<span id="page-2-0"></span><sup>1</sup>Throughout this paper, **M** denotes a multiplication, **A** a single-precision addition or subtraction, and **A***<sup>d</sup>* a double-precision addition.

{3}------------------------------------------------

The factor d is applied once to  $U\beta$ —typically via bit shifts—rather than embedded in every upper-triangle multiplication. Three types of  $k \times k$  sub-matrices arise: diagonal blocks of L are lower triangular Toeplitz (reduced cost), off-diagonal blocks are full Toeplitz, and diagonal blocks of U are strictly upper triangular Toeplitz (cheapest). This decomposition is not required by all methods: when d is amenable to fast scaling, one may retain the full block Toeplitz structure and apply TMVP directly at the block level.

### <span id="page-3-2"></span>2.4.2 Composition Strategies.

Two computation levels arise: the *outer level* organizes the block products either by schoolbook enumeration or by applying a TMVP formula at the block level (e.g., size-3 TMVP reduces products from 9 to 6); the *inner level* computes each  $k \times k$  sub-product by schoolbook ( $k^2$  multiplications) or recursive TMVP. The choice at each level is independent; Section 3 explores combinations for the specific dimensions arising from the radix- $2^{61}$  and radix- $2^{51}$  representations.

## 3.1 Representing Elements of $\mathbb{F}_{5453}$ with Radix- $2^{61}$

Let  $\alpha \in \mathbb{F}_{5453}$  where  $p = 2^{545} - 3$ . The element  $\alpha$  is represented using 9 limbs in a radix- $2^{61}$  representation: 8 limbs of 61 bits and one top limb of 57 bits. The limb representation and its corresponding polynomial form are:

$$\alpha = [\alpha_0/61, \dots, \alpha_2/61, \alpha_8/57]$$

$$\alpha(x) = \sum_{i=0}^{8} \alpha_i 2^{\lceil 61i \rceil} x^i = \alpha_0 + \alpha_1 2^{61} x + \alpha_2 2^{122} x^2 + \dots + \alpha_8 2^{488} x^8.$$
(5)

Since  $p = 2^{545} - 3$ , evaluating  $x^9 = (2^{61})^9 = 2^{549}$  modulo p gives

$$x^9 = 2^4 \cdot 2^{545} \equiv 2^4 \cdot 3 = 48 \pmod{p},$$

so the reduction polynomial is  $x^9-48$ : any term  $a_ix^{9+j}$  arising from multiplication reduces as  $48 \cdot a_ix^j$ .

### **Using TMVP for 3 × 3 Products**

Each sub-product is computed using the size-3 TMVP [\(3\)](#page-2-2). Since A is lower triangular, its Toeplitz parameters are (*α*0*,* 0*,* 0*, α*1*, α*2); the product *P*<sup>4</sup> = 0 is eliminated, reducing the cost to 5**M** + 5**A** + 4**A***<sup>d</sup>* per product. The full Toeplitz matrices B and C incur the complete formula at 6**M** + 8**A** + 6**A***<sup>d</sup>* each. The strictly upper triangular D has parameters (0*, α*8*, α*7*,* 0*,* 0); only three products are non-zero, giving 3**M** + 2**A** + 2**A***d*. In total: 3 × 5 + 6 × 6 + 3 × 3 = **60M** + 69**A** + 54**A***d*.

### **Using Schoolbook for 3 × 3 Products**

Alternatively, each sub-product is computed by direct matrix-vector multiplication. The lower triangular A requires 1 + 2 + 3 = 6**M** + 3**A***<sup>d</sup>* per product, the full matrices B and C require 9**M** + 6**A***<sup>d</sup>* each, and the strictly upper triangular D costs 3**M** + 1**A***d*. The total is 3 × 6 + 6 × 9 + 3 × 3 = **81M** + 48**A***d*.

The final unreduced result is *h<sup>i</sup>* = *ℓ<sup>i</sup>* + 48 · *u<sup>i</sup>* for *i* = 0*, . . . ,* 8, where 48 = 2<sup>5</sup> + 2<sup>4</sup> is implemented as (*u<sup>i</sup>* ≪ 5) + (*u<sup>i</sup>* ≪ 4). #### Schoolbook Outer with Size-3 Inner Products

The matrix also admits a  $4 \times 4$  block partition with k = 3, organized as a two-level hierarchy:  $2 \times 2$  blocks of  $6 \times 6$ , each subdivided into  $2 \times 2$  blocks of  $3 \times 3$  sub-matrices, yielding five distinct blocks  $\mathcal{X}_0, \ldots, \mathcal{X}_4$ . Applying the L + dU decomposition (Section 2.4.1) at the top level with schoolbook composition produces six  $6 \times 6$  products; expanding each into  $\mathcal{X}_i$ -vector sub-products of size 3 and applying (3) gives  $\mathcal{X}_0$  (lower triangular) at  $5\mathbf{M}$ ,  $\mathcal{X}_1 - \mathcal{X}_3$  (full) at  $6\mathbf{M}$ , and  $\mathcal{X}_4$  (strictly upper triangular) at  $3\mathbf{M}$ , for a total of  $2(2 \times 5 + 6) + 2(4 \times 6) + 2(2 \times 3 + 6) = \mathbf{104M}$ . This exceeds the 10-limb baseline of  $77\mathbf{M}$ , motivating a coarser block partition.

#### Schoolbook Outer with Size-4 Inner Products

Returning to the  $3 \times 3$  block partition with k = 4, the L + dU decomposition (Section 2.4.1) with schoolbook outer composition (Section 2.4.2) yields nine  $4 \times 4$  Toeplitz products. Since  $\mathcal{F}$  already contains d-scaled entries in its upper triangle, all nine blocks are full  $4 \times 4$  Toeplitz matrices. Each  $4 \times 4$  TMVP is computed by applying (2) at the block level with  $2 \times 2$  sub-matrices, requiring  $3 \times 3 = 9\mathbf{M}$ . The total is  $9 \times 9 = 81\mathbf{M}$ . A variant, denoted  $\mathbf{sbtmvp2}_{12-51}$ , applies the size-2 TMVP only at the base level of each  $4 \times 4$  inner product rather than recursively; it is included in the benchmarks of Section 4.

## <span id="page-8-0"></span>5 Conclusion

We have shown that alternative radix representations for the fields underlying Curve5453 and Curve6071 yield substantially more efficient TMVP-based multiplication than the 10-limb baseline of [COKTY25]. For  $\mathbb{F}_{5453}$ , the 9-limb radix- $2^{61}$  representation reduces the multiplication cost from 77M to 60M (22%), owing to a 3 × 3 block partition whose sub-matrices align directly with the size-3 TMVP formula. For  $\mathbb{F}_{6071}$ , the 12-limb radix- $2^{51}$  representation enables a hierarchical 3 × 3 block TMVP that achieves 54M (30% reduction). Dual-platform benchmarks confirm that these algebraic savings translate into practical speedups: sbtmvp<sub>9-61</sub> delivers 16% faster field multiplication and 13% faster scalar multiplication on ARM64 compared to the 10-limb TMVP, while tmvp<sub>12-51</sub> achieves 9.5% faster field multiplication on both platforms. An ECDH throughput comparison against OpenSSL on the same platform shows that Curve5453 reaches 90.6% of NIST P-521's throughput with 12 additional bits of security, and Curve6071 provides 302-bit security at 80.8% of P-521's throughput—both using portable C rather than hand-optimized assembly.
