Shuichi Katsumata<sup>1</sup> , Ryo Nishimaki<sup>2</sup> , Shota Yamada<sup>1</sup> , Takashi Yamakawa<sup>2</sup>

#### **Abstract**

A non-interactive zero-knowledge (NIZK) protocol enables a prover to convince a verifier of the truth of a statement without leaking any other information by sending a single message. The main focus of this work is on exploring short pairing-based NIZKs for all **NP** languages based on standard assumptions. In this regime, the seminal work of Groth, Ostrovsky, and Sahai (J.ACM'12) (GOS-NIZK) is still considered to be the state-of-the-art. Although fairly efficient, one drawback of GOS-NIZK is that the proof size is *multiplicative* in the circuit size computing the **NP** relation. That is, the proof size grows by *O*(|*C*|*κ*), where *C* is the circuit for the **NP** relation and *κ* is the security parameter. By now, there have been numerous follow-up works focusing on shortening the proof size of pairing-based NIZKs, however, thus far, all works come at the cost of relying either on a non-standard knowledge-type assumption or a non-static *q*-type assumption. Specifically, improving the proof size of the original GOS-NIZK under the same standard assumption has remained as an open problem.

Our main result is a construction of a pairing-based NIZK for all of **NP** whose proof size is *additive* in |*C*|, that is, the proof size only grows by |*C*| + poly(*κ*), based on the decisional linear (DLIN) assumption. Since the DLIN assumption is the same assumption underlying GOS-NIZK, our NIZK is a strict improvement on their proof size.

As by-products of our main result, we also obtain the following two results: (1) We construct a *perfectly zeroknowledge* NIZK (NIPZK) for **NP** relations computable in **NC**<sup>1</sup> with proof size |*w*| · poly(*κ*) where |*w*| is the witness length based on the DLIN assumption. This is the first pairing-based NIPZK for a non-trivial class of **NP** languages whose proof size is independent of |*C*| based on a standard assumption. (2) We construct a universally composable (UC) NIZK for **NP** relations computable in **NC**<sup>1</sup> in the erasure-free adaptive setting whose proof size is |*w*| · poly(*κ*) from the DLIN assumption. This is an improvement over the recent result of Katsumata, Nishimaki, Yamada, and Yamakawa (CRYPTO'19), which gave a similar result based on a non-static *q*-type assumption.

The main building block for all of our NIZKs is a constrained signature scheme with *decomposable online-offline efficiency*. This is a property which we newly introduce in this paper and construct from the DLIN assumption. We believe this construction is of an independent interest.

## <span id="page-0-2"></span>**1 Introduction**

#### <span id="page-0-3"></span>**1.1 Background**

Zero-knowledge proof system [\[GMR89\]](#page-39-0) is an interactive protocol that allows a prover to convince a verifier about the validity of a statement without revealing anything beyond the fact that the statement is true. A variant of this, which is both practically and theoretically important, are *non-interactive* zero-knowledge (NIZK) proofs[1](#page-0-0) [\[BFM88\]](#page-37-0) where the prover is only required to send one message to the verifier to prove the validity of the statement in question. Not only have NIZKs shown to be a ubiquitous building block for cryptographic primitives and protocols, but it has also shown to be a mine of theoretical questions with interesting technical challenges.

<span id="page-0-0"></span><sup>1</sup> In the introduction, we do not distinguish between proofs and arguments for simplicity.

<span id="page-1-2"></span>Unfortunately, it is known that NIZKs for non-trivial languages (i.e., **NP**) do not exist in the plain model where there is no trusted setup [GO94]. Therefore, NIZKs for non-trivial languages are typically constructed in the common reference string (CRS) model where the prover and verifier have access to a CRS generated by a trusted entity. We will call such NIZKs in the CRS model simply as NIZKs.

The most successful NIZK for all of **NP** is arguably the pairing-based NIZK of Groth, Ostrovsky, and Sahai [GOS12] (GOS-NIZK). GOS-NIZKs are based on the standard decisional linear (DLIN) or the subgroup decision (SD) assumptions. Due to its simplicity and efficiency, pairing-based NIZKs have flourished into a research topic on its own, and the original GOS-NIZK has been followed by many subsequent works trying to improve on it through various approaches. For example, many works such as [GS12, JR17, JR14, KW15] aim to make GOS-NIZK more efficient by limiting the language to very specific pairing induced languages, while other works such as [Gro10b, Lip12, GGPR13, DFGK14, Gro16] aim to gain efficiency by relying on a much stronger assumption known as knowledge assumptions (i.e., a type of non-falsifiable [Nao03, GW11] assumption). In fact, all works that achieve any notion of "better efficiency" compared to GOS-NIZK only succeeds by either restricting the language or by resorting to use stronger assumptions compared to DLIN or SD.

Similarly with many prior works, the main focus of "efficiency" in our work will be the *proof size* of the NIZK. Denoting C as the circuit computing the **NP** relation, GOS-NIZK requires a proof size as large as  $O(|C|\kappa)$ , where  $\kappa$  is the security parameter. Borrowing terminology from the recent work of Katsumata et al. [KNYY19a, KNYY19b], what we would like instead is a more *compact* proof size, that is, a proof size with only an additive overhead  $|C| + \mathsf{poly}(\kappa)$ rather than a multiplicative overhead. For instance, the above latter approach using knowledge assumptions are known to achieve pairing-based NIZKs for NP with a significantly short proof size that only depends on the security parameter; in particular, the proof size does not even depend on the witness size. However, unfortunately, it is known that NIZKs with such an unusually short proof (i.e., proof size  $poly(\kappa) \cdot (|x| + |w|)^{o(1)}$  where x is the statement and w is the witness) inevitably require strong non-falsifiable assumptions [GW11]. The most compact NIZK based on any falsifiable assumption is due to [Gen09, GGI<sup>+</sup>15] which achieves proof size  $|w| + \text{poly}(\kappa)$ . However, since it uses (circular secure) fully homomorphic encryption (FHE) its instantiation is solely limited to lattice-based assumptions. Other than lattice-based constructions, Groth [Gro10a] proposed a NIZK based on the security of Naccache-Stern public key encryption scheme [NS98] with a proof size |C| · polylog( $\kappa$ ), which is asymptotically shorter than that of GOS-NIZK. Very recently, Katsumata et al. [KNYY19b] provided the first compact NIZK based on any falsifiable pairing-based assumption achieving a proof size of |C| + poly( $\kappa$ ). Their construction relies on a new primitive called homomorphic equivocal commitment (HEC), and they instantiate HEC using a non-static Diffie-Hellman type assumption recently introduced in [KNYY19a]. Unfortunately, the construction of HEC seems to be tailored to their specific non-static assumption, and it seems quite difficult to construct HEC based on a clean static assumption such as DLIN.

In summary, despite the considerable work that has been put into paring-based NIZKs, improving the proof size of GOS-NIZK while simultaneously maintaining the language and assumption has shown to be elusive. Therefore, in this work, the main question we ask is:

Can we construct compact NIZKs for all of NP based on standard assumptions over a pairing group?

<u>DP-NIZK and CS:</u> We first explain the notion of designated prover NIZK (DP-NIZK), which is a relaxed notion of the standard notion of NIZK. In order to differentiate them, we call the latter CRS-NIZK in the following. In DP-NIZK, only a prover who possesses a secret proving key can generate a proof for an NP statement, and the verification can be done publicly by any entity. Here, the secret proving key is generated along with the CRS by a trusted entity. We require that soundness holds against a malicious prover who possesses the secret proving key and that zero-knowledge holds against a malicious verifier who only accesses the CRS and the proofs, but not the secret proving key. We then explain the notion of CS, which is a slightly simplified version of attribute-based signature [MPR11]. CS is an advanced form of signature where a signing key is associated with some circuit  $C: \{0,1\}^\ell \to \{0,1\}$  and using the signing key, one can sign on a message x if C(x) = 1. The signature can be verified by a public verification key. As for security, we require unforgeability and privacy. The former requires that one cannot forge a valid signature on a message x if it only has a signing key CS.skC for C such that C(x) = 0. The latter requires that an honestly generated signature reveals nothing about the circuit C associated with the signing key that is used for generating the signature. In addition to the above security notions, we also require CS to have compact signatures in the sense that the size of the signatures is a fixed polynomial that is independent of the size of the circuit C and the length of the message x.

DP-NIZK from CS [KW18]: We then explain the generic construction of DP-NIZK from CS shown by Kim and Wu [KW18]. This will serve as a good starting point for us because their conversion allows us to convert a compact CS into a compact DP-NIZK as we will see. Let us fix an NP language L that is verified by a circuit R that takes as input a statement x and a witness w and outputs  $R(x, w) \in \{0, 1\}$ . In their construction, they set the CRS of the DP-NIZK to be a verification key of the CS. Furthermore, they set the secret proving key for the DP-NIZK to be a secret key K of an SKE and a CS signing key CS.sk $_{C_K}$  for circuit  $C_K$ . Here,  $C_K$  is a circuit that takes as input an SKE ciphertext SKE.ct and a statement x and outputs 1 if R(x, SKE.Dec(K, SKE.ct)) = 1 and 0 otherwise. To generate a proof for an NP statement x corresponding to a witness w, the prover encrypts the witness w by the SKE to obtain SKE.ct = SKE.Enc(K, w) and then signs on the message (x, SKE.ct) using the CS signing key for  $C_K$ . By the correctness of the SKE, we have  $C_K(x, \mathsf{SKE}.\mathsf{ct}) = R(x, w) = 1$ , which implies the completeness of the DP-NIZK. The soundness of the protocol follows from the unforgeability of the underlying CS. This is because any valid proof for an invalid statement  $x^* \notin L$  is a valid signature on  $(x^*, SKE.ct^*)$  for some  $SKE.ct^*$ , for which we have  $C_K(x^*,\mathsf{SKE.ct}^*) = R(x^*,\mathsf{SKE.Dec}(K,\mathsf{SKE.ct}^*)) = 0$ . The zero-knowledge property of the protocol follows from the following intuition. From the privacy of the CS, information of K hardwired into the circuit  $C_K$  is not leaked from the CS signature. We, therefore, can use the security of SKE to conclude that SKE.ct leaks no information of the witness w.

We now focus on the efficiency of the resultant DP-NIZK. If we instantiate the DP-NIZK with an SKE with additive ciphertext overhead and a CS with compact signatures, this gives us a compact DP-NIZK. Note that an SKE scheme with additive ciphertext overhead can be realized from very mild assumptions such as CDH. Therefore, their result suggests that it suffices to construct compact CS in order to construct a compact DP-NIZK.

#### 1.3.2 Overview of Our Approach

Here, we provide an overview of our approach. In high level, we follow the same approach as Katsumata et al. [KNYY19a, KNYY19b], who constructed a compact CRS-NIZK from a non-static assumption over bilinear maps.

<span id="page-4-0"></span>Specifically, we will first construct a CS, then convert it into a DP-NIZK, and then modify it into a CRS-NIZK. However, our approach significantly differs from theirs in low level details. We will provide a comparison with their work after describing our approach in the following.

*Compact DP-NIZK from a standard assumption:* We set the construction of compact DP-NIZK from a static assumption as an intermediate goal. Thanks to the Kim-Wu conversion, the problem is reduced to the construction of a CS scheme with compact signatures from a static assumption. To achieve the goal, we follow the folklore conversion that converts an attribute-based encryption (ABE) into a CS that is somewhat reminiscent of the Naor conversion [\[BF01\]](#page-37-2) (See e.g., [\[OT11\]](#page-41-1)). In order to obtain the CS scheme with the desired properties, it turns out that we need to construct an adaptively secure ABE scheme whose ciphertext size is bounded by some fixed polynomial. Although there is no ABE scheme with the required properties from a static assumption in the literature, we are able to construct it by modifying the very recent ABE scheme proposed by Kowalczyk and Wee [\[KW19\]](#page-40-10), who resolved the long-standing open problem of constructing adaptively secure ABE for **NC**<sup>1</sup> whose ciphertext length is independent of the circuit size from a static assumption by cleverly adapting the piecewise guessing frameworks [\[FKPR14,](#page-38-5) [FJP15,](#page-38-6) [HJO](#page-39-11)<sup>+</sup>16, [JW16,](#page-40-11) [JKK](#page-40-12)<sup>+</sup>17, [KW19\]](#page-40-10) to the setting of ABE. We modify their scheme so that it has even shorter ciphertexts by aggregating the ciphertext components and adding extra components to the secret keys as was done in previous works on ABE with short ciphertexts [\[ALdP11,](#page-37-3) [AHL](#page-37-4)<sup>+</sup>12, [HW13\]](#page-39-12). The security proof for the scheme is again similar to that of Kowalczyk and Wee, where we decompose the secret keys into smaller pieces and gradually randomize them via carefully chosen sequence of hybrid games. The additional challenge for the proof in our setting is to deal with the extra components in the secret keys. We handle this by observing that the originally proof strategy by Kowalczyk and Wee for randomizing the secret keys works even with these extra components. From this ABE scheme, we can obtain a CS scheme with the desired properties. Furthermore, by applying the Kim-Wu conversion to the CS scheme, we obtain a new compact DP-NIZK from a static assumption. Although this is not our main goal, we note that this improves the compact DP-NIZK scheme from a non-static assumption by Katsumata et al. [\[KNYY19a\]](#page-40-5).

*Removing Secret Proving Key:* We then try to remove the necessity of the secret proving key from the DP-NIZK described above to obtain a CRS-NIZK. Toward this goal, our first idea is to make the signing key of the CS scheme public by including it into the CRS. When we do so, we stop hardwiring the secret key *K* of the SKE into the circuit associated with the signing key and change the circuit so that it takes *K* as an input. The obvious reason for this is because we would like to use the security of SKE at some later point. More concretely, we include CS*.*sk*<sup>C</sup>* into the CRS, where *C* is a circuit that takes as input the secret key *K* of SKE, a statement *x*, and a ciphertext SKE*.*ct of SKE and outputs *R*(*x,* SKE*.*Dec(*K,* SKE*.*ct)). When generating a proof, the prover chooses a random *K* on its own, computes SKE*.*ct \$← SKE*.*Enc(*K, w*), and signs on the message (*x,* SKE*.*ct*, K*) by using CS*.*sk*<sup>C</sup>* to obtain a signature CS*.σ*, which is possible because we have *C*(*x,* SKE*.*ct*, K*) = 1 by the definition of *C*. The problem with this approach is that we do not know what components to publish as the final proof. More specifically, we run into the following deadlock: If we include *K* into the proof, then the scheme is not zero-knowledge anymore because one can decrypt SKE*.*ct by using *K* to retrieve *w*. On the other hand, if we do not include *K* into the proof, we can no longer verify the validity of CS*.σ* since *K*, which is now a part of the message, is required to verify the signature.

*Introducing Non-Compact NIZK:* We resolve the above issue by using a CRS-NIZK that is not necessarily compact (non-compact NIZK in the following) and change the scheme so that it proves the validity of the CS signature without revealing *K* nor the signature. In more detail, the prover generates *K,* SKE*.*ct \$← SKE*.*Enc(*K, w*)*,* CS*.σ* \$← CS*.*Sign(CS*.*sk*<sup>C</sup> ,*(*x,* SKE*.*ct*, K*)) as above. It then proves that there exists (*K,* CS*.σ*) such that CS*.σ* is a valid signature on a message (*x,* SKE*.*ct*, K*) under the verification key CS*.*vk by using the non-compact NIZK. It then outputs (SKE*.*ct*,* CS*.σ, π*) as the final proof, where *π* is the non-compact proof for the above statement.

We then explain that the scheme satisfies soundness and zero-knowledge. To see this, we first observe that to break the soundness of the resultant NIZK scheme, it is necessary to break the soundness of the underlying non-compact NIZK or generate a valid CS signature on (*x* ∗ *,* SKE*.*ct<sup>∗</sup> *, K*<sup>∗</sup> ) such that *x* <sup>∗</sup> 6∈ *L*. By our assumption, the former is impossible. Furthermore, the latter is also impossible, since we have *C*(*x* ∗ *,* SKE*.*ct<sup>∗</sup> *, K*<sup>∗</sup> ) = 0 for any choice of *K*<sup>∗</sup> and SKE*.*ct<sup>∗</sup> and thus it implies a forgery against the CS scheme. The zero-knowledge property of the scheme holds since the proof consists of the SKE ciphertext and the proof of the non-compact NIZK. Intuitively, since the latter does not leak the information about *K*, we can use the security of SKE to conclude that *w* is hidden from the adversary.

While this gives a secure construction, it is unclear whether this is a step forward at this point since we merely constructed a NIZK from a CS by further assuming a NIZK, which seems to be a vacuous statement. Furthermore, the construction we described so far is not compact since the relation proven by the underlying non-compact NIZK is verified by a circuit whose size depends on |*C*|. To see this, we recall that the verification circuit for the relation proven by the non-compact NIZK takes as input the statement *x* <sup>0</sup> = (CS*.*vk*, x,* SKE*.*ct) and witness *w* <sup>0</sup> = (*K,* CS*.σ*) and outputs 1 if and only if CS*.σ* is a valid signature on (*x,* SKE*.*ct*, K*). This circuit is not compact, since it takes as input *x*, which can be as large as |*C*| in general and CS*.*vk, which is much larger than |*C*| in our specific CS scheme.

*Exploiting the Special Efficiency Property of the CS:* We observe that what should be kept secret in the above construction are *K* and CS*.σ*,[4](#page-5-0) and (*x,* SKE*.*ct) can be made public without losing the zero-knowledge property. To get a clearer understanding of the problem, we slightly generalize and simplify the problem as follows. What we would like to do is to give a compact proof that we have a valid signature CS*.σ* on a message (*y, z*) for public *y* and secret *z* without revealing *z* nor CS*.σ* using a non-compact NIZK. Here, *y* is not compact while *z* and CS*.σ* are compact. In our context, *y* = (*x,* SKE*.*ct) and *z* = *K*. In this generalized setting, the above approach is equivalent to proving that CS*.σ* is a valid signature on (*y, z*) under the verification key CS*.*vk. This relation is verified by a circuit that directly takes (CS*.*vk*,*(*y, z*)*,* CS*.σ*) as inputs. This approach does not work simply because the input is not compact.

Our first observation is that if we were somehow able to compress the verification circuit size of the relation proven by the non-compact NIZK to be a fixed polynomial without changing the functionality, then the resultant NIZK scheme will have compact proofs. Fortunately, our CS scheme has a nice property that brings us closer to this goal. Namely, in the scheme, the verifier can aggregate the verification key CS*.*vk depending on a message *m* to obtain an aggregated verification key CS*.*vk*m*, which is of fixed polynomial size. Then, a signature CS*.σ* can be verified by using *only* the aggregated verification key CS*.*vk*m*. In particular, the verification circuit no longer takes *m* as an input. Typically, the aggregation of the verification key is done offline, where one is allowed to perform heavy computation, and the actual verification step is done online, where the computation is very fast even if *m* is a very long string. We call this property *online-offline efficiency*. We note that our CS scheme inherits this property from the underlying ABE scheme, where secret keys can be aggregated depending on an attribute in offline phase so that the decryption of a ciphertext corresponding to the same attribute in the online phase is very fast.

A natural approach to compress the verification circuit (for the non-compact NIZK) would be to replace the inputs CS*.*vk and (*y, z*) with its aggregated version CS*.*vk(*y,z*) . In particular, we replace the verification circuit which takes as input CS*.*vk, (*y, z*), and CS*.σ* and verifies the signature with the corresponding online verification circuit which takes CS*.*vk(*y,z*) and CS*.σ* as inputs. This circuit is compact thanks to the online-offline efficiency of the CS. However, since CS*.*vk(*y,z*) cannot be publicly computed, we would have to move the term CS*.*vk(*y,z*) into the witness. Furthermore, we additionally have to prove that CS*.*vk(*y,z*) is honestly computed from CS*.*vk and (*y, z*) using the non-compact NIZK. The problem is that the resulting proof is not compact since this is a statement that involves non-compact terms. Put differently, even though we can compactly prove that we have a signature that passes the online verification under a compressed verification key, we cannot compactly prove that we honestly execute the offline phase to compute the compressed verification key.

As we saw above, the idea of compressing CS*.*vk depending on the entire string (*y, z*) does not work. Our idea is to "partially" compress CS*.*vk depending on the public part *y* and then use this compressed version of the verification key to construct the verification circuit for the non-compact NIZK. To enable the idea, let us assume that we can compress CS*.*vk with respect to a string *y* and obtain CS*.*vk*y*. Then, further assume that we can compress CS*.*vk*<sup>y</sup>* into CS*.*vk(*y,z*) using *z*, so that the verification of a message (*y, z*) is possible using CS*.*vk(*y,z*) . Furthermore, we require that the computational cost of compressing CS*.*vk*<sup>y</sup>* into CS*.*vk(*y,z*) depends only on |*z*|, not on |*y*|. Therefore if *z* is compact, we can compute CS*.*vk(*y,z*) from CS*.*vk*<sup>y</sup>* and *z* by a compact circuit. Assuming this property, we can solve the above generalized problem as follows: We first compress CS*.*vk depending on *y* to obtain CS*.*vk*y*. We then prove that there exists CS*.σ* and *z* such that CS*.σ* is a valid signature under CS*.*vk(*y,z*) , where CS*.*vk(*y,z*) is obtained by compressing CS*.*vk*<sup>y</sup>* depending on the string *z*. This statement can be proven compactly, since both verification under the verification key CS*.*vk(*y,z*) and the compression of CS*.*vk*<sup>y</sup>* into CS*.*vk(*y,z*) can be done compactly. Furthermore, unlike the previous attempt, we do not have to prove that we honestly executed the offline computation. Namely, we do not have to prove the consistency between CS*.*vk, *y*, and CS*.*vk*y*, since CS*.*vk*<sup>y</sup>* is publicly computable from CS*.*vk and *y*. Therefore, it suffices to show that our CS scheme has the structure that allows one to compress the verification key in two steps. We name this property *online-offline decomposability* and show that our construction indeed has the property.[5](#page-5-1)

<span id="page-5-0"></span><sup>4</sup>Note that CS*.σ* should be kept secret since it reveals partial information of *K*.

<span id="page-5-1"></span><sup>5</sup>Actually, the definition of online-offline decomposability is slightly different from the one in the main body, but the latter implies the former.

#### <span id="page-6-0"></span>**1.3.3 Comparison with Katsumata et al. [\[KNYY19b\]](#page-40-6)**

Here, we compare our approach with the one by Katsumata et al. [\[KNYY19a,](#page-40-5) [KNYY19b\]](#page-40-6), who showed a similar result from a non-static assumption. As we already mentioned, at the highest level, their approach is the same as ours in that they first construct a CS [\[KNYY19a\]](#page-40-5), then convert it into a DP-NIZK, and then modify it into a CRS-NIZK [\[KNYY19b\]](#page-40-6). However, the way they obtained the CS, and the way they modify their DP-NIZK into a CRS-NIZK is significantly different from ours. We elaborate on this below.

*Compact CS Scheme by Katsumata et al. [\[KNYY19a\]](#page-40-5)*: Similarly to us, their approach is to construct an ABE scheme and then convert it into a CS scheme. However, the requirements for the ABE are different from ours. For the ABE scheme, they require short secret keys, whereas we require short ciphertexts. Furthermore, they require the ABE scheme to be secure following a so-called "single-shot" reduction, where the reduction algorithm runs the adversary only once and perfectly simulates the view of the game. Roughly, this is equivalent to saying that the proof cannot go through hybrid arguments. Therefore, their approach does not seem to be promising when we try to construct a compact CS scheme from a *static* assumption. Notably, their single-shot reduction requirement excludes the dual system encryption methodology [\[Wat09\]](#page-41-2), which is a powerful tool for proving the security of an ABE scheme from static assumptions. On the other hand, we manage to employ the dual system encryption methodology to obtain an ABE scheme with the desired properties from static assumptions.

*From DP-NIZK to CRS-NIZK in Katsumata et al. [\[KNYY19b\]](#page-40-6)*: They construct a DP-NIZK (as an intermediate goal) by applying the Kim-Wu conversion on their CS scheme. They then modify their DP-NIZK to a CRS-NIZK scheme by a non-generic technique. Here, we review their approach and compare it with ours. Recall that, in general, a DP-NIZK constructed from a CS via the Kim-Wu conversion, the CRS consists of the verification key of the CS CS*.*vk, and the secret proving key consists of the secret key of an SKE *K* and a signing key of the CS CS*.*sk*C<sup>K</sup>* . Their observation was that they can divide the CS verification key CS*.*vk into two components CS*.*vk := (CS*.*vk0*,* CSvk1) such that CS*.*vk<sup>1</sup> is very short and anyone can compute CS*.*vk<sup>1</sup> from CS*.*sk*C<sup>K</sup>* and *K*. Note that as a stand-alone CS scheme, the secret key CS*.*sk*C<sup>K</sup>* is computed using the master key of the CS only *after* CS*.*vk = (vk0*,* vk1) is defined. What they observe is that the other direction of the computation is possible using the specific structure of their CS scheme. In order to construct a CRS-NIZK using this special structure, they remove CS*.*vk<sup>1</sup> from the CRS. Then they let the prover pick *K* and CS*.*sk*C<sup>K</sup>* on their own and let it compute CS*.*vk1. At this point, the prover can generate a proof as in the original DP-NIZK. In order to prevent the adversary to maliciously choose *K*, CS*.*sk*C<sup>K</sup>* , and CS*.*vk1, they let the prover prove consistency among the components using a non-compact NIZK and outputs the proof along with CS*.*vk1. The additional consistency proof by the non-compact NIZK as well as CS*.*vk<sup>1</sup> appended to the final proof does not harm the compactness of the resulting NIZK, since all parameters involved are compact.

We note that their approach is not applicable to our specific CS scheme. The reason is that our signing key for the CS is as large as the circuit size and we cannot prove the consistency between *K*, CS*.*sk*C<sup>K</sup>* , and CS*.*vk<sup>1</sup> compactly no matter how we divide the CS verification key. We, therefore, take a different path from theirs and this entails several challenges that are not present in their approach.

## <span id="page-6-1"></span>**1.4 Related Work**

The first NIZK for **NP** was given by [\[BFM88\]](#page-37-0) based on the quadratic residuosity assumption and Feige et al. [\[FLS99\]](#page-38-2) weakened the assumption to the existence of trapdoor permutations (whose arguments were later refined by several works [\[BY96,](#page-38-7) [Gol04\]](#page-39-10)). The next generation of NIZK following a completely different set of approaches were provided by Groth, Ostrovsky, and Sahai [\[GOS12\]](#page-39-2) (GOS-NIZK) based on pairings. Due to its simplicity and efficiency, pairing-based NIZKs have flourished into a research topic on its own, and the original GOS-NIZK has been followed by many subsequent works [\[Gro10a,](#page-39-9) [Gro10b,](#page-39-4) [Lip12,](#page-40-3) [GS12,](#page-39-3) [GGPR13\]](#page-39-5). More than roughly a decade later, a new type of NIZKs based on indistinguishable obfuscation (iO) were proposed [\[SW14,](#page-41-3) [BP15,](#page-37-5) [BPW16,](#page-37-6) [CL18\]](#page-38-8). Finally, very recently, a different path for designing NIZKs based on correlation intractable hash functions (CIH) [\[KRR17,](#page-40-13) [CCRR18,](#page-38-9) [CCH](#page-38-10)<sup>+</sup>19] have gained much attention and has finally lead to the closing of a long-standing problem of constructing NIZKs based on lattice-based assumptions [\[PS19\]](#page-41-4).

## <span id="page-7-2"></span>2.1 Preliminaries on Bilinear Maps

A bilinear group generator GGen takes as input  $1^{\kappa}$  and outputs a group description  $\mathbb{G}=(p,G_1,G_2,G_T,e,g_1,g_2)$ , where p is a prime such that  $p>2^{2\kappa}$ ,  $G_1$ ,  $G_2$ , and  $G_T$  are cyclic groups of order  $q,e:G_1\times G_2\to G_T$  is a non-degenerate bilinear map, and  $g_1$  and  $g_2$  are generators of  $G_1$  and  $G_2$ , respectively. We require that the group operations in  $G_1$ ,  $G_2$ , and  $G_T$  as well as the bilinear map e can be efficiently computed. We employ the implicit representation of group elements: for a matrix  $\mathbf{A}$  over  $\mathbb{Z}_q$ , we define  $[\mathbf{A}]_1:=g_1^{\mathbf{A}}$ ,  $[\mathbf{A}]_2:=g_2^{\mathbf{A}}$ ,  $[\mathbf{A}]_T:=g_T^{\mathbf{A}}$ , where exponentiation is carried out component-wise.

**Definition 2.1** (MDDH<sub>k</sub> assumption [EHK<sup>+</sup>13]). Let GGen be a group generator. We say that the matrix DDH (MDDH<sub>k</sub>) assumption holds on  $G_1$  with respect to GGen, if for all PPT adversaries A, we have

$$\mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{mddh}}_{\mathcal{A}}(\lambda) \coloneqq \left| \Pr\left[ \mathcal{A}(\mathbb{G}, [\mathbf{M}]_1, [\mathbf{Ms}]_1) \to 1 \right] - \Pr\left[ \mathcal{A}(\mathbb{G}, [\mathbf{M}]_1, [\mathbf{u}]_1) \to 1 \right] \right|$$

is negligible, where the probability is taken over the choice of  $\mathbb{G} \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{GGen}(1^{\kappa})$ ,  $\mathbf{M} \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathbb{Z}_p^{(k+1)\times k}$ ,  $\mathbf{s} \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathbb{Z}_p^k$ , and  $\mathbf{u} \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathbb{Z}_p^{k+1}$ . We can similarly define  $\mathsf{MDDH}_k$  assumption on  $G_2$ .

In fact, the above assumption is called  $\mathrm{MDDH}_k$  assumption for uniform distribution by Escala et al. [EHK<sup>+</sup>13] since  $\mathbf{M}$  is chosen uniformly at random. As shown by them,  $\mathrm{MDDH}_k$  assumptions for uniform distribution is weaker than  $\mathrm{MDDH}_k$  assumption for all other distributions and in particular is implied by the k-LIN assumption.

## <span id="page-7-3"></span>2.2 Symmetric Key Encryption

Here, we define symmetric key encryption (SKE). Our definition of SKE is taken from [KNYY19b]. In this definition, we explicitly introduce a setup algorithm that generates a public parameter. If we just think about an SKE scheme alone, then such a setup algorithm is redundant since we can think of a public parameter as part of a secret key. On the other hand, we need an SKE scheme with a short secret key size (among other properties) in the construction of our compact NIZK. For achieving such a short secret key, we explicitly introduce the setup algorithm so that a secret key need not include values that can be made public. We also note that we only require one-time security instead of CPA-security.

Let  $\{\mathcal{M}_{\kappa}\}_{\kappa\in\mathbb{N}}$  be a family of message space. In the following, we occasionally drop the subscript and simply write  $\mathcal{M}$  when the meaning is clear.

**Definition 2.2 (Symmetric Key Encryption).** *A symmetric key encryption (SKE) scheme*  $\Pi_{SKE}$  *for message space*  $\mathcal{M}$  *consists of PPT algorithms* (SKE.Setup, SKE.KeyGen, SKE.Enc, SKE.Dec).

- SKE.Setup $(1^{\kappa}) \to pp_{SKE}$ : The setup algorithm takes as input the security parameter  $1^{\kappa}$  and outputs a public parameter  $pp_{SKE}$ .
- SKE.KeyGen( $pp_{SKE}$ )  $\rightarrow$  K<sub>SKE</sub>: The key generation algorithm takes as input a public parameter  $pp_{SKE}$  and outputs a secret key K<sub>SKE</sub>.
- SKE.Enc( $pp_{SKE}$ ,  $K_{SKE}$ , M)  $\rightarrow$  ct: The encryption algorithm takes as input a public parameter  $pp_{SKE}$ , a secret key  $K_{SKE}$ , and a message  $M \in \mathcal{M}$  and outputs a ciphertext ct.
- $SKE.Dec(pp_{SKE}, K_{SKE}, ct) \rightarrow M \ or \ \bot: \ The \ decryption \ algorithm \ takes \ as \ input \ a \ public \ parameter \ pp_{SKE}, \ a \ secret \ key \ K_{SKE}, \ and \ a \ ciphertext \ ct \ and \ outputs \ a \ message \ M \in \mathcal{M} \ or \ a \ special \ symbol \ \bot \ indicating \ decryption \ failure.$

<u>Correctness.</u> For all  $\kappa \in \mathbb{N}$ ,  $M \in \mathcal{M}$ ,  $\mathsf{pp}_{\mathsf{SKE}} \in \mathsf{SKE}.\mathsf{Setup}(1^{\kappa})$ , and  $\mathsf{K}_{\mathsf{SKE}} \in \mathsf{SKE}.\mathsf{KeyGen}(\mathsf{pp}_{\mathsf{SKE}})$ , we have  $\mathsf{SKE}.\mathsf{Dec}(\mathsf{pp}_{\mathsf{SKE}},\mathsf{K}_{\mathsf{SKE}},\mathsf{SKE}.\mathsf{Enc}(\mathsf{pp}_{\mathsf{SKE}},\mathsf{K}_{\mathsf{SKE}},\mathsf{M})) = \mathsf{M}$ .

<u>One-Time Security.</u> For all PPT adversaries  $\mathcal{A}$  and  $(\mathsf{M}_0, \mathsf{M}_1) \in \mathcal{M}^2$ , if we run  $\mathsf{pp}_{\mathsf{SKE}} \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{SKE}.\mathsf{Setup}(1^\kappa)$ ,  $\mathsf{K}_{\mathsf{SKE}} \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{SKE}.\mathsf{KeyGen}(\mathsf{pp}_{\mathsf{SKE}})$ , and  $\mathsf{ct}_b \stackrel{\$}{\sim} \mathsf{SKE}.\mathsf{Enc}(\mathsf{pp}_{\mathsf{SKE}}, \mathsf{K}_{\mathsf{SKE}}, \mathsf{M}_b)$  for  $b \in \{0, 1\}$ , then we have

$$|\Pr[\mathcal{A}(\mathsf{pp}_\mathsf{SKE},\mathsf{M}_0) = 1] - \Pr[\mathcal{A}(\mathsf{pp}_\mathsf{SKE},\mathsf{M}_1) = 1]| = \mathsf{negl}(\kappa).$$

<span id="page-8-3"></span>For our construction of NIZKs in the following sections, we require an SKE scheme whose ciphertext overhead (i.e., |ct| - |m|) and secret key length are  $poly(\kappa)$  and whose decryption algorithm can be represented as a circuit in  $\mathbf{NC}^1$ . Such an SKE exists under the CDH assumption in a subgroup of  $\mathbb{Z}_p^*$  as shown in [KNYY19b].

<span id="page-8-2"></span>**Lemma 2.3.** If the CDH assumption holds in a subgroup of  $\mathbb{Z}_p^*$ , then for any  $m = \mathsf{poly}(\kappa)$ , there exists a one-time secure SKE scheme with message space  $\{0,1\}^m$ , ciphertext overhead  $\mathsf{poly}(\kappa)^6$ , and secret key length  $\mathsf{poly}(\kappa)$  (independently of m) whose decryption algorithm can be represented as a circuit in  $NC^1$ .

## <span id="page-8-4"></span>2.3 One-Time Signature

Let  $\{\mathcal{M}_{\kappa}\}_{\kappa\in\mathbb{N}}$  be a family of message space. In the following, we occasionally drop the subscript and simply write  $\mathcal{M}$  when the meaning is clear.

**Definition 2.4 (One-time Signature).** *A one-time signature (OTS) scheme*  $\Pi_{OTS}$  *for message space*  $\mathcal{M}$  *consists of PPT algorithms* (OTS.KeyGen, OTS.Sign, OTS.Verify).

- OTS.KeyGen $(1^{\kappa}) \to (vk_{OTS}, sigk_{OTS})$ : The key generation algorithm takes as input the security parameter  $1^{\kappa}$  and outputs a verification key  $vk_{OTS}$  and a signing key  $sigk_{OTS}$ .
- OTS.Sign(sigk<sub>OTS</sub>, M)  $\rightarrow \sigma$ : The signing algorithm takes as input a signing key sigk<sub>OTS</sub> and a message M  $\in \mathcal{M}$  and outputs a signature  $\sigma$ .
- OTS. Verify( $vk_{OTS}$ , M,  $\sigma$ )  $\to \top$  or  $\bot$ : The decryption algorithm takes as input a verification key  $vk_{OTS}$ , a message M and a signature  $\sigma$  and outputs  $\top$  to indicate acceptance of the signature and  $\bot$  otherwise.

<u>Correctness.</u> For all  $\kappa \in \mathbb{N}$ ,  $M \in \mathcal{M}$ ,  $(\mathsf{vk}_{\mathsf{OTS}}, \mathsf{sigk}_{\mathsf{OTS}}) \in \mathsf{OTS}.\mathsf{KeyGen}(1^\kappa)$ , and  $\sigma \in \mathsf{OTS}.\mathsf{Sign}(\mathsf{sigk}_{\mathsf{OTS}}, \mathsf{M})$ , we have  $\mathsf{OTS}.\mathsf{Verify}(\mathsf{vk}_{\mathsf{OTS}}, \mathsf{M}, \sigma) = \top$ .

**Strong One-Time Unforgeability.** For all  $\kappa \in \mathbb{N}$  and all PPT adversaries  $\mathcal{A} = (\mathcal{A}_1, \mathcal{A}_2)$ , we have

$$\Pr\left[\begin{array}{c|c} \mathsf{OTS.Verify}(\mathsf{vk}_{\mathsf{OTS}},\mathsf{M}^*,\sigma^*) = \top & (\mathsf{vk}_{\mathsf{OTS}},\mathsf{sigk}_{\mathsf{OTS}}) & \overset{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{OTS.KeyGen}(1^\kappa) \\ (\mathsf{M},\mathsf{st}) & \overset{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathcal{A}_1(\mathsf{vk}_{\mathsf{OTS}}), \\ (\mathsf{M},\mathsf{st}) & \overset{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{OTS.Sign}(\mathsf{sigk}_{\mathsf{OTS}},\mathsf{M}) \\ (\mathsf{M}^*,\sigma^*) & \overset{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathcal{A}_2(\mathsf{st},\sigma) \end{array}\right] \leq \mathsf{negl}(\kappa).$$

#### <span id="page-8-5"></span>2.4 Non-Interactive Zero-Knowledge Arguments

Let  $\mathcal{R} \subseteq \{0,1\}^* \times \{0,1\}^*$  be a polynomial time recognizable binary relation. For  $(x,w) \in \mathcal{R}$ , we call x as the statement and w as the witness. Let  $\mathcal{L}$  be the corresponding **NP** language  $\mathcal{L} = \{x \mid \exists w \text{ s.t. } (x,w) \in \mathcal{R}\}$ . Below, we define non-interactive zero-knowledge arguments for **NP** languages.

**Definition 2.5 (NIZK Arguments).** A non-interactive zero-knowledge (NIZK) argument  $\Pi_{NIZK}$  for the relation  $\mathcal{R}$  consists of PPT algorithms (Setup, Prove, Verify).

Setup $(1^{\kappa}) \to \text{crs.}$  The setup algorithm takes as input the security parameter  $1^{\kappa}$  and outputs a common reference string crs.

Prove(crs, x, w)  $\to \pi$ : The prover's algorithm takes as input a common reference string crs, a statement x, and a witness w and outputs a proof  $\pi$ .

<span id="page-8-0"></span><sup>&</sup>lt;sup>6</sup> In fact, the SKE scheme achieves a ciphertext length that equals the message length. However, any SKE scheme with a fixed-polynomial additive ciphertext overhead and secret key length suffice for our work.

<span id="page-8-1"></span><sup>&</sup>lt;sup>7</sup> We say it is a non-interactive zero-knowledge *proofs* when the soundness property holds for even unbounded adversaries. In this paper, we will only be interested in computationally bounded adversaries.

<span id="page-9-0"></span>Verify(crs,  $x, \pi$ )  $\to \top$  or  $\bot$ : The verifier's algorithm takes as input a common reference string, a statement x, and a proof  $\pi$  and outputs  $\top$  to indicate acceptance of the proof and  $\bot$  otherwise.

We consider the following requirements for a NIZK argument  $\Pi_{NIZK}$ , where the probabilities are taken over the random choice of the algorithms.

**Completeness.** For all pairs  $(x, w) \in \mathcal{R}$ , if we run  $\operatorname{crs} \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \operatorname{Setup}(1^{\kappa})$ , then we have

$$\Pr[\pi \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{Prove}(\mathsf{crs},x,w) : \mathsf{Verify}(\mathsf{crs},x,\pi) = \top] = 1.$$

**Adaptive Soundness.** For all PPT adversaries A, if we run  $\operatorname{crs} \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \operatorname{\mathsf{Setup}}(1^\kappa)$ , then we have

$$\Pr[(x,\pi) \xleftarrow{\$} \mathcal{A}(1^{\kappa},\mathsf{crs}) : x \not\in \mathcal{L} \land \mathsf{Verify}(\mathsf{crs},x,\pi) = \top] = \mathsf{negl}(\kappa).$$

<u>Non-Adaptive Soundness.</u> We also consider the slightly weaker variant of adaptive soundness above. For all PPT adversaries A and for all  $x \notin \mathcal{L}$ , if we run crs  $\stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow}$  Setup $(1^{\kappa})$ , then we have

$$\Pr[\pi \overset{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathcal{A}(1^{\kappa},\mathsf{crs},x) : \mathsf{Verify}(\mathsf{crs},x,\pi) = \top] = \mathsf{negl}(\kappa).$$

**Zero-Knowledge.** For all adversaries  $\mathcal{A}$ , there exists a PPT simulator  $\mathcal{S} = (\mathcal{S}_1, \mathcal{S}_2)$  such that if we run  $\operatorname{crs} \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \operatorname{Setup}(1^{\kappa})$  and  $(\overline{\operatorname{crs}}, \bar{\tau}) \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathcal{S}_1(1^{\kappa})$ , then we have

$$\left|\Pr[\mathcal{A}^{\mathcal{O}_0(\mathsf{crs},\cdot,\cdot)}(1^\kappa,\mathsf{crs})=1] - \Pr[\mathcal{A}^{\mathcal{O}_1(\mathsf{c\bar{r}s},\bar{\tau},\cdot,\cdot)}(1^\kappa,\overline{\mathsf{crs}})=1]\right| = \mathsf{negl}(\kappa),$$

where  $\mathcal{O}_0(\operatorname{crs}, x, w)$  outputs  $\operatorname{Prove}(\operatorname{crs}, x, w)$  if  $(x, w) \in \mathcal{R}$  and  $\bot$  otherwise, and  $\mathcal{O}_1(\overline{\operatorname{crs}}, \overline{\tau}, x, w)$  outputs  $\mathcal{S}_2(\overline{\operatorname{crs}}, \overline{\tau}, x)$  if  $(x, w) \in \mathcal{R}$  and  $\bot$  otherwise. We say it is computational (resp. statistical) zero-knowledge if the adversary is computationally bounded (resp. unbounded). Moreover, we further say it is perfect zero-knowledge if the above r.h.s. equals 0 for computationally unbounded adversaries.

We also define a stronger notion of soundness called *extractability* following [KNYY19b].

**Definition 2.6 (Extractability).** An NIZK argument is said to be extractable if the following is satisfied: **Extractability.** There is a deterministic algorithm Extract (called extractor) such that for all PPT adversary A, we have

$$\Pr\left[\begin{array}{c|c} \mathsf{Verify}(\mathsf{crs},x,\pi) = \top \\ (x,w) \notin \mathcal{R} \end{array} \middle| \begin{array}{c} \mathsf{crs} \xleftarrow{\$} \mathsf{Setup}(1^\kappa), (x,\pi) \xleftarrow{\$} \mathcal{A}(\mathsf{crs}), \\ w \xleftarrow{\$} \mathsf{Extract}(r_{\mathsf{Setup}},\pi) \end{array} \right] \le \mathsf{negl}(\kappa).$$

where  $r_{Setup}$  is the randomness used in Setup to generate crs.

We can convert any adaptively sound NIZK into an extractable one additionally assuming the existence of PKE [KNYY19b].

**Lemma 2.7.** If there exist an adaptively sound NIZK for all of NP and a CPA-secure PKE scheme, then there exists an extractable NIZK for all of NP.

#### <span id="page-9-1"></span>2.5 Key-Policy Attribute-Based Encryption

Here, we define key-policy attribute-based encryption (KP-ABE) for monotone Boolean formulae.

**Definition 2.8 (Attribute-Based Encryption).** A key-policy attribute-based encryption (KP-ABE) scheme with attribute space  $\{0,1\}^n$  for family of Boolean formulae  $\mathcal{F}=\{f:\{0,1\}^n\to\{0,1\}\}$  consists of PPT algorithms (Setup, KeyGen, Enc, Dec).

- <span id="page-10-1"></span>Setup $(1^{\kappa}, 1^n) \to (\text{mpk}, \text{msk})$ : The setup algorithm on input the security parameter  $1^{\lambda}$  and the input length  $1^n$ , outputs a master secret key msk and a master public key mpk.
- $\mathsf{KeyGen}(\mathsf{msk},f) \to \mathsf{sk}_f$ : The key generation algorithm on input a master secret key  $\mathsf{msk}$  and a monotone Boolean formula  $f \in \mathcal{F}$ , outputs a secret key  $\mathsf{sk}_f$ .
- $\mathsf{Enc}(\mathsf{mpk},x,M) \to \mathsf{ct}$ : The encryption algorithm on input the master public key  $\mathsf{mpk}$ , an attribute  $x \in \{0,1\}^n$ , and a message M and outputs a ciphertext  $\mathsf{ct}_x$ .
- $\mathsf{Dec}(\mathsf{mpk},\mathsf{sk}_f,\mathsf{ct}_x) \to M \ or \perp : \ \mathit{The decryption algorithm on input the verification key } \mathsf{mpk}, \ a \ secret \ key \ \mathsf{sk}_f, \ and \ a \ ciphertext \ \mathsf{ct}_x \ and \ outputs \ either \ message \ M \ or \perp \ (indicating \ the \ ciphertext \ is \ valid).$

A KP-ABE scheme must satisfy the following requirements.

<u>Correctness.</u> For all  $\kappa \in \mathbb{N}$ ,  $n = n(\kappa) \in \mathbb{N}$ ,  $(\mathsf{mpk}, \mathsf{msk}) \overset{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{Setup}(1^\kappa, 1^n)$ ,  $x \in \{0, 1\}^n$ ,  $f \in \mathcal{F}$  such that f(x) = 1, message M, and  $\mathsf{sk}_f \overset{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{KeyGen}(\mathsf{msk}, f)$ , we have

$$\Pr[\mathsf{Dec}(\mathsf{mpk},\mathsf{sk}_f,\mathsf{Enc}(\mathsf{mpk},x,M))=M]=1.$$

Adaptive Security. We define (adaptive) security of ABE. The security notion is defined by the following game between a challenger and an adversary A.

**Setup:** The challenger runs (mpkmsk)  $\stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow}$  Setup $(1^{\kappa}, 1^n)$  and gives mpk to  $\mathcal{A}$ . It also prepares an empty list  $\mathcal{Q}$ .

- **Key Queries:**  $\mathcal{A}$  can adaptively make key queries unbounded polynomially many times throughout the game. When  $\mathcal{A}$  queries  $f \in \mathcal{F}$ , the challenger runs  $\mathsf{sk}_f \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{KeyGen}(\mathsf{msk},f)$  and returns  $\mathsf{sk}_f$  to  $\mathcal{A}$ . Finally, the challenger updates  $\mathcal{Q} \leftarrow \mathcal{Q} \cup \{f\}$ .
- **Challenge Phase:** At some point,  $\mathcal{A}$  specifies its target attribute  $x^*$  and messages  $(M_0, M_1)$  it would like to be challenged on. Then, the challenger flips a random coin coin  $\stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \{0,1\}$ , encrypt the corresponding message ct  $\stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow}$  Enc(mpk,  $x, M_{\text{coin}}$ ), and returns ct to  $\mathcal{A}$ .

**Guess:** Eventually,  $\mathcal{A}$  outputs coin' as its guess for coin. We say  $\mathcal{A}$  wins if coin' = coin. Furthermore, we say that  $\mathcal{A}$  is admissible if  $f(x^*) = 0$  holds for all  $f \in \mathcal{Q}$  at the end of the game.

We say the ABE scheme is adaptively secure if the winning probability for all admissible PPT adversaries  $\mathcal{A}$  in the above game is  $\operatorname{negl}(\kappa)$ , where the probability is taken over the randomness of all algorithms.

#### <span id="page-10-2"></span>2.6 NC<sup>1</sup> Circuits and Monotone Formulae

Here, we define Monotone Boolean formula following Kowalczyk and Wee [KW19].

Monotone Boolean formula. A monotone Boolean formula  $f:\{0,1\}^n \to \{0,1\}$  is specified by a directed acyclic graph (DAG) with three kinds of nodes: input gate nodes, gate nodes, and a single output node. Input nodes have in-degree 0 and out-degree 1, AND/OR nodes have in-degree (fan-in) 2 and out-degree (fan-out) 1, and the output node has in-degree 1 and out-degree 0. We number the edges (wires)  $1,2,\ldots,m$ , and each gate node is defined by a tuple  $(g,a_g,b_g,c_g)$  where  $g:\{0,1\}^2 \to \{0,1\}$  is either AND or OR,  $a_g$  and  $b_g$  are the incoming wires,  $c_g$  is the outgoing wire and  $a_g,b_g< c_g$ . The size of a formula m is the number of edges in the underlying DAG and the depth of a formula d is the length of the longest path from the output node.

<span id="page-11-2"></span>**Pebbling Games.** Fix a formula *f* : {0*,* 1} *<sup>n</sup>* → {0*,* 1} and an input *x* ∈ {0*,* 1} *<sup>n</sup>* for which *f*(*x*) = 0. We are allowed to place or remove pebbles on nodes and gates in *f*, subject to the following rules. Formally, we consider the following rules.

#### <span id="page-12-3"></span>**Definition 2.11 (Pebbling Rules).**

- 1. We can place or remove a pebble on any AND gate for which (at least) one input wire comes out of a node with pebble on it.
- 2. We can place or remove a pebble on any OR gate for which all of the incoming wires come out of nodes which have pebbles on them.
- 3. We can place or remove a pebble on any input node for which  $x_i = 0$ .

The goal of the pebbling game is to end up in a configuration where only the root gate has a pebble, starting from the configuration where there is no pebble at all on any gate, through a sequence of the pebbling instructions that abide by the above rules. Since the description size for an intermediate configuration and the number of pebbling instructions to reach the goal affect the security loss, we want them to be as small as possible. We introduce the following lemma, which was shown by Kowalczyk and Wee who improves the bound on the description size for a configuration compared to the similar results shown in  $[JKK^+17]$ .

<span id="page-12-1"></span>**Lemma 2.12 (Pebbling for NC**<sup>1</sup> [KW19, Theorem 1]). For every input  $x \in \{0,1\}^n$  and any monotone formula f of depth d and fan-in two for which f(x) = 0, there exists a sequence of  $L(d) = 8^d$  pebbling instructions such that every intermediate pebbling configurations can be described using R'(d) = 3d bits. Furthermore, there exists an algorithm Pebble that takes as input f and x and outputs such a sequence in time  $poly(2^d)$ . In particular, Pebble(f,x) runs in time  $poly(\kappa)$  when  $d = O(\log \kappa)$ .

First, we review the secret sharing scheme for monotone Boolean formulae used by Kowalczyk and Wee, which is based on secret sharing schemes in  $[IK02, VNS^+03, JKK^+17]$ .

<span id="page-12-0"></span>**Definition 3.1 (Secret Sharing).** A secret sharing scheme consists of two algorithms (share, reconstruct).

share $(f,\mu)$ : This algorithm takes a (monotone) Boolean formula  $f:\{0,1\}^n \to \{0,1\}$  and  $\mu \in \mathbb{Z}_p$  and outputs shares  $\mu_1,\ldots,\mu_{\hat{m}} \in \mathbb{Z}_p$  and a function  $\rho:[\hat{m}] \to \{0,1,\ldots,n\}$ . We assume that  $\rho$  is deterministically determined from f.

reconstruct $(f, x, \{\mu_j\}_{j \in S})$ : This algorithms takes an input  $x \in \{0, 1\}^n$  for f, f, and a subset of shares  $\{\mu_j\}_{j \in S}$  where  $S \subseteq [\hat{m}]$  and outputs the original value  $\mu$ .

A secret sharing scheme satisfies the following properties.

**Correctness:** For all  $x \in \{0,1\}^n$ ,  $f: \{0,1\}^n \to \{0,1\}$ ,  $\mu \in \mathbb{Z}_p$ ,  $(\{\mu_j\}_{j \in [\hat{m}]}, \rho) \leftarrow \operatorname{share}(f,\mu)$  such that f(x) = 1, it holds that  $\operatorname{reconstruct}(f, x, \{\mu_j\}_{\rho(j) = 0 \lor x_{\rho(j)} = 1}) = \mu$ .

**Security:** For all  $x \in \{0,1\}^n$ ,  $f: \{0,1\}^n \to \{0,1\}$ ,  $\mu, \mu' \in \mathbb{Z}_p$  such that f(x) = 0, the following distributions are the same:

$$\{\{\mu_j\}_{\rho(j)=0 \vee x_{\rho(j)}=1} \mid (\{\mu_j\}_{j \in [\hat{m}]}, \rho) \leftarrow \mathsf{share}(f, \mu)\} \equiv \{\{\mu_j'\}_{\rho(j)=0 \vee x_{\rho(j)}=1} \mid (\{\mu_j'\}_{j \in [\hat{m}]}, \rho) \leftarrow \mathsf{share}(f, \mu')\}$$

**Linearity:** The algorithm reconstruct is a linear function of the shares over  $\mathbb{Z}_p$ . That is, there exists  $\omega_j \in \mathbb{Z}_p$  for  $j \in [\hat{m}]$  and we can compute  $\mu = \sum_{\rho(j) = 0 \lor x_{\rho(j)} = 1} \omega_j \mu_j$ .

To do so, we define a modified secret sharing scheme share in Figure 2 and hybrid distributions in Definition 3.3. In share  $(f, \mu)$ , we first generate shares of  $\mu$  as in share. However, we then replace all shares that are associated with output wires of the pebbled gates with random values.

#### <span id="page-15-2"></span> $\mathsf{share}^u(f,\mu)$

Input: A formula  $f:\{0,1\}^n \to \{0,1\}$ , a secret  $\mu \in \mathbb{Z}_p$ , and a pebbling configuration u of the nodes of f.

- 1. Compute  $(\{\mu_i'\}_{i \in [\hat{m}]}, \rho) \leftarrow \mathsf{share}(f, \mu)$  as defined in Figure 1.
- 2. For each  $\mu'_j$ , if  $j \in u$  (i.e., if j is the output wire of a pebbled node), then choose  $\mu_j \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathbb{Z}_p$ . Otherwise, set  $\mu_j := \mu'_j$ .
- 3. Output  $(\{\mu_j\}_{j\in[\hat{m}]}, \rho)$ .

**Definition 3.3 (hybrid distribution and function taken from [KW19]).** Let  $\mathsf{H}^u$  be the same as  $\mathsf{G}_0^{1\text{-abe}}$  except that we use  $\mathsf{share}^u(f,\mu^{(0)})$  in the implementation of oracle  $\mathcal{O}_{\mathsf{F},0}(\cdot)$ . Let  $h_\ell: \mathit{NC}^1 \times \{0,1\}^n \to \{0,1\}^{R'}$  be as follows:  $h_\ell$  takes as input formula f and input  $x \in \{0,1\}^n$  where f(x) = 0 and outputs the pebbling configuration created from following the first  $\ell$  instructions from  $\mathsf{Pebble}(f,x)$  defined in Lemma 2.12.

From the definition, when  $\ell=0$ ,  $h_0$  is a constant function for all f and x since there is no pebble in the configuration. When  $\ell=L$ ,  $h_L$  is also a constant function for all f and x where f(x)=0 since the pebbling strategy in Lemma 2.12 yields a configuration with single pebble on the root gate.

By the description of share  $^{u}$ , it holds that for all such f and x,

- $H^{h_0(f,x)} \equiv G_0^{1-abe}$  since share  $H^{h_0(f,x)}(f,\mu^{(0)}) = \operatorname{share}(f,\mu^{(0)})$ .
- $\mathsf{H}^{h_L(f,x)} \equiv \mathsf{G}_1^{\text{1-abe}}$  since  $\mathsf{share}^{h_L(f,x)}(f,\mu^{(0)}) = \mathsf{share}(f,\mu^{(1)})$  for an independently random  $\mu^{(1)}$ .

As in Section 2.7, we define games  $\widehat{H}_{\ell,0}(u_0,u_1)$  and  $\widehat{H}_{\ell,1}(u_0,u_1)$  for  $\ell \in [0,L]$ . For these games, we have the following lemma.

<span id="page-15-1"></span>**Lemma 3.4** (neighboring indistinguishability). For all  $\ell \in [L]$  and  $u_0, u_1 \in \{0, 1\}^{R'}$ , it holds that

$$\Pr[\langle \mathcal{A}, \widehat{\mathsf{H}}_{\ell,0}(u_0, u_1) \rangle = 1] - \Pr[\langle \mathcal{A}, \widehat{\mathsf{H}}_{\ell,1}(u_0, u_1) \rangle = 1] \leq n \cdot \mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{mddh}}_{\mathcal{B}}(\kappa).$$

Our proof strategy basically follows that of Kowalczyk and Wee. However, unlike the proofs of Kowalczyk and Wee [KW19], we directly prove Lemma 3.4 by using the  $\mathrm{MDDH}_k$  assumption instead of using CPA-secure secret-key encryption based on the  $\mathrm{MDDH}_k$  assumption. The reason is that  $\mathcal{O}_{\mathsf{F},\beta}(f)$  outputs not only  $\{\mu_j\}_{\rho(j)=0} \cup ([\mathbf{r}_j]_2, [\mu_j + \mathbf{w}_{\rho(j)}^{\top}\mathbf{r}_j]_2)$  but also  $\{[\mathbf{w}_i^{\top}\mathbf{r}_j]_2\}_{i\in[n]\setminus\{\rho(j)\}}$ , which shares the same randomness of  $[\mu_j + \mathbf{w}_{\rho(j)}^{\top}\mathbf{r}_j]_2$  (This is the SKE part of Kowalczyk and Wee  $\mathcal{O}_{\mathsf{F}}$ ). Therefore, we cannot use the abstraction of SKE.

Before we prove Lemma 3.4, we prove Lemma 3.2 by using Lemma 3.4.

*Proof of Lemma 3.2.* By Lemma 3.4 and adaptive security lemma (Lemma 2.10), we obtain

$$\Pr[\langle \mathcal{A}, \mathsf{G}_0^{1\text{-abe}} \rangle = 1] - \Pr[\langle \mathcal{A}, \mathsf{G}_1^{1\text{-abe}} \rangle = 1] \leq 2^{2R'} \cdot L \cdot n \cdot \mathsf{Adv}_{\mathcal{B}}^{\mathsf{mddh}}(\kappa)$$

where R' is the description size of a pebbling configuration and L is the length of the sequence of the pebbling instructions. By pebbling lemma (Lemma 2.12), we have  $R' \leq 3d$  and  $L \leq 8^d$  and thus

$$\Pr[\langle \mathcal{A}, \mathsf{G}_0^{\text{1-abe}} \rangle = 1] - \Pr[\langle \mathcal{A}, \mathsf{G}_1^{\text{1-abe}} \rangle = 1] \leq 2^{6d} \cdot 8^d \cdot n \cdot \mathsf{Adv}_{\mathcal{B}}^{\mathsf{mddh}}(\kappa).$$

Since  $d = O(\log \kappa)$ , this completes the proof of Lemma 3.2.

Now, we move to the proof of Lemma 3.4.

*Proof of Lemma 3.4.* The only difference between  $\widehat{H}_{\ell,0}(u_0,u_1)$  and  $\widehat{H}_{\ell,1}(u_0,u_1)$  is that the former uses share  $u_0$  in  $\mathcal{O}_{\mathsf{F},0}$  and the latter uses share  $u_0$  in  $\mathcal{O}_{\mathsf{F},0}$ . There are two cases to consider.

• There does not exist  $x' \in \{0,1\}^n$  such that  $h_{\ell-1}(f,x') = u_0 \wedge h_{\ell}(f,x') = u_1$ .

• There exists  $x' \in \{0,1\}^n$  such that  $h_{\ell-1}(f,x') = u_0 \wedge h_{\ell}(f,x') = u_1$ .

In the first case, both  $\langle \mathcal{A}, \widehat{\mathsf{H}}_{\ell,0}(u_0,u_1) \rangle$  and  $\langle \mathcal{A}, \widehat{\mathsf{H}}_{\ell,1}(u_0,u_1) \rangle$  abort with probability 1 and two games are perfectly indistinguishable.

Thus, we focus on the second case in the rest of the proof. In this case,  $u_0$  and  $u_1$  are neighboring pebbling configurations in  $\mathsf{Pebble}(f,x')$ . Therefore, the difference between the two games should be caused by an instruction that follows one of the pebbling rules in Definition 2.11. As the proof by Kowalczyk and Wee, we can consider three cases depending on which instruction changes the configuration  $u_0$  into  $u_1$ . The second and third cases below are almost the same as those by Kowalczyk and Wee, but we provide the proofs for completeness.

**Pebble/unpebble input node with out-going wire**  $j^*$ : Let us consider the case where the difference between the games is caused by the third item of pebbling rules in Definition 2.11. In particular, let us assume that input node with out-going wire  $j^* \in [\hat{m}]$  is unpebbled in  $\widehat{H}_{\ell,0}(u_0,u_1)$  and it is pebbled in  $\widehat{H}_{\ell,1}(u_0,u_1)$ . (The proof for the other case is similar.) Note that such  $j^*$  is uniquely determined from  $u_0$  and  $u_1$ . The only difference between the games is that share  $u_0(f,\mu^{(0)})$  sets  $\mu_{j^*}$  to be an honestly generated share of  $\mu^{(0)}$ , but share  $u_0(f,\mu^{(0)})$  sets it to be a random element in  $\mathbb{Z}_p$ . We assume that  $\mathcal{A}$  chooses x and f such that  $u_0(f) = 0$  since otherwise both games output  $u_0(f) = 0$ .

Claim 3.5. Under the  $\mathrm{MDDH}_k$  assumption, for the case of pebble/unpebble input node, the two neighboring hybrids  $\widehat{H}_{\ell,0}(u_0,u_1)$  and  $\widehat{H}_{\ell,1}(u_0,u_1)$  are indistinguishable.

*Proof.* For the sake of contradiction, let us assume that  $\mathcal{A}$  distinguishes the neighboring games. We then construct an algorithm  $\mathcal{B}$ , which is given the  $\mathrm{MDDH}_k$  instance  $([\mathbf{M}]_2, [\mathbf{u}]_2) \in G_2^{(k+1) \times k} \times G_2^{k+1}$  and breaks the  $\mathrm{MDDH}_k$  problem by using  $\mathcal{A}$ . That is,  $\mathcal{B}$  tries to distinguish whether  $[\mathbf{u}]_2 = [\mathbf{Ms}]_2$  for  $\mathbf{s} \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathbb{Z}_p^k$  or  $[\mathbf{u}]_2 \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} (G_2)^{k+1}$  by interacting with  $\mathcal{A}$ . In order to do so,  $\mathcal{B}$  must simulate  $\mathcal{O}_{\mathsf{F},0}(\cdot)$ ,  $\mathcal{O}_{\mathsf{X}}(\cdot)$ , and  $\mathcal{O}_{\mathsf{E}}()$  for the adversary  $\mathcal{A}$ .

**Setup.** Given  $([\mathbf{M}]_2, [\mathbf{u}]_2)$ ,  $\mathcal{B}$  first picks random guess  $i^* \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} [n]$  for  $\rho(j^*)$ , where  $\rho : [\hat{m}] \to \{0, 1, \dots, n\}$  is the function associated with the function f that will be chosen by  $\mathcal{A}$  later in the game.  $\mathcal{B}$  then chooses  $\mathbf{w}_i \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathbb{Z}_p^k$  for  $i \in [n] \setminus \{i^*\}$  and  $\mu^{(0)}, \mu^{(1)} \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathbb{Z}_p$  and sends  $\mu^{(0)}$  to  $\mathcal{A}$ .

Throughout the game,  $\mathcal{B}$  implicitly sets

$$[\mathbf{r}_{j^*}]_2 \coloneqq [\overline{\mathbf{M}}\mathbf{s}]_2 \quad \text{and} \quad \mathbf{w}_{i^*} \coloneqq (\underline{\mathbf{M}} \cdot \overline{\mathbf{M}}^{-1})^\top,$$

where  $\mathbf{r}_{j^*}$  is the randomness that will be used to answer the query to  $\mathcal{O}_{\mathsf{F},0}$ , and  $\overline{\mathbf{M}} \in \mathbb{Z}_p^{k \times k}$  and  $\underline{\mathbf{M}} \in \mathbb{Z}_p^{1 \times k}$  are the first k rows and the last row of  $\mathbf{M}$ , respectively. That is, if  $[\mathbf{u}]_2 = [\mathbf{M}\mathbf{s}]_2$ , we have

<span id="page-16-2"></span><span id="page-16-1"></span><span id="page-16-0"></span>
$$[\mathbf{u}]_2 = \left[\begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{r}_{j^*} \ \mathbf{w}_{i^*}^\top \mathbf{r}_{j^*} \end{pmatrix}\right]_2$$

since  $\mathbf{w}_{i^*}^{\top}\mathbf{r}_{j^*} = (\underline{\mathbf{M}} \cdot \overline{\mathbf{M}}^{-1})\overline{\mathbf{M}}\mathbf{s} = \underline{\mathbf{M}}\mathbf{s}$ . On the other hand, if  $[\mathbf{u}]_2 \overset{\$}{\leftarrow} (G_2)^{k+1}$ , we have  $[\mathbf{u}]_2 = \left[\binom{\mathbf{r}_{j^*}}{u'}\right]_2$  where  $\mathbf{r}_{j^*} \overset{\$}{\sim} \mathbb{Z}_p^k$  and  $u' \overset{\$}{\sim} \mathbb{Z}_p$  are uniformly random.

Simulating  $\mathcal{O}_{\mathsf{F},0}(\cdot)$ . Given a query f to  $\mathcal{O}_{\mathsf{F},0}(\cdot)$  made by  $\mathcal{A}$ ,  $\mathcal{B}$  first checks whether  $i^* = \rho(j^*)$ . If it does not hold, it outputs a random bit and aborts. Otherwise,  $\mathcal{B}$  computes  $(\{\mu_j\}_{j\in[\hat{m}]},\rho) \leftarrow \mathsf{share}^{u_0}(f,\mu^{(0)})$ , chooses  $s_j \stackrel{\$}{\sim} \mathbb{Z}_p^k$  for  $j \in [\hat{m}] \setminus \{j^*\}$ , and returns

$$\{\mu_{j}\}_{j \in [\hat{m}]: \rho(j) = 0}$$

$$\cup \left\{ [\overline{\mathbf{M}}\mathbf{s}_{j}]_{2}, [\mu_{j} + \mathbf{w}_{\rho(j)}^{\top} \overline{\mathbf{M}}\mathbf{s}_{j}]_{2}, \left( \left\{ [\mathbf{w}_{i}^{\top} \overline{\mathbf{M}}\mathbf{s}_{j}]_{2} \right\}_{i \in [n] \setminus \{\rho(j), i^{*}\}}, [\underline{\mathbf{M}}\mathbf{s}_{j}]_{2} \right) \right\}_{j \in [\hat{m}] \setminus \{j^{*}\}: \rho(j) \neq i^{*}}$$

$$(1)$$

$$\cup \left\{ [\overline{\mathbf{M}} \mathbf{s}_j]_2, [\mu_j + \underline{\mathbf{M}} \mathbf{s}_j]_2, \left\{ [\mathbf{w}_i^\top \overline{\mathbf{M}} \mathbf{s}_j]_2 \right\}_{i \in [n] \setminus \{i^*\}} \right\}_{j \in [\hat{m}] \setminus \{j^*\} : \rho(j) = i^*}$$
(2)

$$\cup \left\{ [\overline{\mathbf{u}}]_2, [\mu_{j^*} + \underline{\mathbf{u}}]_2, \{ [\mathbf{w}_i^\top \overline{\mathbf{u}}]_2 \}_{i \in [n] \setminus \{i^*\}} \right\}$$
(3)

in the appropriate order, where  $\overline{\mathbf{u}}$  and  $\underline{\mathbf{u}}$  are the first k elements and the (k+1)-th element of  $\mathbf{u}$ , respectively. Note that the above terms can be efficiently computed since  $\mathcal{B}$  has  $\{\mathbf{w}_i\}_{i\in[n]\setminus\{i^*\}}$ ,  $[\mathbf{M}]_2$ ,  $[\mathbf{u}]_2$  and  $\{\mathbf{s}_j\}_{j\in[m]\setminus\{j^*\}}$ .

It is easy to see that  $\{\mu_j\}_{j\in [\hat{m}]: \rho(j)=0}$  generated as above are distributed as in the games. We claim that the terms in Equation (1) and (2) are distributed as the terms in

$$\left\{ [\mathbf{r}_j]_2, [\mu_j + \mathbf{w}_{\rho(j)}^{\top} \mathbf{r}_j]_2, \left\{ [\mathbf{w}_i^{\top} \mathbf{r}_j]_2 \right\}_{i \in [n] \setminus \{\rho(j)\}} \right\}_{j \in [\hat{m}]}$$

$$(4)$$

with corresponding j after the appropriate rearrangement of the terms, where  $\mathbf{r}_j \overset{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathbb{Z}_k$  for  $j \in [\hat{m}]$  and  $\{\mu_j\}_{j \in [\hat{m}]}$  are chosen as  $\widehat{\mathsf{H}}_{\ell,0}(u_0,u_1)$  in Equation (4). We also claim that the terms in Equation (3) are distributed as the terms in Equation (4) with  $j=j^*$  in  $\widehat{\mathsf{H}}_{\ell,0}(u_0,u_1)$  if  $\mathbf{u}=\mathbf{M}\mathbf{s}$  and as in  $\widehat{\mathsf{H}}_{\ell,1}(u_0,u_1)$  if  $\mathbf{u}\overset{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathbb{Z}_p^{k+1}$ . We prove them in the following.

• We first focus on the terms in Equation (1). If we set  $\mathbf{r}_j \coloneqq \overline{\mathbf{M}} \mathbf{s}_j$ , we have

$$[\overline{\mathbf{M}}\mathbf{s}_j]_2 = [\mathbf{r}_j]_2 \quad \text{and} \quad [\underline{\mathbf{M}}\mathbf{s}_j]_2 = [(\underline{\mathbf{M}}\cdot\overline{\mathbf{M}}^{-1})(\overline{\mathbf{M}}\mathbf{s}_j)]_2 = [\mathbf{w}_{\rho(j^*)}^\top\mathbf{r}_j]_2.$$

We can see that  $\mathbf{r}_j$  is distributed uniformly at random over  $\mathbb{Z}_q^k$  since so is  $\mathbf{s}_j$  and  $\overline{\mathbf{M}}$  is invertible. Therefore, the distribution of the terms in Equation (1) is the same as those in Equation (4) for  $j \in [\hat{m}] \setminus \{j^*\}$  such that  $\rho(j) \neq i^*$ .

- We then focus on the terms in Equation (2). Similarly to the case above, by setting  $\mathbf{r}_j := \overline{\mathbf{M}}\mathbf{s}_j$ , we can see that the distribution of the terms in Equation (2) is the same as those in Equation (4) for  $j \in [\hat{m}] \setminus \{j^*\}$  such that  $\rho(j) = i^*$ .
- We finally focus on the terms in Equation (3). If  $[\mathbf{u}]_2 = [\mathbf{M}\mathbf{s}]_2$ , then  $\mathcal{B}$  perfectly simulates  $\mathcal{O}_{\mathsf{F},0}(f)$  since  $[\mathbf{u}]_2 = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{r}_{j^*} \\ \mathbf{w}_{i^*}^{\mathsf{T}}\mathbf{r}_{j^*} \end{bmatrix}_2$  in this case. That is, the terms in Equation (3) can be represented as

$$([\mathbf{r}_{j^*}]_2, [\mu_{j^*} + \mathbf{w}_{i^*}^{\top} \mathbf{r}_{j^*}]_2, \{[\mathbf{w}_i^{\top} \mathbf{r}_{j^*}]_2\}_{i \in [n] \setminus \{i^*\}}),$$

where we recall that  $i^* = \rho(j^*)$ . If  $\mathbf{u} \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathbb{Z}_p^{k+1}$ , then  $[\mu_{j^*} + \underline{\mathbf{u}}]_2$  and  $[\overline{\mathbf{u}}]_2 = [\mathbf{r}_{j^*}]_2$  are uniformly random over  $G_2$  and  $G_2^k$ , respectively. Thus,  $\mathcal{B}$  perfectly simulates the game where we use share  $u_1(f, \mu^{(0)})$ .

Simulating  $\mathcal{O}_X(\cdot)$ . Given a query x to  $\mathcal{O}_X(\cdot)$  made by  $\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{B}$  aborts and outputs a random bit if  $x_{i^*}=1$ . Otherwise,  $\mathcal{B}$  sends  $\{\mathbf{w}_i\}_{i:x_i=1}$  to  $\mathcal{A}$ . Note that although  $\mathcal{B}$  does not have  $\mathbf{w}_{i^*}$ , it can answer the query as long as the guess  $i^*$  for  $\rho(j^*)$  is correct, since we have  $x_{i^*}=x_{\rho(j^*)}=0$  in that case.

**Simulating**  $\mathcal{O}_{\mathsf{E}}()$ . For a query to  $\mathcal{O}_{\mathsf{E}}()$ ,  $\mathcal{B}$  chooses  $\mathbf{s}' \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathbb{Z}_p^k$  and computes  $[\mathbf{M}\mathbf{s}']_2$  (we can compute this from  $[\mathbf{M}]_2$  and  $\mathbf{s}'$ ). It then sets  $[\mathbf{r}']_2 \coloneqq [\overline{\mathbf{M}}\mathbf{s}']_2$  and returns

$$\left([\mathbf{r}']_2, \{[\mathbf{w}_i^{\top}\mathbf{r}']_2\}_{i \in [n] \setminus \{i^*\}}, [\underline{\mathbf{M}}\mathbf{s}']_2\right)$$

in the appropriate order. We can see  $[\underline{\mathbf{M}}\mathbf{s}']_2 = [(\underline{\mathbf{M}}\cdot\overline{\mathbf{M}}^{-1})(\overline{\mathbf{M}}\mathbf{s}')]_2 = [\mathbf{w}_{i^*}^{\top}\mathbf{r}']_2$  since  $\mathbf{w}_{i^*} = (\underline{\mathbf{M}}\cdot\overline{\mathbf{M}}^{-1})^{\top}$ . Therefore,  $\mathcal{B}$  perfectly simulates  $\mathcal{O}_{\mathsf{E}}()$ .

**Guess.** When A halts and outputs a bit, B outputs the same bit as its guess.

Based on the argument above,  $\mathcal{B}$  perfectly simulates  $\widehat{\mathsf{H}}_{\ell,0}(u_0,u_1)$  if  $[\mathbf{u}]_2 = [\mathbf{M}\mathbf{s}]_2$  and  $\widehat{\mathsf{H}}_{\ell,1}(u_0,u_1)$  if  $[\mathbf{u}]_2 \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} G_2^{k+1}$  for  $\mathcal{A}$  conditioned that the guess  $i^*$  for  $\rho(j^*)$  is correct. Since  $i^*$  is chosen uniformly at random and hidden from the adversary, the guess is correct with probability 1/n. Therefore, if  $\mathcal{A}$  has distinguishing advantage  $\epsilon$  for these two games,  $\mathcal{B}$  has advantage  $\epsilon/n$  against the  $\mathrm{MDDH}_k$  problem. This completes the proof of the claim.

<span id="page-18-1"></span>Pebble/unpebble AND gate with out-going wire c and input wires a,b corresponding to nodes  $g_a,g_b$ : The difference between the two games is that share  $u_0(f,\mu^{(0)})$  sets  $\mu_c := \hat{\mu}_a + \hat{\mu}_b + \hat{\mu}_c$  whereas share  $u_1(f,\mu^{(0)})$  chooses it uniformly at random as  $\mu_c \overset{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathbb{Z}_p$  (or vice-versa in the unpebble case). These distributions are perfectly indistinguishable by the following reason. By the pebbling rule for AND gate, either  $g_a$  or  $g_b$  must be pebbled. We consider the case  $g_a$  is pebbled (we can similarly treat the case  $g_b$  is pebbled). When  $g_a$  is pebbled,  $\hat{\mu}_a$  is independently and uniformly random in both share  $u_0(f,\mu^{(0)})$  and share  $u_1(f,\mu^{(0)})$  since the fan-out of  $g_a$  is 1 and  $\hat{\mu}_a$  does not appear in any other place. Therefore,  $u_0 = \hat{\mu}_a + \hat{\mu}_b + \hat{\mu}_c$  in  $\hat{H}_{\ell,0}(u_0,u_1)$  is independently and uniformly random. The indistinguishability holds even if  $\{\mathbf{w}_i\}_{i\in[n]}$  is given to  $\mathcal{A}$  since this argument is purely information theoretic. Therefore, these two neighboring hybrids are perfectly indistinguishable in this case.

Pebble/unpebble OR gate with out-going wire c and input wires a,b corresponding to nodes  $g_a,g_b$ : The difference between the two games is that share  $u_0(f,\mu^{(0)})$  sets  $\mu_{c_a}:=\hat{\mu}_a+\hat{\mu}_c$  and  $\mu_{c_b}:=\hat{\mu}_b+\hat{\mu}_c$  whereas share  $u_1(f,\mu^{(0)})$  chooses them uniformly at random as  $\mu_{c_a},\mu_{c_b}\stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathbb{Z}_p$  (or vice-versa in the unpebble case). These distributions are perfectly indistinguishable by the following reason. By the pebbling rule for OR gate, both  $g_a$  and  $g_b$  must be pebbled. When  $g_a$  and  $g_b$  are pebbled,  $\hat{\mu}_a$  and  $\hat{\mu}_b$  are independently and uniformly random in both share  $u_1(f,\mu^{(0)})$  and share  $u_1(f,\mu^{(0)})$  since the fan-out of  $g_a$  and  $g_b$  is 1 and  $g_a$  and  $g_b$  do not appear in any other place. Therefore,  $g_a=\hat{\mu}_a+\hat{\mu}_c$  and  $g_b=\hat{\mu}_b+\hat{\mu}_c$  in  $\hat{H}_{\ell,0}(u_0,u_1)$  are independently and uniformly random. The indistinguishability holds even if  $\{\mathbf{w}_i\}_{i\in[n]}$  is given to  $\mathcal{A}$  since this argument is purely information theoretic. Therefore, these two neighboring hybrids are perfectly indistinguishable in this case.

In the second and third cases, the indistinguishability between two neighboring games is purely information theoretic and we can simulate all the oracles. In the first case, the indistinguishability between two neighboring games is reduced to the  $MDDH_k$  assumption as we see saw in the above. This completes the proof of the following: For all  $\ell \in [L]$  and  $u_0, u_1 \in \{0,1\}^{R'}$ , it holds that

$$\Pr[\langle \mathcal{A}, \widehat{\mathsf{H}}_{\ell,0}(u_0, u_1) \rangle = 1] - \Pr[\langle \mathcal{A}, \widehat{\mathsf{H}}_{\ell,1}(u_0, u_1) \rangle = 1] \leq n \cdot \mathsf{Adv}_{\mathcal{B}}^{\mathsf{mddh}}(\kappa).$$

This completes the proof of Lemma 3.4.

#### <span id="page-18-2"></span>3.4 Security Proof

Here, we prove the following theorem, which addresses the security of the scheme. The proof of the theorem is again similar to the equivalent in [KW19], but with some appropriate adaptations.

<span id="page-18-0"></span>**Theorem 3.6.** The above construction is adaptively secure under the  $MDDH_k$  assumption.

*Proof.* We prove the theorem by considering a sequence of hybrid games. To define the hybrid distributions, it would be helpful to first give names of various forms of ciphertext and secret keys that will be used. A ciphertext (of message M under attribute x) can be one of the following forms:

**Normal**: A normal ciphertext is generated as in the scheme.

**SF**: This is the same as normal ciphertext except that  $\mathbf{s}^{\top}\mathbf{A}$  is replaced by a random vector  $\mathbf{c}^{\top} \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathbb{Z}_p^{k+1}$ . That is,

$$\mathsf{ct}_x \coloneqq \left( \, \mathsf{ct}_1 \coloneqq \left[ \boxed{\mathbf{c}^\top} \right]_1, \, \mathsf{ct}_2 \coloneqq \left[ \boxed{\mathbf{c}^\top} \sum_{i: x_i = 1} \mathbf{W}_i \right]_1, \, \mathsf{ct}_3 \coloneqq e\left( \left[ \boxed{\mathbf{c}^\top} \right]_1, [\mathbf{k}]_2 \right) \cdot M \right).$$

A secret key (for a Boolean formula f) can be one of the following forms:

**Normal**: A normal key is generated by KeyGen.

<span id="page-19-2"></span>**SF**: An SF key is sampled as a normal key except that  $\mathbf{v}$  is replaced by  $\mathbf{v} + \delta \mathbf{a}^{\perp}$ , where a fresh  $\delta$  is chosen per SF key and  $\mathbf{a}^{\perp}$  is any fixed  $\mathbf{a}^{\perp} \in \mathbb{Z}_p^{k+1} \setminus \{\mathbf{0}\}$ . That is,

$$\mathsf{sk}_f = \left( \left\{ \mathsf{sk}_j \coloneqq [\mathbf{r}_j]_2, \quad \mathsf{sk}_{\rho(j),j} \coloneqq [\mathbf{v}_j + \mathbf{W}_{\rho(j)}\mathbf{r}_j]_2, \quad \left\{ \ \mathsf{sk}_{i,j} \coloneqq [\mathbf{W}_i\mathbf{r}_j]_2 \ \right\}_{i \in [n] \backslash \{\rho(j)\}} \right\}_{j \in [\hat{m}]} \right)$$

$$\text{ where } (\{\mathbf{v}_j\}_{j \in [\hat{m}]}, \rho) \xleftarrow{\$} \text{ share} (f, \boxed{\mathbf{v} + \delta \mathbf{a}^{\perp}}), \mathbf{r}_j \xleftarrow{\$} \mathbb{Z}_p^k.$$

We then define the following sequence of games to prove the security. Let the number of key generation queries made by an adversary be Q.

- H<sub>0</sub>: This is the real security game for adaptive security where all ciphertexts and keys are normal.
- $H_1$ : This game is the same as  $H_0$  except that the challenge ciphertext is SF.
- $H_{2,\ell}$ : This game is the same as  $H_1$  except that the first  $\ell$  keys are SF and the remaining  $Q-\ell$  keys are normal. The game is defined for  $\ell=0,1,\ldots,Q$ .
- $H_3$ : This is the same as  $H_Q$  except that the message to be encrypted is replaced by a random group element  $\widetilde{M}$ .

Let us fix a PPT adversary  $\mathcal{A}$  and denote the advantage of  $\mathcal{A}$  in  $H_{xx}$  by  $Adv_{xx}$ . We can easily see that  $H_1 \equiv H_{2,0}$  and  $Adv_3 = 0$ . Therefore, to complete the proof of Theorem 3.6, it suffices to prove Lemmata 3.7 to 3.9 in the following. Note that proofs for Lemmata 3.7 and 3.9 are exactly the same as their counterparts in [KW19], whereas we need some adaptations for the proof of Lemma 3.8. For the sake of completeness, we give all the proofs in the following.

<span id="page-19-0"></span>**Lemma 3.7.** Under the MDDH<sub>k</sub> assumption on 
$$G_1$$
, we have  $|\Pr[\langle \mathcal{A}, \mathsf{H}_0 \rangle = 1] - \Pr[\langle \mathcal{A}, \mathsf{H}_1 \rangle = 1]| = \mathsf{negl}(\kappa)$ .

*Proof.* For the sake of contradiction, we assume that  $\mathcal{A}$  distinguishes  $\mathsf{H}_0$  and  $\mathsf{H}_1$  with non-negligible advantage and show that we can construct another adversary  $\mathcal{B}$  that solves the  $\mathrm{MDDH}_k$  assumption. On input  $(\mathbb{G}, [\mathbf{A}]_1, [\mathbf{z}]_1)$ , where either  $\mathbf{z}^\top = \mathbf{s}^\top \mathbf{A}$  or  $\mathbf{z} = \mathbf{c}$  for  $\mathbf{s} \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathbb{Z}_p^k$  and  $\mathbf{c} \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathbb{Z}_p^{k+1}$ ,  $\mathcal{B}$  proceeds as follows.

**Setup.**  $\mathcal{B}$  chooses  $\mathbf{W}_i$  for  $i \in [n]$  and  $\mathbf{v}$  and sets mpk and msk as in the scheme. Note that  $[\mathbf{A}\mathbf{W}_i]_1$  can be computed from  $[\mathbf{A}]_1$  and  $\mathbf{W}_i$ .

**Secret Keys.**  $\mathcal{B}$  can answer any secret key query made by  $\mathcal{A}$  because it has msk.

**Ciphertext.** When **A** asks for the challenge ciphertext with respect to messages  $(M_0, M_1)$  and attribute x,  $\mathcal{B}$  samples  $coin \leftarrow \{0, 1\}$  and sets the challenge ciphertext as

$$\mathsf{ct}_x := \left( [\mathbf{z}^\top]_1, \quad \left[ \mathbf{z}^\top \sum_{i: x_i = 1} \mathbf{W}_i \right]_1, \quad e([\mathbf{z}^\top]_1, [\mathbf{v}]_2) \cdot M_{\mathsf{coin}} \right).$$

**Guess.** When A halts with output coin', B outputs 1 if coin' = coin and 0 otherwise.

It is straightforward to see that  $\mathcal{B}$  simulates  $\mathsf{H}_0$  for  $\mathcal{A}$  when  $\mathbf{z} = \mathbf{s}^\top \mathbf{A}$  and  $\mathsf{H}_1$  otherwise. This completes the proof of Lemma 3.7.

<span id="page-19-1"></span>**Lemma 3.8.** Under the  $MDDH_k$  assumption on  $G_2$ , we have  $|\Pr[\langle \mathcal{A}, \mathsf{H}_{2,\ell-1} \rangle = 1] - \Pr[\langle \mathcal{A}, \mathsf{H}_{2,\ell} \rangle = 1]| = \mathsf{negl}(\kappa)$  for  $\ell \in [Q]$ .

*Proof.* For the sake of contradiction, we assume that  $\mathcal{A}$  distinguishes  $\mathsf{H}_{2,\ell-1}$  and  $\mathsf{H}_{2,\ell}$  with non-negligible advantage and show that we can construct another adversary  $\mathcal{B}$  that distinguishes the oracles in Lemma 3.2. By the same lemma, this implies an attacker against the  $\mathrm{MDDH}_k$  assumption. Given  $\mu^{(0)}$  as an input and equipped with oracles  $\mathcal{O}_{\mathsf{F},\beta}$ ,  $\mathcal{O}_{\mathsf{X}}$ , and  $\mathcal{O}_{\mathsf{E}}$ ,  $\mathcal{B}$  proceeds as follows.

**Setup.** First,  $\mathcal{B}$  chooses  $\mathbf{A} \stackrel{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathbb{Z}_p^{k \times (k+1)}$ ,  $\widetilde{\mathbf{W}}_i \stackrel{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathbb{Z}_p^{(k+1) \times k}$  for  $i \in [n]$ , and  $\widetilde{\mathbf{v}} \stackrel{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathbb{Z}_p^{k+1}$ , computes  $\mathbf{a}^{\perp} \in \mathbb{Z}_p^{k+1} \setminus \{\mathbf{0}\}$  such that  $\mathbf{A}\mathbf{a}^{\perp} = \mathbf{0}$ . It then sets  $\widetilde{\mathbf{W}}_0 := \mathbf{0}$  and implicitly defines

$$\mathbf{v} \coloneqq \widetilde{\mathbf{v}} + \boldsymbol{\mu}^{(0)} \mathbf{a}^{\perp}, \quad \mathbf{W}_i \coloneqq \widetilde{\mathbf{W}}_i + \mathbf{a}^{\perp} \mathbf{w}_i^{\top} \quad \text{for} \quad i \in [0, n]$$

where  $\mathbf{w}_i \in \mathbb{Z}_p^k$  and  $\mu^{(0)} \in \mathbb{Z}_p$  are chosen by the game. Then,  $\mathcal B$  gives

$$\mathsf{mpk} \coloneqq \left( [\mathbf{A}]_1, [\mathbf{A}\widetilde{\mathbf{W}}_1]_1, \dots, [\mathbf{A}\widetilde{\mathbf{W}}_n]_1, e([\mathbf{A}]_1, [\mathbf{v}]_2) \right)$$

to A.

**Secret Keys.** B handles the secret key queries made by  $\mathcal{A}$  as follows.

- For the first  $\ell-1$  secret key queries, say for formula f of size m,  $\mathcal B$  computes

$$(\{\mathbf{v}_j\}_{j\in[\hat{m}]},\rho) \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \operatorname{share}(f,\underbrace{\tilde{\mathbf{v}}+\tilde{\delta}\mathbf{a}^{\perp}}_{=\mathbf{v}+\delta\mathbf{a}^{\perp}})$$

where  $\tilde{\delta} \overset{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathbb{Z}_p$  is drawn independently for each key (here, the per-key  $\delta = \tilde{\delta} - \mu^{(0)}$  implicitly). Next, for each  $j \in [\hat{m}]$ , it queries  $\mathcal{O}_{\mathsf{E}} \to ([\mathbf{r}_j]_2, \{[\mathbf{w}_i^\top \mathbf{r}_j]_2\}_{i \in [n]})$  and forms the following SF key as

$$\mathsf{sk}_f = \left( \left\{ [\mathbf{r}_j]_2, \quad [\underbrace{\mathbf{v}_j + \widetilde{\mathbf{W}}_{\rho(j)}\mathbf{r}_j + \mathbf{a}^{\perp}\mathbf{w}_{\rho(j)}^{\top}\mathbf{r}_j}_{=\mathbf{v}_j + \mathbf{W}_{\rho(j)}\mathbf{r}_j}]_2, \quad \left\{ [\underbrace{\widetilde{\mathbf{W}}_i\mathbf{r}_j + \mathbf{a}^{\perp}\mathbf{w}_i^{\top}\mathbf{r}_j}_{=\mathbf{W}_i\mathbf{r}_j}]_2 \right. \right\}_{i \in [n] \setminus \{\rho(j)\}} \right\}_{j \in [\hat{m}]} \right).$$

Then, it returns  $sk_f$  to A.

- For the last  $Q-\ell$  secret key queries, say for formula f of size  $m,\mathcal{B}$  proceeds as before for the first  $\ell-1$  queries except

$$(\{\mathbf{v}_j\}_{j\in[\hat{m}]},\rho) \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \operatorname{share}(f,\underbrace{\tilde{\mathbf{v}}+\boldsymbol{\mu}^{(0)}\mathbf{a}^{\perp}}_{=\mathbf{v}}).$$

It is easy to see that it forms a normal key.

- For the  $\ell$ -th secret key queries, say for formula f of size m,  $\mathcal{B}$  computes  $(\{\mathbf{v}_j\}_{j\in[\hat{m}]}, \rho) \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \operatorname{share}(f, \tilde{\mathbf{v}})$ , queries  $\mathcal{O}_{\mathsf{F},\beta}(f) \to \left(\left\{[\mathbf{r}_j]_2, [\mu_j + \mathbf{w}_{\rho(j)}\mathbf{r}_j]_2, \left\{[\mathbf{w}_i^{\top}\mathbf{r}_j]_2\right\}_{i\in[n]\setminus\{\rho(j)\}}\right\}_{j\in[\hat{m}]}\right)$  and uses these components to return:

$$\mathsf{sk}_f = \left( \left\{ [\mathbf{r}_j]_2, \quad [\underbrace{\mathbf{v}_j + \widetilde{\mathbf{W}}_{\rho(j)}\mathbf{r}_j + \mathbf{a}^\perp (\mu_j + \mathbf{w}_{\rho(j)}^\top \mathbf{r}_j)}_{=(\mathbf{v}_j + \mu_j \mathbf{a}^\perp) + \mathbf{W}_{\rho(j)} \mathbf{r}_j} \right]_2, \quad \left\{ [\underbrace{\widetilde{\mathbf{W}}_i \mathbf{r}_j + \mathbf{a}^\perp \mathbf{w}_i^\top \mathbf{r}_j}_{=\widetilde{\mathbf{W}}_i \mathbf{r}_j}]_2 \right. \right\}_{i \in [n] \backslash \{\rho(j)\}} \right\}_{j \in [\hat{m}]}$$

We claim that if  $\beta = 0$ , then  $\mathsf{sk}_f$  is a normal key, and if  $\beta = 1$ , then  $\mathsf{sk}_f$  is an SF key. This follows from the fact that thanks to linearity, the shares

$$(\{\mathbf{v}_j + \mu_j \mathbf{a}^\perp\}_{j \in [\hat{m}]}, \rho), \quad \text{where} \quad (\{\mathbf{v}_j\}_{j \in [\hat{m}]}, \rho) \xleftarrow{\$} \mathsf{share}(f, \tilde{\mathbf{v}}), \quad (\{\mu_j\}_{j \in [\hat{m}]}, \rho) \xleftarrow{\$} \mathsf{share}(f, \mu^{(\beta)})$$

are identically distributed to share  $(f, \tilde{\mathbf{v}} + \mu^{(\beta)} \mathbf{a}^{\perp})$ . The claim then follows from the fact that  $\mathbf{v} = \tilde{\mathbf{v}} + \mu^{(0)} \mathbf{a}^{\perp}$  and that  $\tilde{\mathbf{v}} + \mu^{(1)} \mathbf{a}^{\perp}$  is identically distributed to  $\mathbf{v} + \delta \mathbf{a}^{\perp}$ , where we set  $\delta \coloneqq \mu^{(1)} - \mu^{(0)}$  is a fresh random value for the key.

**Ciphertext.** When **A** asks for the challenge ciphertext with respect to messages  $(M_0, M_1)$  and attribute x,  $\mathcal{B}$  queries  $\mathcal{O}_X$  on input x to obtain  $\{\mathbf{w}_i\}_{i:x_i=1}$  It then samples coin  $\leftarrow \{0,1\}$ ,  $\mathbf{c} \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathbb{Z}_p^{k+1}$  and returns the following challenge ciphertext for  $\mathcal{A}$ :

$$\mathsf{ct}_x := \left( [\mathbf{c}^\top]_1, \quad \left[ \mathbf{c}^\top \sum_{i: x_i = 1} ( \underbrace{\widetilde{\mathbf{W}}_i + \mathbf{a}^\perp \mathbf{w}_i^\top}_{= \mathbf{W}_i} ) \right]_1, \quad e([\mathbf{c}^\top]_1, [\widetilde{\mathbf{v}} + \boldsymbol{\mu}^{(0)} \mathbf{a}^\perp]_2) \cdot M_{\mathsf{coin}} \right).$$

**Guess.** When  $\mathcal{A}$  halts with output coin',  $\mathcal{B}$  outputs 1 if coin' = coin and 0 otherwise.

<span id="page-20-0"></span>Putting everything together, we can see that  $\mathcal{B}$  simulates  $\mathsf{H}_{2\ell-1}$  for  $\mathcal{A}$  when  $\beta=0$  and  $\mathsf{H}_{2\ell}$  otherwise. This completes the proof of Lemma 3.8.

**Lemma 3.9.** We have  $|\Pr[\langle \mathcal{A}, \mathsf{H}_{2,\ell} \rangle = 1] - \Pr[\langle \mathcal{A}, \mathsf{H}_{3} \rangle = 1]| = 1/p$  unconditionally.

*Proof.* We show that these two hybrids are identically distributed conditioned on  $\mathbf{c}^{\top}\mathbf{a}^{\perp} \neq 0$ . To see this, consider two ways of sampling  $\mathbf{v}$ : as  $\tilde{\mathbf{v}} \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathbb{Z}_p^{k+1}$  and as  $\tilde{\mathbf{v}} + \tilde{m}\mathbf{a}^{\perp}$  for an independent  $\tilde{m} \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathbb{Z}_p$ . Note that both result in  $\mathbf{v}$  having a uniform distribution.

Using  $\tilde{\mathbf{v}}$  to simulate hybrid  $\mathsf{H}_{2,Q}$  obviously results in  $\mathsf{H}_{2,Q}$  (where  $\mathbf{v}=\tilde{\mathbf{v}}$ ). However, using the identically distributed  $\mathbf{v}=\tilde{\mathbf{v}}+\tilde{m}\mathbf{a}^{\perp}$  to simulate  $\mathsf{H}_{2,Q}$  results in  $\mathsf{H}_3$  with  $\widetilde{M}=M\cdot e([\mathbf{c}^{\top}]_1,[\tilde{m}\mathbf{a}^{\perp}]_2)$  and re-defined randomness  $\tilde{\delta}_j:=\delta_j+\tilde{m}$  in the secret keys. Note that the information of  $\tilde{m}$  is not revealed to  $\mathcal{A}$  from the secret key queries because  $\tilde{m}$  is masked by per-key randomness  $\delta_j$ . Therefore,  $\widetilde{M}$  is distributed uniformly at random over  $G_T$  as long as  $\mathbf{c}^{\top}\mathbf{a}\neq 0$ .

Since  $\mathbf{c}$  is chosen at random and independent from  $\mathbf{a}^{\perp} \neq \mathbf{0}$ , so  $\mathbf{c}^{\top} \mathbf{a}^{\perp} = 0$  with probability 1/p, and since we know that  $\mathsf{H}_{2,Q} \equiv \mathsf{H}_3$  conditioned on  $\mathbf{c}^{\top} \mathbf{a}^{\perp} \neq 0$ , the lemma follows.

This completes the proof of Theorem 3.6.

## <span id="page-21-0"></span>4 Compact Constrained Signature

## <span id="page-21-1"></span>4.1 Constrained Signature

We provide definition of a constrained signature (CS) scheme. We also provide an additional feature (i.e., online/offline efficiency) for CS schemes which will play a vital role in our compact NIZK construction in Section 5.

**Definition 4.1 (Constrained Signature).** A constrained signature (CS) scheme with message space  $\{0,1\}^n$  for a circuit class  $C = \{C : \{0,1\}^n \to \{0,1\}\}$  consists of PPT algorithms (CS.Setup, CS.KeyGen, CS.Sign, CS.Vrfy).

- CS.Setup $(1^{\kappa}, 1^n) \to (\text{msk}, \text{vk})$ : The setup algorithm on input the security parameter  $1^{\lambda}$  and the input length  $1^n$ , outputs a master secret key msk and a verification key vk.
- CS.KeyGen(msk, C)  $\rightarrow$  sk $_C$ : The key generation algorithm on input a master secret key msk and a circuit  $C \in C$ , outputs a signing key sk $_C$ .
- CS.Sign( $\operatorname{sk}_C, x) \to \sigma$ : The signing algorithm on input the signing key  $\operatorname{sk}_C$  and message  $x \in \{0,1\}^n$ , outputs a signature  $\sigma$ .
- CS.Vrfy(vk,  $x, \sigma$ )  $\to \top$  or  $\bot$ : The verification algorithm on input the verification key vk, message x, and signature  $\sigma$ , outputs either  $\bot$  (indicating the signature is valid) or  $\top$  (indicating the signature is invalid).

A CS scheme must satisfy the following requirements.

<u>Correctness.</u> For all  $\kappa \in \mathbb{N}$ ,  $n = n(\kappa) \in \mathbb{N}$ ,  $(\mathsf{msk}, \mathsf{vk}) \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{CS}.\mathsf{Setup}(1^{\kappa}, 1^n), x \in \{0,1\}^n, C \in \mathcal{C} \text{ such that } C(x) = 1,$  and  $\mathsf{sk}_C \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{CS}.\mathsf{KeyGen}(\mathsf{msk}, C)$ , we have

$$\Pr[\mathsf{CS.Vrfy}(\mathsf{vk}, x, \mathsf{CS.Sign}(\mathsf{sk}_C, x)) = \top] = 1$$

<u>Unforgeability.</u> We define (adaptive) unforgeability for a CS scheme. The security notion is defined by the following game between a challenger and an adversary A.

**Setup:** The challenger runs  $(\mathsf{msk}, \mathsf{vk}) \overset{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{CS}.\mathsf{Setup}(1^\kappa, 1^n)$  and gives  $\mathsf{vk}$  to  $\mathcal{A}$ . It also prepares an empty list  $\mathcal{Q}$ .

**Key Queries:**  $\mathcal{A}$  can adaptively make key queries unbounded polynomially many times throughout the game. When  $\mathcal{A}$  queries  $C \in \mathcal{C}$ , the challenger runs  $\mathsf{sk}_C \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{CS}.\mathsf{KeyGen}(\mathsf{msk},C)$  and returns  $\mathsf{sk}_C$  to  $\mathcal{A}$ . Finally, the challenger updates  $\mathcal{Q} \leftarrow \mathcal{Q} \cup \{C\}$ .

**Forgery:** Eventually,  $\mathcal{A}$  outputs  $(x^*, \sigma^*)$  as the forgery. We say  $\mathcal{A}$  wins if CS.Vrfy $(vk, x^*, \sigma^*) = \top$  holds. Furthermore, we say that  $\mathcal{A}$  is *admissible* if  $C(x^*) = 0$  holds for all  $C \in \mathcal{Q}$  at the end of the game.

We say the CS scheme is (adaptively) *unforgeable* if the winning probability for all admissible PPT adversaries A in the above game is  $negl(\kappa)$ , where the probability is taken over the randomness of all algorithms.

The following property is optional in the sense that our CS scheme can achieve the following property, but the property is not strictly necessary for our application of CS to the construction of compact NIZKs.

$$\{\sigma \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{CS}.\mathsf{Sign}(\mathsf{sk}_{C_0},x)\} \overset{\mathrm{stat}}{\approx} \{\sigma \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{CS}.\mathsf{Sign}(\mathsf{sk}_{C_1},x)\}$$

where the probability is only over the randomness used by CS.Sign.

Additionally to the above essential requirements for CS, we introduce a natural notion of *decomposable online-offline* efficiency. At a high level, this notion states that if we (partially) knew the message x to be signed in advance, then we can modify the verification key vk to a message specific verification key vk $_x$  which allows for an efficient verification of signature  $\sigma$  with running time independent of |x|. More formally, the notion is defined as follows.

<span id="page-22-0"></span>**Definition 4.2 (Decomposable Online-Offline Efficiency).** A constrained signature with message space  $\{0,1\}^n$  for a circuit class  $\mathcal{C} = \{C: \{0,1\}^n \to \{0,1\}\}$  is said to have decomposable online-offline efficiency if there further exists PPT algorithms (CS.Aggrgt, CS.VrfyOnL) exhibiting the following properties.

- The verification key vk can be decomposed into  $vk = (vk_0, \{vk_{i,b} \in \mathcal{VK}\}_{i \in [n], b \in \{0,1\}})$ , where  $\mathcal{VK}$  is a space of verification key component.
- Any component in VK, any honestly generated  $vk_0$ , and any honestly generated signature  $\sigma$  can be represented as binary strings of fixed polynomial length  $poly(\kappa)$ . In particular, length of these components are independent from n.
- Algorithm CS.Aggrgt takes as input an element of  $VK^* = \bigcup_{\ell \in \mathbb{N}} VK^{\ell}$  and outputs an element in VK. We require that for any  $y, z \in \{0, 1\}^*$  such that  $x = y || z \in \{0, 1\}^n$ , we have

$$\begin{split} & \mathsf{CS.Aggrgt}\left(\{\mathsf{vk}_{i,x_i}\}_{i\in[n]}\right) \\ =& \mathsf{CS.Aggrgt}\left(\mathsf{CS.Aggrgt}\left(\{\mathsf{vk}_{i,y_i}\}_{i\in[|y|]}\right), \mathsf{CS.Aggrgt}\left(\{\mathsf{vk}_{|y|+i,z_i}\}_{i\in[|z|]}\right)\right). \end{split}$$

• Algorithm CS.VrfyOnL takes as input  $vk_0$ , a component in VK and a signature in  $\sigma$ , and outputs either  $\top$  or  $\bot$ . We require that for any  $x \in \{0,1\}^n$ , for any honestly generated vk, and for any (possibly maliciously generated)  $\sigma$ , we have

$$\mathsf{CS.Vrfy}(\mathsf{vk},x,\sigma) = \mathsf{CS.VrfyOnL}\left(\mathsf{vk}_0,\mathsf{CS.Aggrgt}\left(\{\mathsf{vk}_{i,x_i}\}_{i\in[n]}\right),\sigma\right).$$

Observe that the input length of CS.VrfyOnL is independent from n, which follows from the second item of this definition. We require that the running time of CS.VrfyOnL is independent from n as well.

#### <span id="page-22-1"></span>4.2 Construction and Security

Here, we give the construction of our constrained signature (CS) scheme that will be used for the construction of the compact NIZK. The CS scheme has very compact signature size and the decomposable online-offline efficiency defined in Definition 4.2. In order to get the CS scheme, we apply the folklore conversion that converts ABE into CS to our compact KP-ABE scheme in Section 3, where the signing key  $sk_f$  for the function f in the CS scheme is the same as the secret key  $sk_f$  for the same function f in the ABE scheme, and the signature on a string x in the CS scheme is certain "aggregated form" of the secret key that is derived when decrypting an ABE ciphertext encrypted for the attribute x. To verify a signature on x in the CS, we encrypt a random message for x in the underlying ABE and then see if the message is recovered or not when decrypting the ciphertext using the signature as an (aggregated form of) secret key.

The CS scheme obtained by the above conversion can only deal with monotone Boolean formulae, since the original ABE is for the same class of functions. For our purpose, we need CS scheme for  $NC^1$  circuits, which is more general class than monotone Boolean formulae. This gap can be filled using Lemma 2.9.

#### Online-Offline Decomposability.

**Theorem 4.3.** The CS scheme above has decomposable online-offline efficiency defined as per Definition 4.2.

*Proof.* To prove the theorem, we define  $\mathcal{VK}$ ,  $\mathsf{vk}_0$ , and  $\mathsf{vk}_{i,b}$  for  $i \in [n], b \in \{0,1\}$  as

$$\mathcal{VK}\coloneqq G_1^{k\times k}, \quad \mathsf{vk}_0\coloneqq \left([\mathbf{A}]_1, e([\mathbf{A}]_1, [\mathbf{v}]_2)\right), \quad \mathsf{vk}_{i,b}\coloneqq [\mathbf{AW}_{2i-b}]_1.$$

It is easy to see that the first and the second items in Definition 4.2 are satisfied. We then define additional algorithms CS.VrfyOnL and CS.Aggrgt as follows:

 $\mathsf{CS.Aggrgt}(\{\mathsf{vk}_i\}_{i\in[n']})\text{:}\quad \text{If there exists } i\in[n'] \text{ such that } \mathsf{vk}_i\not\in\mathcal{VK}=G_1^{k\times k}, \text{ output }\bot. \text{ Otherwise, output }\bot.$ 

$$X \coloneqq \prod_{i \in [n']} \mathsf{vk}_i,$$

where the product represents the component-wise multiplication in  $G_1$ .

CS.VrfyOnL(vk<sub>0</sub>, vk',  $\sigma$ ): Parse vk<sub>0</sub>  $\rightarrow$  ( $A \in G_1^{k \times (k+1)}, V \in G_T^k$ ), vk'  $\in G_1^{k \times k}$ , and  $\sigma \rightarrow (\sigma_1, \sigma_2) \in G_2^k \times G_2^k$ . Then output  $\top$  if the following holds and  $\bot$  otherwise:

$$e(A, \sigma_1) \cdot e(\mathsf{vk}', \sigma_2)^{-1} = V.$$

The third item in Definition 4.2 follows from the fact that the following equation holds for any  $x = y || z \in \{0, 1\}^n$ :

$$\begin{split} \prod_{i \in [2n]} \underbrace{[\mathbf{A}\mathbf{W}_{i,2i-x_i}]}_{=\mathsf{vk}_{i,x_i}} &= \prod_{i \in [|y|]} [\mathbf{A}\mathbf{W}_{i,2i-x_i}] \cdot \prod_{i \in [|y|+1,|y|+|z|]} [\mathbf{A}\mathbf{W}_{i,2i-x_i}] \\ &= \prod_{i \in [|y|]} [\mathbf{A}\mathbf{W}_{i,2i-y_i}] \cdot \prod_{i \in [|y|+1,|y|+|z|]} [\mathbf{A}\mathbf{W}_{i,2i-z_{i-|y|}}] \\ &= \prod_{i \in [|y|]} \underbrace{[\mathbf{A}\mathbf{W}_{i,2i-y_i}]}_{=\mathsf{vk}_{i,y_i}} \cdot \prod_{j \in [|z|]} \underbrace{[\mathbf{A}\mathbf{W}_{|y|+j,2(|y|+j)-z_j}]}_{=\mathsf{vk}_{|y|+j,z_j}}. \end{split}$$

To prove the fourth item, it suffices to show that  $\mathsf{vk}'$  computed as Equation (5) equals to  $\mathsf{CS.Aggrgt}(\{\mathsf{vk}_{i,x_i}\}_{i\in[n]})$ . This follows since the former is the product of  $[\mathbf{AW}_i]_1$  over i in  $S \coloneqq \{i \in [2n] : \hat{x}_i = 1\}$  and the latter is over i in  $S' \coloneqq \{2j - x_j : j \in [n]\}$ , and we have S = S' by the definition of  $\hat{x}$  (See Lemma 2.9).

**Security.** In the following, we show that the above construction is unforgeable and then discuss how to extend the scheme to satisfy context-hiding. While the latter property is not necessary for our application of CS in Section 5, this property may be useful when we use the CS scheme stand-alone.

**Theorem 4.4.** The above construction is (adaptively) unforgeable under the  $MDDH_k$  assumption.

*Proof.* For the sake of contradiction, suppose that there exists an adversary  $\mathcal{A}$  that breaks unforgeability of the  $\Pi_{CS}$  with non-negligible probability  $\epsilon$ . We then construct a PPT adversary  $\mathcal{B}$  that breaks the adaptive security of the ABE with advantage  $\epsilon$  for the attribute length 2n as follows.

 $\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{mpk})$ : It sets  $\mathsf{vk} \coloneqq \mathsf{mpk}$  and gives the master public key to  $\mathcal{A}$ . When  $\mathcal{A}$  makes a signing key query for a circuit  $C, \mathcal{B}$  runs  $\mathsf{EncCir}(C) \to f$  and makes a key generation query for f to obtain  $\mathsf{sk}_f$ . Then,  $\mathcal{B}$  passes  $\mathsf{sk}_f$  to  $\mathcal{A}$ . At some point,  $\mathcal{A}$  outputs a forgery  $(x^*, \sigma^*)$ . Then,  $\mathcal{B}$  outputs a random bit and abort if  $\mathsf{CS.Vrfy}(\mathsf{vk}, x^*, \sigma^*) = \bot$ . Otherwise,  $\mathcal{B}$  samples two random distinctive messages  $M_0, M_1 \in G_T$  and makes a challenge query for  $(\hat{x}^*, (M_0, M_1))$ , where  $\hat{x}^* = \mathsf{EncInp}(x^*)$ . Given the challenge ciphertext ct, it first parses  $\mathsf{ct} \to (\mathsf{ct}_1 \in G_1^{k+1}, \mathsf{ct}_2 \in G_1^k, \mathsf{ct}_3 \in G_T)$  and  $\sigma^* \to (\sigma_1^* \in G_2^{k+1}, \sigma_2^* \in G_2^{k+1})$  and computes  $M' \coloneqq e(\mathsf{ct}_1, \sigma_1^*)^{-1} \cdot e(\mathsf{ct}_2, \sigma_2^*) \cdot \mathsf{ct}_3$ . It outputs 0 if  $M' = M_0$  and 1 otherwise.

We first check that  $\mathcal B$  is an admissible adversary if so is  $\mathcal A$ , since we have  $C(x^*)=0$  iff  $f(\hat x^*)=0$  for any C and  $f=\mathsf{EncCir}(C)$  from Lemma 2.9. We then claim that whenever CS.Vrfy $(\mathsf{vk},x^*,\sigma^*)=\top$ , we have  $M'=M_{\mathsf{coin}}$ . To prove the claim, let us assume that CS.Vrfy $(\mathsf{vk},\hat x^*,\sigma^*)=\top$  holds. Then, we have

$$e([\mathbf{A}]_1, \sigma_1^*) \cdot e(\prod_{i: \hat{x}_1^* = 1} [\mathbf{A} \mathbf{W}_i]_1, \sigma_2^*)^{-1} = e([\mathbf{A}]_1, [\mathbf{v}]_2)$$

by the definition of CS.Vrfy. Furthermore, there exists  $\mathbf{s} \in \mathbb{Z}_p^k$  such that  $\mathsf{ct}_1 = [\mathbf{s}^\top \mathbf{A}]_1$ ,  $\mathsf{ct}_2 = [\mathbf{s}^\top \sum_{i:y_i^*=1} \mathbf{A} \mathbf{W}_i]_1$ , and  $\mathsf{ct}_3 = e([\mathbf{s}^\top \mathbf{A}]_1, [\mathbf{v}]_2) \cdot M_{\mathsf{coin}}$  by the definition of Enc. Then, the above equation implies

$$e(\mathsf{ct}_1, \sigma_1^*) \cdot e(\mathsf{ct}_2, \sigma_2^*)^{-1} = e([\mathbf{s}^{\top} \mathbf{A}]_1, [\mathbf{v}]_2)$$

which in turns implies  $M' = M_{\text{coin}}$ . Thus,  $\mathcal{B}$  correctly guesses coin when  $\mathcal{A}$  breaks the unforgeability of  $\Pi_{\text{CS}}$  and outputs a random bit otherwise. This implies that the advantage of  $\mathcal{B}$  is  $\epsilon$ , which is non-negligible as desired.

*Remark* 4.5 (Adding Context-Hiding for the Scheme). We remark that it is possible to make the above scheme context-hiding by adding the following modification. Namely, we change the scheme so that it contains

$$[\mathbf{R}]_2, [\mathbf{W}_1\mathbf{R}]_2, \dots, [\mathbf{W}_{2n}\mathbf{R}]_2,$$

for random  $\mathbf{R} \in \mathbb{Z}_p^{k \times k}$  in vk. This modification allows us to randomize  $\mathbf{r}$  in Equation (6), which makes the scheme context-hiding. We discuss that even with this change, the scheme remains adaptively unforgeable. For proving this, it suffices to show that our KP-ABE scheme in Section 3 remains adaptively secure even if we add  $([\mathbf{R}]_2, [\mathbf{W}_1\mathbf{R}]_2, \dots, [\mathbf{W}_n\mathbf{R}]_2)$  to the master public key. To show this, we explain the necessary modifications for the proof of Theorem 3.6, which consists of Lemmata 3.7 to 3.9. We first observe that the proof of Lemma 3.7 is unchanged by this change, since the reduction algorithm in the proof knows all of  $\{\mathbf{W}_i\}_i$  and can simulate the extra terms. Similarly, the proof for Lemma 3.9 is unchanged, since this is shown by an information theoretic argument that holds even if the adversary knows all of  $\{\mathbf{W}_i\}_i$ . As for the proof of Lemma 3.8, slight care is needed since  $\{\mathbf{W}_i\}_i$  are not known to the simulator. In this case, the simulator makes multiple queries for  $\mathcal{O}_{\mathbf{E}}()$  to get  $\{[\mathbf{r}_j]_2, \{[\mathbf{w}_i^{\top}\mathbf{r}]_2\}_{i\in[n]}\}_{j\in[n]}\}_{j\in[n]}$  and forms  $\{[\mathbf{R}]_2, \{[\mathbf{w}_i^{\top}\mathbf{R}]_2\}_{i\in[n]}\}$ , where  $\mathbf{R} \in \mathbb{Z}_p^{k \times k}$  is the concatenation of  $\{\mathbf{r}_j\}_j$ . Then the simulator can simulate the terms  $\{[\mathbf{W}_i\mathbf{R}]_2\}_{i\in[n]}\}$  from  $([\mathbf{R}]_2, \{[\mathbf{w}_i^{\top}\mathbf{R}]_2\}_{i\in[n]})$ ,  $\mathbf{a}^{\perp}$ , and  $\widetilde{\mathbf{W}}_i$ , since we have

$$[\mathbf{W}_i \mathbf{R}]_2 = [\widetilde{\mathbf{W}}_i \mathbf{R} + \mathbf{a}^{\perp} (\mathbf{w}_i^{\top} \mathbf{R})]_2.$$

# <span id="page-25-1"></span>5 Compact NIZK from Compact Constrained Signatures

#### <span id="page-25-0"></span>5.1 Main Construction

Here, we construct a compact NIZK based on the compact CS scheme which we constructed in Section 4. Let  $\mathcal{L}$  be an **NP** language defined by a relation  $\mathcal{R} \subseteq \{0,1\}^* \times \{0,1\}^*$ . Let  $n(\kappa)$  and  $m(\kappa)$  be any fixed polynomials. Let C be a circuit that computes the relation  $\mathcal{R}$  on  $\{0,1\}^n \times \{0,1\}^m$ , i.e., for  $(x,w) \in \{0,1\}^n \times \{0,1\}^m$ , we have C(x,w)=1 if and only if  $(x,w) \in \mathcal{R}$ .

The construction will be given by combining following ingredients.

- A symmetric key encryption (SKE) scheme  $\Pi_{\mathsf{SKE}} = (\mathsf{SKE}.\mathsf{Setup},\mathsf{SKE}.\mathsf{KeyGen},\mathsf{SKE}.\mathsf{Enc},\mathsf{SKE}.\mathsf{Dec})$  with message space  $\{0,1\}^m$ , key space  $\{0,1\}^\ell$  and ciphertext space  $\{0,1\}^{|\mathsf{ct}|}$ . We require that it is one-time secure, its decryption circuit can be computed in  $\mathbf{NC}^1$ , it has an additive ciphertext overhead (i.e.,  $|\mathsf{ct}| = m + \mathsf{poly}(\kappa)$ ), and  $\ell$  is independent of m. As shown in Lemma 2.3, such an SKE scheme exists under the CDH assumption in a subgroup of  $\mathbb{Z}_p^*$ .
- A constrained signature scheme (CS.Setup, CS.KeyGen, CS.Sign, CS.Vrfy, CS.Aggrgt, CS.VrfyOnL) we constructed in Section 4. The scheme should support the circuit  $f_{pp_{SKF}}$  that computes

$$f_{\mathsf{pp}_{\mathsf{SKE}}}(K, x, \mathsf{ct}) = C(x, \mathsf{SKE}.\mathsf{Dec}(\mathsf{pp}_{\mathsf{SKE}}, K, \mathsf{ct}))$$

for all  $pp_{SKE} \in SKE.Setup(1^{\kappa})$ .

• (Not necessarily compact) extractable NIZK scheme  $\Pi_{\mathsf{NIZK}} = (\mathsf{Setup}, \mathsf{Prove}, \mathsf{Verify})$  for the language corresponding to the relation  $\widetilde{\mathcal{R}}$  defined below:

 $((\mathsf{vk}_0, \{\mathsf{vk}_{i,b}\}_{i\in[\ell],b\in\{0,1\}}, Y), (K,\sigma)) \in \widetilde{\mathcal{R}}$  if and only if the followings are satisfied:

- 1.  $K \in \{0,1\}^{\ell}$ ,
- $\text{2. CS.VrfyOnL}(\mathsf{vk}_0, Z, \sigma) = \top \text{ where } Z = \mathsf{CS.Aggrgt}\big(\mathsf{CS.Aggrgt}(\{\mathsf{vk}_{i,K_i}\}_{i \in \lceil \ell \rceil}), Y\big)$

Our compact NIZK is described as follows.

Setup' $(1^{\kappa})$ :

1. Generate crs  $\stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow}$  Setup $(1^{\kappa})$ .

- <span id="page-26-0"></span>2. Generate  $pp_{SKE} \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} SKE.Setup(1^{\kappa})$ .
- $3. \ \ \text{Generate} \ (\mathsf{vk} = (\mathsf{vk}_0, \{\mathsf{vk}_{i,b}\}_{i \in \lceil \ell+n+|\mathsf{ct}| \rceil, b \in \{0,1\}}), \mathsf{msk}) \xleftarrow{\$} \mathsf{CS}.\mathsf{Setup}(1^\kappa, 1^{\ell+n+|\mathsf{ct}|}).$
- $\textbf{4. Generate sk}_f \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{CS}.\mathsf{KeyGen}(\mathsf{msk},f_{\mathsf{PP}_{\mathsf{SKE}}}).$
- 5. Output  $crs' = (crs, pp_{SKE}, vk, sk_f)$ .

## Prove'(crs', x, w):

- 1. Abort if  $\mathcal{R}(x, w) = 0$ . Otherwise, do the following.
- $2. \ \text{Parse crs}' \rightarrow (\text{crs}, \mathsf{pp}_{\mathsf{SKE}}, \mathsf{vk} = (\mathsf{vk}_0, \{\mathsf{vk}_{i,b}\}_{i \in [\ell+n+|\mathsf{ct}|], b \in \{0,1\}}), \mathsf{sk}_f).$
- $\textbf{3. Generate } K \xleftarrow{\hspace{0.1em} \$} \mathsf{SKE}.\mathsf{KeyGen}(\mathsf{pp}_{\mathsf{SKE}}) \text{ and } \mathsf{ct} \xleftarrow{\hspace{0.1em} \$} \mathsf{SKE}.\mathsf{Enc}(\mathsf{pp}_{\mathsf{SKE}},K,w).$
- 4. Compute  $\sigma \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{CS}.\mathsf{Sign}(\mathsf{sk}_f, (K, x, \mathsf{ct})).$
- 5. Compute  $Y := \mathsf{CS.Aggrgt}(\{\mathsf{vk}_{\ell+i,y_i}\}_{i \in [n+|\mathsf{ct}|]})$  where  $y := (x,\mathsf{ct}) \in \{0,1\}^{n+|\mathsf{ct}|}$ .
- 6. Compute  $\pi \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{Prove}(\mathsf{crs}, (\mathsf{vk}_0, \{\mathsf{vk}_{i,b}\}_{i \in [\ell], b \in \{0,1\}}, Y), (K, \sigma)).$
- 7. Output  $\pi' := (\mathsf{ct}, \pi)$ .

## Verify'(crs', x, $\pi'$ ):

- 1. Parse  $\pi' \to (\mathsf{ct}, \pi)$ . If it is not in this form, reject it. Otherwise, do the following.
- 2. Parse  $crs' \to (crs, pp_{SKE}, vk = (vk_0, \{vk_{i,b}\}_{i \in [\ell+n+|ct|], b \in \{0,1\}}), sk_f).$
- $3. \ \ \text{Compute} \ Y \coloneqq \mathsf{CS.Aggrgt}(\{\mathsf{vk}_{\ell+i,y_i}\}_{i \in [n+|\mathsf{ct}|]}) \ \text{where} \ y \coloneqq (x,\mathsf{ct}) \in \{0,1\}^{n+|\mathsf{ct}|}.$
- 4. Output  $\top$  if Verify(crs,  $(\mathsf{vk}_0, \{\mathsf{vk}_{i,b}\}_{i \in [\ell], b \in \{0,1\}}, Y), \pi) = \top$  and otherwise  $\bot$ .

**Correctness.** Suppose that  $(\mathsf{ct}, \pi)$  is an honestly generated proof on  $(x, w) \in \mathcal{R}$ . Then we have  $\mathsf{ct} \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{SKE}.\mathsf{Enc}(\mathsf{pp}_{\mathsf{SKE}}, K, w)$  and  $\pi \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{Prove}(\mathsf{crs}, (\mathsf{vk}_0, \{\mathsf{vk}_{i,b}\}_{i \in [\ell], b \in \{0,1\}}, Y), (K, \sigma))$  where  $\mathsf{pp}_{\mathsf{SKE}} \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{SKE}.\mathsf{Setup}(1^\kappa), K \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{SKE}.\mathsf{KeyGen}(\mathsf{pp}_{\mathsf{SKE}}), \sigma \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{CS}.\mathsf{Sign}(\mathsf{sk}_f, (K, x, \mathsf{ct})), \mathsf{and}$ 

$$Y = \mathsf{CS}.\mathsf{Aggrgt}(\{\mathsf{vk}_{\ell+i,y_i}\}_{i \in [n+|\mathsf{ct}|]}).$$

By the correctness of  $\Pi_{SKE}$ , we have  $f_{pp_{SKE}}(K, x, ct) = 1$ . Furthermore, by the correctness of  $\Pi_{CS}$ , we have  $CS.Vrfy(vk, (K, x, ct), \sigma) = \top$ , which is equivalent to

$$\mathsf{CS.VrfyOnL}(\mathsf{vk}_0, Z, \sigma) = \top \quad \text{where} \quad Z = \mathsf{CS.Aggrgt}\big(\mathsf{CS.Aggrgt}(\{\mathsf{vk}_{i, K_i}\}_{i \in [\ell]}), Y\big).$$

Therefore we have  $((\mathsf{vk}_0, \{\mathsf{vk}_{i,b}\}_{i \in [\ell], b \in \{0,1\}}, Y), (K, \sigma)) \in \widetilde{\mathcal{R}}$  and thus we have  $\mathsf{Verify}(\mathsf{crs}, (\mathsf{vk}_0, \{\mathsf{vk}_{i,b}\}_{i \in [\ell], b \in \{0,1\}}, Y), \pi) = \top$  by the correctness of  $\Pi_{\mathsf{NIZK}}$ .

Efficiency. We first observe that the size of the verification circuit for the relation  $\widetilde{\mathcal{R}}$  is  $\mathsf{poly}(\kappa)$ , which is independent of the size of the verification circuit for  $\mathcal{R}$ . This is because  $Z = \mathsf{CS.Aggrgt}(\mathsf{CS.Aggrgt}(\{\mathsf{vk}_{i,K_i}\}_{i\in[\ell]}),Y)$  can be computed in polynomial time in  $\kappa$  and the length  $\ell = \mathsf{poly}(\kappa)$  of K and the running time of  $\mathsf{CS.VrfyOnL}(\mathsf{vk}_0,Z,\sigma)$  does not depend on the length of  $(x,\mathsf{ct})$  (and in particular the complexity of the circuit f) as required in Definition 4.2. Therefore, the size of  $\pi$  is  $\mathsf{poly}(\kappa)$  and independent of |x|,|w|, or |C| even though we do not require any compactness requirement for the underlying NIZK  $\Pi_{\mathsf{NIZK}}$ . Since we assume  $|\mathsf{ct}| = m + \mathsf{poly}(\kappa)$ , the total proof size is  $|w| + \mathsf{poly}(\kappa)$ . We note that this scheme can be directly implemented only when the relation  $\mathcal R$  can be verified in  $\mathsf{NC}^1$ . Otherwise, we have to first expand the witness to make the relation verifiable in  $\mathsf{NC}^1$  similarly to  $[\mathsf{GGH}^+16,\mathsf{KNYY19b}]$ . This is done by considering all values corresponding to all gates when computing the circuit C on input (x,w) to be the new witness and have the new circuit verify the consistency of the values for all gates in C. In this case, the proof size becomes  $|C| + \mathsf{poly}(\kappa)$ .

Since the relation R is well-suited to be proven by the Groth-Sahai proof, a fairly efficient instantiation is possible based on the Groth-Sahai proof. Especially, a proof consists of |C| bits,  $6\ell + 14$  elements of  $\mathbb{G}_1$  and  $6\ell + 24$  elements of

<span id="page-27-1"></span> $\mathbb{G}_2$  when instantiated under the SXDH assumption where  $\ell$  denotes the key length of the SKE scheme. See Appendix B for more details. We also note that if the relation  $\mathcal{R}$  can be verified by a "leveled circuit" [BGI16], we can further reduce the proof size to  $|w| + |C|/\log \kappa + \mathsf{poly}(\kappa)$  which is sublinear in |C| similarly to [KNYY19b]. (See [KNYY19b] for details.)

**Security.** In the following, we prove the soundness and the zero-knowledge property of  $\Pi'_{\mathsf{NIZK}}$ .

<span id="page-27-2"></span>**Theorem 5.1 (Soundness).** The above NIZK scheme  $\Pi'_{NIZK}$  is computationally (adaptive) sound if  $\Pi_{NIZK}$  satisfies extractability and  $\Pi_{CS}$  is unforgeable.

*Proof.* Suppose that there is a PPT adversary A that breaks soundness. Then we construct a PPT adversary B that breaks the unforgeability of  $\Pi_{CS}$  as follows.

 $\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{vk})$ : It generates  $\mathsf{pp}_\mathsf{SKE} \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{SKE}.\mathsf{Setup}(1^\kappa)$  and queries  $f_\mathsf{pp}_\mathsf{SKE}$  to the key generation oracle to obtain  $\mathsf{sk}_f$  where  $f_\mathsf{pp}_\mathsf{SKE}$  is the circuit as defined in the description of the scheme. Then it generates  $\mathsf{crs} \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{Setup}(1^\kappa; r_\mathsf{Setup})$ ,  $\mathsf{runs} \ \mathcal{A}(\mathsf{crs}')$  to obtain  $(x^*, \pi'^* = (\mathsf{ct}, \pi))$  where  $\mathsf{crs}' \coloneqq (\mathsf{crs}, \mathsf{pp}_\mathsf{SKE}, \mathsf{vk}, \mathsf{sk}_f)$ . Then it computes  $(K, \sigma) \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{Extract}(r_\mathsf{Setup}, \pi)$  and outputs  $((K, x^*, \mathsf{ct}), \sigma)$  as a forgery.

This completes the description of  $\mathcal{B}$ . In the following, we show that  $\mathcal{B}$  breaks the unforgeability of  $\Pi_{CS}$ . Let  $\mathsf{VK}_{[0,\ell]} \coloneqq (\mathsf{vk}_0, \{\mathsf{vk}_{i,b}\}_{i \in [\ell], b \in \{0,1\}})$ . Since we assume  $\mathcal{A}$  breaks the soundness of  $\Pi'_{\mathsf{NIZK}}$ ,

$$\Pr[x^* \notin \mathcal{L} \land \mathsf{Verify}(\mathsf{crs}, (\mathsf{VK}_{[0,\ell]}, Y^*), \pi) = \top]$$

is non-negligible where  $Y^* = \mathsf{CS}.\mathsf{Aggrgt}(\{\mathsf{vk}_{\ell+i,y_i^*}\}_{i \in [n+|\mathsf{ct}|]})$  and  $y^* \coloneqq (x^*,\mathsf{ct}) \in \{0,1\}^{n+|\mathsf{ct}|}$ . On the other hand, by the extractability of  $\Pi_{\mathsf{NIZK}}$ ,

$$\Pr[\mathsf{Verify}(\mathsf{crs},(\mathsf{VK}_{[0,\ell]},Y^*),\pi) = \top \wedge ((\mathsf{VK}_{[0,\ell]},Y^*),(K,\sigma)) \notin \widetilde{\mathcal{R}}]$$

is negligible. Therefore

$$\Pr[x^* \notin \mathcal{L} \land \mathsf{Verify}(\mathsf{crs}, (\mathsf{VK}_{[0,\ell]}, Y^*), \pi) = \top \land ((\mathsf{VK}_{[0,\ell]}, Y^*), (K,\sigma)) \in \widetilde{\mathcal{R}}]$$

is non-negligible. Suppose that this event happens. Since we have  $x^* \notin \mathcal{L}$ , we have  $f_{\mathsf{PPSKE}}(K, x^*, \mathsf{ct}) = 0$ . On the other hand,  $((\mathsf{VK}_{[0,\ell]}, Y^*), (K, \sigma)) \in \widetilde{\mathcal{R}}$  implies that we have  $K \in \{0,1\}^\ell \land \mathsf{CS.VrfyOnL}(\mathsf{vk}_0, Z, \sigma) = \top$  where  $Z = \mathsf{CS.Aggrgt}\big(\mathsf{CS.Aggrgt}\big(\mathsf{vk}_{i,K_i}\}_{i \in [\ell]}\big), Y^*\big)$ , which implies  $\mathsf{CS.Vrfy}(\mathsf{vk}, (K, x^*, \mathsf{ct}), \sigma) = \top$ . This means that  $\mathcal{B}$  succeeds in breaking the unforgeability of  $\Pi_{\mathsf{CS}}$ .

<span id="page-27-0"></span>**Theorem 5.2 (Zero-Knowledge).** The above NIZK scheme  $\Pi'_{NIZK}$  is computationally zero-knowledge if  $\Pi_{NIZK}$  is computationally zero-knowledge and  $\Pi_{SKE}$  is one-time secure.

*Proof.* Let  $(S_1, S_2)$  be the simulator for  $\Pi_{NIZK}$ . We describe the simulator  $(S'_1, S'_2)$  for  $\Pi'_{NIZK}$  below.

 $\mathcal{S}_1'(1^\kappa)\text{: It generates }(\mathsf{crs},\tau_\mathsf{V}) \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathcal{S}_1(1^\kappa), \, \mathsf{pp}_\mathsf{SKE} \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{SKE}.\mathsf{Setup}(1^\kappa), \, (\mathsf{vk} = (\mathsf{vk}_0, \{\mathsf{vk}_{i,b}\}_{i\in[\ell+n+|\mathsf{ct}|],b\in\{0,1\}}), \, \mathsf{msk}) \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{CS}.\mathsf{Setup}(1^\kappa, 1^{\ell+n+|\mathsf{ct}|}), \, \mathsf{and} \, \mathsf{sk}_f \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{CS}.\mathsf{KeyGen}(\mathsf{msk}, f_{\mathsf{pp}_\mathsf{SKE}}), \, \mathsf{and} \, \, \mathsf{outputs} \, \, \mathsf{crs}' \coloneqq (\mathsf{crs}, \mathsf{pp}_\mathsf{SKE}, \mathsf{vk}, \mathsf{sk}_f) \, \, \mathsf{and} \, \, \, \, \, \, \, \, \, \, \, \, \, \, \, \, \, \, \,$

This completes the description of the simulator. We prove that proofs simulated by the above simulator are computationally indistinguishable from the honestly generated proofs. To prove this, we consider the following sequence of games between a PPT adversary  $\mathcal A$  and a challenger.

G<sub>0</sub>: In this game, proofs are generated honestly. Namely,

- <span id="page-28-2"></span>1. When  $\mathcal{A}$  queries (x, w), if  $(x, w) \notin \mathcal{R}$ , then the oracle returns  $\bot$ . Otherwise, it picks  $K \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{SKE}.\mathsf{KeyGen}(\mathsf{pp}_{\mathsf{SKE}})$ , computes  $\mathsf{ct} \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{SKE}.\mathsf{Enc}(\mathsf{pp}_{\mathsf{SKE}}, K, w), \sigma \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{CS}.\mathsf{Sign}(\mathsf{sk}_f, (K, x, \mathsf{ct})), Y \coloneqq \mathsf{CS}.\mathsf{Aggrgt}(\{\mathsf{vk}_{\ell+i, y_i}\}_{i \in [n+|\mathsf{ct}|}) \text{ where } y \coloneqq (x, \mathsf{ct}) \in \{0, 1\}^{n+|\mathsf{ct}|}, \text{ and } \pi \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{Prove}(\mathsf{crs}, (\mathsf{vk}_0, \{\mathsf{vk}_{i,b}\}_{i \in [\ell], b \in \{0, 1\}}, Y), (K, \sigma)), \text{ and returns a proof } \pi' \coloneqq (\mathsf{ct}, \pi).$

- G<sub>1</sub>: This game is identical to the previous game except that crs and  $\pi$  are generated differently. Namely, the challenger generates  $(\operatorname{crs}, \tau_{\mathsf{V}}) \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathcal{S}_{1}(1^{\kappa})$  at the beginning of the game, and  $\pi$  is generated as  $\pi \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathcal{S}_{2}(\operatorname{crs}, \tau_{\mathsf{V}}, (\operatorname{vk}_{0}, \{\operatorname{vk}_{i,b}\}_{i \in [\ell], b \in \{0,1\}}, Y))$  for each oracle query.
- G<sub>2</sub>: This game is identical to the previous game except that ct is generated as ct  $\stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow}$  SKE.Enc(pp<sub>SKE</sub>, K,  $0^m$ ) for each oracle query.

Let  $T_i$  be the event that A returns 1 in  $G_i$  for i = 0, 1, 2. It is easy to see that proofs are generated by  $S' = (S'_1, S'_2)$  in  $G_2$ . Thus we have to prove that  $|\Pr[T_0] - \Pr[T_2]|$  is negligible. The following lemmas are straightforward to prove.

**Lemma 5.3.** If  $\Pi_{NIZK}$  satisfies computational zero-knowledge w.r.t. the simulator S, then  $|\Pr[\mathsf{T}_0] - \Pr[\mathsf{T}_1]| = \mathsf{negl}(\kappa)$ .

*Proof.* We observe that every proof  $\pi$  given to  $\mathcal{A}$  is created for a correct statement in both games. Therefore, the indistinguishability of the games can be reduced to the zero-knowledge property of  $\Pi_{NIZK}$ .

**Lemma 5.4.** *If*  $\Pi_{\mathsf{SKE}}$  *is one-time secure, then*  $|\Pr[\mathsf{T}_1] - \Pr[\mathsf{T}_2]| = \mathsf{negl}(\kappa)$ .

*Proof.* Due to the change we introduced in  $G_1$ , the secret key K of SKE that is used to generate ct is not used anywhere else in both games. Moreover, each K is used only once. Therefore, the indistinguishability of these games can be reduced to the one-time security of  $\Pi_{SKE}$ .

This completes the proof of Theorem 5.2.

<span id="page-28-0"></span>Remark 5.5. For instantiating this construction, we need to assume the MDDH assumption in a pairing group and the CDH assumption in a subgroup of  $\mathbb{Z}_p^*$ . The latter assumption is needed for constructing an SKE scheme with the required properties, which we do not know if we can construct in a pairing group. In more detail, for constructing such an SKE scheme, we need to assume the CDH assumption in a group in which a multiple product can be computed in  $\mathbf{NC}^1$ . (See [KNYY19b] for details.) While that is known to be possible in a subgroup of  $\mathbb{Z}_p^*$  [BCH86], we are unaware of a similar result for a pairing group. Thus, we assume the CDH assumption in a subgroup of  $\mathbb{Z}_p^*$  as an additional assumption.

#### <span id="page-28-1"></span>5.2 Perfect Zero-Knowledge Variant

Here, we give a variant of our NIZK given in Section 5.1 that satisfies perfect zero-knowledge. The construction supports relations that are verified in  $\mathbf{NC}^1$  and the proof size is  $|w| \cdot \mathsf{poly}(\kappa)$ . This is the first pairing-based construction of a NIZK with perfect zero-knowledge for a non-trivial calss of languages whose proof size is independent of the size of the circuit that verifies the corresponding relation.

**Dual-Mode NIZK.** Before stating our construction, we define the notion of dual-mode NIZK, which is used as a building block of our construction.

**Definition 5.6 (Dual-Mode NIZK).** A dual-mode NIZK for a language  $\mathcal{L}$  corresponding to a relation  $\mathcal{R}$  consists of 6 *PPT algorithms*  $\Pi^{dm}_{NIZK} = (SimSetup, ExtSetup, Prove, Verify, Sim, Extract).$ 

- <span id="page-29-1"></span>SimSetup( $1^{\kappa}$ )  $\rightarrow$  (crs,  $\tau_{Sim}$ ): The setup algorithm in the hiding mode takes as input the security parameter  $1^{\kappa}$  and outputs a common reference string crs and a simulation trapdoor  $\tau_{Sim}$ .
- $\mathsf{ExtSetup}(1^\kappa) \to (\mathsf{crs}, \tau_\mathsf{Extract})$ : The setup algorithm in the binding mode takes as input the security parameter  $1^\kappa$  and outputs a common reference string  $\mathsf{crs}$  and a extraction trapdoor  $\tau_\mathsf{Extract}$ .
- Prove(crs, x, w)  $\to \pi$ : The prover's algorithm takes as input a common reference string crs, a statement x, and a witness w and outputs a proof  $\pi$ .
- Verify(crs,  $x, \pi$ )  $\to \top$  or  $\bot$ : The verifier's algorithm takes as input a common reference string, a statement x, and a proof  $\pi$  and outputs  $\top$  to indicate acceptance of the proof and  $\bot$  otherwise.
- Sim:  $(\tau_{Sim}, x) \to \pi$ : The simulation algorithm takes as input a simulation trapdoor  $\tau_{Sim}$  and a statement x and outputs a simulated proof  $\pi$ .
- $\mathsf{Extract}(\tau_{\mathsf{Extract}}, \pi) \to w$ : The extraction algorithm takes as input an extraction trapdoor  $\tau_{\mathsf{Extract}}$  and a proof  $\pi$  and outputs an extracted witness w.

A NIZK proof  $\Pi_{\text{NIZK}}$  must satisfy the following requirements for all  $\kappa \in \mathbb{N}$ , where the probabilities are taken over the random choice of the algorithms.

 $\underline{\textit{Completeness.}}_{\textit{then we have}} \textit{For all pairs } (x,w) \in \mathcal{R}, \textit{if we run } (\mathsf{crs},\tau_{\mathsf{Sim}}) \overset{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{Sim} \mathsf{Setup}(1^{\kappa}) \textit{ (or } (\mathsf{crs},\tau_{\mathsf{Extract}}) \overset{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{Ext} \mathsf{Setup}(1^{\kappa})),$ 

$$\Pr[\pi \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{Prove}(\mathsf{crs},x,w) : \mathsf{Verify}(\mathsf{crs},x,\pi) = \top] = 1.$$

<u>Perfect Simulation in Hiding Mode.</u> For all unbounded-time adversaries A, if we run  $(crs, \tau_{Sim}) \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} SimSetup(1^{\kappa})$ , then we have

$$\left|\Pr[\mathcal{A}^{\mathcal{O}_0(\mathsf{crs},\cdot,\cdot)}(1^\kappa,\mathsf{crs})=1]-\Pr[\mathcal{A}^{\mathcal{O}_1(\tau_{\mathsf{Sim}},\cdot,\cdot)}(1^\kappa,\mathsf{crs})=1]\right|=0,$$

where  $\mathcal{O}_0(\mathsf{crs},x,w)$  outputs  $\mathsf{Prove}(\mathsf{crs},x,w)$  if  $(x,w) \in \mathcal{R}$  and  $\bot$  otherwise, and  $\mathcal{O}_1(\tau_{\mathsf{Sim}},x,w)$  outputs  $\mathsf{Sim}(\tau_{\mathsf{Sim}},x)$  if  $(x,w) \in \mathcal{R}$  and  $\bot$  otherwise.

**Perfect Extraction in Binding Mode.** If we run  $(crs, \tau_{Extract}) \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} ExtSetup(1^{\kappa})$ , then for all x and  $\pi$  such that  $Verify(crs, x, \pi) = T$ , we have

$$\Pr[w \xleftarrow{\hspace{0.1em}^{\$}} \mathsf{Extract}(\tau_{\mathsf{Extract}}, \pi) : (x, w) \in \mathcal{R}] = 1.$$

(Non-Uniform) Mode Indistinguishability. For all non-uniform polynomial-time adversaries A,  $^{10}$  we have

$$\left|\Pr[(\mathsf{crs},\tau_\mathsf{Sim}) \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{Sim}\mathsf{Setup}(1^\kappa): \mathcal{A}(\mathsf{crs}) = 1] - \Pr[(\mathsf{crs},\tau_\mathsf{Extract}) \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{Extract}(1^\kappa): \mathcal{A}(\mathsf{crs}) = 1] \right| = \mathsf{negl}(\kappa),$$

**Lemma 5.7** ([GOS12, GS12]). There exists a dual-mode NIZK under the DLIN or SXDH assumption.

**Construction.** Now, we describe our construction of a NIZK with perfect zero-knowledge. Let  $\mathcal{L}$  be an **NP** language defined by a relation  $\mathcal{R} \subseteq \{0,1\}^* \times \{0,1\}^*$ . Let  $n(\kappa)$  and  $m(\kappa)$  be any fixed polynomials. Let C be a circuit that computes the relation  $\mathcal{R}$  on  $\{0,1\}^n \times \{0,1\}^m$ , i.e., for  $(x,w) \in \{0,1\}^n \times \{0,1\}^m$ , we have C(x,w) = 1 if and only if  $(x,w) \in \mathcal{R}$ .

The construction will be given by combining following ingredients.

• A constrained signature scheme (CS.Setup, CS.KeyGen, CS.Sign, CS.Vrfy, CS.Aggrgt, CS.VrfyOnL) we constructed in Section 4. The scheme should support the circuit *C*.

<span id="page-29-0"></span><sup>&</sup>lt;sup>10</sup>We need security against non-uniform adversaries in the proof of soundness in our NIZK with perfect zero-knowledge.

- (Not necessarily compact) dual-mode NIZK scheme  $\Pi^{dm}_{NIZK} = (SimSetup, ExtSetup, Prove, Verify, Sim, Extract)$  for the language  $\widetilde{\mathcal{L}}$  corresponding to the relation  $\widetilde{\mathcal{R}}$  defined below:  $((vk_0, \{vk_{i,b}\}_{i\in[m],b\in\{0.1\}}, X), (w, \sigma)) \in \widetilde{\mathcal{R}}$  if and only if the followings are satisfied:
  - 1.  $w \in \{0,1\}^m$ ,
  - 2.  $\mathsf{CS.VrfyOnL}(\mathsf{vk}_0, Z, \sigma) = \top \text{ where } Z = \mathsf{CS.Aggrgt}(X, \mathsf{CS.Aggrgt}(\{\mathsf{vk}_{i.w.}\}_{i \in [m]}))$

Our NIZK with perfect zero-knowledge  $\Pi^{pzk}_{\mathsf{NIZK}} = (\mathsf{Setup}^{\mathsf{pzk}}, \mathsf{Prove}^{\mathsf{pzk}}, \mathsf{Verify}^{\mathsf{pzk}})$  is described as follows.

## Setup<sup>pzk</sup> $(1^{\kappa})$ :

- 1. Generate  $(crs, \tau_S) \stackrel{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{SimSetup}(1^{\kappa})$ .
- 2. Generate  $(\mathsf{vk} = (\mathsf{vk}_0, \{\mathsf{vk}_{i,b}\}_{i \in [n+m], b \in \{0,1\}}), \mathsf{msk}) \overset{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{CS}.\mathsf{Setup}(1^\kappa, 1^{n+m}).$
- 3. Generate  $\mathsf{sk}_C \xleftarrow{\$} \mathsf{CS}.\mathsf{KeyGen}(\mathsf{msk},C)$ .
- 4. Output  $crs' = (crs, vk, sk_C)$ .

## Prove $^{pzk}(crs', x, w)$ :

- 1. Abort if  $\mathcal{R}(x, w) = 0$ . Otherwise, do the following.
- 2. Parse  $crs' \to (crs, vk = (vk_0, \{vk_{i,b}\}_{i \in [n+m], b \in \{0,1\}}), sk_C)$ .
- 3. Compute  $\sigma \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{CS}.\mathsf{Sign}(\mathsf{sk}_C,(x,w))$ .
- $\text{4. Compute } X \coloneqq \mathsf{CS}.\mathsf{Aggrgt}(\{\mathsf{vk}_{i,x_i}\}_{i \in [n]}).$
- 5. Compute  $\pi \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{Prove}((\mathsf{vk}_0, \{\mathsf{vk}_{n+i,b}\}_{i \in [m], b \in \{0,1\}}, X), (w, \sigma)).$
- 6. Output  $\pi$ .

## $\mathsf{Verify}^{\mathsf{pzk}}(\mathsf{crs'},x,\pi) \textbf{:}$

- 1. Parse  $\operatorname{crs}' \to (\operatorname{crs}, \operatorname{vk} = (\operatorname{vk}_0, \{\operatorname{vk}_{i,b}\}_{i \in [n+m], b \in \{0,1\}}).$
- $\text{2. Compute } X \coloneqq \mathsf{CS.Aggrgt}(\{\mathsf{vk}_{i,x_i}\}_{i \in [n]}).$
- 3. Output  $\top$  if  $\mathsf{Verify}((\mathsf{vk}_0, \{\mathsf{vk}_{n+i,b}\}_{i \in [m], b \in \{0,1\}}, X), \pi) = \top$  and otherwise  $\bot$ .

**Correctness.** Suppose that  $\pi$  is an honestly generated proof on  $(x,w) \in \mathcal{R}$ . Then we have  $\pi \overset{\$}{\leftarrow} \operatorname{Prove}((\mathsf{vk}_0, \{\mathsf{vk}_{n+i,b}\}_{i \in [m], b \in \{0,1\}}, X), (w,\sigma))$  where  $\sigma \overset{\$}{\leftarrow} \operatorname{CS.Sign}(\mathsf{sk}_C, (x,w))$  and  $X = \operatorname{CS.Aggrgt}(\{\mathsf{vk}_{i,x_i}\}_{i \in [n]})$ . By the correctness of  $\Pi_{\mathsf{CS}}$ , we have  $\operatorname{CS.Vrfy}(\mathsf{vk}, (x,w), \sigma) = \top$ , which is equivalent to

$$\mathsf{CS.VrfyOnL}(\mathsf{vk}_0, Z, \sigma) = \top \quad \text{where} \quad Z = \mathsf{CS.Aggrgt}\big(X, \mathsf{CS.Aggrgt}(\{\mathsf{vk}_{n+i, w_i}\}_{i \in [m]})\big).$$

Therefore we have  $((\mathsf{vk}_0, \{\mathsf{vk}_{n+i,b}\}_{i\in[m],b\in\{0,1\}}, X), (w,\sigma)) \in \widetilde{\mathcal{R}}$  and thus  $\mathsf{Verify}((\mathsf{vk}_0, \{\mathsf{vk}_{n+i,b}\}_{i\in[m],b\in\{0,1\}}, Z), \pi) = \mathsf{vk}$  by the correctness of  $\Pi^{\mathsf{dm}}_{\mathsf{NIZK}}$ .

Efficiency. Since the relation  $\widetilde{\mathcal{R}}$  which is proven by using  $\Pi_{\mathsf{NIZK}}$  can be verified by a  $\mathsf{poly}(\kappa,|w|)$ -sized circuit independently of x or |C|, the size of  $\pi$  is  $\mathsf{poly}(\kappa,|w|)$  and independent of x or |C|. Similarly to the scheme in Section 5.1, since the relation  $\widetilde{\mathcal{R}}$  is well-suited to be proven by the Groth-Sahai proof, if we instantiate the scheme based on the Groth-Sahai proof, then the protocol is quite efficient, and the proof size can be made linear in the witness size, i.e.,  $|\pi| = |w| \cdot \mathsf{poly}(\kappa)$ . More precisely, by a similar calculation to the one done in Appendix B, a proof consists of 6|w| + 14 elements of  $\mathbb{G}_1$  and 6|w| + 24 elements of  $\mathbb{G}_2$  when instantiated under the SXDH assumption. We note that it is not useful to extend the scheme to support all NP languages by expanding the witness since this would result in a scheme with proof size  $|C| \cdot \mathsf{poly}(\kappa)$  which is no better than the Groth-Ostrovsky-Sahai NIZK.

<span id="page-30-0"></span>**Security.** In the following, we prove the soundness and the perfect zero-knowledge property of  $\Pi_{\text{NIZK}}^{\text{pzk}}$ .

<span id="page-31-1"></span>**Theorem 5.8 (Soundness).** The above NIZK scheme  $\Pi_{NIZK}^{pzk}$  satisfies computational (non-adaptive) soundness if  $\Pi_{NIZK}^{dm}$  satisfies perfect extractability in the binding mode, non-uniform mode-indistinguishability, and  $\Pi_{CS}$  is non-uniformly unforgeable.

*Proof.* Suppose that  $\Pi^{\mathsf{pzk}}_{\mathsf{NIZK}}$  does not satisfy computational non-adaptive soundness. Then there exists  $x^* \notin \mathcal{L}$  and a non-uniform polynomial-time adversary  $\mathcal{A}$  such that if we generate  $\mathsf{crs}' = (\mathsf{crs}, \mathsf{vk}, \mathsf{sk}_C) \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{Setup}^{\mathsf{pzk}}(1^\kappa)$  and  $\pi^* \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathcal{A}(\mathsf{crs}')$ , then  $\Pr[\mathsf{Verify}^{\mathsf{pzk}}(\mathsf{crs}', x^*, \pi^*) = \top]$  is non-negligible. By the non-uniform mode-indistinguishability of  $\Pi^{\mathsf{dm}}_{\mathsf{NIZK}}$ , if we generate  $\mathsf{crs}$  by  $\mathsf{ExtSetup}(1^\kappa)$  instead of  $\mathsf{SimSetup}(1^\kappa)$ ,  $\Pr[\mathsf{Verify}^{\mathsf{pzk}}(\mathsf{crs}', x^*, \pi^*) = \top]$  is still non-negligible. Then we construct a PPT adversary  $\mathcal{B}$  that breaks the unforgeability of  $\Pi_{\mathsf{CS}}$  as follows.

 $\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{vk})$ : It queries C to the key generation oracle to obtain  $\mathsf{sk}_C$ . Then it generates  $(\mathsf{crs}, \tau_\mathsf{Ext}) \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{ExtSetup}(1^\kappa)$ , runs  $\mathcal{A}(\mathsf{crs}')$  to obtain  $\pi^*$  where  $\mathsf{crs}' \coloneqq (\mathsf{crs}, \mathsf{vk}, \mathsf{sk}_C)$ . Then it computes  $(w, \sigma) \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{Extract}(\tau_\mathsf{Ext}, \pi^*)$  and outputs  $((x^*, w), \sigma)$  as a forgery.

This completes the description of  $\mathcal{B}$ . In the following, we show that  $\mathcal{B}$  breaks the unforgeability of  $\Pi_{\mathsf{NIZK}}^{\mathsf{dm}}$  in the binding mode, we have  $((\mathsf{vk}_0, \{\mathsf{vk}_{i,b}\}_{i \in [m], b \in \{0,1\}}, X), (w,\sigma)) \in \mathcal{R}$  whenever Verify<sup>pzk</sup>(crs',  $x^*$ ,  $\pi^*$ ) =  $\top$ , which happens with non-negligible probability. In this case, we have  $w \in \{0,1\}^m \land \mathsf{CS.VrfyOnL}(\mathsf{vk}_0, Z, \sigma) = \top$  where  $Z = \mathsf{CS.Aggrgt}(X, \mathsf{CS.Aggrgt}(\{\mathsf{vk}_{n+i,w_i}\}_{i \in [m]}))$ , which implies  $\mathsf{CS.Vrfy(vk}, (x^*, w), \sigma) = \top$ . On the other hand, we never have  $(x^*, w) \in \mathcal{R}$  for any  $w \in \{0,1\}^m$  since  $x^* \notin \mathcal{L}$  and thus we have  $C(x^*, w) = 0$ . This means that  $\mathcal{B}$  succeeds in breaking the unforgeability of  $\Pi_{\mathsf{CS}}$ . This completes the proof of Theorem 5.8.

Remark 5.9. In the above proof, we assumed non-uniform security for underlying primitives since we rely on non-uniform reduction algorithms into which  $x^*$  is hardwired.

Remark 5.10. It is not clear how to extend the above proof to the case of the adaptive soundness. The problem is that the adversary's advantage may non-negligibly differs when changing crs from hiding mode to binding mode even under the mode-indistinguishability since the winning condition of the adaptive soundness is not efficiently verifiable. Indeed, Pass [Pas13] ruled out a black-box reduction from the adaptive soundness of a NIZK with statistical (and thus also perfect) zero-knowledge to any falsifiable assumption. On the other hand, we can prove a relaxed variant of the adaptive soundness called the *adaptive culpable soundness* similarly to [GOS12]. Intuitively, the adaptive culpable soundness ensures the target statement  $x^*$  as long as it provides a witness for  $x^* \notin \mathcal{L}$ . With this relaxed definition, the winning condition is efficiently verifiable, and the above problem does not occur. In this sense, our scheme satisfies essentially the same level of soundness under the same assumption as that of Groth-Ostrovsky-Sahai proof [GOS12].

<span id="page-31-0"></span>**Theorem 5.11 (Perfect Zero-Knowledge).** The above NIZK scheme  $\Pi^{\mathsf{pzk}}_{\mathsf{NIZK}}$  is perfectly zero-knowledge if  $\Pi^{\mathsf{dm}}_{\mathsf{NIZK}}$  is perfectly zero-knowledge in the hiding mode.

*Proof.* We describe the simulator  $(\mathcal{S}_1',\mathcal{S}_2')$  for  $\Pi_{\text{NIZK}}^{\text{pzk}}$  below.

 $\mathcal{S}_1'(1^\kappa) \text{: It generates } (\mathsf{crs}, \tau_{\mathsf{Sim}}) \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{Sim} \mathsf{Setup}(1^\kappa), (\mathsf{vk} = (\mathsf{vk}_0, \{\mathsf{vk}_{i,b}\}_{i \in [n+m], b \in \{0,1\}}), \mathsf{msk}) \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{CS}. \mathsf{Setup}(1^\kappa, 1^{n+m}), \\ \mathsf{and} \ \mathsf{sk}_C \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{CS}. \mathsf{KeyGen}(\mathsf{msk}, C), \ \mathsf{and} \ \mathsf{outputs} \ \mathsf{crs}' \coloneqq (\mathsf{crs}, \mathsf{vk}, \mathsf{sk}_C) \ \mathsf{and} \ \tau_\mathsf{V}' \coloneqq \tau_{\mathsf{Sim}}.$ 

 $\mathcal{S}_2'(\mathsf{crs}' \coloneqq (\mathsf{crs}, \mathsf{vk}, \mathsf{sk}_C), \tau_\mathsf{V}' = \tau_\mathsf{Sim}, x) \text{:} \text{ It first computes } X \coloneqq \mathsf{CS}.\mathsf{Aggrgt}(\{\mathsf{vk}_{i,x_i}\}_{i \in [n]}). \text{ It then computes } \pi \xleftarrow{\$} \mathsf{Sim}(\tau_\mathsf{Sim}, (\mathsf{vk}_0, \{\mathsf{vk}_{n+i,b}\}_{i \in [m], b \in \{0,1\}}, X)) \text{ and outputs } \pi.$ 

This completes the description of the simulator. Since we have  $(\mathsf{vk}_0, \{\mathsf{vk}_{n+i,b}\}_{i\in[m],b\in\{0,1\}}, X) \in \widetilde{\mathcal{L}}$  whenever  $x \in \mathcal{L}$  by the correctness of  $\Pi_{\mathsf{CS}}$ , the proof generated by the above simulator is perfectly indistinguishable from the real one by the perfect zero-knowledgeness of  $\Pi^{\mathsf{dm}}_{\mathsf{NIZK}}$  in the hiding mode. This completes the proof of Theorem 5.11.

#### <span id="page-32-4"></span>6.1 UC Framework

Here, we briefly recall the UC framework. We refer to [Can01] for the full descriptions. The following description is taken verbatim from [KNYY19b]. Readers familiar with the UC framework can safely skip this section.

The UC framework. The UC-security is formalized by indistinguishability of real and ideal worlds. In the real world, parties  $P_1,...,P_N$  execute a protocol  $\Pi$ , and an adversary  $\mathcal A$  may corrupt some of them. We say that  $\mathcal A$  is adaptive if it adaptively decides which party to corrupt. In the ideal world, dummy parties  $\tilde{P}_1,...,\tilde{P}_N$  execute an ideal functionality  $\mathcal F$ , and a simulator  $\mathcal S$  may corrupt some of them. In addition to (dummy) parties and an adversary/simulator, we consider another entity  $\mathcal Z$  which tries to distinguish these two worlds. In the real (resp. ideal) world, an environment  $\mathcal Z$ , which takes the security parameter  $1^\kappa$  and an auxiliary input z as input, can arbitrarily interact with  $\mathcal A$  (resp.  $\mathcal S$ ), and it can also feed any input to any uncorrupted party (resp. dummy party) to let it honestly run the protocol  $\Pi$  (resp. the ideal functionality) on the input and report the output to  $\mathcal Z$ . Finally,  $\mathcal Z$  outputs a bit as its guess of in which world it is. **Definition 6.1.** Let  $\mathcal{X} = \{X(1^{\kappa}, z)\}_{\kappa \in \mathbb{N}, z \in \{0,1\}^*}$  and  $\mathcal{Y} = \{Y(1^{\kappa}, z)\}_{\kappa \in \mathbb{N}, z \in \{0,1\}^*}$  be two distribution ensembles over  $\{0,1\}$ . Remark 6.4 (Static/Adaptive Security and Erasure). In some works of the UC-security, one considers a weaker security called the *static* security, which only considers adversaries that declare which party to corrupt at the beginning of the experiment. Also, one often considers the adaptive UC-security in the setting where parties can securely erase their internal state information so that an adversary that later corrupts the party cannot see the erased information. In this paper, we consider the strongest UC-security against *adaptive* adversaries *without assuming secure erasures*.

Then it computes  $\sigma_{\mathsf{OTS}} \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{OTS.Sign}(\mathsf{sigk}_{\mathsf{OTS}}, (x, \pi_{\mathsf{NIZK}}, \mathsf{vk}_{\mathsf{OTS}})))$  and returns (proof, sid,  $x, (\pi_{\mathsf{NIZK}}, \mathsf{vk}_{\mathsf{OTS}}, \sigma_{\mathsf{OTS}}))$  to  $\mathcal{F}^{\mathcal{R}}_{\mathsf{NIZK}}$ .  $\mathcal{S}_{\mathsf{Sim}}$  stores all randomness used in the above simulation (i.e., randomness used in OTS.KeyGen and OTS.Sign) along with the corresponding party  $P_i$ , session ID sid, the statement x, and the proof  $\pi = (\pi_{\mathsf{NIZK}}, \mathsf{vk}_{\mathsf{OTS}}, \sigma_{\mathsf{OTS}})$ .
- When the adversary  $\mathcal A$  corrupts a party  $P_i$ , it corrupts  $\tilde P_i$  who has generated k proofs  $\pi_1,...,\pi_k$ . Then it knows from the internal state of  $\tilde P_j$  that  $\pi_j$  was generated on an input  $(x_j,w_j)$  for each  $j\in[k]$ . For each  $j\in[k]$ ,  $\mathcal S_{\mathsf{Sim}}$  computes  $\sigma_{\mathsf{CS},j} \overset{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{CS.Sign}(\mathsf{sk}_C,(x_j,w_j))$ . Now,  $\mathcal S_{\mathsf{Sim}}$  knows the all internal information that is supposed to be held by  $P_i$  (i.e.,  $w_j,\sigma_{\mathsf{CS},j}$  and all randomness used in OTS.KeyGen, OTS.Sign, and CS.Sign for generating  $\pi_j$  and  $\sigma_{\mathsf{CS},j}$ ). It uses this information to simulate the interaction between  $\mathcal A$  and the corrupted party  $P_i$ .

**Lemma 6.8.** We have  $IDEAL_{\mathcal{F}^{\mathcal{R}}_{NIZK}, \mathcal{S}_{Ext}, \mathcal{Z}} \approx IDEAL_{\mathcal{F}^{\mathcal{R}}_{NIZK}, \mathcal{S}_{Sim}, \mathcal{Z}}$ .

*Proof.* The only difference between simulations by  $\mathcal{S}_{\mathsf{Ext}}$  and  $\mathcal{S}_{\mathsf{Sim}}$  is that  $\mathcal{S}_{\mathsf{Ext}}$  generates  $\pi_{\mathsf{NIZK}}$  through the ideal functionality  $\mathcal{F}^{\widetilde{\mathcal{R}}}_{\mathsf{NIZK}}$  as specified in the proving algorithm of  $\Pi_{\mathsf{UCNIZK}}$  whereas  $\mathcal{S}_{\mathsf{Sim}}$  generates  $\pi_{\mathsf{NIZK}}$  by sending (prove, sid,  $(\mathsf{vk}_0, \{\mathsf{vk}_{n+i,b}\}_{i\in[m],b\in\{0,1\}}, X, \mathsf{vk}_{\mathsf{OTS}})$ ) to the adversary  $\mathcal{A}$ . They are exactly identical from the view of the environment since the proving functionality of  $\mathcal{F}^{\widetilde{\mathcal{R}}}_{\mathsf{NIZK}}$  also generates  $\pi_{\mathsf{NIZK}}$  in the latter way as is seen in the definition of  $\mathcal{F}^{\widetilde{\mathcal{R}}}_{\mathsf{NIZK}}$ .

By combining the above lemmas, we have  $\text{REAL}_{\Pi_{\text{UCNIZK}},\mathcal{A},\mathcal{Z}} \approx \text{IDEAL}_{\mathcal{F}_{\text{NIZK}}^{\mathcal{R}},\mathcal{S}_{\text{Sim}},\mathcal{Z}}$ . Here, we notice that  $\mathcal{S}_{\text{Sim}}$  no longer uses any extended capability, and it is a legitimate simulator in the ideal world. This concludes the proof of Theorem 6.5

# <span id="page-41-10"></span>**A Homomorphic Signature from CS**

Here, we give a construction of homomorphic signature (HomSig) scheme from CS. To do so, we apply the CS-to-HomSig conversion proposed by Tsabary [\[Tsa17\]](#page-41-0). Our version of the conversion here is slightly different from hers in that we sign on *circuits* rather than signing on *messages* (or data). We provide discussion on this difference in Remark [A.4.](#page-42-0) We note that from a theoretical stand point, the two formalizations are equivalent (up to some small differences) since one can always view circuits and messages interchangeably using universal circuits [\[Val76,](#page-41-8) [CH85\]](#page-38-15).

## <span id="page-41-11"></span>**A.1 Definition**

In the following, we only define single-data HomSig for simplicity. The definition for the multi-data version can be found in [\[GVW15,](#page-39-15) [KNYY19a\]](#page-40-5).

**Syntax.** Let {{0*,* 1} *`*(*κ*)}*κ*∈<sup>N</sup> be a family of message spaces. Let {C*κ*}*κ*∈<sup>N</sup> be a family of circuits, where C*<sup>κ</sup>* is a set of polynomial sized circuits with domain {0*,* 1} *`*(*κ*) and range {0*,* 1}.[12](#page-41-9) Below, we omit the subscripts for simplicity.

**Definition A.1 (Homomorphic Signatures).** *A homomorphic signature (HomSig) scheme* ΠHS *with message space* {0*,* 1} *` for the circuit class* C *is defined by the following five algorithms:*

HS*.*KeyGen(1*<sup>κ</sup> ,* 1 *`* ) → (pk*,*sk)*: The key generation algorithm takes as input the security parameter* 1 *<sup>κ</sup> and the message length* 1 *` and outputs a public verification key* pk *and a signing key* sk*.*

<span id="page-41-9"></span><sup>12</sup> We can consider more general message and circuit classes, however, we consider this simplistic version to match the CS definition we provided.

- $\mathsf{HS.Sign}(\mathsf{sk},C) \to \sigma$ : The signing algorithm takes as input a signing key  $\mathsf{sk}$  and a circuit  $C \in \mathcal{C}$ , and outputs a signature  $\sigma$ .
- HS.Eval(pk, C,  $\mathbf{x}$ ,  $\boldsymbol{\sigma}$ )  $\to \sigma$ : The signature-evaluation algorithm takes as input a verification key pk, a circuit C:  $\{0,1\}^\ell \to \{0,1\}$  in C, messages  $\mathbf{x} \in \{0,1\}^\ell$ , and a signature  $\boldsymbol{\sigma}$  and outputs an evaluated signature  $\boldsymbol{\sigma}$ .

 $\mathsf{HS.Verify}(\mathsf{pk},\mathbf{x},z,\sigma) \to \top \ or \ \bot : \ \mathit{The verification algorithm splits into a pair of algorithms} \ (\mathsf{HS.VerifyOffL},\mathsf{HS.VerifyOnL}) :$ 

- $HS.VerifyOffL(pk, \mathbf{x}) \to pk_{\mathbf{x}}$ : The offline verification algorithm takes as input a verification key  $pk_{\mathbf{x}}$  messages  $\mathbf{x} \in \{0,1\}^{\ell}$ , and outputs an evaluated verification key  $pk_{\mathbf{x}}$ .
- HS.VerifyOnL(pk',  $z, \sigma$ )  $\to \top$  or  $\bot$ : The online verification algorithm takes as input an (evaluated) verification key pk', a message  $z \in \{0, 1\}$ , and a evaluated signature  $\sigma$ , and outputs  $\top$  if the signature is valid and outputs  $\bot$  otherwise.

**Correctness.** We define correctness for evaluated messages.

**Definition A.2 (Correctness).** We say a homomorphic signature scheme  $\Pi_{\mathsf{HS}}$  is correct, if for all  $\kappa \in \mathbb{N}$ ,  $\ell \in \mathsf{poly}(\kappa)$ , messages  $\mathbf{x} = (x_1, \cdots, x_\ell) \in \{0, 1\}^\ell$ , circuits  $C \in \mathcal{C}$ , and  $(\mathsf{pk}, \mathsf{sk}) \in \mathsf{HS}.\mathsf{KeyGen}(1^\kappa, 1^\ell)$  we have

$$\Pr[\mathsf{HS}.\mathsf{Sign}(\mathsf{sk},C) \to \pmb{\sigma}; \; \mathsf{HS}.\mathsf{Eval}(\mathsf{pk},C,\mathbf{x},\pmb{\sigma}) \to \sigma: \; \mathsf{HS}.\mathsf{Verify}(\mathsf{pk},\mathbf{x},C(\mathbf{x}),\sigma) = \top] = 1,$$

where the probability is taken over the randomness used by all of the algorithms.

<u>Unforgeability.</u> We now define unforgeability for a HomSig scheme. The security notion is defined formally by the following game between a challenger and an adversary A.

**Setup**: At the beginning of the game, the adversary  $\mathcal{A}$  is given  $1^{\kappa}$  as input and sends  $1^{\ell}$  to the challenger. Then the challenger generates a signing-verification key pair (pk, sk)  $\stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow}$  HS.KeyGen $(1^{\kappa}, 1^{\ell})$  and gives pk to  $\mathcal{A}$ .

**Signing Query**: The adversary  $\mathcal{A}$  submits a circuit  $C \in \mathcal{C}$  to be signed. The challenger responds by creating a signature  $\sigma \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{HS.Sign}(\mathsf{sk},C)$  and sends  $\sigma$  to  $\mathcal{A}$ . Here,  $\mathcal{A}$  can query a circuit only once.

Forgery: Then the adversary  $\mathcal{A}$  outputs messages  $\mathbf{x}^*$ , a message  $z^* \in \{0,1\}$ , and a signature  $\sigma^*$  as the forgery. We say that  $\mathcal{A}$  wins the game if:

- 1.  $\mathbf{x}^* \in \{0,1\}^{\ell}$ ; and
- 2.  $C(\mathbf{x}^*) \neq z^*$ ; and
- 3. HS.Verify(pk,  $\mathbf{x}^*, z^*, \sigma^*$ ) =  $\top$ .

The advantage of an adversary winning the above game is defined by  $\Pr[\mathcal{A} \text{ wins}]$ , where the probability is taken over the randomness used by the challenger and the adversary.

**Definition A.3** (Unforgeability). A homomorphic signature scheme  $\Pi_{HS}$  is said to satisfy unforgeability if for any PPT adversary A the advantage  $\Pr[A \text{ wins}]$  of the above game is negligible.

<span id="page-42-0"></span>Compactness and Offline/Online Efficiency. We say a HomSig scheme  $\Pi_{HS}$  is compact if there exists a universal polynomial  $poly(\cdot)$  such that the evaluated signature  $\sigma$  (output by HS.Eval) satisfies  $|\sigma| \leq poly(\kappa)$ . Moreover, we say  $\Pi_{HS}$  is online-offline efficient if there exists a universal polynomial  $poly(\cdot)$  such that the running time of HS.VerifyOnL is  $poly(\kappa)$ . Here, we allow the running time of the offline phase, i.e., running HS.VerifyOffL, to depend on the size of the messages  $|\mathbf{x}|$ . In particular, once the messages  $\mathbf{x}$  is fixed and we preprocess the evaluation key  $pk_{\mathbf{x}} \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} HS.VerifyOffL(pk, \mathbf{x})$ , then we can verify an evaluated signature with respect to the messages  $\mathbf{x}$  and any circuit C in time independent of |C| or  $|\mathbf{x}|$ .

<span id="page-43-0"></span>Remark A.4 (Comparison with HS signing on messages). It is common in the literatures to define the signing algorithm HS.Sign with respect to messages  $\mathbf x$  rather than a circuit C, e.g., [BF11, GVW15]. In many natural applications, e.g., a client wants to outsource a computation of many algorithms on a fixed dataset, it may be more convenient to work with our formalization of HS. However, in either cases, we note that the two formalizations are equivalent from a theoretical point view in that both definitions are interchangeable using universal circuits. Namely, given messages  $\mathbf x$ , we can view it as a universal circuit  $U(\cdot, \mathbf x)$  which takes as input a circuit C (represented as say bit strings) output  $C(\mathbf x)$ . Since Cook and Hoover [CH85] showed that  $U(\cdot, \mathbf x)$  can be only a constant times deeper than that of C, this conversion can be made without incurring any strong restrictions on the circuit class supported by the underlying HS. A shortcoming of this approach is that the signature size for the message  $\mathbf x$  depends on the description size of C since the input-length of the universal circuit  $U(\cdot, \mathbf x)$  should be taken as large as the description size of C. On the other hand, compactness for an evaluated signature is preserved.

## <span id="page-43-1"></span>A.2 Construction of Homomorphic Signatures from CS

We construct a HomSig scheme with message space  $\{0,1\}^\ell$  for circuit class  $\mathcal{C}=\{C:\{0,1\}^\ell\to\{0,1\}\}$  from a CS scheme. Concretely, let  $\Pi_{\text{CS}}=(\text{CS.Setup},\text{CS.KeyGen},\text{CS.Sign},\text{CS.Vrfy})$  be a CS scheme with message space  $\{0,1\}^{\ell+1}$  for a circuit class  $\tilde{\mathcal{C}}$  defined as

$$\tilde{\mathcal{C}} = \{ \tilde{C} : \{0,1\}^{\ell+1} \to \{0,1\} \mid \text{for all } C \in \mathcal{C}, (\mathbf{x},z) \in \{0,1\}^{\ell} \times \{0,1\}, \text{we have } \tilde{C}(\mathbf{x},z) = \left(C(\mathbf{x}) \stackrel{?}{=} z\right) \}.$$

More specifically,  $\tilde{\mathcal{C}}$  is a set of circuits with input length  $\ell+1$  such that a circuit  $\tilde{C}\in\tilde{\mathcal{C}}$  on input  $(\mathbf{x},z)\in\{0,1\}^\ell\times\{0,1\}$  outputs 1 if and only if the associated circuit  $C\in\mathcal{C}$  outputs z on input  $\mathbf{x}$ . Since  $(z'\stackrel{?}{=}z)$  can be expressed by a constant-sized circuit, every circuit in  $\tilde{\mathcal{C}}$  has depth and size bounded by d+O(1) and s+O(1) respectively, where d and s denotes the maximal depth and size of the circuits in C respectively. In particular, if C is in  $\mathbf{NC}^1$ , then so is  $\tilde{\mathcal{C}}$ . Further, assume the CS scheme has decomposable online-offline efficiency (see Definition 4.2), and in particular, we have algorithms (CS.Aggrgt, CS.VrfyOnL).

Our construction of offline-online efficient HomSig with message space  $\{0,1\}^{\ell}$  for circuit class  $\mathcal{C}$  is provided as follows:

- $\mathsf{HS}.\mathsf{KeyGen}(1^\kappa,1^\ell)\colon \text{ On input the security parameter } 1^\kappa \text{ and the message length } 1^\ell, \text{ generate } (\mathsf{msk},\mathsf{vk}) \xleftarrow{\$} \mathsf{CS}.\mathsf{Setup}(1^\kappa,1^\ell), \\ \text{and output } \mathsf{pk} := \mathsf{vk} \text{ and } \mathsf{sk} := \mathsf{msk}.$
- $\text{HS.Sign}(\mathsf{sk},C) \text{: On input } \mathsf{sk} = \mathsf{msk} \text{ and } C \in \mathcal{C}, \text{ compute the associated circuit } \tilde{C} \in \tilde{\mathcal{C}} \text{ defined above. I.e., } \\ \tilde{C}(\mathbf{x}',z') = \left(C(\mathbf{x}') \stackrel{?}{=} z'\right) \text{ for all } (\mathbf{x}',z') \in \{0,1\}^{\ell} \times \{0,1\}. \text{ Then compute } \mathsf{sk}_{\tilde{C}} \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{CS.KeyGen}(\mathsf{msk},\tilde{C}), \text{ and output } \boldsymbol{\sigma} = \mathsf{sk}_{\tilde{C}}.$
- HS.Eval(pk,  $C, \mathbf{x}, \boldsymbol{\sigma}$ ): On input  $C \in \mathcal{C}, \mathbf{x} \in \{0, 1\}^{\ell}$ , and  $\boldsymbol{\sigma} = \mathsf{sk}_{\tilde{C}}$ , compute  $z = C(\mathbf{x})$ . Then compute  $\sigma_{\mathsf{CS}} \xleftarrow{\$} \mathsf{CS}.\mathsf{Sign}(\mathsf{sk}_{\tilde{C}}, \mathbf{x} \| z)$ , and output  $\sigma = \sigma_{\mathsf{CS}}.$  Here  $\cdot \| \cdot$  denotes concatenation of bit strings.

 $\mathsf{HS.Verify}(\mathsf{pk},\mathbf{x},z,\boldsymbol{\sigma})$ : Run the following algorithms in order:

- HS.VerifyOffL(pk,  $\mathbf{x}$ ): Parse pk = vk  $\rightarrow$  (vk<sub>0</sub>, {vk<sub>i,b</sub>}<sub>i \in [n+1],b \in \{0,1\}) and compute vk<sub>x</sub> = CS.Aggrgt( {vk<sub>i,x<sub>i</sub></sub>}<sub>i \in [n]</sub>) where  $\mathbf{x}_i$  denotes the *i*-th bit of  $\mathbf{x}$ . Finally, output pk<sub>x</sub> = (vk<sub>0</sub>, vk<sub>x</sub>, {vk<sub>n+1,b</sub>}<sub>b \in \{0,1\}</sub>).</sub>
- HS.VerifyOnL(pk',  $z, \sigma$ ): Parse pk'  $\rightarrow$  (vk<sub>0</sub>, vk<sub>x</sub>, {vk<sub>n+1,b</sub>}<sub>b∈{0,1}</sub>) and  $\sigma \rightarrow \sigma_{CS}$  and compute vk<sub>x||z</sub> = CS.Aggrgt(vk<sub>x</sub>, vk<sub>n+1,z</sub>). Then output whatever returned by running CS.VrfyOnL(vk<sub>0</sub>, vk<sub>x||z</sub>,  $\sigma_{CS}$ ).

Security and correctness of the scheme directly follow from the result by Tsabary [Tsa17]. However, since we adapt slightly different syntax from hers, we show the proofs for the sake of completeness.

#### Correctness.

**Theorem A.5** (Correctness). *If*  $\Pi_{CS}$  *satisfies correctness, then*  $\Pi_{HS}$  *satisfies correctness.* 

<span id="page-44-1"></span>*Proof.* It can be checked by inspection. Fix messages  $\mathbf{x} \in \{0,1\}^\ell$  and a circuit  $C \in \mathcal{C}$  and let  $z = C(\mathbf{x})$ , Then by correctness of  $\Pi_{CS}$ , we have CS.Vrfy(vk,  $\mathbf{x} \| z$ , vk<sub>CS</sub>) =  $\top$  where vk<sub>CS</sub>  $\stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow}$  CS.Sign(sk<sub> $\tilde{C}$ </sub>,  $\mathbf{x} \| z$ ), since  $\tilde{C}(\mathbf{x} \| z) = 1$  by definition of  $\tilde{C}$ . Therefore, due to the decomposable online-offline efficiency property of  $\Pi_{CS}$  (see Definition 4.2), it can be checked that HS.Verify output  $\top$  as well.

#### Security.

**Theorem A.6** (Unforgeability). If  $\Pi_{CS}$  satisfies (adaptive one-key) unforgeability, then  $\Pi_{HS}$  satisfies unforgeability.

*Proof.* We prove by contradiction. Suppose that there exists a PPT adversary  $\mathcal{A}$  that breaks unforgeability of  $\Pi_{\mathsf{HS}}$  with non-negligible probability  $\epsilon$ . We then construct a PPT adversary  $\mathcal{B}$  that breaks the (adaptive one-key) security of  $\Pi_{\mathsf{CS}}$  with probability  $\epsilon$  as follows.

 $\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{vk})$ : It sets  $\mathsf{pk} := \mathsf{vk}$  and gives the verification key  $\mathsf{pk}$  to  $\mathcal{A}$ . When submits a circuit  $C \in \mathcal{C}$ ,  $\mathcal{B}$  first computes the associated circuit  $\tilde{C} \in \tilde{\mathcal{C}}$  defined as  $\tilde{C}(\mathbf{x}',z') = \left(C(\mathbf{x}') \stackrel{?}{=} z'\right)$  for all  $(\mathbf{x}',z') \in \{0,1\}^{\ell} \times \{0,1\}$ .  $\mathcal{B}$  then makes a key generation query for  $\tilde{C}$  to its own  $\Pi_{\mathsf{CS}}$  challenger to obtain  $\mathsf{sk}_{\tilde{C}}$ , and gives  $\mathsf{sk}_{\tilde{C}}$  to  $\mathcal{A}$ . When  $\mathcal{A}$  outputs a forgery  $(\mathbf{x}^\star,z^\star,\sigma^\star)$ ,  $\mathcal{B}$  outputs  $(\mathbf{x}^\star\|z^\star,\sigma^\star)$  as its forgery.

It is clear that  $\mathcal{B}$  perfectly simulates the view of the unforgeability game of  $\Pi_{\text{HS}}$  to  $\mathcal{A}$ . We claim that whenever  $\mathcal{A}$  wins the unforgeability game of  $\Pi_{\text{HS}}$ ,  $\mathcal{B}$  wins the unforgeability game of  $\Pi_{\text{CS}}$ . By the winning condition,  $\mathcal{A}$ 's forgery satisfies  $(\mathbf{x}^{\star}, z^{\star}) \in \{0, 1\}^{\ell} \times \{0, 1\}$ ,  $C(\mathbf{x}^{\star}) \neq z^{\star}$ , and HS.Verify(pk,  $\mathbf{x}^{\star}, z^{\star}, \sigma^{\star}) = \top$ . In particular, this implies  $\tilde{C}(\mathbf{x}^{\star}, z^{\star}) = 0$  and CS.Vrfy(vk,  $\mathbf{x}^{\star} \| z^{\star}, \sigma^{\star}) = \top$ . Therefore,  $\mathcal{B}$  wins the unforgeability game of  $\Pi_{\text{CS}}$  with advantage  $\epsilon$ , which is non-negligible as desired.

Remark A.7. (Instantiations.) If we instantiate the scheme based on the decomposable online-offline efficient CS scheme in Section 4, we obtain a compact offline-online efficient HomSig scheme based on the DLIN assumption where "compact" means that the evaluated signature size does not depend on the size of the circuit on which the pre-evaluation signature is generated.

Remark A.8. (Extension to multi-data scheme) By using the generic transformation from single-data scheme to multi-data scheme by Gorbunov et al. [GVW15], we obtain multi-data HomSig scheme based on CS. Similarly to [KNYY19b], the resulting scheme *does not* satisfy amortized verification efficiency, which means that a verifier only needs to perform a computation depending on the size of the circuit only once and then he can reuse it for verifying signatures generated by multiple signers w.r.t. multiple data sets.

## <span id="page-44-0"></span>**B** Efficient Instantiation based on Groth-Sahai Proof.

Here, we give an efficient instantiation of the scheme given in Section 5.1 based on the Groth-Sahai proof under the SXDH assumption.

**Groth-Sahai Proof.** First, we briefly recall the Groth-Sahai proof. We refer the full description to [GS12]. We note that we focus on the instantiation based on the SXDH assumption here.

Roughly speaking, the Groth-Sahai proof is an efficient NIZK for a specific class of languages associated with a pairing-group. Specifically, it enables one to prove that committed values under an associated extractable 13 commitment scheme satisfy certain algebraic relations. We denote the setup algorithm of the associated commitment scheme by GS.Setup and committing algorithm in  $G_b$  by GS.Commit $G_b$  for b=1,2. The setup algorithm GS.Setup takes a description of a pairing group and outputs a common reference string  $\operatorname{crs}_{\mathsf{GS}}$ . The committing algorithm GS.Commit $G_b$  in  $G_b$  takes the common reference string  $\operatorname{crs}_{\mathsf{GS}}$  and an element of  $G_b$  or  $\mathbb{Z}_p$  as input, and returns a commitment that consists of 2 elements  $G_b$ . For  $X=(X_1,...,X_m)\in G_b^m$  (resp.  $x=(x_1,...,x_m)\in \mathbb{Z}_p^m$ ), we denote GS.Commit $G_b$  ( $\operatorname{crs}_{\mathsf{GS}},X$ ) (resp. GS.Commit $G_b$  ( $\operatorname{crs}_{\mathsf{GS}},X$ )) to mean (GS.Commit $G_b$  ( $\operatorname{crs}_{\mathsf{GS}},X_1$ ), ..., GS.Commit $G_b$  ( $\operatorname{crs}_{\mathsf{GS}},X_m$ )))for notational simplicity.

<span id="page-44-2"></span><sup>&</sup>lt;sup>13</sup>Actually, the GS proof is a dual-mode NIZK, and satisfies perfect extractability or perfect zero-knowledge depending on the mode. Here, we focus on the binding mode where the perfect extractability is satisfied.

Suppose that  $(X_1,...,X_m) \in G_1^m$  and  $(x_1,...,x_{m'}) \in \mathbb{Z}_p^{m'}$  are committed under GS.Commit $_{G_1}$  and  $(Y_1,...,Y_n) \in G_2^n$  and  $(y_1,...,y_{n'}) \in \mathbb{Z}_p^{n'}$  are committed under GS.Commit $_{G_2}$ . Then the GS proof enables one to prove an equation of the following forms.

## Pairing product equation with target $1 \in G_T$ :

$$\prod_{i=1}^{n} e(A_i, Y_i) \cdot \prod_{i=1}^{m} e(X_i, B_i) \cdot \prod_{i=1}^{m} \prod_{j=1}^{n} e(X_i, Y_j)^{\gamma_{ij}} = 1,$$

for constants  $A_i \in G_1, B_i \in G_2, \gamma_{ij} \in \mathbb{Z}_p$ .

## Multi-scalar multiplication equation in $G_1$ :

$$\prod_{i=1}^{n'} A_i^{y_i} \cdot \prod_{i=1}^m X_i^{b_i} \cdot \prod_{i=1}^m \prod_{j=1}^{n'} X_i^{\gamma_{ij}y_j} = T_1$$

for constants  $A_i, T_1 \in G_1, b_i, \gamma_{ij} \in \mathbb{Z}_p$ .

#### Multi-scalar multiplication equation in $G_2$ :

$$\prod_{i=1}^{n} Y_{i}^{a_{i}} \cdot \prod_{i=1}^{m'} B_{i}^{x_{i}} \cdot \prod_{i=1}^{m'} \prod_{j=1}^{n} Y_{j}^{\gamma_{ij}x_{i}} = T_{2}$$

for constants  $B_i, T_2 \in G_2, a_i, \gamma_{ij} \in \mathbb{Z}_p$ .

## Quadratic equation in $\mathbb{Z}_p$ :

$$\sum_{i=1}^{n'} a_i y_i + \sum_{i=1}^{m'} x_i b_i + \sum_{i=1}^{m'} \sum_{j=1}^{n'} \gamma_{ij} x_i y_j = t \mod p$$

for constants  $a_i, b_i, \gamma_{ij}, t \in \mathbb{Z}_p$ .

We summarize the number of group elements that are needed to prove these equations in Table 3.

**Our NIZK.** Let  $\mathcal{L}$  be an **NP** language defined by a relation  $\mathcal{R} \subseteq \{0,1\}^* \times \{0,1\}^*$ . Let  $n(\kappa)$  and  $m(\kappa)$  be any fixed polynomials. Let C be an  $\mathbf{NC}^1$  circuit that computes the relation  $\mathcal{R}$  on  $\{0,1\}^n \times \{0,1\}^m$ , i.e., for  $(x,w) \in \{0,1\}^n \times \{0,1\}^m$ , we have C(x,w)=1 if and only if  $(x,w)\in \mathcal{R}$ . Let  $\Pi_{\mathsf{SKE}}=(\mathsf{SKE}.\mathsf{Setup},\mathsf{SKE}.\mathsf{KeyGen},\mathsf{SKE}.\mathsf{Enc},\mathsf{SKE}.\mathsf{Dec})$  be an SKE scheme with message space  $\{0,1\}^m$ , key space  $\{0,1\}^\ell$  and ciphertext space  $\{0,1\}^{\mathsf{lct}}$ . We require that it is one-time secure, its decryption circuit can be computed in  $\mathbf{NC}^1$ , it has no ciphertext overhead (i.e.,  $|\mathsf{ct}|=m$ ), and  $\ell$  does not depend on m. We define a circuit  $f_{\mathsf{PP}_{\mathsf{SKE}}}: \{0,1\}^{n'} \to \{0,1\}$  that computes

$$f_{\mathsf{pp_{\mathsf{SKE}}}}(K,x,\mathsf{ct}) = C(x,\mathsf{SKE}.\mathsf{Dec}(\mathsf{pp_{\mathsf{SKE}}},K,\mathsf{ct}))$$

for  $\operatorname{pp}_{\mathsf{SKE}} \in \mathsf{SKE}.\mathsf{Setup}(1^\kappa)$  where  $n' := \ell + n + |\mathsf{ct}|$ . Since we assume that both C and  $\mathsf{SKE}.\mathsf{Dec}$  can be computed in  $\mathbf{NC}^1$ ,  $f_{\mathsf{pp}_{\mathsf{SKE}}}$  can be computed in  $\mathbf{NC}^1$ . Let share be as defined in Definition 3.1 and EncInp and EncCir be as defined in Lemma 2.9.

Our scheme is described as follows.

## $\mathsf{Setup}(1^\kappa)$ :

- 1. Generate  $pp_{SKF} \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} SKE.Setup(1^{\kappa})$ .
- 2. Run  $\mathbb{G} = (p, G_1, G_2, G_T, e) \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{GGen}(1^{\kappa})$ . and generate  $\mathsf{crs}_\mathsf{GS} \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{GS}.\mathsf{Setup}(\mathbb{G})$ .
- 3. Sample  $\mathbf{a} \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathbb{Z}_p^2$ ,  $\mathbf{w}_i \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathbb{Z}_p^2$  for  $i \in [2n']$  and  $\mathbf{v} \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathbb{Z}_p^2$  and set

$$\mathsf{vk} = ([\mathbf{a}^\top]_1, [\mathbf{a}^\top \mathbf{w}_1]_1, \dots, [\mathbf{a}^\top \mathbf{w}_{2n'}]_1, e([\mathbf{a}^\top]_1, [\mathbf{v}]_2)).$$

4. Compute  $\operatorname{EncCir}(f_{\operatorname{pp}_{\operatorname{SKE}}}) \to f'$ , sample  $(\{\mathbf{v}_j\}_{j \in [\hat{m}]}, \rho) \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \operatorname{share}(f', \mathbf{v})$  and  $r_j \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathbb{Z}_p$  for  $j \in [\hat{m}]$  and compute

$$\mathsf{sk}_f = \left( \left\{ \mathsf{sk}_j \coloneqq [r_j]_2, \quad \mathsf{sk}_{\rho(j),j} \coloneqq [\mathbf{v}_j + \mathbf{w}_{\rho(j)} r_j]_2, \quad \left\{ \right. \mathsf{sk}_{i,j} \coloneqq [\mathbf{w}_i r_j]_2 \left. \right\}_{i \in [2n'] \backslash \left\{ \rho(j) \right\}} \right\}_{j \in [\hat{m}]} \right)$$

where  $\mathbf{w}_0 = \mathbf{0}$  and  $\hat{m}$  is the number of shares that is generated by share  $(f', \mathbf{v})$ .

5. Output  $crs = (pp_{SKE}, \mathbb{G}, crs_{GS}, vk, sk_f)$ .

## $\mathsf{Prove}(\mathsf{crs}, x, w)$ :

- 1. Abort if  $\mathcal{R}(x, w) = 0$ . Otherwise, do the following.
- 2. Parse crs  $\rightarrow$  (pp<sub>SKE</sub>,  $\mathbb{G}$ , crs<sub>GS</sub>, vk, sk<sub>f</sub>).
- 3. Generate  $K \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{SKE}.\mathsf{KeyGen}(\mathsf{pp}_{\mathsf{SKE}})$  and ct  $\stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{SKE}.\mathsf{Enc}(\mathsf{pp}_{\mathsf{SKE}},K,w).$
- 4. Set  $z \coloneqq \mathsf{EncInp}(K \| x \| \mathsf{ct})$  and compute  $\omega_j$  such that  $\mathbf{v} = \sum_{j: \rho(j) = 0 \lor y_{\rho(j)} = 1} \omega_j \mathbf{v}_j$  and

$$\sigma = \left(\sigma_1 = \prod_{j: \rho(j) = 0 \lor z_{\rho(j)} = 1} \left(\prod_{i: z_i = 1} \mathsf{sk}_{i,j}\right)^{\omega_j}, \quad \sigma_2 = \prod_{j: \rho(j) = 0 \lor z_{\rho(j)} = 1} \mathsf{sk}_j^{\omega_j}\right) \in G_2^3.$$

- 5. Compute  $Y \coloneqq \prod_{i \in [n+|\mathsf{ct}|]} [\mathbf{a}^\top \mathbf{w}_{2\ell+2i-y_i}]_1$  where  $y \coloneqq (x,\mathsf{ct}) \in \{0,1\}^{n+|\mathsf{ct}|}$ .
- 6. Compute  $\operatorname{com}_K \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \operatorname{\mathsf{GS.Commit}}_{G_1}(\operatorname{\mathsf{crs}}_{\mathsf{GS}}, \{K_i\}_{i \in [\ell]})$  and  $\operatorname{\mathsf{com}}_{\widetilde{K}_i} \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \operatorname{\mathsf{GS.Commit}}_{G_2}(\operatorname{\mathsf{crs}}_{\mathsf{GS}}, \{\widetilde{K}_i\}_{i \in [\ell]})$  where  $\widetilde{K} \coloneqq K$ .
- 7. Compute  $\operatorname{com}_Z \stackrel{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \operatorname{\mathsf{GS}}.\operatorname{\mathsf{Commit}}_{G_1}(\operatorname{\mathsf{crs}}_{\operatorname{\mathsf{GS}}},Z)$  where  $Z\coloneqq\prod_{i\in[\ell]}[\mathbf{a}^{\top}\mathbf{w}_{2i-K_i}]_1\cdot Y\in G_1.$
- 8. Compute  $\mathrm{com}_V \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{GS}.\mathsf{Commit}_{G_2}(\mathsf{crs}_{\mathsf{GS}},V)$  where  $V\coloneqq [\mathbf{v}]_2\in G_2^2$ .
- 9. Compute  $\mathsf{com}_\sigma \overset{\hspace{0.1em}\mathsf{\scriptscriptstyle\$}}{\leftarrow} \mathsf{GS}.\mathsf{Commit}_{G_2}(\mathsf{crs}_{\mathsf{GS}},\sigma).$
- 10. Generate a proof  $\pi_K$  that proves

$$K_i - \tilde{K}_i = 0 \mod p,$$

$$K_i \cdot \tilde{K}_i - K_i = 0 \mod p$$

for all  $i \in [\ell]$ . (This especially ensures  $K_i, \widetilde{K}_i \in \{0,1\}$  for  $i \in [\ell]$ .)

11. Generate a proof *π<sup>Z</sup>* that proves

$$Z = \prod_{i \in [\ell]} ([\mathbf{a}^\top \mathbf{w}_{2i-1}]_1^{\widetilde{K}_i} \cdot [\mathbf{a}^\top \mathbf{w}_{2i}]_1^{1-\widetilde{K}_i}) \cdot Y.$$

Note that this is equivalent to

$$Z = \prod_{i \in [\ell]} [\mathbf{a}^\top \mathbf{w}_{2i - \widetilde{K}_i}]_1 \cdot Y$$

for *<sup>K</sup>*e*<sup>i</sup>* ∈ {0*,* <sup>1</sup>}.

12. Generate a proof *π<sup>V</sup>* that proves

$$V = [\mathbf{v}]_2.$$

13. Generate a proof *π<sup>σ</sup>* that proves

$$e([\mathbf{a}^{\top}]_1, \sigma_1) \cdot e(Z, \sigma_2)^{-1} \cdot e([\mathbf{a}^{\top}]_1, V)^{-1} = 1$$

14. Output *<sup>π</sup>* := (ct*,* com*K,* com*<sup>K</sup>*<sup>e</sup> *,* com*Z,* com*<sup>V</sup> ,* com*σ, πK, πZ, π<sup>V</sup> , πσ*).

Verify(crs*, x, π*)**:**

- 1. Parse *<sup>π</sup>* <sup>→</sup> (ct*,* com*K,* com*K*<sup>e</sup> *,* com*Z,* com*<sup>V</sup> ,* com*σ, πK, πZ, π<sup>V</sup> , πσ*). If it is not in this form, reject it. Otherwise, do the following.
- 2. Parse crs → (ppSKE*,* G*,* crsGS*,* vk*,*sk*<sup>f</sup>* ).
- 3. Compute *Y* := Q *i*∈[*n*+|ct|] [**a** <sup>&</sup>gt;**w**2*`*+2*i*−*y<sup>i</sup>* ]<sup>1</sup> where *y* := (*x,* ct) ∈ {0*,* 1} *n*+|ct| .
- 4. Output <sup>&</sup>gt; if all proofs (*πK, πZ, π<sup>V</sup> , πσ*) are valid w.r.t. commitments (com*K,* com*K*<sup>e</sup> *,* com*Z,* com*<sup>V</sup> ,* com*σ*) and otherwise ⊥.

**Theorem B.2 (Zero-Knowledge).** *The above NIZK scheme* ΠNIZK *is computationally zero-knowledge if the SXDH assumption holds and* ΠSKE *is one-time secure.*
