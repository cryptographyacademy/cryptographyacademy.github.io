# Practical Deniable Post-Quantum X3DH: A Lightweight Split-KEM for K-Waay

Guilhem Niot1,2

<sup>1</sup>PQShield <sup>2</sup>Univ Rennes, CNRS, IRISA

This is the full version of a paper to appear in the proceedings of the 20th ACM ASIA Conference on Computer and Communications Security (ACM ASIACCS 2025).

#### Abstract

The Signal Protocol, underpinning secure messaging for billions, faces the challenge of migrating to a post-quantum world while preserving critical properties like asynchrony and deniability. While Signal's PQXDH key exchange protocol addresses post-quantum confidentiality, full migration of the X3DH protocol remains elusive. Relying on a split KEM (K-Waay, USENIX '24) offers a promising migration path, but it has so far suffered from size limitations compared to concurrent works leveraging ring signatures.

This work introduces Sparrow-KEM and Sym-Sparrow-KEM, novel asymmetric and symmetric split KEMs respectively, i.e. for which keys can be used interchangeably for sending and receiving, or only in one direction. They are designed to optimize the K-Waay protocol for size efficiency. Leveraging the MLWE assumption, these constructions reduce by a factor 5.1× the communication of prior postquantum X3DH based on split KEMs, plus provides a 40× speedup. Additionally, Sym-Sparrow-KEM is the first symmetric split-KEM to offer deniability, IND-1KCA, and IND-1BatchCCA security, capturing implicit authentication properties. We provide formal security proofs for both schemes, including deniability. Our results demonstrate the feasibility of a compact and deniable post-quantum X3DH protocol based on split KEMs.

# 1 Introduction

At the heart of many messaging platforms like Signal or Whatsapp lies the X3DH (Triple Diffie-Hellman) key exchange protocol [\[23\]](#page-22-0), which enables two parties to establish a shared secret key for encrypted communication, even in asynchronous settings where one party might be offline. However, the security of X3DH hinges on the computational difficulty of the Diffie-Hellman problem, which becomes vulnerable with the advent of powerful quantum computers.

Recognizing this critical threat, researchers in academia and the industry (Signal, Apple, etc.) have actively explored post-quantum secure alternatives for X3DH. In 2023, Signal deployed their PQXDH solution [\[19\]](#page-21-0), an initial post-quantum hybrid key exchange protocol. This first step importantly provides post-quantum confidentiality and prevents "store-now-decrypt-later" attacks. However, post-quantum authentication remains unresolved, and an active quantum attacker would be able to perform impersonation attacks. It seems desirable to design an alternative to X3DH that is fully post-quantum secure.

Standard X3DH revolves around the interplay of long-term and ephemeral key pairs. Parties initially upload to a central server a set of short-lived keys in a so-called prekey bundle, authenticated using their long-term keys. Subsequently, they can initiate communication and derive session keys by downloading a counterpart's bundle and executing multiple Diffie-Hellman key exchanges using a mix of these key types. This intricate design guarantees confidentiality even if ephemeral or long-term keys are compromised.

Additionally, X3DH offers a degree of deniability, as formalized by Vatandas et al. [\[31\]](#page-22-1), due to its implicit authentication, relying on the Diffie-Hellman key exchanges rather than explicit signatures. Essentially, a 

{1}------------------------------------------------

participant can plausibly deny having engaged in a key exchange with another party, providing a crucial privacy safeguard in scenarios like whistleblowing or activism.

Two main approaches have emerged to migrate X3DH to a post-quantum setting while preserving its essential properties: ring signatures and split key encapsulation mechanisms (split-KEMs). Ring signatures offer a promising path by enabling a group member to anonymously sign a message on behalf of the entire group. In the context of X3DH, this could be used to authenticate prekey bundles without revealing the specific signer as studied in [\[15,](#page-21-1) [16,](#page-21-2) [6\]](#page-21-3). Alternatively, split-KEMs provide a different paradigm for key exchange, where two parties can directly exchange a key, akin to Diffie-Hellman, allowing a quite natural update of X3DH. In a split KEM, a sending party B encapsulates to a counterpart A by using their own secret key sk<sup>B</sup> and the public key pk<sup>A</sup> to produce a ciphertext ct. A then decapsulates ct using sk<sup>A</sup> and pkB.

Split KEMs were introduced by Brendel et al. [\[7\]](#page-21-4), who define several levels of security depending on the adversary's access to encapsulation and decapsulation oracles, as a tool akin to Diffie-Hellman for implementing Authenticated Key Exchange (AKE) protocols. They notably identified the conception of a symmetric primitive, i.e. where the key can be used both for sending and receiving, as a desirable property to implement secure messaging. [\[7,](#page-21-4) [14,](#page-21-5) [3\]](#page-20-0) proposed several split KEM constructions achieving from the weakest to the highest level of security defined in [\[7\]](#page-21-4). Collins et al. [\[8\]](#page-21-6) however showed that these security notions are not sufficient for use in Signal's X3DH, as they do not guarantee implicit authentication and prevention of man-in-the-middle attacks. They formally defined additional security notions that enabled them to introduce K-Waay, a post-quantum variant of the X3DH protocol relying on deniable split KEMs. They rely on intermediate notions of security, coined IND-1KCA and IND-1BatchCCA, allowing only one encapsulation, and one decapsulation query (but for many ciphertexts at once). Building on Frodo KEM [\[5\]](#page-21-7), they introduced FrodoKex+, an asymetric split KEM provably plugable in K-Waay.

While K-Waay presents a significant step forward, and notably provides an improved computational cost over previous works implementing a post-quantum X3DH, its reliance on the Frodo KEM [\[5\]](#page-21-7) to build a split KEM leads to relatively large sizes (pre-keys and ciphertexts) compared to ring signature-based constructions. This can be a major hurdle for practical adoption, especially in bandwidth-constrained environments like those encountered on mobile devices. Reducing the size of split KEM becomes crucial for seamless integration of post-quantum security into existing messaging protocols like Signal.

Note that deniability is a complex notion in cryptography, influenced by adversarial models and the information available to a judge. Various models are used depending on whether parties behave maliciously or semi-honestly in the protocol, and which information is leaked to the judge. Both HKKP [\[16\]](#page-21-2) and K-Waay [\[8\]](#page-21-6) notably considers semi-honest parties for deniability, but HKKP does not cover any secret material leakage, while K-Waay allows leakage of the receiver's secret key.

From previous works, it remains an open question whether K-Waay can achieve the same level of compactness as X3DH protocols based on ring signatures, while keeping its low computational cost advantage.

# 1.1 Our contributions

In this work, we present novel split-KEM constructions based on MLWE, that significantly improve over previous constructions in both size and efficiency. We integrate them in K-Waay to show that split KEMs can be used to design a post-quantum deniable X3DH protocol as compact as using ring signatures, and with a lower computational cost. We also introduce the first symmetric split KEM pluggable in K-Waay and discuss the implied improvements to the practical deniability properties of the K-Waay protocol.

Our specific contributions include:

- 1. Design of novel split-KEM constructions. We design Sparrow-KEM the first split KEM relying on MLWE that satisfies the IND-1KCA and IND-1BatchCCA security properties, capturing implicit authentication and required for integration in the K-Waay protocol. We also introduce a novel ciphertext truncation technique which allows us to instantiate the first symmetric split KEM pluggable in K-Waay, that we coin Sym-Sparrow-KEM. Its symmetry allows one to decide at a later stage whether it shall be used for sending or receiving messages.
- 2. New proof techniques for split KEM deniability. We prove the deniability of Sparrow-KEM and

{2}------------------------------------------------

Sym-Sparrow-KEM relying on the recent problem Hint-MLWE, that reduces to MLWE, and answers a question left open in K-Waay [\[8\]](#page-21-6) on how to prove deniability in the module setting.

- 3. Improved deniability of K-Waay. We discuss the improved practical deniability of K-Waay when using a symmetric split KEM, and show that at a small cost, our contributions allow one to use K-Waay with a stronger deniability model and increase the confidence in it.
- 4. A compact split KEM, competitive with ring signatures. We perform an extensive security and parameter analysis for a tight instantiation of our split KEM. Combined with our optimized design, this allows us to shrink the public key size of our split KEM by a factor 10.5× compared to the prior state-of-the-art split KEM from K-Waay [\[8\]](#page-21-6). When considering the complete transcript of the K-Waay protocol, we also reduce the communication cost by a factor 5.1×! This closes the gap in size between the two approaches considered to migrate X3DH to the post-quantum setting either based on ring signatures or on split KEMs, and our split KEM-based instantiation has competitive sizes with concurrent approaches relying on ring signatures.
- 5. The most efficient post-quantum X3DH protocol. We provide an implementation for our split KEM Sparrow-KEM. Our benchmarks show that Sparrow-KEM is 40× faster than the split KEM FrodoKex+, which was already the computationally fastest option for a post-quantum X3DH and several times faster than the ring signature approach. Our optimized construction hence increases the gap in terms of efficiency between split KEMs and ring signatures, and shows that split KEMs are strong contenders, especially considering that these algorithms are typically run on user devices, where battery saving is crucial.

# <span id="page-3-0"></span>2.3 Split Key Encapsulation Mechanism (Split KEM)

The primitive at the core of this work is split-KEM. It was introduced by Brendel et al. in [7] as a practical tool to provide post-quantum security in X3DH. K-Waay [8] extended their work by introducing an asymmetric split KEM, which allows the exchange of a key between two parties, where one is typically the sender, while the other is the receiver. They also provide a generic construction to achieve a weak variant of IND-CCA security, and deniability properties within their proposal of a post-quantum X3DH protocol. We recall definitions and security notions from [8].

For clarity of the notations, we consider that Alice "A" takes the role of the receiver, and Bob "B" takes the role of the sender in the asymmetric split KEM.

**Definition 3 (Split-KEM** [7, 8]). An (asymmetric) split-KEM sKEM is a tuple of four efficient algorithms (KeygenA, KeygenB, Encaps, Decaps) defined as follows:

- $(pk_A, sk_A) \leftarrow KeygenA(1^{\lambda})$ : The key generation function of the receiving party takes the security parameter  $\lambda$  as input, and outputs a pair of public/secret keys  $(pk_A, sk_A)$ .
- $(pk_B, sk_B) \leftarrow KeygenB(1^{\lambda})$ : The key generation function of the sending party takes the security parameter  $\lambda$  as input, and outputs a pair of public/secret keys  $(pk_B, sk_B)$ .
- K,  $\mathsf{ct} \leftarrow \mathsf{Encaps}(\mathsf{pk}_\mathsf{A}, \mathsf{sk}_\mathsf{B})$ : The encapsulation function takes the public key  $\mathsf{pk}_\mathsf{A}$  of a party  $\mathsf{A}$  and the other party's secret key  $\mathsf{sk}_\mathsf{B}$  as inputs, and outputs a ciphertext  $\mathsf{ct}$  and a key K.
- $K/\bot \leftarrow \mathsf{Decaps}(\mathsf{pk_B}, \mathsf{sk_A}, \mathsf{ct})$ : The decapsulation function takes the secret key  $\mathsf{sk_A}$  of a party  $\mathsf{A}$ , the other party's public key  $\mathsf{pk_B}$  and a ciphertext  $\mathsf{ct}$  as inputs, and outputs a key K or the error symbol  $\bot$ .

A split KEM sKEM is said symmetric if KeygenA = KeygenB.

Definition 4 (split-KEM Correctness). A split-KEM sKEM is  $\delta$ -correct if

$$\Pr \left[ K \neq K' : \begin{array}{l} (\mathsf{pk_A}, \mathsf{sk_A}) \leftarrow \mathsf{KeygenA}(1^\lambda); \\ (\mathsf{pk_B}, \mathsf{sk_B}) \leftarrow \mathsf{KeygenB}(1^\lambda); \\ K, \mathsf{ct} \leftarrow \mathsf{Encaps}(\mathsf{pk_A}, \mathsf{sk_B}); \\ K' \leftarrow \mathsf{Decaps}(\mathsf{pk_B}, \mathsf{sk_A}, \mathsf{ct})] \end{array} \right] \leqslant \delta$$

Split KEMs were introduced as a mean to allow an encapsulating party to contribute to the shared key, while normal KEMs only allow the shared key to be derived from the receiver's key. Their main advantage over normal KEMs is the possibility to implicitly authenticate a sender, which is of great use within a key exchange protocol.

The original work on Split-KEMs [7] introduced the security notion of mm-IND-CCA, which captures the secrecy of a shared secret between two parties. However, Collins et al. [8] explained that this notion is not sufficient to capture implicit authentication, and to prevent impersonation. They introduced the notion of UNF-1KCA security, ensuring that decapsulation will only succeed if encapsulation was performed by the expected party.

#### 2.4 The K-Waay key exchange protocol

K-Waay builds a secure key exchange protocol with a Split KEM relying on the implicit authentication property UNF-1KCA, and on a weakened notion of IND-CCA, coined IND-1BatchCCA, allowing only one batched decapsulation query. In particular, if any of d ciphertexts fails to decapsulate, the entire batched decapsulation aborts. This allows one to limit the leakage from a failure to a single information bit, instead of d bits. Remark that UNF-1KCA (resp. IND-1BatchCCA) only allows one encapsulation query (resp. decapsulation query), which is sufficient for K-Waay where keys are used only once. Designing a sKEM simultaneously achieving both security notions with an unlimited number of queries remains an open question. We recall the high-level construction of K-Waay in Fig. 1, and property definitions below.

**Definition 5 (split-KEM UNF-1KCA and IND-1BatchCCA).** We consider the UNF-1KCA and IND-1BatchCCA games defined in Figure 2. A split-KEM scheme sKEM is UNF-1KCA and IND-1BatchCCA if for any efficient adversary A we have

$$\mathsf{Adv}^{\mathrm{unf-1kca}}_{\mathsf{sKEM}}(\mathcal{A}) := \left| \Pr\left[ \mathrm{UNF-1KCA}_{\mathsf{sKEM}}(\mathcal{A}) \Rightarrow 1 \right] - \frac{1}{2} \right| = \mathsf{negl}$$

Finally, we recall the notion of deniability for split-KEM from K-Waay [8]. Intuitively, a split-KEM is deniable if a ciphertext/key pair generated by the receiver Alice is indistinguishable from a ciphertext/key pair generated by the sender Bob. That implies that, assuming the parties behave honestly, a judge cannot be convinced that Bob actually sent a ciphertext to Alice that decapsulated to K (i.e. Alice could have computed this ciphertext herself).

Different notions of deniability are used throughout the literature, differing in the material that is leaked to the judge. The definition of deniability from [9] is the most generic and is agnostic to the form of leakage. Works such as [15] proved deniability only when secret materials are not leaked. As in the original work on K-Waay [8], we consider a deniability notion with partial leakage only, i.e. where only the secret receiver key  $sk_A$  leaks, and the sender's key remains secret.

**Definition 8 (Deniability under leak of receiver's key** [8]). A split-KEM sKEM is deniable if there exists an efficient simulator sim s.t. for all efficient adversaries A, we have

### 2.5 Hardness Assumptions

We recall the Module Learning with Errors assumption.

**Definition 9** (MLWE). Let  $\ell, k, q$  be integers,  $\chi$  be a probability distributions over  $\mathcal{R}_q^{k+\ell}$ . The advantage  $\mathsf{Adv}_{\mathcal{A}}^{\mathsf{MLWE}}$  of an adversary  $\mathcal{A}$  against the Module Learning with Errors problem  $\mathsf{MLWE}_{k,\ell,q,\chi}$  is defined as:

$$\left| \Pr \left[ 1 \leftarrow \mathcal{A} \left( \mathbf{A}, \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{I}_k & \mathbf{A} \end{bmatrix} \cdot \mathbf{s} \right) \right] - \Pr \left[ 1 \leftarrow \mathcal{A} \left( \mathbf{A}, \mathbf{u} \right) \right] \right|,$$

where  $(\mathbf{A}, \mathbf{u}) \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathcal{R}^{k \times \ell} \times \mathcal{R}_q^k$ ,  $\mathbf{s} \leftarrow \chi$ . The  $\mathsf{MLWE}_{k,\ell,q,\chi}$  assumption states that any efficient adversary  $\mathcal{A}$  has a negligible advantage.

{7}------------------------------------------------

We also recall the Hint-MLWE assumption [18], and extended by Espitau, Niot and Prest [11] to capture hints on linear combinations of the MLWE secret and error, i.e. values of the form  $\mathbf{z} := \mathbf{c}^{\top} \cdot \mathbf{s} + \mathbf{y}$  where  $\mathbf{y}$  is a masking value. We state here a simplified version of [11, Definition 3] limited to polynomial combinations of the secret and error.

**Definition 10** (Hint-MLWE). Let  $k, \ell, q, Q$  be integers,  $\chi, \xi$  be probability distributions over  $\mathcal{R}^{k+\ell}$  and  $\mathcal{R}$  respectively, and  $\mathcal{C}$  be a distribution of challenges over  $\mathcal{R}^{k+\ell}$ . The advantage  $\mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{Hint-MLWE}}_{\mathcal{A}}$  of an adversary  $\mathcal{A}$  against the Hint Module Learning with Errors problem Hint-MLWE $_{k,\ell,q,Q,\chi,\xi,\mathcal{C}}$  is defined as:

$$\left| \Pr \left[ 1 \leftarrow \mathcal{A} \left( \mathbf{A}, \left[ \mathbf{I}_k \ \mathbf{A} \right] \mathbf{s}, (\mathbf{c}_i, \mathbf{z}_i)_{i \in [Q]} \right) \right] - \Pr \left[ 1 \leftarrow \mathcal{A} \left( \mathbf{A}, \mathbf{u}, (\mathbf{c}_i, \mathbf{z}_i)_{i \in [Q]} \right) \right] \right|$$

where  $(\mathbf{A}, \mathbf{u}) \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathcal{R}_q^{k \times \ell} \times \mathcal{R}_q^k$ ,  $\mathbf{s} \leftarrow \chi$  and for  $i \in [Q]$ :  $\mathbf{c}_i \leftarrow \mathcal{C}$ ,  $\mathbf{y}_i \leftarrow \xi$ , and  $\mathbf{z}_i = \mathbf{c}_i^{\top} \cdot \mathbf{s} + \mathbf{y}_i$  are hints on the secret. The Hint-MLWE<sub>k,\ell,q,Q,\chi,\xi,\mathcal{C}</sub> assumption states that any efficient adversary  $\mathcal{A}$  has a negligible advantage.

The spectral norm  $s_1(\mathbf{M})$  of a matrix  $\mathbf{M}$  is defined as the value  $\max_{\mathbf{x}\neq\mathbf{0}}\frac{\|\mathbf{M}\mathbf{x}\|}{\|\mathbf{x}\|}$ . We recall that if a matrix is symmetric, then its spectral norm is also its largest eigenvalue. Given a polynomial  $c \in \mathcal{R}$ , we may abusively use the term "spectral norm  $s_1(c)$  of c" when referring to the spectral norm of the anticirculant matrix  $\mathcal{M}(c)$  associated to c. Finally, if  $c(x) = \sum_{0 \leq i < n} c_i x^i$ , then the Hermitian adjoint of c, which we denote by  $c^*$ , is defined as  $c^*(x) = c_0 - \sum_{0 < i < n} c_{n-i} x^i$ . Note that  $\mathcal{M}(c)^t = \mathcal{M}(c^*)$ .

<span id="page-7-1"></span>Theorem 2 (Hardness of Hint-MLWE, adapted from [18] and [11]). Let  $\mathcal{C}$  be a distribution over  $\mathcal{R}$ , and let  $B_{\mathsf{HMLWE}}$  be a real number such that  $s_1(D) \leqslant B_{\mathsf{HMLWE}}$  with overwhelming probability, where  $D = \sum_Q \langle \mathbf{c}_i, \mathbf{c}_i^* \rangle \in \mathcal{R}_q$ . Let  $\sigma, \sigma_{\mathsf{sk}}, \sigma_{\mathsf{pert}} > 0$  such that  $\frac{1}{\sigma^2} = 2 \left( \frac{1}{\sigma_{\mathsf{sk}}^2} + \frac{B_{\mathsf{HMLWE}}}{\sigma_{\mathsf{pert}}^2} \right)$ . If  $\sigma \geqslant \sqrt{2} \eta_{\varepsilon}(\mathbb{Z}^{n(k+\ell)})$  for  $0 < \varepsilon \leqslant 1/2$ , where  $\eta_{\varepsilon}(\mathbb{Z}^{n(k+\ell)})$  is the smoothing parameter of  $\mathbb{Z}^{n(k+\ell)}$ , then the problem  $\mathsf{MLWE}_{k,\ell,q,\sigma}$  efficiently reduces to  $\mathsf{Hint-MLWE}_{k,\ell,q,Q,\sigma_{\mathsf{sk}},\sigma_{\mathsf{pert}},\mathcal{C}}$  with an advantage increased by at most  $2\varepsilon$ .

#### <span id="page-7-0"></span>2.6 Lattice toolbox

**Rounding functions.** Given two parameters q and  $B < \log q - 1$ , we define the rounding function, and the cross-rounding function  $\langle \cdot \rangle_{q,B}$  as follows:

$$\lfloor \cdot \rceil_{q,B} : v \mapsto \left\lfloor \frac{2^B}{q} \cdot v \right\rfloor \mod 2^B, \quad \langle \cdot \rangle_{q,B} : v \mapsto \left\lfloor \frac{2^{B+1}}{q} \cdot v \right\rfloor \mod 2$$

where  $\lfloor \cdot \rceil : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{Z}$  is the "rounding half-up"  $\lfloor x \rceil = \lfloor x + \frac{1}{2} \rfloor$  and half-way values are rounded up: e.g.  $\lfloor 2.5 \rceil = 3$  and  $\lfloor -2.5 \rceil = -2$ .

**Reconciliation function.** We recall the (generalized) reconciliation mechanism from Bos et al. and Peikert [5, 27], which for every approximate agreement in  $\mathbb{Z}_q$  allows extracting shared bits. We refer the reader to the aforementioned works for more details. Let q be a positive integer. Let B be the number of bits we want to extract from one coefficient in  $\mathbb{Z}_q$  so that  $B < \log q - 1$ . Now, for any  $v \in \mathbb{Z}_q$ , which is represented as an integer in [0, q), we define the following functions.

**Definition 11 (Randomised doubling function** dbl). For any  $v \in \mathbb{Z}_q$ , we define dbl(·) as follows:

$$dbl(v): v \mapsto 2v - e, \quad e \leftarrow Bin$$

Now, we are ready to define the reconciliation function  $\text{Rec}: \mathbb{Z}_{2q} \times \mathbb{Z}_2 \to \mathbb{Z}_{2^B}$ .

**Definition 12 (Reconciliation function** Rec). For any  $w \in \mathbb{Z}_{2q}$  and bit  $b \in \{0,1\}$ , let v be the closest element to  $w \in \mathbb{Z}_{2q}$  s.t.  $\langle v \rangle_{2q,B} = b$ . Then, we define Rec as

$$Rec_B(w,b) := |v|_{2q,B}$$

{8}------------------------------------------------

The next result gives an important property of the reconciliation function Rec, as described by Peikert [27, Section 3.2].

<span id="page-8-1"></span>**Lemma 1.** Let q be odd and  $\bar{v} \leftarrow \mathsf{dbl}(v)$ . If  $|v - w| \leq \left\lfloor \frac{q}{2^{B+2}} \right\rfloor$  then

$$Rec_B(2w, \langle \bar{v} \rangle_{2q,B}) = |\bar{v}|_{2q,B}$$

Finally, we define the  $\mathsf{HelpRec}_B : \mathbb{Z}_q \mapsto \{0,1\}$  function as follows:

**Definition 13** (HelpRec function). On any input  $v \in \mathbb{Z}_q$ ,

$$\mathsf{HelpRec}_B(v) \coloneqq \langle \bar{v} \rangle_{2q,B}, \quad \textit{where } \bar{v} \leftarrow \mathsf{dbl}(v)$$

All the functions above can be naturally generalized to take as input polynomials in  $\mathcal{R}_q$  by applying the function to each of the coefficients.

# 3 Sparrow-KEM: a lightweight split KEM for K-Waay

This section introduces Sparrow-KEM (and its variant Sym-Sparrow-KEM), our split-KEM construction designed to optimize the K-Waay protocol for compactness while maintaining strong security guarantees.

Sparrow-KEM takes inspiration in the prior lattice-based sKEM constructions from Brendel at al. [7] and K-Waay [8] to build a scheme relying on the hardness of MLWE.

At a high level, both of these constructions sample keys of the form  $\mathbf{B}_A \coloneqq \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{S}_A + \mathbf{E}_A$  and  $\mathbf{B}_B \coloneqq \mathbf{A}^\top \cdot \mathbf{S}_B + \mathbf{E}_B$  for some  $\mathbf{S}_A, \mathbf{S}_B, \mathbf{E}_A, \mathbf{E}_B$  with small coefficients. The core idea for them to exchange a key lies in realizing that the values  $\mathbf{S}_B^\top \cdot \mathbf{B}_A$  and  $\mathbf{B}_B^\top \cdot \mathbf{S}_A$  are at a short distance, and hard to compute for any other party than A and B. Leveraging a reconciliation mechanism such as the one described in Section 2.6, it is possible to extract shared bits from these values.

However, both of these constructions present important limitations. The Split-KEM from [7] is rather compact as it is instantiated from module lattice assumptions, but it is only proven IND-CPA secure, and thus cannot be used within a key-exchange protocol. FrodoKex+ from [8] achieves the stronger property UNF-1KCA ensuring implicit authentication, however it builds on plain lattice assumptions and suffers from large sizes and overall inefficiency.

{9}------------------------------------------------

Sparrow-KEM offers the best of both schemes by providing the compact sizes enabled by MLWE, and the stronger security properties required for a key-exchange protocol.

During a global setup phase formalized in Algorithm 1, a shared public matrix  $\mathbf{A} \in \mathcal{R}_q^{k \times \ell}$  is sampled. Then, the receiver A samples its public key as  $\mathbf{b}_{\mathsf{A}} \coloneqq \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{s}_{\mathsf{A}} + \mathbf{e}_{\mathsf{A}}$ , where  $\mathbf{s}_{\mathsf{A}}, \mathbf{e}_{\mathsf{A}} \leftarrow \mathcal{D}_{\mathsf{A}}^{\ell} \times \mathcal{D}_{\mathsf{A}}^{k}$ , and the sender B samples its public key as  $\mathbf{b}_{\mathsf{B}} \coloneqq \mathbf{A}_{\mathsf{A}}^{\top} \cdot \mathbf{s}_{\mathsf{B}} + \mathbf{e}_{\mathsf{B}}$ , where  $\mathbf{s}_{\mathsf{B}}, \mathbf{e}_{\mathsf{B}} \leftarrow \mathcal{D}_{\mathsf{B}}^{k} \times \mathcal{D}_{\mathsf{B}}^{\ell}$ .

Again realizing that the values  $\mathbf{s}_\mathsf{B}^\top \cdot \mathbf{b}_\mathsf{A}$  and  $\mathbf{b}_\mathsf{B}^\top \cdot \mathbf{s}_\mathsf{A} \in \mathcal{R}_q$  are at a short distance, characterized by the MLWE errors, we can extract shared bits for A and B. Thanks to the – by now standard – reconciliation tools in lattice cryptography described in Section 2.6, extraction from the two above values is performed at the cost of additionally exchanging a small ciphertext. In order to have a secure scheme, we also add some masking terms during encapsulation and decapsulation, and extract shared bits from only ctbits coordinates of the above values to limit the leakage induced by ct.

Concretely, in order for B to encapsulate to a counterpart's public key  $\mathbf{b}_{\mathsf{A}}$ , B first samples a masking term  $y \leftarrow \mathcal{D}_y$  and computes  $v \coloneqq \mathbf{s}_{\mathsf{B}}^\top \cdot \mathbf{b}_{\mathsf{A}} + y$ . Next, it computes  $\mathsf{ct} \coloneqq \mathsf{HelpRec}_B(v_{1,\dots,\mathsf{ctbits}})$ . The extracted shared key is  $K \coloneqq \mathsf{Rec}_B(v_{1,\dots,\mathsf{ctbits}},\mathsf{ct})$ , where B is a parameter of the scheme. Given  $\mathbf{b}_{\mathsf{B}}$  and  $\mathsf{ct}$ , the receiver A is then able to recover the shared secret by computing  $v' \coloneqq \mathbf{b}_{\mathsf{B}}^\top \cdot \mathbf{s}_{\mathsf{A}} + y'$ , where  $y' \leftarrow \mathcal{D}_y$ , and recovers  $K' \coloneqq \mathsf{Rec}_B(v'_{1,\dots,\mathsf{ctbits}},\mathsf{ct})$ .

One can observe that the use of MLWE samples as keys is reminiscent of the standardized KEM Kyber [30], however Sparrow-KEM uses one's private key during encapsulation instead of a fresh random vector so that the shared secret is derived using the sender's key. Sparrow-KEM also differs by using the reconciliation mechanism of FrodoKEM [25] instead of sending a full polynomial as ciphertext. This limits the leakage in case an adversary would act as a man-in-the-middle and send an invalid public key to extract the secret key of the sender, see the Decaps-OW-CPA security analysis in Theorem 4.

### <span id="page-10-1"></span>3.2 Integrating Sparrow-KEM in K-Waay

K-Waay requires advanced security properties for use in their protocol:

- UNF-1KCA, which ensures that if Alice receives a message allegedly sent by Bob, either Bob really sent it or the decapsulation will fail. This enables implicit authentication.
- IND-1BatchCCA, which is reminiscent of the more usual IND-CCA property. IND-1BatchCCA allows only decapsulation query, but with several keys and ciphertexts at once, and returns either the decapsulated shared keys, or  $\bot$  if at least one decapsulation fails.

In the following section, we will only prove that Sparrow-KEM is OW-CPA and Decaps-OW-CPA secure, and rely on the generic split-KEM transform recalled in Fig. 4 and proven in Theorem 1 to obtain a split KEM that is UNF-1KCA and IND-1BatchCCA secure.

This transform is similar to the one introduced in [17], applied to split KEMs. It works at a high-level by appending a hash of the shared key to the ciphertext, ensuring that if the adversary does not know the shared key before encapsulation, decapsulation will fail with overwhelming probability.

{11}------------------------------------------------

Our finalized Sparrow-KEM and Sym-Sparrow-KEM apply the transform with H and H' modeled as random oracles with output size of  $2\kappa$  bits. We then consider that proving the OW-CPA and Decaps-OW-CPA security of Sparrow-KEM suffices. Notably, we consider that the security loss  $Q_H + Q_{H'}$  in Theorem 1 is a proof artifact in [8] due to the use of the random oracle model (ROM) for recovering a secret value and ignore it for practical parameters.

# 4 Security analysis

This section rigorously assesses the security properties of Sparrow-KEM. We provide formal security proofs of the security notions OW-CPA and Decaps-OW-CPA required for integration in K-Waay. Additionally, we prove the deniability of Sparrow-KEM, even under leakage of the receiver's secret key.

The security analyses presented in this section rely on the standard technique of the Random Oracle Model (ROM). In this idealized model, any cryptographic hash functions used are treated as perfect random oracles, providing a unique, uniformly random output for each distinct input query. While proofs in the ROM do not guarantee security when instantiated with real-world hash functions (which have inherent structures), they provide strong heuristic evidence of security against generic adversaries and significantly simplify the analysis.

# 4.1 OW-CPA security

<span id="page-11-0"></span>**Theorem 3.** Sparrow-KEM is OW-CPA secure, as defined in Definition 7.

Formally, there exist adversaries  $\mathcal{B}_1$  against the security of the problem  $\mathsf{MLWE}_{k,\ell,q,\sigma_\mathsf{A}}$ , and  $\mathcal{B}_2$  against  $\mathsf{MLWE}_{k'=\ell+1,\ell'=k,q,\sigma_\mathsf{B}}$ , with running times  $\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}_1} \approx \mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}_2} \approx \mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{A}}$ , such that

$$\mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{ow-cpa}}_{\mathsf{Sparrow-KEM}}(\mathcal{A}) \leqslant \mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{MLWE}}_{\mathcal{B}_1} + \mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{MLWE}}_{\mathcal{B}_2} + 8\varepsilon + 2^{-\mathsf{ctbits} \cdot B}$$

where  $\varepsilon \leqslant 1/2$  is such that  $\frac{1}{\sigma_{\mathsf{B}}^2} + \frac{1}{\sigma_y^2 - \sigma_{\mathsf{B}}^2} \leqslant \eta_{\varepsilon}'(\mathbb{Z}^n)^{-2}$ .

*Proof.* We proceed with a series of hybrids starting from the OW-CPA game.

Hybrid<sub>1</sub>. This is the real OW-CPA game.

Hybrid<sub>2</sub>. In this hybrid, we replace the public key  $\mathsf{pk}_A$  by a random value in  $\mathcal{R}$ . This reduces to the MLWE problem with standard deviation  $\sigma_{\mathsf{A}}$ .

$$|\mathsf{Adv}_{\mathcal{A}}^{\mathsf{Hybrid}_1} - \mathsf{Adv}_{\mathcal{A}}^{\mathsf{Hybrid}_2}| \leqslant \mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{MLWE}_{k,\ell,q,\sigma_{\mathsf{A}}}}$$

Hybrid<sub>3</sub>. In this hybrid, we rewrite the value v in Encaps as:

$$v = (\mathbf{b}_{\mathsf{A}}^{\top} \cdot \mathbf{s}_{\mathsf{B}} + y_1) + y_2$$

where  $y_1, y_2$  are sampled respectively from  $D_{\sigma_B}$  and  $D_{\sqrt{\sigma_y^2 - \sigma_B^2}}$ .

The only difference is the separation of y into two Gaussians  $y_1 + y_2$ . We can apply the convolution lemma from Lemma 2 to show that the statistical distance between the two hybrids is negligible.

Assuming  $\frac{1}{\sigma_{\mathsf{R}}^2} + \frac{1}{\sigma_{\mathsf{u}}^2 - \sigma_{\mathsf{R}}^2} \leq \eta_{\varepsilon}'(\mathbb{Z}^n)^{-2}$ , we obtain by applying Lemma 2:

$$|\mathsf{Adv}_{\mathcal{A}}^{\mathsf{Hybrid}_2} - \mathsf{Adv}_{\mathcal{A}}^{\mathsf{Hybrid}_3}| \leqslant 8\varepsilon$$

Hybrid<sub>4</sub>. In this hybrid, we see  $\begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{b}_{\mathsf{B}} \\ \mathbf{b}_{\mathsf{A}}^{\top} \cdot \mathbf{s}_{\mathsf{B}} + y_1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{A}^{\top} \\ \mathbf{b}_{\mathsf{A}}^{\top} \end{bmatrix} \cdot \mathbf{s}_{\mathsf{B}} + \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{e}_{\mathsf{B}} \\ y_1 \end{bmatrix}$  as an MLWE sample that we replace by a random value in  $\mathcal{R}^{\ell+1}$ .

This reduces to  $\mathsf{MLWE}_{k'=\ell+1,\ell'=k,q,\sigma_{\mathsf{B}}}$ :

$$|\mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{Hybrid}_3}_{\mathcal{A}} - \mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{Hybrid}_4}_{\mathcal{A}}| \leqslant \mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{MLWE}_{k'=\ell+1,\ell'=k,q,\sigma_{\mathsf{B}}}}$$

**Conclusion.** At this stage, it is easy to see that the polynomial v in Encaps is uniform in  $\mathcal{R}$ . According to [8, Lemma 1], the vector w, conditioned on ct, looks uniformly random for the adversary. Hence, its guessing probability is  $2^{-\text{ctbits} \cdot B}$ .

#### 4.2 Decaps-OW-CPA security

We now prove the Decaps-OW-CPA security of Sparrow-KEM. Similarly to the original work [8] introducing Decaps-OW-CPA security, an exponential loss  $2^{\text{ctbits}}$  in factor of  $\mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{MLWE}}$  appears in our proof. In concrete parameters, we can compensate for this loss by targeting a higher MLWE security, and keep negligible the advantage in the proof below. That is, we could for instance asymptotically select  $\mathsf{ctbits} = \kappa$ , and MLWE parameters so that  $\mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{MLWE}} \leqslant 2^{-2\kappa}$ . Then,  $2^{\mathsf{ctbits}} \cdot \mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{MLWE}}$  is still a negligible function of the security parameter.

Remark 1. As explained in Section 3.2, the introduction of ctbits improves over a naive loss  $2^n$  that would translate from adapting the proof of [8], and opens the path for a symmetric sKEM that is Decaps-OW-CPA secure. Indeed, n in that case would be at least a few hundreds, while we can select a much lower ctbits at the cost of a slightly increased modulus q.

<span id="page-12-0"></span>**Theorem 4.** Sparrow-KEM is Decaps-OW-CPA secure, as defined in Definition 7.

Formally, there exist adversaries  $\mathcal{B}_1$  against the security of  $\mathsf{MLWE}_{k'=\ell,\ell'=k,q,\sigma_\mathsf{B}}$ , and  $\mathcal{B}_2$  against the security of  $\mathsf{MLWE}_{k+1,\ell,q,\sigma_\mathsf{A}}$ , and running times  $\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{A}} \approx \mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}_1} \approx \mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}_2}$ , such that

$$\mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{decaps-ow-cpa}}_{\mathsf{Sparrow-KEM}}(\mathcal{A}) \leqslant 2^{\mathsf{ctbits}} \cdot (\mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{MLWE}}_{\mathcal{B}_1} + \mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{MLWE}}_{\mathcal{B}_2} + \delta^{\mathsf{ctbits}}_{\mathsf{CPA}})$$

where

$$\delta_{\mathsf{CPA}} \coloneqq \max_{\substack{\mathsf{ct} \in \{0,1\} \\ u \in \mathbb{Z}_2^B}} \Pr_{w \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathbb{Z}_q} \left[ \mathsf{Rec}_B(2w,\mathsf{ct}) = u \right]$$

*Proof.* We proceed with a series of hybrids.

Hybrid<sub>1</sub>. This is the original Decaps-OW-CPA game.

Hybrid<sub>2</sub>. In this hybrid, we remove the encapsulation performed by the challenger Section 2.4, and instead sample a random ciphertext ct in  $\{0,1\}^{\text{ctbits}}$ .

In  $\mathsf{Hybrid}_2$ , the ciphertext that was computed in  $\mathsf{Hybrid}_1$  is sampled with probability  $2^{-\mathsf{ctbits}}$ , and we deduce that the advantage of the adversary verifies:

$$\mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{Hybrid}_1}_{\mathcal{A}} \leqslant 2^{\mathsf{ctbits}} \mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{Hybrid}_2}_{\mathcal{A}}$$

Hybrid<sub>3</sub>. In this hybrid, we replace the public vector  $\mathbf{b}_{\mathsf{B}}$  of Bob by a random value in  $\mathcal{R}$ . This reduces to the security of  $\mathsf{MLWE}_{k'=\ell,\ell'=k,q,\sigma_{\mathsf{B}}}$ :

$$|\mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{Hybrid}_3}_{\mathcal{A}} - \mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{Hybrid}_2}_{\mathcal{A}}| \leqslant \mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{MLWE}}$$

Hybrid<sub>4</sub>. In this hybrid, we replace  $\begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{b}_{\mathsf{A}} \\ v' \end{bmatrix}$  by a uniform value in  $\mathcal{R}^{k+1}$ .

Seeing y' as a MLWE noise of standard deviation  $\sigma_A$ , this reduces to the security of MLWE<sub>k+1,ℓ,q,\sigma\_A</sub>:

$$|\mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{Hybrid}_4}_{\mathcal{A}} - \mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{Hybrid}_3}_{\mathcal{A}}| \leqslant \mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{MLWE}}$$

Conclusion. As v' is now uniform, the probability that any adversary wins in  $\mathsf{Hybrid}_4$  is upper-bounded by  $\delta_\mathsf{CPA}^\mathsf{ctbits}$ .

The theorem statement follows by combining the hybrids.

#### 5.1 Correctness

In order to achieve an overwhelming correctness, we need to ensure that the values  $v_A$  and  $v_B$  computed by Alice and Bob are at a close distance. Precisely, when q is odd, Lemma 1 states that Alice and Bob recovers the same shared secret in  $\{0,1\}^{\text{ctbits}\cdot B}$  if:

$$||v_{\mathsf{A}} - v_{\mathsf{B}}||_{\infty} \leqslant \left| \frac{q}{2^{B+2}} \right|$$

where B is the number of bits recovered from each coordinate.

When both parties behave correctly:

• 
$$v_{\mathsf{B}} \coloneqq \mathbf{s}_{\mathsf{B}}^{\top} \cdot \mathbf{b}_{\mathsf{A}} + y = \mathbf{s}_{\mathsf{B}}^{\top} \cdot (\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{s}_{\mathsf{A}} + \mathbf{e}_{\mathsf{A}}) + y = \mathbf{s}_{\mathsf{B}}^{\top} \cdot \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{s}_{\mathsf{A}} + \underbrace{(\mathbf{s}_{\mathsf{B}}^{\top} \cdot \mathbf{e}_{\mathsf{A}} + y)}_{\text{small}}$$

• 
$$v_{\mathsf{A}} \coloneqq \mathbf{b}_{\mathsf{B}}^{\top} \cdot \mathbf{s}_{\mathsf{A}} + y' = (\mathbf{s}_{b}^{\top} \cdot \mathbf{A} + \mathbf{e}_{\mathsf{B}}^{\top}) \cdot \mathbf{s}_{\mathsf{A}} + y' = \mathbf{s}_{\mathsf{B}}^{\top} \cdot \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{s}_{\mathsf{A}} + \underbrace{(\mathbf{e}_{\mathsf{B}}^{\top} \cdot \mathbf{s}_{\mathsf{A}} + y')}_{\mathsf{small}}$$

We have

$$v_{\mathsf{A}} - v_{\mathsf{B}} = \mathbf{e}_{\mathsf{B}}^{\top} \cdot \mathbf{s}_{\mathsf{A}} + y' - (\mathbf{s}_{\mathsf{B}}^{\top} \cdot \mathbf{e}_{\mathsf{A}} + y)$$

For concrete parameters, we adapted https://github.com/pq-crystals/security-estimates to exactly evaluate the distribution of the coordinates of  $v_{\mathsf{A}} - v_{\mathsf{B}}$ , and find an overwhelming bound  $\beta$  (probability  $\geq 1 - 2^{-\kappa}$ ) on  $||v_{\mathsf{A}} - v_{\mathsf{B}}||_{\infty}$ . We then take for B the maximal value such that  $\beta \leq \left\lfloor \frac{q}{2^{B+2}} \right\rfloor$ , ensuring correct decapsulation with overwhelming probability.

We make our script available at https://github.com/GuilhemN/sparrow-kem/blob/main/security/correctness/eval\_sparrow.py.

# 6 Evaluation of Sparrow-KEM within the full protocol K-Waay

To assess the practical impact of Sparrow-KEM, we evaluate its performance within the K-Waay protocol and compare it to concurrent approaches for a post-quantum Signal key exchange protocol based on ring signatures. We evaluate two key metrics for a post-quantum X3DH: communication overhead, and computational cost. The communication overhead is primarily determined by the size of long term keys, pre-key bundles, and ciphertexts, while the computational cost is typically dominated by the cost of the split KEM or ring signature used.

We will consider three protocols for our evaluation: the ring signature based protocols HKKP [\[16\]](#page-21-2) and SPQR [\[6\]](#page-21-3), and the split KEM based protocol K-Waay [\[8\]](#page-21-6). We start with an overview of the components used in each of these protocols.

Overview of HKKP. HKKP [\[16\]](#page-21-2) uses ring signatures in order to build a deniable post-quantum X3DH protocol. They derive session keys from both a long term KEM, and an ephemeral KEM. The public key of the ephemeral KEM is provided as part of the pre-key bundle of the receiver, at the same time as a ring verification key. We consider HKKP with signed pre-keys as is the case in current Signal's X3DH; this ensures stronger forward-secrecy guarantees.

- The long term key is composed of: (i) a long-term KEM key, (ii) a long-term ring signature verification key, (iii) a long-term signature verification key.
- The receiver pre-key bundle is composed of: (i) an ephemeral KEM public key, (ii) an ephemeral ring signature verification key, (iii) a signature.
- The ciphertext is composed of: (i) a long-term KEM ciphertext, (ii) an ephemeral KEM ciphertext, (iii) a ring signature.

Overview of SPQR. SPQR [\[6\]](#page-21-3) relies on Designated Verifier Signature (DVS) for its post-quantum X3DH protocol. DVS were proven equivalent to ring signatures in the two-parties setting in [\[6,](#page-21-3) [16\]](#page-21-2). Similarly to HKKP, we consider that pre-key bundles are signed for our comparison.

- The long term key is composed of: (i) a long-term KEM key, (ii) a long-term DVS verification key, (iii) a long-term signature verification key.
- The receiver pre-key bundle is composed of: (i) a semi-static KEM public key, (ii) an ephemeral KEM public key, (iii) an ephemeral DVS verification key, (iv) a signature.
- The ciphertext is composed of: (i) a long-term KEM ciphertext, (ii) a semi-static KEM ciphertext, (iii) an ephemeral KEM ciphertext, (iv) a DVS signature.

Overview of K-Waay. K-Waay [\[8\]](#page-21-6) is built using a deniable split KEM. The protocol differs from the two previous constructions in that both the sender and receiver upload a pre-key bundle, which is retrieved by the counterpart for the key derivation. K-Waay also relies on a long-term KEM, and an ephemeral KEM to provide both key compromise resistance, and forward secrecy. The split KEM keys are used for implicit authentication of the parties and are included in pre-key bundles.

• The long term key is composed of: (i) a long-term KEM key, (ii) a long-term signature verification key.

{18}------------------------------------------------

- The receiver pre-key bundle is composed of: (i) an ephemeral KEM public key, (ii) an ephemeral receiver split KEM key, (iii) a signature.
- The sender pre-key bundle is composed of: (i) an ephemeral sender split KEM key, (ii) a signature.
- The ciphertext is composed of: (i) a long-term KEM ciphertext, (ii) an ephemeral KEM ciphertext, (iii) an ephemeral split KEM ciphertext.

# 6.2 Speed benchmarks

Collins et al. [8] observed that the non-standard primitive (ring signature or split KEM) is the bottleneck in post-quantum X3DH. This can be explained as post-quantum KEMs are typically inexpensive to run, as opposed to the expensive computations used in the shortest ring signatures for trapdoor sampling (lattice-based), cut-and-choose (hash functions) for Falafl, isogenies computations for CSIDH, or the matrix multiplication in FrodoKex+. Note that the cost of the key generation of the ring signature scheme or split KEM is as important as the other algorithms in the case of X3DH. Indeed, these primitives are used for ephemeral values, and thus keys are sampled for every session as part of a prekey bundle and then used only once.

We focus in this section on studying the performance improvement provided by Sparrow-KEM, compared to previous works on split KEMs and ring signatures.

We implemented Sparrow-KEM in C<sup>1</sup>. We implemented Montgomery modular reduction, an NTT for fast ring multiplications, and table-based discrete Gaussian samplers for sampling the secrets and noises. We compare its efficiency to existing implementations of FrodoKex+, and against the ring signatures Raptor, Calamari and Falafl. DualRing-LB and Gandalf do not provide implementations, hence, we measured the performance of Falcon 512 as a baseline, and as a likely upper bound on future performance improvements of short ring signature implementations.

We provide our measurements in Table 4, performed on a Ryzen Pro 7 5850U-based @ 3GHz based laptop. We observe a  $40\times$  performance improvement of using Sparrow-KEM over FrodoKex+, which was already previously shown to significantly outperform Raptor. Sparrow-KEM even outperforms Falcon, and is  $6\times$  faster. Thus, even assuming significant optimizations in the implementation of the aforementioned ring signature schemes, it is likely that K-Waay with Sparrow-KEM will keep outperforming X3DH based on ring signatures.

# 7 Discussion on advantages and limitations

This section delves into a comparative analysis of using our improved instantiation of split KEM and K-Waay for a post-quantum X3DH protocol, and the approach based on ring signatures. We focus on key performance metrics, and practical considerations to elucidate the advantages and limitations of each approach.

Communication overhead. Sparrow-KEM significantly improves the communication cost of K-Waay to the point that the two approaches to build a post-quantum X3DH based on a split KEM or ring signature appear closeby. The total communication overhead of the proposed protocols differ only by a small margin, depending on the exact underlying primitives used. Communication overhead is thus no longer a significantly differentiating metric in favor of the ring signature approach, and K-Waay is a competitive proposal.

Security model and deniability. While both split-KEMs and ring signatures offer post-quantum security, their use in a post-quantum X3DH differ in their underlying security models and the level of deniability they provide. The K-Waay protocol, relying on split-KEMs, inherently involves two pre-key bundles, which might introduce additional complexities when arguing deniability compared to single-bundle approaches based on ring signatures which only introduce a pre-key bundle for the receiver. Pratical deniability of each of these models however rather seems to be a legal question, as well as dependent on the broader protocol implementation – for instance whether users are authenticated by the server when sending their ciphertext. It is interesting further work to explore practical deniability [\[29,](#page-22-11) [32\]](#page-22-12).

Efficiency and flexibility. Split-KEMs generally have simpler and more efficient designs compared to ring signatures. [\[8\]](#page-21-6) observed that K-Waay with FrodoKex+ was faster than ring signatures based schemes. Sparrow-KEM increases this gap and is 40× faster than FrodoKex+. We also remark that secure messaging applications are likely to be deployed in adversarional environments, where side-channel countermeasures are desirable. Masking techniques countermeasures can be more readily applied to split KEMs compared to ring signatures, providing stronger confidence in the protocol.

Modular integration. While Sparrow-KEM was designed to optimize K-Waay, the Sparrow-KEM themselves are cryptographic primitives. Other protocols could potentially integrate these KEMs as building blocks without needing to adopt the entire K-Waay protocol structure. They could leverage the technique (the efficient, deniable split KEM construction) independently, thus benefiting from the paper's core innovation without direct dependence on K-Waay as a complete protocol framework. We notably could expect variants of the key-exchange protocols based on ring signatures [\[16,](#page-21-2) [6\]](#page-21-3), to leverage split KEMs.

Before proving the main theorem, we introduce useful lemmas.

<span id="page-25-0"></span>**Lemma 5 (Lemma 4.4 in [21], extended).** For any  $\tau > 1$ , and any lattice  $\Delta \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ ,

$$\Pr\left[\|\mathbf{z}\| > \tau \sigma \sqrt{n} \mid \mathbf{z} \leftarrow \mathcal{D}_{\Delta,\sigma}\right] \leqslant C^n$$

where 
$$C = \tau \cdot e^{\frac{1}{2}(1-\tau^2)} < 1$$
. In particular, if  $(\tau \geqslant 1 + \frac{\kappa \log 2}{3n})$  or if  $(\tau \geqslant 1 + \sqrt{\frac{4\kappa \log 2}{n}})$ , then  $C^n \leqslant 2^{-\kappa}$ .

Lemma 5 is easily derived from the proof of Lemma 4.4 in [21], by observing that [2] applies to any lattice and not only  $\mathbb{Z}^n$ .
