#### Abstract

Lookup arguments are a key technique in SNARKs for reducing the cost of operations which are "arithmetization-unfriendly", such as range checks and bitwise comparisons. The idea is to encode the valid outputs of the operation in a publicly known table, and then prove that every element of the SNARK witness belongs to the table. Existing constructions for lookup arguments, however, are designed for working over fields, making them incompatible with recent post-quantum lattice-based schemes that operate over rings.

In this work we formalize lookup arguments over rings for tables containing arbitrary ring elements. We bring attention to systematic issues that arise when translating techniques from fields to rings by showing several known lookup arguments are susceptible to attacks. We then extend two central polynomial IOPs, Plookup and LogUp, over the ring R = Zq[X]/(X<sup>d</sup> + 1), and show how to compile them with polynomial commitments based on lattice assumptions to get succinct lattice-based lookup arguments. We additionally show how to apply ring lookups to obtain succinct arguments for batch-verification of RAM updates where the RAM entries are arbitrary ring elements.

Keywords: lookup arguments, PIOPs, ring arithmetic, lattices, batch-verifiable RAM

## <span id="page-3-0"></span>1 Introduction

The goal of verifiable computation is to guarantee integrity by ensuring an untrusted party performs a task correctly. A succinct non-interactive argument of knowledge, also known as a SNARK [\[Bit+14\]](#page-63-0), is a key tool for verifiable computation in which a prover convinces a verifier that a statement is true by sending a short proof in a single round of communication.

A recent line of work has explored lookup arguments, which are a central technique to mitigate the cost of SNARK-unfriendly operations (i.e., those that are not efficiently represented as an arithmetic circuit). The idea is to encode all valid outputs of the operation in a publicly known table and then prove that the SNARK witness is contained in the table. Indeed, even the simple task of performing bitwise comparison of two field elements typically requires the binary representation of the elements, which incurs a logarithmic overhead. Other examples of SNARK-unfriendly operations that can be handled by lookup arguments include hash function evaluation and proving a value is within a finite range.

Since they were first introduced by Arya [\[BCGJM18\]](#page-62-2) and Plookup [\[GW20\]](#page-65-0), lookup arguments have grown in popularity (see [\[HACF25\]](#page-65-1) for a recent survey) and have found several applications including zero-knowledge virtual machines [\[STW23;](#page-67-0) [AST23\]](#page-62-3), verifiable computation on encrypted data [\[CCCFGSV25\]](#page-63-1), verifiable RAM computation [\[DGPPS24\]](#page-64-0), non-arithmetic operations in deep learning for zero-knowledge LLMs [\[SLZ24\]](#page-67-1), and "a la carte" SNARKs where the prover cost grows with the size of the circuit [\[DXNT23;](#page-64-1) [CGGSS23\]](#page-64-2). There are several flavors of lookup arguments which are optimized for different settings, including, but not limited to, indexed lookups [\[STW23\]](#page-67-0) (which fixes the order of the lookup query), matrix-lookups [\[CFFLL24;](#page-63-2) [CGGSS23;](#page-64-2) [DXNT23\]](#page-64-1) (which looks up a tuple instead of a single value), and structured-table lookups [\[STW23\]](#page-67-0) (in which the lookup table can be decomposed into several smaller lookup tables).

Today, the most popular, general-purpose SNARKs are designed to handle computations that can be naturally represented as an arithmetic circuit over a large prime order finite field [\[Gro16;](#page-65-2) [GWC19;](#page-65-3) [Mar19\]](#page-66-0). However, these SNARKs usually rely on discrete-log or pairing-based assumptions which are insecure against a quantum adversary. As a result, there has been a recent surge in the design of post-quantum proof systems. One of the most promising post-quantum paradigms is that which relies on polynomial lattice-rings. These lattice-based proof systems [\[BBCPGL18;](#page-62-4) [ENS20;](#page-64-3) [BLNS20;](#page-63-3) [ACK21;](#page-62-5) [LNP22;](#page-66-1) [BS22;](#page-63-4) [BCS23;](#page-63-5) [NS24;](#page-66-2) [BC24;](#page-62-6) [BC25\]](#page-62-7) offer a competitively small proof size, fast verifier, and low memory costs. Even beyond lattice-based proof systems, computation over rings is prevalent in more general settings such as proving operations done on homomorphically encrypted data [\[PLFT25;](#page-66-3) [CCCFGSV25\]](#page-63-1) and computations that involve fixed or floating point arithmetic [\[GNSV21\]](#page-64-4).

Despite the independent progress on both lookup arguments and verifiable computation over rings, their overlap, namely lookup arguments over rings, is scant. In fact, majority of prior lookup argument constructions are designed for finite fields (or depend on a polynomial commitment scheme such as KZG [\[KZG10\]](#page-65-4) which must be instantiated over fields) to ensure compatibility with general purpose SNARKs. A naive attempt to "directly translate" prior works from fields to rings often fails due to subtleties in the algebraic underpinnings of the lookup relations, as their security proofs often rely on field-specific properties such as unique factorization and multiplicative inverses.

Dually motivated by (1) the growing interest in verifiable computation over rings and (2) a systematic oversight in the literature which poses barriers to extending lookup arguments from fields to rings, we ask:

{4}------------------------------------------------

Can we build a post-quantum lookup argument that directly supports arbitrary ring elements?

In this work, we answer in the affirmative and further formalize the study of lookup arguments over rings.

### <span id="page-4-0"></span>1.1 Contributions

In this work, we formalize the study of lookup arguments over rings. We start by identifying challenges that arise when extending prior works from fields to rings. Then, we show how to resolve these issues by introducing two new indexed lookup arguments over rings, which we call Ring-Plookup and Ring-LogUp. We first describe polynomial IOPs for the lookup relations, and then discuss the process of compiling our IOPs to succinct arguments using lattice-based polynomial commitments over rings. Finally, as an application of our ring lookups, we give a new protocol for batch verification of RAM programs over rings.

A more detailed summary of our contributions is as follows.

- 1. Challenges with extending prior works to rings. In this work, we identify two major obstacles in grand-product arguments which prevent prior works on lookup arguments over field elements from being directly extended to arbitrary ring elements. We take inspiration from prior works [\[BLMG25;](#page-63-6) [CMM19\]](#page-64-5) which give attacks on shuffle arguments (which are a special case of lookups). We generalize these attack techniques to lookup arguments and show that trivially restating the Plookup [\[GW20\]](#page-65-0) and Lasso [\[CCCFGSV25;](#page-63-1) [STW23\]](#page-67-0) relations over rings is vulnerable to an attack that swaps entries in the CRT components from which the ring splits. Additionally, we identify a second type of attack due to the presence of zero-divisors in the ring and show how it applies when trivially extending the LogUp proof technique [\[Hab22\]](#page-65-5) over rings.
- 2. New lookup arguments over rings. We give two new protocols for indexed lookup arguments and prove security directly over rings. Specifically, we carefully modify the prior works Plookup [\[GW20\]](#page-65-0) and LogUp [\[Hab22\]](#page-65-5), both of whose lookup relations were originally stated over fields. We call our new lookup arguments Ring-Plookup and Ring-LogUp, respectively. Our results require careful algebraic techniques to handle the aforementioned challenges that arise when using grand-products over polynomial rings instead of fields, such as lack of both unique factorization and multiplicative inverses. Along the way, we formalize a toolkit of several PIOPs over rings for vector relations, offline memory-checking, binary-valued vector tests, and integer-valued vector tests. We also discuss how to use lattice-based proof systems and polynomial commitments [\[Ngu24\]](#page-66-4) to compile our polynomial oracle proofs to a succinct, non-interactive argument.

### <span id="page-6-0"></span>1.2 Related Work

We provide a summary on prior techniques for constructing lookup arguments. Most practically efficient lookup arguments for general lookup tables use one of two main underlying techniques: grand-product or log-derivatives. We briefly explain below that these techniques cannot directly be instantiated over rings, thus requiring new techniques for lookups with arbitrary ring elements.

- 1. Grand product arguments. Early works on lookup arguments, including Plookup [\[GW20\]](#page-65-0), Arya [\[BCGJM18\]](#page-62-2), and Halo2 [\[BGH20\]](#page-63-7), use grand-product claims. At a high level, a grand product argument proves a valid lookup by showing that a randomized product of the values from both the lookup query vector and the table vector are equal. This is translated to a polynomial identity test by "embedding" the lookup table and query values as roots of linear factors of univariate polynomials. However, as observed by prior works on permutation arguments [\[BLMG25;](#page-63-6) [CMM19\]](#page-64-5), grand-product claims must be carefully handled over rings because rings are not unique factorization domains.
- 2. Logarithmic-derivatives. The logarithmic-derivatives approach was first introduced by LogUp [\[Hab22\]](#page-65-5) and later used by several other lookup arguments including Flookup [\[GK22\]](#page-64-6), cq [\[EFG22\]](#page-64-7), LogUp-GKR [\[PH23\]](#page-66-5), cq+, zkcq+, and cq++ [\[CFFLL24\]](#page-63-2), Locq [\[ZSG24\]](#page-68-0), µseek [\[CFG25\]](#page-63-8), LogSpartan [\[GPS25\]](#page-65-6), Logalookup [\[TNLKN25\]](#page-67-2), Logup<sup>∗</sup> [\[Sou25\]](#page-67-3), and tlookup [\[SLZ24\]](#page-67-1).

The log-derivatives technique remains a popular approach in the lookup argument literature for several reasons. First, the PIOP from [\[Hab22\]](#page-65-5) improves upon Plookup in the batched lookup setting, where the validity of multiple lookup queries is proved simultaneously. This is because the LogUp prover need only send a constant number of oracles by combining all lookup instances into a single vector. However in Plookup the prover must create an auxiliary vector and send an oracle for each instance in the batch. Additionally, the log-derivatives technique achieves an asymptotically faster prover compared to other approaches (see [\[ZSG24,](#page-68-0) Table 1]) when table preprocessing is allowed.

The idea of LogUp is to express the lookup claim as a summation of rational terms, where each denominator contains a value from the lookup query or a value from the lookup table. To prove the log-derivatives claim, [\[Hab22\]](#page-65-5) uses a special grand-product statement where soundness relies on all field elements having multiplicative inverses. In the interactive lookup protocol, the prover computes the individual rational terms within the summation, treats them as polynomial evaluations, and then reduces the lookup claim to polynomial identity tests via sum-check. As a result, this approach does not immediately extend to rings because multiplicative inverses are not guaranteed.

Lookups for Structured Tables. Lasso [\[STW23\]](#page-67-0) is a recent lookup argument that supports tables with special structure. Subsequent works on structured lookups include Jolt [\[AST23\]](#page-62-3), TaSSLE [\[Dor24\]](#page-64-8), Shout[\[ST25\]](#page-67-4), and the Lasso over rings protocol from [\[CCCFGSV25\]](#page-63-1). According to Lasso, a structured table satisfies two properties, decomposability and MLE-structure. Decomposability means that the table can be expressed as a tensor product of smaller tables, so that a single lookup into the full table corresponds to several lookups into the small tables. MLE-structure means that the low-degree (not necessarily multilinear) extension polynomial of each small table can be evaluated in logarithmic time. These protocols are based on proving a special form of matrix-vector 

{7}------------------------------------------------

multiplication based on the observation that if  $\mathbf{a} \subseteq \mathbf{b}$ , then for each  $\mathbf{a}_i$  there is a one-hot vector  $\mathbf{v}_i$  such that  $\mathbf{v}_i^T \mathbf{b} = \mathbf{a}_i$ . Then, it suffices for the prover to show that there exists a binary matrix  $V \in \{0,1\}^{M \times N}$  (whose rows are one-hot vectors) such that  $V\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{a}$ . By taking advantage of the underlying table structure, the prover cost depends on the size of their lookup query and the size of a sub-table, instead of the size of the full table. While this line of work offers competitive performance by taking advantage of the sparse matrix-vector constraints, it does not support arbitrary, unstructured tables.

Lookups with Sublinear Prover. Early works on lookup arguments have a prover cost that depends on the size of the table itself. However, by preprocessing the table in (quasi)-linear time, subsequent works have achieved an online prover whose cost is sublinear in the table length (i.e., depends on the length of the lookup query). Sublinear prover lookups have been achieved in both the log-derivatives and matrix-vector product setting. This line of work includes Baloo [ZGKMR22], Caulk [ZBKMNS22], Caulk+ [PK22], Flookup [GK22], cq [EFG22], cq+ [CFFLL24], Locq [ZSG24], and  $\mu$ -seek [CFG25]. All protocols use the univariate KZG polynomial commitment scheme [KZG10] for table preprocessing and (except for  $\mu$ -seek [CFG25]) also use KZG for the lookup protocol itself. However, the KZG commitment scheme currently does not have a lattice-based analogue and therefore it is unclear how to leverage quotient polynomials over lattices for faster lookups. Indeed, [ACLMT22] presented a promising lattice-based vector commitment scheme that mimics the algebraic techniques from pairing-based schemes. However, the underlying assumption, k-R-ISIS, was later morally broken by [WW24] leading to doubts about the scheme's knowledge soundness.

Separately, recent work [CCCFFG25] give a sublinear prover lookup argument that, at a high-level, combines Kedlaya-Umans polynomial preprocessing [KU08; KU11] with Generlized-Lasso [STW23] and only makes black-box use of an underlying vector commitment scheme. While Kedlaya-Umans technique is known to work for extension rings of the form  $\mathbb{Z}_q[x]/(E(x))$  for any monic polynomial E(x), we do not explore this approach due to its extreme practical inefficiency.

Duplex [HYOK25] and Notus [XHTP24] are recent works that achieve a sublinear prover and deviate from prior approaches by building a lookup argument using RSA accumulators over an RSA group. However, this approach is inherently not post-quantum secure.

Finally, we remark that Baloo [ZGKMR22] uses the matrix-vector approach which exploits sparsity to a succinctly describe a binary matrix  $V \in \{0,1\}^{M \times N}$ . It is not immediately clear how to efficiently handle these sparse matrices over lattice rings for lookup arguments, and we leave an exploration of this approach to future work.

**Proof Systems over Rings.** LatticeFold [BC24] and LatticeFold+ [BC25] are recent works that give lattice-based folding schemes and range proofs over rings to prove a vector of ring elements has small norm. The LatticeFold+ range proof can be extended to a lookup, but it has limited expressivity since it only supports integral-valued ring elements (i.e., constant term polynomials).

Zinc [GWHD25] introduces a hash-based SNARK to prove statements over the integers. One of the contributions is a framework for proving NP statements over  $\mathbb{Q}$  wherein the witness must contain entries in  $\mathbb{Z}$ .

Finally, recent work on verifiable FHE [CCCFGSV25] extends Lasso from fields to rings. We identify a bug in the lookup argument theorem in the first version of this work. This issue has been fixed in a subsequent version by instead proving the lookup argument over fields directly and using a table which consists of small integers that are units in the full ring.

## <span id="page-9-0"></span>2 Technical Overview

Lookup arguments. A lookup argument is commonly expressed as a set membership check. The goal is to prove that a ⊆ b, where the lookup table is a vector b ∈ Σ <sup>N</sup> with length N and the lookup query is a vector a ∈ Σ<sup>M</sup> with length M for some alphabet Σ. This is equivalent to proving that for every index i ∈ [M] in the lookup query, there exists some index j ∈ [N] in the table such that a<sup>i</sup> = b<sup>j</sup> . Throughout this work, we allow the lookup query a to contain repeated entries but we assume that the entries in the public table b are unique. We do not consider zero-knowledge, though our results can be made zero-knowledge using standard proof composition techniques.

Nearly all prior works consider a lookup argument for entries in some finite field F. In this work we consider lookups for unstructured tables containing arbitrary ring elements from the polynomial ring R = Zq[x]/(x <sup>d</sup> + 1) for prime q and power-of-two d. This ring is a standard choice for latticebased cryptography.

### <span id="page-9-1"></span>2.1 Common Challenges to building lookups over rings

Our goal is to construct lookup arguments that support tables containing arbitrary ring elements from R. We bring awareness to common challenges that arise when translating lookup techniques over field to that over rings.

A straightforward approach to build a ring lookup is to simply take prior work over fields and directly run it over rings using, e.g., sum-check for rings [\[BCS21\]](#page-62-9) and lattice-based polynomial commitments [\[CMNW24;](#page-64-9) [NS24;](#page-66-2) [HSS24;](#page-65-11) [NOZ26\]](#page-66-7). However, in this work we systematically demonstrate that this approach is susceptible to attacks, since the security of many lookup arguments crucially relies on field-specific properties, including unique factorization and multiplicative inverses. In particular, we identify two common attacks.

- 1. CRT-component swapping attack. We generalize the attack technique identified in prior literature on permutation arguments over rings [\[BLMG25;](#page-63-6) [CMM19\]](#page-64-5). Specifically, we can view a ring lookup argument over the CRT basis and show that each CRT slot may be a valid lookup instance, while their combination yields an invalid lookup over the full ring. We show that Plookup [\[GW20\]](#page-65-0) and Lasso over rings presented in the first version of [\[CCCFGSV25\]](#page-63-1) are susceptible to this attack.
- 2. Zero-divisor attack. Fields are integral domains (i.e., the product of non-zero elements is non-zero) which is a useful property to rely on when proving soundness of grand-products. We show that the presence of zero-divisors in a ring allows a cheating prover to construct a malicious grand-product polynomial that always satisfies a zero-test, thus breaking soundness. We use this to demonstrate that the LogUp proof technique [\[Hab22\]](#page-65-5) is unsound over rings.

In this work we avoid these common pitfalls and instead provide techniques to safely extend popular lookup arguments from fields to rings.

#### <span id="page-9-2"></span>2.2 Ring-Plookup

Original Plookup over fields. Plookup [\[GW20\]](#page-65-0) is one of the first lookup arguments and uses a grand-product argument to prove that the evaluations of a committed polynomial over a finite field F are contained in a publicly known table of values.

{10}------------------------------------------------

We recall the original Plookup relation to show that  $\mathbf{a} \subseteq \mathbf{b}$ . Let  $\mathbf{s} := \mathsf{merge}_{\mathbf{b}}(\mathbf{a}) \in \mathbb{F}^{N+M}$  be the list given by  $\mathbf{a}$  concatenated with  $\mathbf{b}$ , sorted by  $\mathbf{b}$ , i.e., the different values in  $\mathbf{s}$  appear in the same order as they do in  $\mathbf{b}$ . The key observation of Plookup is that if  $\mathbf{a} \subseteq \mathbf{b}$ , the non-zero elements of  $\{(\mathbf{s}_i + \beta \mathbf{s}_{i+1})\}_{i \in [N+M-1]}$  and  $\{(\mathbf{b}_j + \beta \mathbf{b}_{j+1})\}_{j \in [N-1]}$  are the same, where  $\beta \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathbb{F}$ . Choosing a random  $\gamma \stackrel{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathbb{F}$ , these lists of tuples are embedded into two separate bivariate polynomials

$$F(\beta, \gamma) := (1 + \beta)^M \prod_{i \in [M]} (\gamma + \mathbf{a}_i) \prod_{j \in [N-1]} (\gamma(1+\beta) + \mathbf{b}_j + \beta \mathbf{b}_{j+1})$$

$$\tag{1}$$

and

<span id="page-10-1"></span><span id="page-10-0"></span>
$$G(\beta, \gamma) := \prod_{i \in [N+M-1]} (\gamma(1+\beta) + \mathbf{s}_i + \beta \mathbf{s}_{i+1}). \tag{2}$$

Claim 3.1 of [GW20] proves that, over a finite field  $\mathbb{F}$ ,  $F \equiv G$  as bivariate polynomials if and only if  $\mathbf{a} \subseteq \mathbf{b}$  and  $\mathbf{s}$  is the vector obtained by concatenating  $\mathbf{a}$  to  $\mathbf{b}$  and sorting according to  $\mathbf{b}$ . At a high-level, the proof relies on unique factorization to show that every factor in F corresponds to a factor in G, which means for all  $i \in [M]$ , there is a  $j \in [N]$  such that  $\mathbf{a}_i = \mathbf{b}_j$  over the field. This polynomial relation is used to build an interactive argument: evaluating the bivariates F and G at the random point  $(\beta, \gamma)$  and checking that  $\frac{F(\beta, \gamma)}{G(\beta, \gamma)} = 1$  reduces the lookup claim to a polynomial identity test.

This work: Ring-Plookup. In Section 4, we show that the original Plookup relation is unsound when instantiated over rings by giving an attack in which **a** is an invalid lookup into **b** yet the grand-product relation is still satisfied over the ring. Our attack takes inspiration from [BLMG25] by swapping the values within one CRT component to produce an invalid lookup query. Since the order of the lookup argument is not fixed across different components, this swapping is not "detected" by the Plookup relation and allows an invalid lookup query to still be valid within each component.

In Section 5.1, we show how to safely extend the original Plookup to the ring  $\mathcal{R}$ , avoiding the aforementioned attack. We first generalize the Plookup bivariate polynomial relation to rings using the technique introduced in [BLMG25]; we extend F and G to polynomials of three variables where the coefficient of the new variable is carefully chosen from a *challenge space* to guarantee that the two polynomials are equal if and only if  $\mathbf{a} \subseteq \mathbf{b}$  over the ring. Informally, a challenge space is a subset of the ring such that the pairwise difference between elements is invertible. The coefficient on this new variable also enforces that the order of the lookup is consistent in each CRT component (i.e., it induces an *indexing* map on the lookup). Our new indexed Ring-Plookup relation is as follows

**Lemma 2.1** (Ring-Plookup relation, informal). Let  $\mathcal{R}$  be a suitable ring. The following equality holds

$$\prod_{k=1}^{M+N-1} \left( \mathbf{s}_{k} + \mathbf{s}_{k+1} \cdot x_{1} + \boldsymbol{\sigma}_{k} \cdot x_{2} - x_{3} \right) = \prod_{i=1}^{M} \left( \mathbf{a}_{i} + \mathbf{a}_{i} \cdot x_{1} + \mathbf{c}_{i} \cdot x_{2} - x_{3} \right) \cdot \prod_{j=1}^{N-1} \left( \mathbf{b}_{j} + \mathbf{b}_{j+1} \cdot x_{1} + g(j) \cdot x_{2} - x_{3} \right) .$$
(3)

over  $\mathcal{R}[x_1, x_2, x_3]$ , if and only if

{11}------------------------------------------------

- 1. a ∈ R<sup>M</sup> is a valid indexed lookup into table b ∈ R<sup>N</sup> with index vector c ∈ CM;
- 2. s ∈ RN+<sup>M</sup> is the vector (a, b) sorted by b;
- 3. c ∈ C<sup>M</sup> and σ ∈ CM+<sup>N</sup> such that σi+j−<sup>1</sup> = c<sup>i</sup> = g(j) if and only if a<sup>i</sup> = b<sup>j</sup> ,

where g : [N] → C is a publicly known injective map and C ⊆ R is a challenge space.

Finally, we present Ring-Plookup (Construction [5.5\)](#page-24-0), a PIOP for the Ring-Plookup polynomial relation over rings. Our PIOP avoids performing a rational test on <sup>F</sup>(β,γ) G(β,γ) and instead proves the trivariate polynomial equality directly extending the sub-protocols and techniques from [\[BCG20\]](#page-62-10) to rings.

### <span id="page-11-0"></span>2.3 Ring-LogUp

Original LogUp over fields. The logarithmic-derivatives approach was first introduced by LogUp [\[Hab22\]](#page-65-5). The key observation in LogUp is that a grand product statement can be transformed into a summation of rational terms by first taking its logarithm and then taking the first derivative. Informally, the LogUp relation (Lemma 5, [\[Hab22\]](#page-65-5)) says that a ⊆ b if and only if there exists a vector m ∈ Z <sup>N</sup> , counting the multiplicity of each element of b as it appears in the look up query a, such that

<span id="page-11-2"></span><span id="page-11-1"></span>
$$\sum_{i \in [N]} \frac{1}{x - \mathbf{a}_i} = \sum_{j \in [N]} \frac{\mathbf{m}[\mathbf{b}_j]}{x - \mathbf{b}_j}.$$
(4)

The backwards direction of this bijective claim (i.e., if Equation [\(4\)](#page-11-1) holds, then a ⊆ b) is proven in [\[Hab22\]](#page-65-5) by rewriting Equation [\(4\)](#page-11-1) as a summation over the entire field and clearing the denominator,

$$\sum_{v \in \mathbb{F}} (\mathbf{m_a}[v] - \mathbf{m_b}[v]) \prod_{z \in \mathbb{F}, z \neq v} (x - z) = 0$$
(5)

where ma[v] counts the number of times v appears in a and mb[v] = m[v] for v ∈ b, otherwise mb[v] = 0. At a high level, the argument is that if Equation [\(5\)](#page-11-2) holds, it must be because mb[v] = ma[v] for all v ∈ F since the product of non-zero elements in Q z∈F,z̸=v (x − z) is non-zero. So, by definition of m<sup>a</sup> and mb, a is a valid lookup into b.

The LogUp relation is used to build a PIOP in which the prover constructs an auxiliary polynomial by evaluating the individual rational terms in the summation from Equation [\(4\)](#page-11-1) at a random field element. However, these values may not be computable over rings due to the lack of multiplicative inverses. Beyond this, we note that in [\[Hab22\]](#page-65-5) the rational terms are viewed in the field of fractions. However, since rings are not integral domains it is possible that the denominator of a rational term is a zero-divisor and therefore the fraction is not well-defined.

This work: Ring-LogUp. In Section [4,](#page-17-0) we show that LogUp is unsound over rings by giving an attack that exploits the presence of zero-divisors in a ring. The idea is that a cheating prover can construct a polynomial that, when evaluated at any point in the ring, will always contain a product of zero-divisors. This polynomial will always satisfy a zero-test, even when the lookup query is invalid.

In Section [5.2](#page-25-0) we fix this issue by introducing Ring-LogUp, a new lookup argument that safely extends LogUp to suitable lattice-rings. Similarly to the Plookup case, we achieve Ring-LogUp by 

{12}------------------------------------------------

introducing a new indeterminate variable, i.e., a "tag", in the denominator of the rational terms whose coefficient is carefully chosen from a special subset C of the ring. This also yields an indexed lookup relation, as the special coefficient will induce a map over the full ring between each entry in the lookup query and the corresponding index in the table. We observe that the proof technique from LogUp (1) relies on fields being integral domains and (2) involves rewriting Lemma [5.7](#page-26-0) as a summation over the entire ring, at which point there are not enough special "tags" to embed in the denominator since C ⊂ R. We overcome these issues by proving our lookup relation using a different technique. Our new Ring-LogUp relation is as follows.

Lemma 2.2 (Ring-LogUp relation, informal). Let R = R<sup>1</sup> × R<sup>2</sup> be a suitable ring. The following equality holds

$$\sum_{i \in [M]} \frac{1}{\mathbf{a}_i + \mathbf{c}_i x_1 - x_2} = \sum_{j \in [N]} \frac{\mathbf{m}[\mathbf{b}_j]}{\mathbf{b}_j + g(j)x_1 - x_2}$$

$$(6)$$

if and only if

- 1. a ∈ R<sup>M</sup> is a valid indexed lookup into table b ∈ R<sup>N</sup> with index map c ∈ CM;
- 2. there exists a vector m ∈ Z N q that counts the multiplicity of each element in a as it appears in b;
- 3. c<sup>i</sup> = g(j) if and only if a<sup>i</sup> = b<sup>j</sup> ,

where g : [|C|] → C is a publicly known bijection and C ⊂ R is a challenge space.

Finally, we present a Ring-LogUp PIOP (Construction [5.11\)](#page-31-0) for our new relation over rings that uses the standard sum-check approach from prior works. By working over a ring that decomposes into exactly two fields, we argue that the denominator of each rational term computed in the PIOP is invertible with high probability since it is a uniformly random ring element. We also ensure that each rational term in Equation [\(4\)](#page-11-1) is well-defined by viewing it in the total ring of fractions, a generalization of the total field of fractions to non-integral domains.

#### <span id="page-12-0"></span>2.4 Succinct Lattice-Based Lookup Arguments

We describe how the polynomial IOP protocols in our work can be used to construct succinct lattice-based lookup arguments.

<span id="page-12-1"></span>Compiling PIOPs to Succinct Arguments. Following works such as [\[CHMMVW20\]](#page-64-10), it is well-known that polynomial IOPs can be used to construct succinct arguments by compiling them using a polynomial commitment scheme. A polynomial commitment scheme works like a standard commitment scheme whose message space consists of polynomials over a suitable domain, with the addition of an evaluation protocol. The evaluation protocol is an interactive argument that allows a prover to convince a verifier that the polynomial inside a particular commitment satisfies a given input/output relation. To obtain a succinct argument, each oracle message sent by the prover as part of the polynomial IOP is replaced with a commitment to that message, and the answer to every polynomial evaluation query made by the verifier during the polynomial IOP is supplied by the prover. Thus, to compile the polynomial IOP protocols in our work to succinct lookup arguments, we require a succinct argument for polynomial evaluation as defined in the following relation.

{13}------------------------------------------------

**Definition 2.3** (Evaluation relation, informal). The **evaluation** relation  $R_{\text{Eval}}(pp)$  is the set of tuples  $(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{w}) = (\mathcal{R}, N, C, y, z), p, \rho)$  where  $\mathcal{R}$  is a ring,  $N \in \mathbb{N}$  is a degree bound, C is a commitment,  $\rho$  lies in the randomness space of the commitment scheme, and  $y, z \in \mathcal{R}$  are respectively an evaluation point and an evaluation, C is a commitment to p using randomness  $\rho$  and p(y) = z.

Example Instantiation with Lattice-Based Polynomial Commitments. We explain how to instantiate the above framework using the Greyhound polynomial commitment scheme [Ngu24]. Greyhound provides a lattice-based argument system for the following relation which has proof size  $O(\log n)$  and verification time  $O(\sqrt{n})$ :

<span id="page-13-1"></span>**Definition 2.4.** Let  $R_{b_0,b_1}$  be defined as the set of tuples

$$(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{w}) = ((\mathcal{R}, A, B, D, \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}, \mathbf{u}, y, b_0, b_1, n, m, r), ((\mathbf{s}_i)_{i \in [r]}, (\mathbf{t}_i)_{i \in [r]}))$$

where  $\mathcal{R} := \mathbb{Z}_p[x]/(x^d+1)$  for prime p and power-of-two d,  $A \in \mathcal{R}^{n \times m}$ ,  $B \in \mathcal{R}^{n \times nr \log_{b_1}(p)}$ ,  $D \in \mathcal{R}^{n \times r \log_{b_1}(p)}$ ,  $\mathbf{a} \in \mathcal{R}^m$ ,  $\mathbf{b} \in \mathcal{R}^r$ ,  $\mathbf{u} \in \mathcal{R}^n$ ,  $y \in \mathcal{R}$ ,  $\mathbf{s}_i \in \mathcal{R}^m$  and  $\mathbf{t}_i \in \mathcal{R}^{n \log_{b_1}(p)}$ , satisfying

- $\forall i \in [r], A\mathbf{s}_i = G_{b_1,n}\mathbf{t}_i$ , where  $G_{b_1,n}$  is a gadget matrix containing powers of  $b_1$  which takes the base  $b_1$  decompositions of n integers and produces the n integers;
- $B(\mathbf{t}_1||\cdots||\mathbf{t}_r) = \mathbf{u};$
- $\mathbf{a}^T[\mathbf{s}_1|\cdots|\mathbf{s}_r]\mathbf{b}=y;$
- $\forall i \in [r], |\mathbf{s}_i|_{\infty} \leq b_0/2 \text{ and } \forall i \in [r], |\mathbf{t}_i|_{\infty} \leq b_1/2.$

Here, the matrices A and B are used to produce a commitment **u** to witness vectors  $s_i$ .

Suppose that we would like to instantiate our polynomial IOPs and the message space from Definition 2.3 for polynomials over a ring  $\mathcal{R}_q = \mathbb{Z}_q[x]/(x^d+1)$ . In this case, a first simple instantiation would be to set  $b_0$  in Definition 2.4 equal to q, and set  $\mathbf{s}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{s}_r$  to be the coefficients of a multilinear polynomial, arranged into a matrix S, and  $\mathbf{t}_i$  and commitment  $\mathbf{u}$  as specified, computed over a larger modulus  $p \gg q$ .

Our polynomial IOPs involve polynomial evaluation queries to multilinear extensions. Given a vector  $\mathbf{s} = (s_0, \dots, s_{rm-1}) \in \mathcal{R}_q^{rm}$ , and assuming for simplicity that rm is a power of 2, an evaluation y of its multilinear extension polynomial  $\tilde{s}(X_1, \dots, X_{\log(rm)})$  at  $x_1, \dots, x_{\log(rm)} \in \mathcal{R}_q$  can be written as  $\langle \mathbf{s}, \bigotimes_{j=1}^{\log(rm)} (1-x_j, x_j) \rangle$ . Parsing  $\mathbf{s}$  as a matrix  $S \in \mathcal{R}_q^{r \times m}$ , we can write  $y = \mathbf{a}^T S \mathbf{b}$  where  $\mathbf{a} := \bigotimes_{j=1}^{\log(r)} (1-x_j, x_j)$  and  $\mathbf{b} := \bigotimes_{j=\log(r)+1}^{\log(rm)} (1-x_j, x_j)$ . Thus, Greyhound is able to verify that evaluations of committed multilinear polynomials were computed correctly.

For improved efficiency, and to allow a smaller choice of p for Greyhound, it makes sense to decompose  $S \in \mathcal{R}_q^{r \times m}$  into smaller integers using bit decomposition or another small base before committing to it. Replacing each column of S with bit decompositions of its entries, stacked vertically, this would produce a matrix  $S' \in \mathcal{R}_2^{r \lceil \log(q) \rceil \times m}$ . Greyhound can still be used to verify polynomial evaluations in this case by setting  $\mathbf{b} = (1, 2, \dots, 2^{\lceil \log q \rceil - 1}) \otimes \bigotimes_{j=\log(r)+1}^{\log(rm)} (1 - x_j, x_j)$ .

#### <span id="page-13-0"></span>2.5 Application to Batch-Verification of RAM Updates

In Section 6 we describe an application of our lattice-based lookup arguments to realizing a new PIOP for batch-verification of RAM programs, where a prover convinces a verifier that the RAM

{14}------------------------------------------------

has been accessed by a sequence of read/write operations in a correct and consistent manner. It is standard to view a RAM V as a mapping between unique addresses and values. In particular, V is a vector of (address, value) pairs where addresses are integers and values are arbitrary ring elements. We allow two operations, reads and writes, to be performed on a RAM. A sequence of operations is a vector o where each operation is described by a tuple of three elements (operation, address, value). At a high-level, a batch-verification of RAM protocol seeks to prove that an initial RAM V and final RAM V ′ are consistent with respect to a known sequence of operations o.

To achieve batch-verification of RAM, we broadly follow the lookup argument-based blueprint for realizing batch-verification of RAM from [\[DGPPS24\]](#page-64-0), which consists of three steps.

- 1. Indexed lookup. The prover isolates W, the portion of RAM that was accessed by the operations in o and proves that the values in W are consistent with the initial RAM V . This can be achieved using an indexed lookup argument, where the lookup query is the isolated portion W and the table is the full RAM V . The prover repeats this procedure on the isolated portion W′ for the final RAM V ′ after applying the operations in o to V .
- 2. Memory consistency. The prover uses offline-memory checking [\[BEGKN91\]](#page-63-10) to show that the isolated portions of initial and final RAM, W and W′ (resp.), are consistent with respect to the operations in o.
- 3. Almost identical RAM states. The prover shows the initial RAM V and final RAM V ′ still have identical values at every address outside of the locations that were accessed by o. In [\[DGPPS24\]](#page-64-0), this is done with a univariate PIOP.

Extending prior work to rings. The original protocol of [\[DGPPS24\]](#page-64-0) works over finite fields of large prime order , and does not readily extend to RAMs containing arbitrary ring elements, making it incompatible with lattice-based proof systems. In particular, in the first step above, [\[DGPPS24\]](#page-64-0) use a log-derivative style indexed lookup argument over finite fields. However, in this work we demonstrate that the original log-derivatives claim does not immediately extend to rings due to the presence of zero-divisors (see Section [5.2\)](#page-25-0). To resolve this, we use our new Ring-LogUp lookup protocol which can handle RAM tables with arbitrary ring elements.

In both the second and third step, [\[DGPPS24\]](#page-64-0) give a univariate PIOP which uses univariate polynomial division operations over fields. To extend the second step to rings, we introduce a new multilinear PIOP for offline-memory checking (see Section [6.1\)](#page-35-0) over rings by adapting the techniques from [\[BEGKN91\]](#page-63-10). Our protocol avoids polynomial division by instead using Hadamard products (see Appendix [B.2\)](#page-48-0) and lookup arguments (see Section [5\)](#page-22-0) over rings.

To address the third step, we give a new multilinear PIOP over rings for showing that two vectors are equal everywhere outside of a given set of indices. We describe the intuition of the protocol here with vectors. Let u be an indicator vector such that u[i] = 1 if address i was accessed during the RAM operations in o and u[i] = 0 otherwise. Then we observe that V and V ′ are equal everywhere outside of the indices in u if and only if (1 − u[i]) (V [i] − V ′ [i]) = 0 for all addresses i in the RAM. Our sum-check style PIOP for this relation is formally presented in Construction [6.9.](#page-39-1)

Putting these sub-protocols together yields the first PIOP for batch-verification of updates over RAMs where all entries are arbitrary ring elements, while being fully compatible with state-of-theart lattice-based arguments and commitment schemes, e.g. Greyhound [\[NS24\]](#page-66-2).

#### <span id="page-15-1"></span>3.1 Notation

For an integer  $N \in \mathbb{N}$  we let  $[N] := \{1, \ldots, N\}$ . We use boldface  $\mathbf{a}$  to denote a vector. To obtain the vector entry at index i, we use subscript  $\mathbf{a}_i$ . For a vector that represents a key-value map, we use bracket notation  $\mathbf{a}[\mathbf{b}_i]$  to denote the value stored in  $\mathbf{a}$  at the entry indexed by key  $\mathbf{b}_i$ . We use  $\mathbf{1}^N$  to denote the vector of all 1s with length N. We use  $\mathsf{int}(\cdot)$  to denote the map that sends a binary string to its integer representation. We use  $\mathsf{bin}(\cdot)$  to denote the map that sends an integer to a string representing its binary decomposition. For an integer q we use  $\mathbb{Z}_q$  to mean the integers modulo q and we use  $\mathbb{Z}_q^{\times}$  to denote the integers modulo q that are relatively prime to q.

#### <span id="page-15-2"></span>3.2 Rings

We work over the polynomial ring  $\mathcal{R} := \mathbb{Z}_q[X]/(X^d+1)$  for power-of-two d and prime q.

**Definition 3.1** (Integral domain). An integral domain (ID) is a non-zero commutative ring in which for all a, b in the ring,  $ab = 0 \implies a = 0$  or b = 0.

**Definition 3.2** (Unique factorization domain). A unique factorization domain (UFD) is an integral domain in which every non-unit element can be written as a product of irreducibles. Furthermore, this decomposition is unique up to ordering and units.

**Definition 3.3** (Localization). The **localization** of a commutative ring  $\mathcal{R}$  by a set  $\mathcal{S} \subset \mathcal{R}$  is a new ring denoted by  $\mathcal{S}^{-1}\mathcal{R}$  consisting of fractions  $\frac{r}{s}$  where  $r \in \mathcal{R}$  and  $s \in \mathcal{S}$ . Furthermore,  $\mathcal{S}$  is closed under multiplication and contains 1.

**Definition 3.4** (Total ring of fractions). The **total ring of fractions** of a ring  $\mathcal{R}$  is a new ring  $Q(\mathcal{R}) := \mathcal{S}^{-1}\mathcal{R}$  obtained by localization where  $\mathcal{S}$  is the multiplicatively closed set that contains all non-zero-divisors in  $\mathcal{R}$ .

<span id="page-15-4"></span>**Definition 3.5** (Sampling space). A sampling space C in a ring R is a subset of R such that for every pair of distinct  $u, v \in C$  the difference u - v is invertible in R.

In this work, we consider sampling spaces of exponentially large size.

Every ring element  $y \in \mathcal{R}$  can be written as a polynomial of degree less than d whose coefficients are in  $\mathbb{Z}_q$ .

$$y \leftrightarrow y_0 + y_1 X + \ldots + y_{d-1} X^{d-1}$$

The  $\ell_2$  and  $\ell_\infty$  norms of a ring element  $y \in \mathcal{R}$  are taken over the vector of coefficients  $(y_0, \dots, y_{d-1}) \in \mathbb{Z}_p^d$ . We define the  $\ell_2$  and  $\ell_\infty$  of a vector of ring elements  $\mathbf{y} \in \mathcal{R}^N$  as  $||\mathbf{y}||_2 := \sqrt{||\mathbf{y}_1||^2 + \dots + ||\mathbf{y}_N||^2}$  and  $||\mathbf{y}||_\infty := \max_{i \in [N]} \{||\mathbf{y}_i||_\infty\}$ .

We recall a generalization of the Schwartz-Zippel Lemma [Sch80; Zip79] over rings.

<span id="page-15-3"></span>**Lemma 3.6** (Schwartz-Zippel for rings). Let  $\mathcal{R}$  be a ring and  $f \in \mathcal{R}[x_1, \ldots, x_n]$  be a non-zero polynomial of total degree D. If  $\mathcal{C}$  is a sampling space for  $\mathcal{R}$  then  $\Pr_{\mathbf{r} \leftarrow \mathcal{C}^n}[f(\mathbf{r}) = 0] \leq \frac{D}{|\mathcal{C}|}$ .

**Lemma 3.7** (Chinese Remainder Theorem for rings). Let  $p(x) \in \mathbb{Z}_q$  be a polynomial that decomposes into irreducible factors  $p_1(x), \ldots, p_t(x) \in \mathbb{Z}_q[x]$  for some  $t \in \mathbb{N}$ . Then

$$\mathbb{Z}_q[x]/(p(x)) \cong \mathbb{Z}_q[x]/(p_1(x)) \times \ldots \times \mathbb{Z}_q[x]/(p_t(x))$$

{16}------------------------------------------------

where each  $\mathbb{Z}_q[x]/(p_i(x))$  is a field. The isomorphism is given by the map  $y \mapsto (y \mod p_1(x), \dots, y \mod p_t(x))$  for  $y \in \mathbb{Z}_q[x]/(p(x))$ .

<span id="page-16-2"></span>**Lemma 3.8** (Corollary 1.2, [LS17]). Let  $d \ge k > 1$  be powers-of-two. Let  $q \equiv 2k + 1 \mod 4k$  be a prime. Then the polynomial  $x^d + 1$  factors over  $\mathbb{Z}_q[x]$  as  $x^d + 1 = \prod_{i=1}^k (x^{d/k} - r_i) \mod q$  where each term  $(x^{d/k} - r_i)$  is irreducible and  $r_i$  are distinct. Additionally, if  $0 < ||y||_{\infty} < \frac{1}{\sqrt{k}} \cdot q^{1/k}$  or  $0 < ||y||_2 < q^{1/k}$ , then y has a multiplicative inverse in  $\mathbb{Z}_q[x]/(x^d + 1)$ .

#### <span id="page-16-0"></span>3.3 Lookup Arguments

Lookup arguments allow a prover to convince a verifier that every entry in a witness vector  $\mathbf{a} \in \mathcal{R}^M$  is contained in a public table  $\mathbf{b} \in \mathcal{R}^N$ , which we will denote using the short-hand notation  $\mathbf{a} \subseteq \mathbf{b}$ .

<span id="page-16-1"></span>**Definition 3.9.** The lookup relation  $R_{LU}$  is the set of tuples

$$(i, x, w) = ((q(I), q([N])), (\mathcal{R}, q, M, N), (\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}))$$

where g is a publicly known map that is injective over the domain [N], |I| = M,  $\mathbf{a} \in \mathbb{R}^M$ ,  $\mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^N$ , and  $\{(\mathbf{a}_i, g(I_i))\}_{i \in [M]} \subseteq \{(\mathbf{b}_j, g(j))\}_{j \in [N]}$ .

Remark 3.10. We remark that the lookup indexed relation is traditionally defined over the indices of the entries in the lookup query and lookup table. In particular, I is a vector such that  $I_i = j$  if and only if  $\mathbf{a}_i = \mathbf{b}_j$ . We generalize this in Definition 3.9 above by instead considering a publicly known injective map g that sends the indices in I to another domain. Then the indexed relation  $R_{\text{LU}}$  is defined over the new set of "lifted" indices  $g(I) = \{g(I_1), \dots, g(I_M)\}$ . If g is the identity map then we are in the traditional setting. In later sections, we denote g(I) by an arbitrary list of elements  $\mathbf{c} \in \mathbb{R}^M$  that has a one-to-one correspondence with the index list I.

In Definition 3.9, the vector I is part of the public index i. To give lookup protocols where the indices of  $\mathbf{a}$  contained in  $\mathbf{b}$  are not publicly fixed, I can be included in the witness.

**Definition 3.11.** The indexed lookup relation  $R_{\text{ILU}}$  is the set of tuples

$$(i, x, w) = (g([N]), (\mathcal{R}, g, M, N), (\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}, g(I)))$$

where g is a publicly known map that is injective over the domain [N], |I| = M,  $\mathbf{a} \in \mathcal{R}^M$ ,  $\mathbf{b} \in \mathcal{R}^N$ , and  $\{(\mathbf{a}_i, g(I_i))\}_{i \in [M]} \subseteq \{(\mathbf{b}_j, g(j))\}_{j \in [N]}$ .

**Remark 3.12.** Later, we give IOPs for  $R_{\text{ILU}}$  in which the index vector I is part of the witness and the verifier has oracle access to it. Since the index vector g(I) is not publicly fixed, an IOP for  $R_{\text{ILU}}$  should check that the vector  $g(I) \in \mathbb{W}$  is well-formed with respect to the publicly known map g (i.e., the prover correctly "lifted" the index vector I to the new domain  $g(\cdot)$ ).

## <span id="page-17-0"></span>4 Barriers to Directly Instantiating Lookup Arguments over Rings

In this section we show that lookup arguments over fields require careful treatment when being "translated" directly to rings. In particular, we give attacks that show that the lookup relations from Plookup [\[GW20\]](#page-65-0), LogUp [\[Hab22\]](#page-65-5), and the Lasso-style protocol from the first version of [\[CC-](#page-63-1)[CFGSV25\]](#page-63-1) are insecure when instantiated directly over rings[1](#page-17-1) . We give specific examples to clearly illustrate our attacks, but our techniques are nonetheless general and can be extended to other grand-product relations.

At a high-level, our attacks bring awareness to bugs in grand-product statements over rings that have been systematically overlooked. Grand-products are a core building block of lookup arguments. In general, a grand-product shows that two lists are identical up to a permutation by showing that the randomized product of the entries in both lists are equal. This can be cast as a PIOP by creating two polynomials whose roots are the entries of each list, respectively, and then evaluating the polynomials at a random point. The intuition for security comes from Schwartz-Zippel and fields being unique factorization domains; if the evaluations are equal then the polynomials must share the same roots which means the lists are related by a permutation.

However, prior work on verifiable mixnets [\[BLMG25\]](#page-63-6) pointed out that grand-products require careful treatment over rings. We observe that these techniques can be generalized to lookup arguments which rely on grand-products. The idea is that if the "order" of the entries in the lookup query is not fixed within each CRT component that the ring splits into, then a cheating prover can produce a lookup query that is valid when projected onto each CRT component, yet invalid over the full ring. We demonstrate that Plookup and Lasso are susceptible to this CRT-swapping attack when their grand-product arguments are instantiated/stated directly over rings. In addition to the CRT-swapping attack, we identify a second bug that arises when the lookup table contains zero-divisors. In particular, a cheating prover can construct a polynomial that always passes a zero-test even though the lookup query is invalid. We show that this attack can be used on LogUp [\[Hab22\]](#page-65-5) over rings.

Lasso. Recent work [\[CCCFGSV25\]](#page-63-1) directly extends Lasso [\[STW23\]](#page-67-0) from fields to rings. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work that gives an explicit construction for ring lookups for structured tables. To demonstrate our attack, we begin by using the technique from [\[Hab22\]](#page-65-5), in which the lookup relation is re-expressed as an equivalent summation over all field elements

where ma[v] counts the number of times the value v appears in a and mb[v] = m[v] if v ∈ b and mb[v] = 0 otherwise. By Lemmas 4 and 5 in [\[Hab22\]](#page-65-5), a lookup argument is valid if and only if ma[v] = mb[v] for all v ∈ F.

We now show that this claim does not hold over rings with zero divisors by giving a simple example for Z6. In particular, we break soundness by showing that an invalid lookup claim can still satisfy the right-hand side of Equation [\(9\)](#page-20-0). Let the table be b := {1, 2, 3, 4} and consider an invalid lookup a := {5}. Clearly a ⊊ b yet we will show that the right-hand side of Equation [\(9\)](#page-20-0) is still satisfied. Notice that when v = 5 we have mb[v] − ma[v] ̸= 0 since 5 is an invalid lookup value (i.e., 5 ∈ a but 5 ∈/ b). Over fields, one can argue that if mb[v] − ma[v] ̸= 0 then Equation [\(9\)](#page-20-0) is not satisfied, as expected. However, this argument cannot be made over rings since there are zerodivisors and a product of non-zero terms can go to zero. In particular, if we rewrite Equation [\(9\)](#page-20-0) over Z6, then the right-hand side expression, denoted f(x), is identically 0 as a polynomial.

<span id="page-20-0"></span>
$$f(x) := \sum_{v \in \mathbb{Z}_6} (\mathbf{m_a}[v] - \mathbf{m_b}[v]) \prod_{z \in \mathbb{Z}_6, z \neq v} (x - z) = 0$$

This is because for any fixed x, v ∈ Z6, the product Q z∈Z6,z̸=v (x − z) goes to zero since it will always contain at least two zero-divisors whose product is zero. We do note, however, that if one were to instantiate this argument over a suitable subset of the ring in which the pairwise difference between distinct elements was a non-zero-divisor, then the proof would go through as it does over fields. We refer to Appendix [E](#page-57-0) for more details on this restricted setting. This concludes our attack.

Plookup does not immediately extend to rings for two main reasons. #### <span id="page-25-0"></span>5.2 Ring-LogUp

The original LogUp lookup protocol is written for multivariate polynomials, supports non-indexed lookups, and can be extended to support a batch of multiple lookup queries (often called a multi-column lookup) into the same table <sup>3</sup>. Subsequent works (1) improve efficiency [PH23; Sou25; TNLKN25], (2) extend to indexed-lookup relations [LZLHQZ25; GPS25], and (3) achieve prover

In this section, we present Ring-LogUp to extend the log-derivatives style lookup argument to arbitrary ring elements. We first recall the original log-derivatives relation and then extend it to rings by giving a new lookup relation (Lemma 5.8). #### 5.2.1 Extending LogUp relation to rings

<span id="page-26-0"></span>We begin by recalling the original log-derivatives lookup relation from Lemma 5 of [Hab22].

**Lemma 5.7** (Lemma 5, LogUp [Hab22]). Let  $\mathbb{F}$  be a field with characteristic p > N. Let  $\mathbf{a} \in \mathbb{F}^N$  and  $\mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{F}^N$  be vectors of field elements. Then we have the following:  $((\bot, \bot), (\mathbb{F}, N, N), (\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b})) \in R_{\text{LU}}$  if and only if there exists a vector  $\mathbf{m} \in \mathbb{F}^N$  such that

$$\sum_{i \in N} \frac{1}{x - \mathbf{a}_i} = \sum_{j \in N} \frac{\mathbf{m}[\mathbf{b}_j]}{x - \mathbf{b}_j}$$

in the field of rational functions  $\mathbb{F}(x)$ .

Recall that in Section 2, it was shown that this claim is unsound over rings due to the presence of zero-divisors. However, if one considers instead the restricted setting with tables **b** and queries **a** whose entries belong to a sampling space  $\mathcal{C} \subseteq \mathcal{R}$ , then the proof technique for Lemma 5.7 (see also the proof of Lemma 4.1 in [Dor24]) can be directly extended. We refer to Appendix E for more details.

To extend Lemma 5.7 to rings, we leverage a technique from the shuffle argument in [BLMG25] that involves "tagging" each ring element with a short norm element from the challenge space. By "tagging", we mean that instead of the rational term  $\frac{1}{x-\mathbf{a}_i}$ , we use  $\frac{1}{\mathbf{a}_i+\mathbf{c}_ix_1-x_2}$  where  $\mathbf{c}_i \in \mathcal{C}$  is a unique tag to be associated with entry  $\mathbf{a}_i$ . To give brief intuition behind tagging, it is helpful to look at just the denominator and consider the following

$$\mathbf{a}_i + \mathbf{c}_i x_1 - x_2 = \mathbf{b}_j + \mathbf{c}_j x_1 - x_2 \implies (\mathbf{a}_i - \mathbf{b}_j) + (\mathbf{c}_i - \mathbf{c}_j) x_1 = 0$$

At a high level, since  $\mathbf{c}_i, \mathbf{c}_j \in \mathcal{C}$ , we know  $\mathbf{c}_i = \mathbf{c}_j$ . Then by a careful argument using Chinese Remainder Theorem for rings, one can also show  $\mathbf{a}_i = \mathbf{b}_j$ , which is precisely the goal of a lookup argument. We refer to the proof of Lemma 5.8 for details.

We show that this modification yields a new *indexed* lookup relation that supports arbitrary ring elements. However, a slight technicality still remains: the "tagged" bivariate polynomial in the denominator of the rational terms may not be well-defined in the bivariate ring  $\mathcal{R}[x_1, x_2]$ , since  $\mathcal{R}$  is not an integral domain. Note that this was not an issue in prior work over fields [Hab22] because each rational term was well-defined in a field of fractions. To fix this, we view each rational term in the total ring of fractions  $Q(\mathcal{R}(x_1, x_2))$  and then show that each denominator is a non-zero-divisor in  $\mathcal{R}(x_1, x_2)$ .

To prove Lemma 5.8, we first observe that the proof technique from prior works [Hab22; Dor24] (rewriting as a summation over all ring elements, shown in Equation (5)) does not readily extend to our new bivariate rational expression. The reason is because there are not enough unique "tags" to form a bijective map with all ring elements. Put differently, since  $\mathcal{C} \subset \mathcal{R}$  there are too few unique choices for  $\mathbf{c}_i$  to embed as a coefficient of  $x_1$  in the denominator. We instead prove Lemma 5.8 directly using induction and avoid the technical issue that arises from the proof technique in prior works.

<span id="page-27-0"></span>**Lemma 5.8** (Ring-LogUp relation). Then  $(g([N]), (\mathcal{R}, M, N), (\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}, \mathbf{c})) \in R_{\text{ILU}}$  if and only if there exists a vector  $\mathbf{m} \in \mathbb{Z}_q^N$  such that

<span id="page-27-1"></span>
$$\sum_{i \in [M]} \frac{1}{\mathbf{a}_i + \mathbf{c}_i x_1 - x_2} = \sum_{j \in [N]} \frac{\mathbf{m}[\mathbf{b}_j]}{\mathbf{b}_j + g(j)x_1 - x_2}$$

$$\tag{16}$$

over the total ring of fractions  $Q(\mathcal{R}(x_1, x_2))$ .

Remark 5.9. The vector  $\mathbf{c}$  implicitly defines an index map  $I : [M] \to [N]$  such that  $\mathbf{c}_i = g(I_i)$  where  $I_i = j$  means that  $\mathbf{a}_i = \mathbf{b}_j$ . Recall from Definition 3.9 that g is a bijection that maps an index to another domain. If g is the identity map, then the lookup argument is defined over the original indices in [M] and [N]. In our formulation of Lemma 5.8 (which uses the log-derivatives expression to instantiate the indexed lookup relation  $R_{\text{ILU}}$ ), we require g to be a bijection that sends an integer in  $[|\mathcal{C}|]$  to an element in  $\mathcal{C}$ . For example, when  $\mathcal{C}$  is the set of ring elements with binary coefficients, then g(i) is the binary decomposition of i. This choice for g is intentional, as the proof for Lemma 5.8 requires the pairwise differences of values g(i), g(j) for  $i \neq j$  to be invertible, a property that is guaranteed over  $\mathcal{C}$ .

Then, we can subtract  $\frac{1}{\mathbf{a}_{i^*} + \mathbf{c}_{i^*} x_1 - x_2}$  from the LHS and  $\frac{1}{\mathbf{b}_k + g(k)x_1 - x_2}$  from the RHS of Equation (21), and reduce to the claim LogDeriv<sub>M-1,N</sub>( $\mathbf{a}'$ ,  $\mathbf{b}$ ,  $\mathbf{m}'$ ,  $\mathbf{c}'$ ) which holds from the inductive hypothesis.

The proof technique is nearly identical to that of the base case. We use the bivariate polynomial  $P(x_1, x_2) := \prod_{i \in [M]} (\mathbf{a}_i + \mathbf{c}_i x_1 - x_2) \prod_{j \in [N]} (\mathbf{b}_j + g(j) x_1 + x_2)$ . Multiplying  $P(x_1, x_2)$  into Equation (21) and setting  $x_2 = b_k + g(k)x_1$  gives us

$$0 = \mathbf{m}[\mathbf{b}_{k}] \cdot \underbrace{\prod_{i \in [M]} (\mathbf{a}_{i} - \mathbf{b}_{k}) + (\mathbf{c}_{i} - g(k))x_{1})}_{(\dagger)} \cdot \underbrace{\prod_{j \in [N], j \neq k} ((\mathbf{b}_{j} - \mathbf{b}_{k}) + (g(j) - g(k))x_{1})}_{(\dagger\dagger)}.$$

$$(22)$$

As before, we have two cases,  $\mathbf{m}[\mathbf{b}_k] = 0$  or  $\mathbf{m}[\mathbf{b}_k] \neq 0$ . If  $\mathbf{m}[\mathbf{b}_k] = 0$ , then entry  $\mathbf{b}_k$  is not being looked up. We argue that there must exist some  $k \in [N]$  such that  $\mathbf{m}[\mathbf{b}_k] \neq 0$  since if not, then Equation (21) implies  $\sum_{i \in [M]} \frac{1}{\mathbf{a}_i + \mathbf{c}_i x_1 + x_2} = 0$  which is a contradiction. Unlike the base case, we allow  $\mathbf{m}[\mathbf{b}_k] \geq 1$  because there may be repeated values in the M-size lookup.

Analogous to the base case, we project Equation (22) onto the CRT basis. Since for all  $j \neq k$ , g(j) - g(k) is a unit in  $\mathcal{R}$ , it holds that  $\prod_{j \in [N], j \neq k} (g(j) - g(k)) \neq 0$  over  $\mathcal{R}_1$  and  $\mathcal{R}_2$ . which implies that  $(\dagger) = 0$  over  $\mathcal{R}_1[x_1]$  and  $\mathcal{R}_2[x_1]$ . Thus, we can remove  $\frac{1}{\mathbf{a}_{i^*} + \mathbf{c}_{i^*} x_1 - x_2}$  from the LHS and  $\frac{1}{\mathbf{b}_k + g(k)x_1 - x_2}$  from the RHS of Equation (21). Let  $\mathbf{m}'[\mathbf{b}_k] = \mathbf{m}[\mathbf{b}_k] - 1$  and  $\mathbf{m}'[\mathbf{b}_j] = \mathbf{m}[\mathbf{b}_j]$  for all  $j \neq k$ . **Remark 5.10.** We elaborate more on the requirement that  $\mathcal{R} \cong \mathcal{R}_1 \times \mathcal{R}_2$  (i.e., we work over a ring that splits into two fields). ## <span id="page-33-0"></span>6 PIOP for Batch-Verification of RAM Updates over Rings

Arithmetic circuit-based representations are typically inefficient in applications of verifiable computation to scenarios involving the result of a program execution on memory/state. Examples of such applications include proving the correctness of query execution on a database, verifiable inference using a decision tree, or proving the validity of (a batch of) updates to a table of account balances. A general and useful abstraction for such applications is verifiable random access memory (RAM), where a prover convinces a verifier that the RAM has been accessed/updated by a sequence of read/write operations in a correct and consistent manner. We specifically focus on batch-verification of RAM, where verifiable update of the RAM state is required for (small) batches of updates. A prominent application of batch-verifiable RAM is blockchain rollup [\[RU19\]](#page-67-11) that allows a blockchain to efficiently verify succinct proofs attesting to the correctness of expensive off-chain computation. We note here that lookup protocols already suffice for batch-verifiable read-only memory (ROM), i.e., when all operations are read operations. However, handling write operations introduces additional complexities, as noted in certain recent works [\[DGPPS24;](#page-64-0) [OLB25\]](#page-66-10).

Current solutions for batch-verification of RAM can be broadly classified into two categories: (i) solutions based on accumulators [\[OWWB20;](#page-66-11) [CFHKKO22\]](#page-63-11) realized using groups of unknown order (e.g., RSA, class-groups) [\[CL02;](#page-64-11) [BBF19;](#page-62-11) [OWWB20;](#page-66-11) [CFHKKO22\]](#page-63-11) [4](#page-33-1) , and (ii) solutions [\[DGPPS24;](#page-64-0) [OLB25\]](#page-66-10) using pairing-based, logarithmic-derivative-style lookup arguments [\[Hab22;](#page-65-5) [GK22;](#page-64-6) [EFG22;](#page-64-7) [CFFLL24\]](#page-63-2), that typically work over finite fields with large prime order. The first approach is inherently incompatible with lattice-rings and the second approach does not readily extend from fields to rings (see Section [4\)](#page-17-0). This leaves open the question of designing protocols for batchverification of RAM that work over rings and are compatible with mainstream lattice-based proof systems [\[BBCPGL18;](#page-62-4) [ENS20;](#page-64-3) [BLNS20;](#page-63-3) [ACK21;](#page-62-5) [LNP22;](#page-66-1) [BS22;](#page-63-4) [BCS23;](#page-63-5) [NS24;](#page-66-2) [BC24;](#page-62-6) [BC25\]](#page-62-7).

In this section, we describe an application of our lookup arguments over rings to realize a new PIOP over rings for batch-verification of random access memory (RAM) updates. We broadly follow the blueprint for realizing batch-verification of RAM updates from [\[DGPPS24\]](#page-64-0). However, a key difference is that we consider RAMs where the entries are arbitrary ring elements, as opposed to elements of a finite field of large prime order as in [\[DGPPS24\]](#page-64-0). This requires us to additionally introduce new multilinear PIOPs over rings for (i) checking read/write consistency of RAM updates (Section [6.1\)](#page-35-0), and (ii) checking that the initial and final RAM states before and after a batch of updates are identical in all memory locations that are "untouched" by the updates (Section [6.2\)](#page-39-0). Putting these sub-protocols together with our lookup arguments over rings and compiling with a suitable polynomial commitment scheme yields the first succinct argument system for batchverification of updates over RAMs where all entries are arbitrary ring elements.

Defining RAM. We begin by establishing a working definition of RAM that is used in the rest of the section. Informally, a RAM maps indices (addresses) to values, where we assume that values come from a ring R with a suitable challenge space, while indices come from a set I ⊂ N. For simplicity of exposition, we limit our focus to RAMs where I is of the form [m] for some integer m ∈ N (which may be different from size of the RAM M). Finally, for an index, there should be at most one value in the RAM, i.e., the association is unambiguous. In this paper, we consider RAMs where the values are elements of a ring R (associated with an appropriate challenge space).

<span id="page-33-1"></span><sup>4</sup>While accumulators can also be realized using Merkle-trees [\[Mer88\]](#page-66-12), they are not batching-friendly; see discussion in [\[DGPPS24\]](#page-64-0).

**Definition 6.1** (Random Access Memory (RAM)). Given integers  $M, m \in \mathbb{N}$  and a ring  $\mathcal{R}$ , a RAM of size M over a set of indices I = [m] is a tuple  $V = (\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{v}) \in [m]^M \times \mathcal{R}^M$  such that  $\forall i, j \in [M], v_i = v_j$  whenever  $a_i = a_j$ . We think of V as a table with vectors  $\mathbf{a}$  and  $\mathbf{v}$  denoting its columns. The set of all such RAMs will be denoted by  $V_{\mathcal{R},m,M}$ .

For a RAM  $V = (\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{v}) \in \mathsf{V}_{\mathcal{R},m,M}$ , we refer to tuples  $(a_i, v_i)$ ,  $i \in [M]$  as records of the RAM V. We use the key-value access notation v = V[a] to mean that (a, v) is a record of V (note there can be multiple such records according to our definition). When we consider RAMs in  $\mathsf{V}_{\mathcal{R},m,M}$  for m = M where the first column (of indices) consists of unique entries in [M] (i.e., the address associated with value  $v_i$  is simply i), we denote such RAMs by  $V \in \mathcal{R}^M$ .

**RAM Operations.** For a RAM  $V \in V_{\mathcal{R},m,M}$ , a RAM operation is a three tuple (op, a, v) with  $op \in \{0,1\}$ ,  $a \in [m]$  and  $v \in \mathcal{R}$ . We consider two kinds of RAM operations: (i) an operation with op = 0 is called a *read* operation which denotes reading a value v mapped to index a in the RAM. Such an operation is formally denoted as a read operation and is represented by the tuple  $(0, a, \bot)$  (where  $\bot$  is a special symbol used to denote an element not in the ring  $\mathcal{R}$ ); (ii) an operation with op = 1 is called a *write* operation, which denotes associating a new value v' with index a in the RAM. Such an operation is formally denoted as a write operation and is represented by the tuple (1, a, v').

**Achieving Batch-Verification of RAM.** We use vectors in  $\mathbb{R}^M$  to denote "large" RAMs, where the index column is implicitly assumed to be the set of unique values  $(1, \ldots, M)$ . Let  $V, V' \in \mathbb{R}^M$  denote the initial and final RAM states. For  $\mathcal{O} = \{0, 1\} \times [M] \times (\mathbb{R} \cup \{\bot\})$ , let  $o \in \mathcal{O}^k$  be a sequence of k RAM operations that  $update\ V$  to V'. More formally, if  $V_0 := V$ , and for all  $i \in [k]$ ,  $V_i$  is obtained from  $V_{i-1}$  by updating according to  $o_i$ , then  $V_k = V'$ . To prove the transformation of V to V' via the operation sequence o, we proceed via the following sub-protocols:

- Sub-protocol-1: Indexed Lookup. We isolate the k-sized sub-RAMs  $W = (\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{v})$  and  $W' = (\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{v}')$  of the large RAMs V and V' consisting of rows corresponding to indices in  $\mathbf{a}$ . This requires proving  $\mathbf{v} = V[\mathbf{a}]$  and  $\mathbf{v}' = V'[\mathbf{a}]$ . Observe that this can be proven using two invocations of our indexed ring-based lookup arguments (see Section 5.1 and Section 5.2), where the same set of lookup addresses  $\mathbf{a}$  is reused across both instances.
- Sub-protocol-2: Memory Consistency. On the isolated sub-RAMs W and W' of size k, we use memory checking arguments to prove that sequence o correctly and consistently updates W to W'. The authors of [DGPPS24] presented a univariate PIOP to prove RAM consistency, which is not suitable for adaptation to the ring setting due to its extensive use of polynomial division operations. In Section 6.1, we propose a new multilinear PIOP for proving memory consistency of RAM updates over rings. Our protocol is based on known offline memory checking techniques from [BEGKN91] and can hence be used in conjunction with our ring-based lookup arguments.
- Sub-protocol-3: Almost Identical RAM States. Finally, we show that the RAMs V and V' are almost identical, i.e., they are identical outside the indices  $\mathbf{v}$  in the sequence of operations. Again, [DGPPS24] realized this step via a univariate PIOP, which extensively uses polynomial division operations. In Section 6.2, we propose a new multilinear PIOP for proving almost identical RAM states over rings.

- <span id="page-36-2"></span>9. We have that all entries of  $T_W - T_R$  are greater than 0.

*Proof.* If  $(x, w) \in R_{MC}$ , then the required  $R, W \in (\mathcal{R} \times [M] \times \mathbb{N})^{2M+k}$  can be obtained by running memcheck. It is easy to see that all conditions with the exception of 4 are satisfied, by construction of memcheck.

For condition 4, note that with the exception of the M final read operations, every record (v, a, t) in W is also stored in R the next time a read or write operation is applied to address a. Since no address is accessed more than once during the M initial write operations, all records from W[0:M+k] appear in R[M:2M+k]. Finally, all records from W[0:M+k] are unique because they have a unique timestamp, so comparing cardinalities shows that W[0:M+k] is equal to R[M:2M+k].

Conversely, suppose that there exist  $R, W \in (\mathcal{R} \times [M] \times \mathbb{N})^{2M+k}$  satisfying conditions 1 to 9. Taking all of the conditions except for 4 together, we see that locally, R and W are consistent with an execution of memcheck, in the sense that the i-th records of R and W are consistent with a read or write operation corresponding to o. However, it remains to show that R and W are globally consistent. This is guaranteed by 4, because it shows that R and W contain the same records, except for the initial and final records, and if a record (v, a, t) in R appeared at an earlier index than the corresponding record in W, it would be impossible for the timestamp of (v, a, t) to increase.

The prover **P** takes as input the instance  $\mathbf{x} = (\mathcal{R}, M, k)$ , and witness  $\mathbf{w} = (V, o, V')$ ; the verifier **V** has query access to the witness  $\mathbf{w}$  and takes as input the instance  $\mathbf{x}$ .

#### <span id="page-39-0"></span>6.2 Multilinear PIOP for Almost Identical RAM states

In this subsection, we present a multilinear PIOP for the almost identical RAM states relation described formally below. The high-level goal is to prove that two RAMs V and V' have the same values everywhere except for a subset of addresses.

**Definition 6.8** (Almost Identical RAM States). The almost identical RAM states relation  $R_{AidRAM}$  is the set of tuples

$$(i, x, w) = (\bot, (\mathcal{R}, M, k), (V, V', \mathbf{a}))$$

where

- $V, V' \in \mathbb{R}^M$  for a positive integer  $M \in \mathbb{N}$ ;
- $\mathbf{a} \in [M]^k$  for a positive integer  $k \in \mathbb{N}$ ; and
- letting uniq(a) denote a vector with entries containing the unique values in  $\mathbf{a}$ , V[i] = V'[i] for all  $i \in [M]$  such that  $i \notin \mathsf{uniq}(\mathbf{a})$ .

**Defining Some Multilinear Polynomials.** Let  $\widehat{V}: \mathcal{R}^{\log M} \to \mathcal{R}$ ,  $\widehat{V'}: \mathcal{R}^{\log M} \to \mathcal{R}$ ,  $\widehat{a}: \mathcal{R}^{\log k} \to \mathcal{R}$ , and  $\widehat{u}: \mathcal{R}^{\log M} \to \mathcal{R}$  be multilinear polynomials defined as follows:

• Let  $V(\mathbf{x}), V'(\mathbf{x})$ , and  $a(\mathbf{x})$  be polynomials defined as:

$$V(\mathbf{x}) := \sum_{\mathbf{i} \in \{0,1\}^{\log M}} \mathsf{EQ}(x,\mathbf{i}) \cdot V[\mathsf{int}(i)]$$

$$V'(\mathbf{x}) := \sum_{\mathbf{i} \in \{0,1\}^{\log M}} \mathsf{EQ}(x,\mathbf{i}) \cdot V'[\mathsf{int}(i)]$$

$$a(\mathbf{x}) := \sum_{\mathbf{i} \in \{0,1\}^{\log k}} \mathsf{EQ}(x,\mathbf{j}) \cdot a[\mathsf{int}(j)]$$

We define the *witness* multilinear polynomials  $\widehat{V}$ ,  $\widehat{V'}$ , and  $\widehat{a}$  to be the multilinear extensions of  $V(\mathbf{x})$ ,  $V'(\mathbf{x})$ , and  $a(\mathbf{x})$ , respectively, over the ring  $\mathcal{R}$ .

• Let  $\mathbf{u} \in \mathcal{R}^M$  be a vector such that for each  $i \in [M]$ ,  $\mathbf{u}_i = 1$  if  $i \in \mathsf{uniq}(\mathbf{a})$  and  $\mathbf{u}_i = 0$ , otherwise. Let  $\mathbf{u}(\mathbf{x})$  be a polynomial defined as:

$$\mathbf{u}(\mathbf{x}) := \sum_{\mathbf{i} \in \{0,1\}^{\log M}} \mathsf{EQ}(x,\mathbf{i}) \cdot \left( \sum_{a_{\mathsf{int}(i)} \in \mathsf{uniq}(\mathbf{a})} \mathsf{EQ}(x,\mathsf{bin}(a_{\mathsf{int}(i)})) \right)$$

We define the auxiliary multilinear polynomial  $\hat{\mathbf{u}}$  to be the multilinear extension of  $\mathbf{u}(\mathbf{x})$  over the ring  $\mathcal{R}$ .

<span id="page-39-1"></span>Construction 6.9 (Multilinear PIOP for Almost Identical RAMs). We construct a PIOP for the relation  $R_{\text{AidRAM}}$ . The prover **P** takes as input the instance  $\mathbf{x} = (\mathcal{R}, M, k)$  and witness  $\mathbf{w} = (V, V', \mathbf{a})$  as described above; the verifier **V** takes as input the instance  $\mathbf{x}$  and has query access to the witness multilinear polynomials  $(\widehat{V}, \widehat{V'}, \widehat{a})$  and the auxiliary multilinear polynomial  $\widehat{\mathbf{u}}$  as defined above.

{40}------------------------------------------------

- 1. V samples a challenge vector  $\mathbf{r} \leftarrow \mathcal{C}^{\log M}$  and sends  $(\mathbf{r}, \mathbf{s})$  to  $\mathbf{P}$ .
- 2.  $\mathbf{P}$  and  $\mathbf{V}$  engage in a multilinear sum-check PIOP to check that the following relation holds:

$$\sum_{\mathbf{i} \in \{0,1\}^{\log M}} \mathsf{EQ}(\mathbf{r},\mathbf{i}) \cdot (1 - \widehat{u}(\mathbf{i})) \cdot \left(\widehat{V}(\mathbf{i}) - \widehat{V'}(\mathbf{i})\right) = 0$$

- 3. V samples a binary challenge vector  $\mathbf{s} \leftarrow \{0,1\}^{\log M}$  and sends  $\mathbf{s}$  to  $\mathbf{P}$ .
- 4. **P** computes  $u = \widetilde{u}(\mathbf{s})$ , vector  $\mathbf{v} = (\mathsf{EQ}(\mathbf{s}, \mathsf{bin}(a_i)))_{i \in [k]}$ , polynomial v(x) as

$$v(x) := \sum_{\mathbf{i} \in \{0,1\}^{\log k}} \mathsf{EQ}(\mathbf{i},x) \cdot \mathsf{EQ}(\mathbf{s},\mathsf{bin}(a_{\mathsf{int}(i)}))$$

and multilinear polynomial  $\widetilde{v}: \mathcal{R}^{\log k} \to \mathcal{R}$  as the multilinear extension of  $\mathbf{v}$ .  $\mathbf{P}$  sends across  $(u, \widetilde{v})$  to  $\mathbf{V}$ .  $\mathbf{V}$  makes an oracle call to  $\widetilde{u}$  to verify that  $u = \widetilde{u}(\mathbf{s})$ .

- 5. P and V then engage in the following sub-protocols:
  - A multilinear sumcheck protocol for the relation  $u = \sum_{\mathbf{i} \in \{0,1\}^{\log k}} \widetilde{v}(\mathbf{i})$  (to check the well-formedness of  $\widetilde{u}$  with respect to  $\mathsf{uniq}(\mathbf{a})$ ).
  - A lookup argument for the indexed lookup relation  $R_{\text{ILU}}$  with respect to the set of tuples  $(i_{\text{ILU}}, \mathbf{x}_{\text{ILU}}, \mathbf{w}_{\text{ILU}})$  where

$$\dot{\mathbf{1}}_{\text{ILU}} = g([M]), \quad \mathbf{x}_{\text{ILU}} = (\mathcal{R}, g, k, M), \quad \mathbf{w}_{\text{ILU}} = (\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{e}, g(\mathsf{uniq}(\mathbf{a})))$$

where the vector  $\mathbf{e} = (\mathsf{EQ}(\mathbf{s},\mathsf{bin}(i)))_{i \in [M]}$ , and g is a publicly known map.

**Lemma 6.10.** For every ring  $\mathcal{R} = \mathbb{Z}_q/(x^d+1)$  as in Section 5 and positive integers  $M, k < |\mathcal{C}|$ , Construction 6.9 is a holographic PIOP for the indexed relation  $R_{AidRAM}$  that has the following parameters:

- $Soundness\ error:\ O(\frac{\log(M+k)}{|\mathcal{C}|}) + \mathsf{Soundness}(\mathsf{Sumcheck}, M) + \mathsf{Soundness}(\mathsf{Lookup}, M, k) + \mathsf{Soundness}(\mathsf{Sumcheck}, k)$
- $\bullet \ \, Round\ complexity{:}\ O(1) + {\sf Rounds}({\sf Sumcheck}, M) + \max({\sf Rounds}({\sf Lookup}, M, k), \\ {\sf Rounds}({\sf Sumcheck}, k))$
- Proof length: O(M+k) + ProofLen(Sumcheck, M) + ProofLen(Lookup, M, k) + ProofLen(Sumcheck, k)
- $Prover\ cost:\ O(M+k)\mathcal{R} + Prover(Sumcheck, M) + Prover(Lookup, M, k) + Prover(Sumcheck, k)$
- $Verifier\ cost:\ O(\log(M+k)) + Verifier(Sumcheck, M) + Verifier(Lookup, M, k) + Verifier(Sumcheck, k)$
- $Query\ complexity:\ O(1)+Queries(Sumcheck,M)+Queries(Lookup,M,k)+Queries(Sumcheck,k)$

where  $\mathcal{C}$  denotes the set of all ring elements with binary coefficients, and where e.g. Soundness(Lookup, M, k) represents the soundness error of an indexed lookup PIOP over the ring  $\mathcal{R}$  where the lengths of the table and the lookup vector are at most M and k, respectively, while Soundness(Sumcheck, k) represents the soundness error of a multilinear sumcheck PIOP over the ring  $\mathcal{R}$  where the number of variables is at most  $\log k$ .

{41}------------------------------------------------

*Proof.* We begin by analyzing the efficiency properties.

**Efficiency.** Round complexity is easy to verify. The prover's cost and the proof length outside of the sumcheck and lookup sub-protocols are O(M+k) as the prover evaluates  $u=\widetilde{u}(\mathbf{s})$  (which requires  $O(M)\mathcal{R}$  work and incurs O(M) proof length) and also computes  $\widetilde{v}$  (which requires  $O(k)\mathcal{R}$  work and incurs O(k) proof length). The verifier samples the challenge vectors of length  $\log M$  and  $\log k$ , respectively, and makes a single oracle call to  $\widetilde{u}$ , outside of the sumcheck and lookup sub-protocols. This concludes the analysis of efficiency.

**Completeness.** Consider an instance tuple  $\mathbf{x} = (\mathcal{R}, M, k)$  and witness tuple  $\mathbf{w} = (V, V', \mathbf{a})$  such that such that  $(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{w})$  satisfies the relation  $R_{\text{AidRAM}}$ . Then we must have

$$\sum_{i \in [M]} (1 - u_i) \cdot (V[i] - V'[i]) = 0$$

which ensures that for each  $i \in [M]$ , if u[i] = 0, then we must have V[i] = V'[i], which is essentially what the first multilinear sum-check verifies. In addition, if  $\tilde{u}$  is well-formed with respect to  $\mathsf{uniq}(\mathbf{a})$ , we must have

$$\sum_{\mathbf{i} \in \{0,1\}^{\log k}} \widetilde{v}(i) = u$$

At a high level, a summation over all  $v(\mathbf{i})$  essentially checks whether  $s \in \mathsf{uniq}(\mathbf{a})$ , while  $u = \widetilde{u}(s)$  checks the same by construction. More formally, we have

$$\begin{split} \sum_{\mathbf{i} \in \{0,1\}^{\log k}} \widetilde{v}(\mathbf{i}) &= \sum_{\mathbf{i} \in \{0,1\}^{\log k}} \mathsf{EQ}(\mathbf{s}, \mathsf{bin}(a_{\mathsf{int}(\mathbf{i})})) \\ &= \mathsf{EQ}(\mathbf{s}, \mathsf{bin}(a_1)) + \ldots + \mathsf{EQ}(\mathbf{s}, \mathsf{bin}(a_m)) \\ &= \sum_{a_i \in \mathsf{uniq}(\mathbf{a})} \mathsf{EQ}(\mathbf{s}, \mathsf{bin}(a_i)) \\ &= \sum_{\mathbf{i} \in \{0,1\}^{\log M}} \mathsf{EQ}(\mathbf{s}, \mathbf{i}) \cdot \left(\sum_{a_i \in \mathsf{uniq}(\mathbf{a})} \mathsf{EQ}(\mathbf{s}, \mathsf{bin}(a_i))\right) \\ &= \widetilde{u}(\mathbf{s}) \\ &= u \end{split}$$

**Soundness.** Consider an instance tuple  $\mathbf{x} = (\mathcal{R}, M, k)$  and witness tuple  $\mathbf{w} = (V, V', \mathbf{a})$  such that  $(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{w})$  does not satisfy the relation  $R_{\text{AidRAM}}$ . Then, by multivariate Schwartz-Zippel, the prover manages to pass the first multilinear sumcheck identity test with probability at most  $O(\frac{M}{|\mathcal{C}|})$  and the second multilinear sumcheck identity with probability at most  $O(\frac{k}{|\mathcal{C}|})$ . The remaining components of the soundness error come from the two invocations of the multilinear sumcheck sub-protocol and one invocation of the lookup sub-protocol. This concludes the analysis of soundness.

Plugging into the above protocol: (i) any one of our lattice lookup PIOPs, and (ii) the multilinear sumcheck protocol over rings from Appendix A.3, we obtain the following corollary.

<span id="page-41-0"></span>Corollary 6.11. For every ring  $\mathcal{R} = \mathbb{Z}_q/(x^d+1)$  as in Section 5 and positive integers  $M, k < |\mathcal{C}|$ , there exists a holographic PIOP for the relation  $R_{AidRAM}$  that has the following parameters:

{42}------------------------------------------------

- Soundness error: O( k+M |C| ) + O( d+log k+log M |S| ))
- Round complexity: O(log M + log k)
- Proof length: O(M + poly(d)k)
- Prover cost: O(M + poly(d)k)R
- Verifier cost: O(log M + d log k)R
- Query complexity: O(d)

where C denotes the set of all ring elements with binary coefficients and S denotes the set of all Zq-valued ring elements (i.e., those with degree 0).

### <span id="page-42-0"></span>6.3 Putting Everything Together

In this section, we describe how to put together (any one of) our lookup PIOPs with the PIOPs in Section [6.1](#page-35-0) and Section [6.2](#page-39-0) to achieve a PIOP for batch-verification of RAM over rings. We first formally define the batch-verification of RAM relation below.

We use vectors in R<sup>M</sup> to denote "large" RAMs, where the index column is implicitly assumed to be the set of unique values (1, . . . , M). Let V, V ′ ∈ R<sup>M</sup> denote the initial and final RAM states. For O = {0, 1} × [M] × (R ∪ {⊥}), let o = (a, v) ∈ O<sup>k</sup> be a sequence of k RAM operations that update V to V ′ .

Definition 6.12 (Batch-verification of RAM). The batch-verification of RAM updates relation RBatchVerRAM is the set of tuples

$$(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{w}) = ((\mathcal{R}, M, k), (V, o = (\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{v}), V'))$$

where

- V, V ′ ∈ R<sup>M</sup> for a positive integer M ∈ N;
- o = (a, v) ∈ O<sup>k</sup> for a positive integer k ∈ N; and
- if V<sup>0</sup> := V , and for all i ∈ [k], V<sup>i</sup> is obtained from Vi−<sup>1</sup> by updating according to oi, then V<sup>k</sup> = V ′ .

To design a PIOP for RBatchVerRAM, we proceed via the following sub-protocols:

• Sub-protocol-1: Indexed Lookup. We isolate the k-sized sub-RAMs W = (a, v) and W′ = (a, v ′ ) of the large RAMs V and V ′ consisting of rows corresponding to indices in a, and prove that v = V [a] and v ′ = V ′ [a]. This requires two invocations of any one of our PIOPs (either from Section [5.1](#page-22-1) or form Section [5.2\)](#page-25-0) for the relation RILU with respect to the following set of tuples (index, statement, witness) tuples.

$$(\mathbf{i}_{\mathrm{ILU},1}, \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{ILU},1}, \mathbf{w}_{\mathrm{ILU},1}) = (g([M]), (\mathcal{R}, g, k, M), (V, \mathbf{v}, g(\mathbf{a})))$$

$$(\mathbf{i}_{\text{ILU},2}, \mathbf{x}_{\text{ILU},2}, \mathbf{w}_{\text{ILU},2}) = (g([M]), (\mathcal{R}, g, k, M), (V', \mathbf{v}', g(\mathbf{a})))$$

In particular, the same set of lookup addresses a is reused as the witness for both cases.

• Sub-protocol-2: Memory Consistency. On the isolated sub-RAMs W and W′ of size k, we use memory checking arguments to prove that sequence o correctly and consistently updates W to W′ . More formally, we invoke the memory-consistency PIOP for RMC from Section [6.1](#page-35-0) on the following tuple (statement, witness):

$$(\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{MC}}, \mathbf{w}_{\mathrm{MC}}) = ((\mathcal{R}, k, k), (W, o, W')$$

• Sub-protocol-3: Almost Identical RAM States. Finally, we show that the RAMs V and V ′ are almost identical, i.e., they are identical outside the indices v in the sequence of operations. More formally, we invoke the memory-consistency PIOP for RAidRAM from Section [6.2](#page-39-0) on the following tuple (statement, witness):

$$(\mathbf{x}_{AidRAM}, \mathbf{w}_{AidRAM}) = ((\mathcal{R}, M, k), (V, V', \mathbf{a}))$$

We summarize the overall PIOP in the following theorem.

<span id="page-43-0"></span>Theorem 6.13 (PIOP for Batch-verification of RAM over Rings). For every ring R = Zq/(x <sup>d</sup> + 1) as in Section [5,](#page-22-0) and positive integers M, k < |C|, there exists a holographic PIOP for the indexed relation RBatchVerRAM that has the following parameters:

- Proof length: O(M + poly(d)k)

### <span id="page-44-0"></span>A Additional Preliminaries

### <span id="page-44-1"></span>A.1 Vector operations

<span id="page-44-3"></span>**Definition A.1** (Cyclic shift). A vector  $\mathbf{b} \in \mathcal{R}^N$  is said to be the **cyclic shift** of  $\mathbf{a} \in \mathcal{R}^N$ , if  $\mathbf{b}_N = \mathbf{a}_1$  and, for all  $i \in [N-1]$ , it holds that  $\mathbf{a}_{i+1} = \mathbf{b}_i$ . In this case, we write  $\mathbf{b} = \mathsf{shift}(\mathbf{a})$ .

<span id="page-44-4"></span>**Definition A.2** (Merge). Let  $\mathbf{a} \in \Sigma^M$  and  $\mathbf{b} \in \Sigma^N$  with  $\mathbf{a} \subseteq \mathbf{b}$ . We define  $\operatorname{merge}_{\mathbf{b}}(\mathbf{a})$  to be the set of all vectors  $\mathbf{w} \in \Sigma^{N+M}$  obtained as follows: initialize a list  $\mathbf{w}$  to be equal to  $\mathbf{b}$ ; then, for each  $j \in [M]$ , insert  $\mathbf{a}_j$  into  $\mathbf{w}$  directly to the left of any entry  $\mathbf{w}_i$  of  $\mathbf{w}$  such that  $\mathbf{a}_j = \mathbf{w}_i$ . (At least one such entry exists because  $\mathbf{a} \subseteq \mathbf{b}$ .)

We recall a lemma from [LNP22] that gives an inner-product relation that holds if an integer vector  $\mathbf{y} \in \mathbb{Z}^N$  contains binary values.

**Lemma A.3** (Lemma 5.2, [LNP22]). Let  $\mathbf{y} \in \mathbb{Z}^N$  for  $N \in \mathbb{N}$ . If  $\langle \mathbf{y}, \mathbf{y} - \mathbf{1}^N \rangle = 0$  then  $\mathbf{y} \in \{0, 1\}^N$ .

**Definition A.4** (Entry product). We say that  $\tau \in \mathcal{R}$  is the entry product of a vector  $\mathbf{a} \in \mathcal{R}^{\ell \cdot N}$ , denoted  $\tau = \operatorname{prod}(\mathbf{a})$ , if  $\tau = \prod_{i \in [\ell \cdot N]} \mathbf{a}_i$ .

Every vector  $\mathbf{a} \in \mathcal{R}^N$  can be extended to a unique multilinear polynomial whose evaluations agree with the original entries of  $\mathbf{a}$  over the log N-bit hypercube. A multivariate polynomial is said to be multilinear if the degree of each variable is at most one. We formalize this as follows.

**Definition A.5** (Multilinear extension). Let  $\mathbf{a} \in \mathcal{R}^N$  be a vector of ring elements. Then  $\widehat{\mathbf{a}}$ :  $\mathcal{R}^{\log N} \to \mathcal{R}$  is the unique  $\log N$ -variate multilinear extension polynomial in which the entries of  $\mathbf{a}$  are embedded as evaluations. In particular,

$$\widehat{\mathbf{a}}(x_1, \dots, x_{\log N}) := \sum_{\mathbf{k} \in \{0,1\}^{\log N}} \mathsf{EQ}(\mathbf{k}, \mathbf{x}) \cdot \mathbf{a}_{\mathsf{int}(\mathbf{k})}$$

where

$$\mathsf{EQ}(\mathbf{k}, \mathbf{x}) := \prod_{i \in [\log N]} \mathbf{k}_i \mathbf{x}_i + (1 - \mathbf{k}_i)(1 - \mathbf{x}_i)$$

#### <span id="page-44-2"></span>A.2 Holographic Polynomial IOPs

A public-coin holographic polynomial IOP over a ring family R for an indexed relation R is specified by a tuple

$$\mathsf{IOP} = (\mathsf{rc}, \mathsf{s}, \mathsf{d}, \mathbf{I}, \mathbf{P}, \mathbf{V})$$
 .

Here,  $rc, s, d: \{0,1\}^* \to \mathbb{N}$  are polynomial-time computable functions and I, P, V are called the *indexer*, *prover*, and *verifier* algorithms. The parameter rc gives the number of rounds of interaction, s gives the number of polynomial oracles sent in each round, and d gives a degree bound on each polynomial.

In the 0-th round, or offline phase, the indexer **I** receives as input a ring  $\mathcal{R} \in \mathbb{R}$  and an index i for R, and outputs s(0) polynomials  $p_1^{(0)}, \ldots, p_{s(0)}^{(0)} \in \mathcal{R}[x]$  of degrees at most  $d(|i|, 0, 1), \ldots, d(|i|, 0, s(0))$ .

In the online phase, given  $(i, x, w) \in R$ , the prover **P** takes as input  $(\mathcal{R}, i, x, w)$ . The verifier **V** takes as input  $(\mathcal{R}, x)$  and receives oracle access to the polynomials produced by  $\mathbf{I}(\mathcal{R}, i)$ . The prover **P** and the verifier **V** interact over  $\mathbf{rc} = \mathbf{rc}(|i|)$  rounds. For  $j \in [\mathbf{rc}]$ , in the j-th round of

{45}------------------------------------------------

interaction, the verifier **V** sends the message  $\rho_j \in \mathcal{R}$  to the prover **P**. The prover **P** responds with s(j) polynomial oracles  $p_1^{(j)}, \ldots, p_{s(j)}^{(j)} \in \mathcal{R}[x]$ . The verifier may query any oracle polynomial oracle  $p_i^{(j)}$  at any evaluation point  $y \in \mathcal{R}$  any number of times, and receive  $p_i^{(j)}(y) \in \mathcal{R}$ . At the end of the interaction, the verifier accepts or rejects.

The degree function d defines the **admissible** prover algorithms considered for completeness and soundness. A possibly malicious prover  $\tilde{\mathbf{P}}$  is called admissible for IOP if for every round  $j \in [rc]$  and oracle index  $i \in [s(j)]$  we have  $\deg(p_i^{(j)}) \leq \mathsf{d}(|\dot{\mathbf{i}}|, j, i)$ . The honest prover  $\mathbf{P}$  must be admissible.

The proof length I is the sum  $I(|i|) := \sum_{j=0}^{rc(|i|)} \sum_{i=1}^{s(j)} d(|i|, j, i)$  of all degree bounds in the offline and online phases. The query complexity q is the total number of oracle queries made by the verifier, including those produced by the indexer <sup>6</sup>. Let  $\epsilon : \{0,1\}^* \to [0,1)$ . We say that IOP has perfect completeness and soundness error  $\epsilon$  if:

**Completeness.** For every ring  $\mathcal{R} \in \mathbb{R}$  and index-instance-witness tuple  $(i, x, w) \in \mathcal{R}$ , the probability that  $\mathbf{P}(\mathcal{R}, i, x, w)$  convinces  $\mathbf{V}^{\mathbf{I}(\mathcal{R}, i)}(\mathcal{R}, x)$  to accept in the interactive oracle protocol is 1.

**Soundness.** For every ring  $\mathcal{R} \in \mathbb{R}$ , index-instance pair  $(i, x) \notin L(R)$ , and admissible prover  $\tilde{\mathbf{P}}$ , the probability that  $\tilde{\mathbf{P}}$  convinces  $\mathbf{V}^{\mathbf{I}(\mathcal{R},i)}(\mathcal{R},x)$  to accept in the interactive oracle protocol is at most  $\epsilon(\mathcal{R},x)$ .

**IOP of Proximity.** An IOP of proximity (IOPP) is a generalization of a PIOP in which the verifier additionally has query access to the witness. In this work, we primarily give constructions for IOPP.

#### <span id="page-45-0"></span>A.3 Sum-check Protocol over Rings

The sum-check protocol [LFKN92] has been extended to polynomials over rings [CCKP19]. The prover  $\mathbf{P}$  and verifier  $\mathbf{V}$  receive a sum-check instance  $\mathbf{x} = (\mathcal{R}, H, \ell, \tau, \mathcal{C})$ , where  $\mathcal{R}$  is a ring, H is a subset of  $\mathcal{R}$ ,  $\ell$  is a number of variables,  $\tau \in \mathcal{R}$  is a claimed sum, and  $\mathcal{C} \subseteq \mathcal{R}$  is a sampling set. The prover  $\mathbf{P}$  additionally receives a polynomial  $p \in \mathcal{R}[x_1, \dots, x_\ell]$  such that  $\sum_{\omega \in H^\ell} p(\omega) = \tau$ . The protocol has  $\ell$  rounds and works as follows.

- 1. For  $i = 1, ..., \ell$ :
  - (a) **P** sends to **V** the polynomial:

$$q_i(x) := \sum_{\omega_{i+1},\dots,\omega_{\ell} \in H} p(r_1,\dots,r_{i-1},x,\omega_{i+1},\dots,\omega_{\ell}) \in \mathcal{R}[x];$$

- (b) V sends to P a random challenge  $r_i \leftarrow C$ .
- 2. V checks that  $\sum_{\omega_1 \in H} q_1(\omega_1) = \tau$  and, for  $i \in \{2, \dots, \ell\}$ , that

$$\sum_{\omega_i \in H} q_i(\omega_i) = q_{i-1}(r_{i-1}).$$

<span id="page-45-1"></span><sup>&</sup>lt;sup>6</sup> "Proof length" is the standard terminology used for interactive oracle proofs and refers to the total length of the oracles that get sent during the interaction. When an IOP is compiled into a succinct protocol with a commitment scheme, the prover will commit to these oracles, which then gets absorbed into the concrete prover cost as "commitment time".

{46}------------------------------------------------

3. If the checks pass then **V** sets  $v := q_{\ell}(r_{\ell}) \in \mathcal{R}$  and outputs the tuple  $((r_1, \ldots, r_{\ell}), v)$ .

The security guarantee of the sum-check protocol requires  $\mathcal{C}$  to be a sampling set and follows from a generalization of the Schwartz-Zippel lemma over rings (see Lemma 3.6). The proof of Lemma 3.6 follows the same approach as the usual inductive proof of the standard Schwartz-Zippel lemma. The properties of  $\mathcal{C}$  are used to establish that a polynomial  $f \in \mathcal{R}[x]$  of degree D has at most D roots in  $\mathcal{C}$ , which in turn is used in the base case and in the inductive step.

**Lemma A.6.** Let  $x_{SC} = (\mathcal{R}, H, \ell, \tau, \mathcal{C})$  be a sum-check instance and a polynomial  $p \in \mathcal{R}[x_1, \ldots, x_\ell]$  of total degree d. If  $\mathcal{C}$  is a sampling set for  $\mathcal{R}$  then the following properties hold.

**Completeness.** If 
$$\sum_{\omega \in H^{\ell}} p(\omega) = \tau$$
, then  $\Pr_{\mathbf{r} \leftarrow \mathcal{C}^{\ell}} [\langle \mathbf{P}(\mathbf{x}_{SC}, p), \mathbf{V}(\mathbf{x}_{SC}; \mathbf{r}) \rangle = (\mathbf{r}, p(\mathbf{r}))] = 1$ .

**Soundness.** If 
$$\sum_{\omega \in H^{\ell}} p(\omega) \neq \tau$$
, then for every  $\tilde{P}_{SC}$ ,  $\Pr_{\mathbf{r} \leftarrow \mathcal{C}^{\ell}} [\langle \tilde{P}_{SC}, \mathbf{V}(\mathbf{x}_{SC}; \mathbf{r}) \rangle = (\mathbf{r}, p(\mathbf{r}))] < \frac{\ell d}{|\mathcal{C}|}$ .

Above  $\langle A, \mathbf{V}(\mathbf{x}_{SC}; \mathbf{r}) \rangle$  is the output of  $\mathbf{V}(\mathbf{x}_{SC})$  when interacting with algorithm A using randomness  $\mathbf{r}$ .

## <span id="page-48-0"></span>B.2 Hadamard Product

Definition B.4. The Hadamard product relation RHP is the set of tuples

$$(i, x, w) = (\bot, (\mathcal{R}, N), (\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}, \mathbf{c}))$$

where a, b, c ∈ R<sup>N</sup> and a ◦ b = c.

<span id="page-48-2"></span>Construction B.5. Let R be a suitable ring with sampling space C and let H = {0, 1}. We construct a PIOP for the indexed relation RHP. $$\sum_{\boldsymbol{\omega} \in H^{\log N}} \mathsf{EQ}(\boldsymbol{\omega}, \mathbf{y}) \cdot (\widehat{\mathbf{a}}(\boldsymbol{\omega}) \cdot \widehat{\mathbf{b}}(\boldsymbol{\omega}) - \widehat{\mathbf{c}}(\boldsymbol{\omega})) = 0$$

• At the end of the sum-check protocol, the verifier outputs a claim <sup>ν</sup> and uses <sup>a</sup>b, <sup>b</sup>b, <sup>b</sup>c, and EQ to make a query at the point ρ = (ρ0, . . . , ρn−1), where ρ0, . . . , ρn−<sup>1</sup> is the verifier's randomness used in the sum-check protocol. $$\mathsf{EQ}(\boldsymbol{\rho}, \mathbf{y}) \cdot (\widehat{\mathbf{a}}(\boldsymbol{\rho}) \cdot \widehat{\mathbf{b}}(\boldsymbol{\rho}) - \widehat{\mathbf{c}}(\boldsymbol{\rho})) = \nu$$

## <span id="page-48-1"></span>B.3 Cyclic-shift test

Definition B.7. The cyclic shift relation RCS is the set of tuples

$$(i, x, w) = (\bot, (\mathcal{R}, N), (\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}))$$

where b = shift(a).

{49}------------------------------------------------

**Lemma B.8.** Let  $\mathcal{R}$  be a suitable ring with challenge space  $\mathcal{C}$ . Let  $\mathbf{a} \in \mathcal{R}^N$  and  $\mathbf{b} \in \mathcal{R}^N$ . Let  $A(x) := \sum_{i=1}^N \mathbf{a}_i x^{i-1}$  and  $B(x) := \sum_{i=1}^N \mathbf{b}_i x^{i-1}$  be polynomials in  $\mathcal{R}[x]$ . Then  $(\bot, (\mathcal{R}, N), (\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b})) \in R_{CS}$  if and only if

$$A(x) - x \cdot B(x) = (1 - x^{N}) \cdot \mathbf{b}_{N}$$

over  $\mathcal{R}[x]$ .

<span id="page-49-0"></span>**Construction B.9.** Let  $\mathcal{R}$  be a ring with sampling space  $\mathcal{C}$ . Let  $N \in \mathbb{N}$ . We construct a PIOP for the relation  $R_{CS}$ . The prover  $\mathbf{P}$  takes as input an index  $i = \bot$ , instance  $\mathbf{x} = (\mathcal{R}, N)$ , and witness  $\mathbf{w} = (\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b})$ . The verifier  $\mathbf{V}$  takes as input the index i and the instance  $\mathbf{x}$  and has query access to the witness given by multilinear extension oracles  $\widehat{\mathbf{a}}, \widehat{\mathbf{b}}$ .

- The verifier **V** samples random  $\gamma \leftarrow \mathcal{C}$  and sends  $\gamma$  to the prover.
- The prover **P** and **V** engage in scalar-product protocols for

$$\langle \mathbf{a}, \boldsymbol{\gamma} \rangle = \nu_0$$

and

$$\langle \mathbf{b}, \boldsymbol{\gamma} \rangle = \nu_1$$

where  $\gamma := (1, \gamma, \dots, \gamma^{N-1})$  and  $\nu_0, \nu_1 \in \mathcal{R}$  are the prover's claims for the scalar product.

- At the end of the scalar-product protocols, the verifier V directly evaluates the multi-linear extension of  $\gamma$  at a random point, where the randomness is chosen during the sum-check within the scalar-product protocol.
- The verifier makes a single oracle query to  $\hat{\mathbf{b}}$  at the vector  $\mathbf{1}^{\log N}$  to obtain the value  $\mathbf{b}_N$ .
- The verifier **V** checks that

$$\nu_0 - \gamma \cdot \nu_1 = (1 - \gamma^N) \cdot \mathbf{b}_N$$

- Round complexity:  $O(\log N)$
- Proof length: O(N)
- Prover cost: O(N)
- Verifier cost:  $O(\log N)$
- Query complexity: O(1)

*Proof.* We omit a formal proof but remark that the proof length is dominated by the cost of the underlying scalar-product protocols. The soundness is  $O(\frac{N}{|\mathcal{C}|})$  from the univariate Schwartz-Zippel test in the last step.

#### <span id="page-51-0"></span>B.5 Integer Check

**Definition B.14.** The integer check relation  $R_{\rm IC}$  is the set of tuples

$$(i, x, w) = (\bot, (\mathcal{R}, N), \mathbf{a})$$

where  $N \in \mathbb{N}$ ,  $\mathbf{a} \in \mathbb{Z}_q^N$ .

<span id="page-51-1"></span>**Construction B.15.** Let  $\mathcal{R} = \mathbb{Z}_q[x]/(x^d+1)$ . We present a PIOP for the relation  $R_{\rm IC}$ . The prover **P** takes as input an instance  $\mathbf{x} = (\mathcal{R}, N)$  and witness  $\mathbf{w} = \mathbf{a}$ . The verifier takes as input the instance  $\mathbf{x}$  and has oracle access to the witness given by  $\widehat{\mathbf{a}}$ .

- The verifier V samples a vector  $\boldsymbol{\rho} \in \mathbb{Z}_q^{\log N}$  and makes a single oracle query to obtain  $\tau = \widehat{\mathbf{a}}(\boldsymbol{\rho})$ .
- If  $\tau \notin \mathbb{Z}_q$ , then the verifier will reject.

We can amplify soundness and achieve negligible error by repeating the above protocol security-parameter many times.

**Lemma B.16.** For every ring  $\mathcal{R} = \mathbb{Z}_q[x]/(x^d+1)$ , positive integer N, Construction B.15 is a PIOP for  $R_{\rm IC}$  with the following efficiency parameters

- Round complexity: O(1)
- Proof length: O(N)
- Prover cost: O(1)
- Verifier cost: O(1)
- Query complexity: O(1)

*Proof.* We first prove completeness and soundness.

Let  $H = \{0, 1\}$ . Completeness follows by inspection, since the evaluation of the MLE takes a linear combination of the integer entries in **a** as follows

$$= \sum_{\boldsymbol{\omega} \in H^{\log N}} \left( \prod_{i \in \log N} \boldsymbol{\omega}_i \boldsymbol{\rho}_i + (1 - \boldsymbol{\omega}_i)(1 - \boldsymbol{\rho}_i) \right) \cdot \mathbf{a}_{\mathsf{int}(\boldsymbol{\omega}) + 1}$$

For  $\mathbf{a} \in \mathbb{Z}_q^N$  and  $\boldsymbol{\rho} \in \mathbb{Z}_q^{\log N}$ , we have  $\tau \in \mathbb{Z}_q$ .

We now prove soundness. Fix a cheating prover  $\tilde{\mathbf{P}}$  such that  $\mathbf{a} \notin \mathbb{Z}_q^N$ . Then there exists some index  $i \in [N]$  such that  $\mathbf{a}_i \in \mathcal{R}$  is a ring element represented by a non-constant polynomial. Namely

$$\mathbf{a}_i \leftrightarrow a_0 + a_1 x + \ldots + a_{d-1} x^{d-1}$$

where there exists  $j \in [d-1]$  such that  $a_j \neq 0$ .

By a Schwartz-Zippel argument over the coefficient basis on the monomial  $x^j$ , the probability that the coefficient on  $x^j$  is zero is at most  $\frac{\log N}{q}$ . #### <span id="page-52-0"></span>B.6 Binary Check

In this section, we adapt the range proof from LatticeFold+ [BC25] to a PIOP (without commitments) for the simpler setting when the range is just  $\{0,1\}$ . **Definition B.17** (Tensor product). For a vector  $\mathbf{r} \in \mathcal{R}^{\log N}$  we define the tensor product

$$\otimes \mathbf{r} := \bigotimes_{i \in [\log N]} (1 - \mathbf{r}_i, \mathbf{r}_i) \in \mathcal{R}^N$$

$$(i, x, w) = (\bot, (\mathcal{R}, N), \mathbf{f})$$

<span id="page-52-1"></span>Construction B.19 (LatticeFold+ Range Check PIOP). Let  $\mathcal{R}$  be a suitable ring with sampling space  $\mathcal{C} = \mathbb{Z}_q$ . Let  $H = \{0, 1\}$  and  $\mathcal{M} := \{0, 1, x, \dots, x^{d-1}\} \subseteq \mathcal{R}$  be the set of monomials of  $\mathcal{R}$ . Prover gets input witness  $f \in \mathcal{R}^n$ . #### Batched monomial set check.

- 2. The verifier **V** sends challenges  $\mathbf{c} \leftarrow \mathcal{C}^{\log N}$  and  $\beta \leftarrow \mathcal{C}$

## <span id="page-57-0"></span>E Ring-LogUp For Tables with Special Entries

In this section, we directly extend the techniques of LogUp [Hab22] to give a lookup argument which works when the lookup table contains only ring elements which are non-zero-divisors and their differences are invertible. As an example the set of short ring elements is a sampling set and contains invertible elements (see Definition 3.5 and Lemma 3.8); thus this construction directly implies a lookup for tables with short entries over these rings. In contrast to Construction 5.11, which requires rings of special form, this construction works for arbitrary rings as long as the lookup contains elements with the above properties. We also require that the size of the lookup table and query are much smaller than the size of the challenge space to ensure that the rational terms in a PIOP are well-defined with high-probability. We begin by presenting a helpful lemma towards proving the full lookup relation.

<span id="page-57-1"></span>**Lemma E.1.** Let  $\mathcal{R}$  be a ring and  $\mathcal{C} \subseteq \mathcal{R}$  be a sampling space containing non-zero-divisors. Let  $f: \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{R}$  be any function. Then for  $L_f: \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{R}$ ,

$$L_f(x) = \sum_{\alpha \in \mathcal{C}} \frac{f(\alpha)}{x - \alpha} = 0$$

over the total quotient ring  $\mathcal{R}(x)$ , if and only if  $f(\alpha) = 0$  for all  $\alpha \in \mathcal{C}$ .

*Proof.* Since  $\alpha \in \mathcal{C}$ , it must be that  $\alpha$  is not a zero-divisor; hence  $\frac{f(\alpha)}{x-\alpha}$  belongs to the total quotient ring  $\mathcal{R}(x)$ .

- $(\longleftarrow)$  Suppose that  $f(\alpha) = 0$  for all  $\alpha \in \mathcal{C}$ . Then clearly  $L_f(x) = 0$ .
- $(\Longrightarrow)$  Suppose that  $L_f(x)=0$ . Then

$$L_f(x) = 0 \Rightarrow L_f(x) \prod_{\beta \in \mathcal{C}} (x - \beta) = 0 \Rightarrow \sum_{\alpha \in \mathcal{C}} f(\alpha) \cdot \prod_{\beta \in \mathcal{C}, \beta \neq \alpha} (x - \beta) = 0$$

We view  $\sum_{\alpha \in \mathcal{C}} f(\alpha) \cdot \prod_{\beta \in \mathcal{C}, \beta \neq \alpha} (x - \beta)$  as a polynomial over  $\mathcal{R}[x]$ . Let  $x = \alpha \in \mathcal{C}$ . The product  $\prod_{\beta \in \mathcal{C}, \beta \neq \alpha} (\alpha - \beta) \neq 0$  is non-zero since every pairwise difference  $\alpha - \beta$  is not a zero-divisor. Indeed if not, then some term  $(\alpha - \beta)$  is a zero-divisor in  $\mathcal{R}$ , which contradicts the fact that  $\alpha, \beta \in \mathcal{C}$ . Thus we must have  $f(\alpha) = 0$  for all  $\alpha \in \mathcal{C}$  in  $\mathcal{R}[x]$ .

The above lemma is used to show the LogUp relation for tables which contain only short ring elements. Similarly, the evaluation vectors of  $\widehat{B}, \widehat{Q}$  over the log N-dimensional hypercube can be computed in O(N) time. The prover will also participate in  $O(\max\{\log M, \log N\})$  rounds of sum-check, wherein each round the prover computes a univariate of degree 1 for a recursive claim, which costs O(M+N) total. The prover performs parallel zero-checks with  $O(\max\{\log M, \log N\})$  rounds and in each round compute a univariate of maximum degree 3, which is again O(M+N) cost. Finally, the prover engages in Construction B.19 to perform the binary-check for the length-M vector  $\mathbf{c}$  which costs  $O(\operatorname{poly}(d)M)$ .

The verifier cost comes from  $O(\max\{\log M, \log N\})$  rounds of sum-check, where in each round the verifier makes a constant number of univariate polynomial evaluations. The verifier overhead from the binary-check by is  $O(d \log M)$ .

The query complexity is O(d) because the verifier must make a constant number of oracle calls in the final round of the sum-checks and engages in a binary-check for  $\mathbf{c}$ . Note that the sum-check evaluations are not oracle calls, but rather the verifier is directly evaluating the univariate polynomial that the prover sent.
