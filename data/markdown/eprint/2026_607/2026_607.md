# <span id="page-0-0"></span>Refined Approx-SVP Rank Reduction Conditions and Adaptive Lattice Reduction for MSIS Security Estimation

Xiaohan Zhang, Zijian Zhou, and Longjing Qu(B)

College of Science, National University of Defense Technology, Changsha 410073, Hunan, China ljqu happy@hotmail.com

Abstract. The security of lattice-based cryptography relies critically on the concrete hardness of the approximate shortest vector problem (Approx-SVP). For cryptographic-sized instances, existing Approx-SVP rank reduction conditions may be overly aggressive, as they implicitly assume access to a large number of extremely short lattice vectors. In this work, we systematize and refine Approx-SVP rank reduction conditions from a feasibility perspective. We identify that, in the context of the dimension-for-free (D4f) technique, the existence of a single sufficiently short vector is the essential requirement, and we derive two refined and compact rank reduction conditions accordingly. The first condition is based on geometric properties of lattice sieving, while the second incorporates a basis-quality-dependent probabilistic bound. These results are validated through extensive experiments on high-dimensional lattices, where the compact condition outperforms prior methods by up to a factor of 60 in dimensions 850 and 925. To reliably realize these conditions in high dimensions, we present APBKZ, an adaptive Pump-based lattice reduction strategy that dynamically selects the blocksize and D4f parameters according to the evolving Gram–Schmidt profile. We further introduce HeadAPBKZ, a head-focused execution mode that restricts reduction to a critical prefix once the rank reduction condition is satisfied. Combining these advances, we develop an improved concrete security estimation framework for the MSIS problem. Applied to Dilithium, our analysis indicates that, when compact rank reduction behavior is integrated with the D4f technique, the estimated concrete security of Dilithium is reduced by 3.65–6.09 bits relative to the conservative Core-SVP baseline, providing a more realistic concrete assessment. We also derive an analytic approximation whose closed-form estimates remain close to the numerical estimation, with discrepancies of only about 1.1 bits.

Keywords: Approximate Shortest Vector Problem · Rank Reduction · Lattice Reduction · BKZ Algorithms · MSIS Security Estimation · Post-Quantum Cryptography

## 1 Introduction

Lattice-based cryptography is a cornerstone of post-quantum cryptography, with security mainly based on the hardness of problems such as the Learning with Errors (LWE) problem [\[22\]](#page-28-0) and the Short Integer Solution (SIS) problem [\[1\]](#page-26-0). In concrete security evaluations, these problems are typically reduced to instances of the approximate shortest vector problem (Approx-SVP). As a result, the reliability of cryptographic parameter selection critically depends on how accurately the concrete feasibility of Approx-SVP can be assessed under realistic algorithmic assumptions.

Rank reduction for Approx-SVP. Modern lattice attacks do not attempt to solve Approx-SVP in the full lattice dimension. Instead, they rely on rank reduction: after sufficient basis reduction, the attacker searches for a short vector in a lower-dimensional prefix sublattice [\[16,](#page-27-0)[2,](#page-26-1)[13,](#page-27-1)[30\]](#page-28-1). The feasibility of such attacks is governed by a rank reduction condition, which specifies how small the prefix dimension k can be while still enabling Approx-SVP with a given approximation factor.

A prominent rank reduction condition for Approx-SVP was proposed by Laarhoven and Mariano [\[16\]](#page-27-0) in the context of progressive lattice sieving. However, this condition implicitly assumes that the sieving procedure can reliably access an exponentially large set of vectors with norms close to the Gaussian Heuristic. While theoretically meaningful, this assumption is often misaligned with practical high-dimensional lattice reduction, where feasibility is determined by the existence of only a small number of sufficiently short vectors.

From idealized rank reduction to practical feasibility. In practice, lattice sieving typically returns vectors whose norms are bounded by a constant factor above the Gaussian Heuristic, most commonly within p 4/3. The above analysis builds on the geometric modeling of lattice sieving introduced by Nguyen and Vidick [\[21\]](#page-28-2) and further refined in subsequent works such as [\[19\]](#page-28-3). Under this model, short lattice vectors are treated as samples from a thin spherical shell, and the success of sieving is linked to the existence of sufficiently many vector pairs with favorable angular relations. This framework has become standard in the analysis of modern sieving-based SVP algorithms [\[27,](#page-28-4)[28,](#page-28-5)[6](#page-26-2)[,15,](#page-27-2)[7,](#page-26-3)[17,](#page-27-3)[8](#page-27-4)[,5,](#page-26-4)[16,](#page-27-0)[2,](#page-26-1)[13](#page-27-1)[,30\]](#page-28-1).

In particular, when the dimension-for-free (D4f) technique is employed to reduce the cost of sieving [\[12\]](#page-27-5), the sieving does not require access to all short vectors, but only to the existence of at least one vector below a certain length threshold [\[4\]](#page-26-5).

This gap between idealized rank reduction and practical feasibility motivates a refined formulation of Approx-SVP rank reduction conditions for cryptographicsized lattices. In this work, we develop two such conditions that explicitly distinguish what is required for feasibility from what is merely sufficient in theory.

Lattice reduction in high dimensions. Accurately realizing refined rank reduction conditions in high dimensions requires lattice reduction algorithms that

behave robustly under practical constraints. Existing BKZ variants, such as PnjBKZ within the G6K framework, rely on simulator-driven parameter choices, fixed jump schedules and aggressive D4f estimates [\[2\]](#page-26-1). In high dimensions, these strategies frequently lead to unsaturated reduction and unstable basis quality, undermining the feasibility assumptions required for rank reduction.

Motivated by these limitations, we develop adaptive lattice reduction strategies that dynamically adjust their behavior based on the evolving quality of the basis. Our proposed algorithm, APBKZ (Adaptive Pump BKZ), adaptively selects blocksizes and dimension-for-free parameters without relying on simulator predictions or jump scheduling. In this framework, APBKZ is of independent interest as a general-purpose adaptive lattice reduction strategy that does not rely on any rank reduction condition. It therefore applies beyond the specific feasibility conditions, while HeadAPBKZ should be viewed as a refinement of APBKZ rather than a replacement. Specifically, HeadAPBKZ becomes useful only after the compact rank reduction condition is satisfied, at which point the reduction scope can be restricted to the head region to obtain a more favorable cost–quality trade-off.

Security estimation of MSIS. A refined understanding of Approx-SVP rank reduction has direct implications for the concrete security estimation of SIS and MSIS-based cryptographic schemes. Notably, the Dilithium specification [\[4\]](#page-26-5) highlights the lack of security estimates that accurately account for modern reduction techniques such as D4f and progressive sieving. Existing estimates based on the conservative Core-SVP model implicitly assume access to vectors at the Gaussian Heuristic bound, which may not reflect realistic attack feasibility.

By combining the compact rank reduction condition with adaptive lattice reduction strategies, we develop an improved security estimation framework for MSIS. Our analysis provides tighter and more realistic lower-bound estimates for the security of Dilithium.

This work does not aim to formally prove the proposed Approx-SVP rank reduction conditions. Instead, it studies them from a practical perspective, focusing on how they reflect the behavior of modern sieve-based reduction on cryptographic-sized instances, including probabilistic sieve outputs and D4f techniques.

#### 2.1 Notations and Basic Definitions

In this paper, column vectors are denoted by bold lowercase letters (e.g., b), and matrices are denoted by bold capital letters (e.g., B). We write a matrix B as 

{4}------------------------------------------------

 $\mathbf{B} = (\mathbf{b}_1, \dots, \mathbf{b}_n)$ , where  $\mathbf{b}_i$  denotes the *i*-th column vector of  $\mathbf{B}$ . If  $\mathbf{B} \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$  has full column rank n, the lattice generated by  $\mathbf{B}$  is denoted by  $\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{B}) = \{\mathbf{B}\mathbf{x} \mid \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{Z}^n\}$ . Let  $(\mathbf{b}_1^*, \dots, \mathbf{b}_n^*)$  denote the Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization of the basis  $(\mathbf{b}_1, \dots, \mathbf{b}_n)$ . For  $i \in \{1, \dots, n\}$ , we denote by  $\pi_i$  the orthogonal projection onto the orthogonal complement of  $\mathrm{span}(\mathbf{b}_1, \dots, \mathbf{b}_{i-1})$ . For  $1 \leq i < j \leq n$ , we define  $\mathbf{B}_{[i:j]} = (\mathbf{b}_i, \dots, \mathbf{b}_j)$  and denote by  $\mathcal{L}_{[i:j]} = \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{B}_{[i:j]})$  the sublattice of  $\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{B})$  generated by these basis vectors. In contrast, we define the locally projected block  $\mathbf{B}_{\pi[i:j]} = (\pi_i(\mathbf{b}_i), \dots, \pi_i(\mathbf{b}_j))$  and denote by  $\mathcal{L}_{\pi[i:j]} = \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{B}_{\pi[i:j]})$  the lattice generated by the projected vectors. For convenience, we use  $\mathbf{B}_{[i]}$  and  $\mathcal{L}_{[i]}$  as shorthands for  $\mathbf{B}_{[i:n]}$  and  $\mathcal{L}_{\pi[i:n]}$ , respectively, and similarly  $\mathbf{B}_{\pi[i]}$  and  $\mathcal{L}_{\pi[i]}$  as shorthands for  $\mathbf{B}_{\pi[i:n]}$  and  $\mathcal{L}_{\pi[i:n]}$ .

Let BKZ- $\beta$  denote one tour of BKZ with blocksize  $\beta$ . A basis is said to be BKZ- $\beta$  reduced if it is obtained after sufficiently many tours of BKZ- $\beta$ . For simplicity, we use  $\beta$  to denote the quality of a BKZ- $\beta$  reduced basis. The Euclidean norm of a vector  $\mathbf{v}$  is denoted by  $\|\mathbf{v}\|$ . The volume of a lattice  $\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{B})$  is  $\operatorname{Vol}(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{B})) = \prod_i \|\mathbf{b}_i^*\|$ , which is an invariant of the lattice. The first minimum of a lattice  $\mathcal{L}$  is the length of a shortest non-zero vector, denoted by  $\lambda_1(\mathcal{L})$ . We use the abbreviations  $\operatorname{Vol}(\mathbf{B}) = \operatorname{Vol}(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{B}))$  and  $\lambda_1(\mathbf{B}) = \lambda_1(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{B}))$ .

**Definition 1 (Gaussian Heuristic [20]).** Let  $S \subset \mathbb{R}^n$  be a measurable body. Then the number of lattice points in S is approximately equal to  $Vol(S)/Vol(\mathcal{L})$ .

We denote by  $|S \cap \mathcal{L}|$  the number of lattice points in S. Note that the number of lattice points predicted by the Gaussian Heuristic is exactly the expected number of lattice points in a random translation of S. When applying the Gaussian Heuristic to an n-dimensional ball of volume  $\operatorname{Vol}(\mathcal{L})$  we obtain that the minimal length  $\lambda_1(\mathcal{L})$  is approximately the radius of this ball, which asymptotically means that  $\lambda_1(\mathcal{L}) \approx \sqrt{n/(2\pi e)} \cdot \operatorname{Vol}(\mathcal{L})^{1/n}$ . For a lattice  $\mathcal{L} \subset \mathbb{R}^n$  we denote this radius by  $\operatorname{gh}(\mathcal{L})$ , and we also denote  $\operatorname{gh}(n)$  for  $\operatorname{gh}(\mathcal{L})$  of any n-dimensional lattice  $\mathcal{L}$  of volume 1:  $\operatorname{gh}(n) = \sqrt{n/2\pi e}$ . Note that a ball of radius  $\omega \cdot \operatorname{gh}(\mathcal{L})$  contains an exponential number of  $\omega^n$  lattice vectors not much longer than the minimal length. We say that a list of lattice vectors saturates a volume S if it contains some significant ratio [12], say 50%, of the lattice vectors in  $\mathcal{L} \cap S$  as predicted by the Gaussian Heuristic. Here the saturation ratio is defined as  $p = N_{\text{list}}/N_{\text{GH}}$ , with  $N_{\text{list}} = |\mathbf{v} \in \text{list} : \mathbf{v} \in S|$  and  $N_{\text{GH}} = \operatorname{Vol}(S)/\operatorname{Vol}(\mathcal{L})$ . And we call the list p-saturated when p reaches a predefined threshold.

**Definition 2 (Root Hermite Factor [10]).** Let  $\mathbf{B} = (\mathbf{b}_1, \dots, \mathbf{b}_n)$  be a reduced basis for an n-dimensional lattice  $\mathcal{L}$ . The root Hermite factor is defined by

$$\delta = \left(\frac{\|\mathbf{b}_1\|}{\operatorname{Vol}(\mathcal{L})^{\frac{1}{n}}}\right)^{\frac{1}{n}}.$$

<span id="page-4-0"></span>**Definition 3 (HKZ reduction and BKZ reduction [24]).** The basis  $\mathbf{B} = (\mathbf{b}_1, \dots, \mathbf{b}_n)$  of a lattice  $\mathcal{L}$  is said to be HKZ reduced if  $\|\mathbf{b}_i^*\| = \lambda_1 \left(\mathcal{L}\left(\mathbf{B}_{\pi[i:n]}\right)\right)$  for all  $i \leq n$ . It is said to be BKZ reduced with blocksize  $\beta$  (for short, BKZ- $\beta$  reduced) if  $\|\mathbf{b}_i^*\| = \lambda_1 \left(\mathcal{L}\left(\mathbf{B}_{\pi[i:\min(i+\beta-1,n)]}\right)\right)$  for all  $i \leq n$ .

{5}------------------------------------------------

Based on Definition 3, the Geometric Series Assumption provides a simplified model for BKZ-reduced bases:

Definition 4 (Geometric Series Assumption (GSA) [23]). Let B be a BKZ- $\beta$  reduced basis of a lattice  $\mathcal{L}$ . The GSA states that:

$$\|\mathbf{b}_i^*\| = \|\mathbf{b}_1^*\| \cdot \alpha_\beta^{i-1},$$

where  $\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \leq \alpha_{\beta} < 1$  and  $\alpha_{\beta}$  is called GSA constant. This model is reasonably accurate in practice for  $\beta > 50$  and  $\beta \ll n$ .

Our analysis also relies on fundamental probability distributions that characterize random lattice properties:

**Definition 5 (Exponential Distribution).** For  $\lambda > 0$ , a continuous random variable X follows the exponential distribution  $\text{Expo}(\lambda)$  if its probability density function is given by

$$f_X(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}, \quad x \ge 0.$$

The cumulative distribution function is  $F_X(x) = 1 - e^{-\lambda x}$  for  $x \ge 0$ .

**Definition 6 (Gaussian distribution).** For  $\mu \in \mathbb{R}$  and  $\sigma > 0$ , let  $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$  denote the Gaussian distribution with mean  $\mu$  and variance  $\sigma^2$ , whose density is

$$\rho_{\mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^2)}(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}}, \qquad x \in \mathbb{R}.$$

Its cumulative distribution function is

$$F_X(x) = \frac{1}{2} \left( 1 + \operatorname{erf}\left(\frac{x - \mu}{\sigma\sqrt{2}}\right) \right),$$

where

$$\operatorname{erf}(x) = \frac{2}{\sqrt{\pi}} \int_0^x e^{-t^2} dt.$$

These distributions play a crucial role in understanding the statistical properties of lattice parameters.

#### 2.2 Lattice Hard Problems

We now introduce the fundamental computational problems that form the foundation of lattice-based cryptography. These problems are believed to be hard even for quantum computers, making them central to post-quantum cryptography.

**Definition 7 (Shortest Vector Problem (SVP)** [14]). The SVP is defined as: given a basis  $\mathbf{B}$  of a lattice  $\mathcal{L}$ , finding the shortest non-zero vector  $\mathbf{v}$  in  $\mathcal{L}$ .

{6}------------------------------------------------

Definition 8 (Approximate Shortest Vector Problem (Approx-SVP) [14]). The Approx-SVP is defined as: let  $\gamma \geq 1$ , given a basis **B** of a lattice  $\mathcal{L}$ , finding a non-zero vector  $\mathbf{v} \in \mathcal{L}$  such that  $\|\mathbf{v}\| \leq \gamma \cdot \lambda_1(\mathcal{L})$ .

While SVP and Approx-SVP are fundamental lattice problems, modern cryptographic constructions typically rely on structured variants that offer better efficiency and security properties:

**Definition 9 (Module-LWE Problem (MLWE) [4]).** For integers m, k, and a probability distribution  $D : \mathbb{R}_q \to [0,1]$ , we say that the advantage of algorithm A in solving the decisional  $\text{MLWE}_{m,k,D}$  problem over the ring  $\mathbb{R}_q$  is

$$\operatorname{Adv}_{m,k,D}^{\operatorname{MLWE}}(\mathcal{A}) := |\operatorname{Pr}\left[b = 1 \mid \mathbf{A} \leftarrow \mathbb{R}_q^{m \times k}; \mathbf{t} \leftarrow \mathbb{R}_q^m; b \leftarrow \mathcal{A}(\mathbf{A}, \mathbf{t})\right] \\ - \operatorname{Pr}\left[b = 1 \mid \mathbf{A} \leftarrow \mathbb{R}_q^{m \times k}; \mathbf{s}_1 \leftarrow D^k; \mathbf{s}_2 \leftarrow D^m; b \leftarrow \mathcal{A}(\mathbf{A}, \mathbf{A}\mathbf{s}_1 + \mathbf{s}_2)\right] |.$$

Definition 10 (Short Integer Solution Problem (SIS) [4]). To an algorithm  $\mathcal{A}$  we associate the advantage function  $\operatorname{Adv}_{m,k,\gamma}^{\operatorname{SIS}}$ , to solve the (Hermite Normal Form)  $\operatorname{SIS}_{m,k,\gamma}$  problem over the ring  $\mathbb{Z}_q$  as

$$\mathrm{Adv}^{\mathrm{SIS}}_{m,k,\gamma}(\mathcal{A}) := \Pr \left[ 0 < \|\mathbf{y}\|_{\infty} \le \gamma \wedge \left[ \mathbf{I} \mid \mathbf{A} \right] \cdot \mathbf{y} = \mathbf{0} \mid \mathbf{A} \leftarrow \mathbb{Z}_q^{m \times k}; \mathbf{y} \leftarrow \mathcal{A}(\mathbf{A}) \right].$$

For vectors over  $\mathbb{R}_q$ , we interpret the infinity norm coefficientwise: each polynomial entry is represented by its centered integer coefficients, and  $\|\mathbf{y}\|_{\infty}$  denotes the maximum absolute value among all coefficients of all entries.

**Definition 11 (Module-SIS Problem (MSIS) [4]).** To an algorithm  $\mathcal{A}$  we associate the advantage function  $\operatorname{Adv}_{m,k,\gamma}^{\operatorname{MSIS}}$ , to solve the (Hermite Normal Form)  $\operatorname{MSIS}_{m,k,\gamma}$  problem over the ring  $\mathbb{R}_q$  as

$$\mathrm{Adv}_{m,k,\gamma}^{\mathrm{MSIS}}(\mathcal{A}) := \Pr \left[ 0 < \|\mathbf{y}\|_{\infty} \le \gamma \wedge \left[ \mathbf{I} \mid \mathbf{A} \right] \cdot \mathbf{y} = \mathbf{0} \mid \mathbf{A} \leftarrow \mathbb{R}_q^{m \times k}; \mathbf{y} \leftarrow \mathcal{A}(\mathbf{A}) \right].$$

#### 2.3 Lattice Sieving and Dimension for Free Technique

Lattice sieving generates many short lattice vectors by repeatedly combining pairs of vectors from a large list and retaining shorter reductions; heuristically, a sieve in dimension n requires a list of size about  $(4/3)^{n/2+o(n)}$  and can return near-shortest vectors [21,5].

The dimension-for-free (D4f) technique [12] provides subexponential speedups and memory savings for sieve algorithms. It exploits the fact that, for a shortest nonzero vector  $\mathbf{v} \in \mathcal{L}$ , its projection  $\pi_{f+1}(\mathbf{v})$  lies in the projected lattice  $\mathcal{L}_{\pi[f+1:n]}$  and may already appear in the sieve output over that lattice. Under the heuristic condition

$$\|\pi_{f+1}(\mathbf{v})\| \le \sqrt{4/3} \ \text{gh}(\mathcal{L}_{\pi[f+1:n]}),$$

one may sieve only in dimension n-f and then recover  $\mathbf{v}$  by lifting, thereby reducing both the time and memory costs.

## <span id="page-7-0"></span>3 Refined Rank Reduction for Approx-SVP

In this section, we revisit the Approx-SVP rank reduction condition proposed by Laarhoven and Mariano [\[16\]](#page-27-0), which is commonly used as a feasibility criterion in concrete security analyses, but may be overly aggressive for cryptographic-sized lattice instances. In particular, this classical condition is based on single-vector geometric bounds and does not explicitly account for the probabilistic output behavior of modern sieve-based SVP oracles.

Motivated by this limitation, we introduce two feasibility-oriented rank reduction conditions for Approx-SVP that reflect different levels of geometric modeling fidelity. The practical rank reduction condition is derived from the standard geometric model of lattice sieving introduced by Nguyen and Vidick [\[21\]](#page-28-2), and captures probabilistic feasibility thresholds based on single-vector geometric bounds within this framework. The compact rank reduction condition follows the same prefix-volume viewpoint, but further incorporates the dependence of sieve output quality on the reduction quality of the basis.

Throughout the remainder of the paper, these two conditions play distinct but complementary roles. The practical condition is primarily used for experimental analysis and validation, while the compact condition serves as the operative criterion for security estimation and algorithmic optimization in the subsequent sections.

#### 3.1 Where Rank Reduction Enters BKZ Reduction

We begin with a high-level description of how rank reduction arises in the BKZ setting and how it interacts with dimension-for-free technique.

BKZ reduction progressively improves the Gram–Schmidt profile of the basis by applying an SVP oracle to projected local blocks. As this process continues, a prefix sublattice L[1:k] may already reach a quality at which a target Approx-SVP vector appears within this prefix.

At that stage, solving Approx-SVP no longer requires searching in the full lattice dimension, but only in the rank-k prefix sublattice. This is where rank reduction enters the BKZ algorithm. When dimension-for-free technique is used, the relevant cost is governed by the effective sieving dimension after projection, so feasibility depends on whether the reduced prefix already contains a sufficiently short recoverable vector.

The next subsection derives a practical rank reduction condition from the geometric sieve model, and the following subsection refines it by incorporating the dependence on basis quality.

#### 3.2 Revisiting the Original Rank Reduction Condition

In [\[16\]](#page-27-0), Laarhoven and Mariano provided a preliminary estimation of Approx-SVP rank reduction.

{8}------------------------------------------------

Under the Geometric Series Assumption, a BKZ-reduced lattice basis  $(\mathbf{b}_1, \dots, \mathbf{b}_n)$  satisfies  $\|\mathbf{b}_i^*\| \approx \delta^{n-2i+2} \cdot \operatorname{Vol}(\mathcal{L})^{1/n}$  for all  $i = 1, \dots, n$ . The first k basis vectors form a rank-k sublattice  $\mathcal{L}_{[1:k]} = \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{b}_1, \dots, \mathbf{b}_k) \subset \mathcal{L}$  of volume:

$$\operatorname{Vol}\left(\mathcal{L}_{[1:k]}\right) = \prod_{i=1}^{k} \|\mathbf{b}_{i}^{*}\| = \operatorname{Vol}(\mathcal{L})^{k/n} \cdot \delta^{k(n-k+1)}. \tag{1}$$

By the Gaussian Heuristic, the shortest vector in  $\mathcal{L}_{[1:k]}$  has relative norm:

<span id="page-8-0"></span>
$$\operatorname{gh}(\mathcal{L}_{[1:k]}) \approx \delta^{(n-k+1)} \sqrt{\frac{k}{n}} \cdot \operatorname{gh}(\mathcal{L}).$$
 (2)

To solve Approx-SVP with approximation factor  $\gamma$ , we require  $\delta^{(n-k+1)}\sqrt{k/n}\approx \gamma$ . This indicates how many dimensions can be effectively "skipped" when seeking approximate solutions.

#### 3.3 Geometric Basis for Sieving Output Lengths

The original Approx-SVP rank reduction condition in [16] is based on the assumption that lattice sieving can reliably find vectors with norms close to the Gaussian Heuristic estimate  $gh(\mathcal{L})$ . However, this represents a theoretical lower bound that is rarely achieved in practical high-dimensional lattice reduction. In practice, the success of rank reduction depends on the probabilistic behavior of lattice sieving rather than on achieving the Gaussian Heuristic bound exactly. To capture this probabilistic behavior, we adopt a geometric perspective on lattice sieving to derive a more practical rank reduction condition.

Importantly, the geometric properties used in our analysis arise from the standard geometric model employed in the analysis of lattice sieving algorithms. In this model, the analysis of lattice sieving treats short vectors encountered during the algorithm as if they are sampled independently and uniformly from a thin spherical shell, a heuristic introduced by Nguyen and Vidick [21]. Within this geometric framework, Nguyen and Vidick showed that the expected number of vector pairs whose mutual angle is at most  $\pi/3$  grows exponentially with the dimension. Equivalently, this implies that the number of vectors of norm at most  $\sqrt{4/3} \cdot \text{gh}(\mathcal{L}_{[1:k]})$  produced by lattice sieving is exponential in k, provided that the basis of  $\mathcal{L}_{[1:k]}$  is sufficiently reduced.

In practice, D4f-enabled sieving does not benefit from enumerating all short vectors returned by the sieve. Instead, feasibility hinges on the existence of a single sufficiently short vector that can initiate the projection-and-lifting procedure. Once such a vector is available, additional short vectors do not further reduce the asymptotic cost [4]. Consequently, rank reduction feasibility should be formulated in terms of a single-vector requirement rather than full list saturation.

Accordingly, we require that lattice sieving on the rank-k prefix sublattice  $\mathcal{L}_{[1:k]}$  produces at least one nonzero vector  $\mathbf{x}$  satisfying

$$\|\mathbf{x}\| \leq \sqrt{\frac{4}{3}} \cdot \operatorname{gh}(\mathcal{L}_{[1:k]}).$$

{9}------------------------------------------------

Under the rank reduction viewpoint, we are interested in the regime where such a valid Approx-SVP witness already occurs in the chosen prefix, namely s ∈ L[1:k] ⊆ L. In that case, it suffices that the following condition holds:

<span id="page-9-0"></span>
$$\gamma \cdot \text{gh}(\mathcal{L}) \le \sqrt{\frac{4}{3}} \cdot \text{gh}\left(\mathcal{L}_{[1:k]}\right).$$
 (3)

Combining the Gaussian Heuristic with inequalities [\(2\)](#page-8-0) and [\(3\)](#page-9-0), we derive:

$$\gamma \cdot \sqrt{\frac{n}{2\pi e}} \cdot \operatorname{Vol}(\mathcal{L})^{1/n} \le \sqrt{\frac{4}{3}} \cdot \sqrt{\frac{k}{2\pi e}} \cdot \operatorname{Vol}(\mathcal{L})^{1/n} \cdot \delta^{(n-k+1)}, \tag{4}$$

The existence of such a vector in L[1:k] therefore requires:

<span id="page-9-1"></span>
$$\gamma \le \sqrt{\frac{4k}{3n}} \cdot \delta^{(n-k+1)}. \tag{5}$$

<span id="page-9-3"></span>Condition 1 (Practical Approx-SVP Rank Reduction Condition). Let L be a lattice of dimension n with a BKZ-β reduced basis and root Hermite factor δ. Let γ denote the approximation factor of the target vector relative to gh(L). A rank-k prefix sublattice L[1:k] is said to satisfy the practical Approx-SVP rank reduction condition if Inequality [\(5\)](#page-9-1) holds.

After sufficient BKZ reduction satisfying GSA, the desired short vector can be found by searching only within the first k dimensions. For example, in an 850 dimensional q-ary lattice with root Hermite factor 1.668906, a short vector with approximation factor 1.007991 can be obtained by searching only the rank-164 sublattice.

#### 3.4 Basis-Quality-Dependent Sieving Output Assumption

In this subsection, we formalize a probabilistic model for the output behavior of lattice sieving. Rather than assuming that a sieve deterministically returns a shortest vector, we model the sieve as a sampling procedure over short lattice vectors, whose output distribution depends on the quality of the reduced basis.

Concretely, we view the sieve as inducing a probability distribution over lattice vectors in L[1:k] , supported on vectors whose norms are bounded by a small constant multiple of the Gaussian Heuristic. Conditioned on the basis quality, a single invocation of the sieve outputs a vector v ∈ L[1:k] satisfying ∥v∥ ≤ B · gh(L[1:k]) with non-negligible probability, where the bound B depends only on the reduction quality and the blocksize.

<span id="page-9-2"></span>Importantly, our analysis does not assume that the sieve outputs all short vectors or succeeds deterministically. Instead, it relies solely on the existence of at least one sufficiently short output per sieve invocation. As a result, the rank reduction condition introduced below depends only on a non-negligible success probability and is independent of implementation-specific details of the sieve.

{10}------------------------------------------------

Lemma 1 ([\[10\]](#page-27-6)). Let Γ<sup>n</sup> = {L ∈ R <sup>n</sup> | Vol(L) = 1} be the set of all full-rank lattices of rank n with unit volume. For a random lattice L uniformly sampled from the set Γ<sup>n</sup> of n-dimensional lattices with unit volume, the scaled first minimum v<sup>n</sup> ·λ1(L) <sup>n</sup> converges in distribution to the exponential distribution Expo(1/2) as the dimension n → ∞, where v<sup>n</sup> denotes the volume of the n-dimensional unit ball.

<span id="page-10-0"></span>Heuristic 1 (Basis-Quality-Dependent Sieving Output Assumption). Given an n-dimensional q-ary random lattice L with a BKZ-β reduced basis, lattice sieving produces, with very high probability, a nonzero vector x ∈ L satisfying

$$\|\mathbf{x}\| \le (8\ln(10))^{1/\beta} \cdot \operatorname{gh}(\mathcal{L}),$$

for blocksize β > 50, where the Gaussian Heuristic accurately captures lattice geometry.

Here, the basis quality is characterized by the BKZ blocksize β. A larger β corresponds to higher basis quality and yields a smaller exponential factor (8 ln(10))1/β in the norm upper bound, resulting in shorter output vectors from lattice sieving. This heuristic thus captures how the output quality of sieving depends on the preprocessing quality of the lattice basis in the context of concrete cryptographic instances. We explicitly note that this probabilistic bound is intended to model the behavior of sieving in practice and does not imply a polynomial-time algorithm for Approx-SVP in the asymptotic sense, as the required blocksize β for validity grows with n. In practical settings considered here, solving Approx-SVP via sieving still incurs exponential computational cost.

We now justify the bound given in Heuristic [1.](#page-10-0) For a β-dimensional random lattice L of unit volume, Lemma [1](#page-9-2) shows that the first minimum satisfies:

$$v_{\beta} \cdot \lambda_1(\mathcal{L})^{\beta} \sim \text{Expo}(1/2),$$
 (6)

where v<sup>β</sup> denotes the volume of the β-dimensional unit ball.

For the exponential distribution Expo(1/2), the cumulative distribution function gives:

$$P\left(v_{\beta} \cdot \lambda_1(\mathcal{L})^{\beta} \le t\right) = 1 - e^{-t/2}.\tag{7}$$

Setting a high success probability (e.g., above 0.9999), we obtain:

$$1 - e^{-t/2} = 0.9999 \Rightarrow t = -2\ln(0.0001) = 8\ln(10). \tag{8}$$

Thus, the probabilistic upper bound satisfies:

$$v_{\beta} \cdot (\lambda_1^{\text{max}})^{\beta} = 8 \ln(10) \Rightarrow \lambda_1^{\text{max}} = \left(\frac{8 \ln(10)}{v_{\beta}}\right)^{1/\beta}. \tag{9}$$

Note that the Gaussian Heuristic indicates gh(L) = v −1/β β for a unit-volume lattice, we have

$$\lambda_1^{\text{max}} = (8\ln(10))^{1/\beta} \cdot \text{gh}(\mathcal{L}). \tag{10}$$

{11}------------------------------------------------

By applying this bound to each local block of dimension β encountered during BKZ-β reduction, we obtain, with high confidence, a sieving capability ensuring the existence of a nonzero vector x satisfying

$$\|\mathbf{x}\| \le (8\ln(10))^{1/\beta} \cdot gh(\mathcal{L}). \tag{11}$$

In particular, for a rank-k prefix sublattice L[1:k] , applying the same sieveoutput heuristic to the local sublattice yields the existence of a vector x ∈ L[1:k] such that

$$\|\mathbf{x}\| \le (8\ln(10))^{1/\beta} \cdot gh(\mathcal{L}_{[1:k]}).$$
 (12)

Expressing the Gaussian Heuristic of L[1:k] in terms of the Gram–Schmidt profile of a BKZ-β reduced basis yields the feasibility inequality

$$\gamma \le (8\ln(10))^{1/\beta} \cdot \sqrt{\frac{k}{n}} \cdot \delta^{(n-k+1)}. \tag{13}$$

Let L be a q-ary lattice of dimension n, let β be the BKZ blocksize with root Hermite factor δ, and let γ be the target approximation factor. The analysis above is therefore primarily theoretical. In practice, lattice reduction implementations often employ additional heuristics and engineering optimizations to improve empirical performance, which may lead to successful reductions beyond the predicted feasibility thresholds.

Where the q-ary structure enters. The rank reduction conditions themselves are derived from the prefix-volume estimates, the Gaussian Heuristic, and a probabilistic model of sieve outputs, and are therefore not intrinsically restricted to q-ary lattices. In the present work, the q-ary assumption mainly enters through the experimental regime and the calibration of the heuristic.

Our experiments are carried out on q-ary random lattices arising from SISstyle challenge instances, and the early stages of reduction are strongly influenced by the presence of q-vectors of norm q, which affects the Gram–Schmidt profile and the practical behavior of adaptive blocksize selection. For this reason, the present empirical constants and rules are justified in the q-ary setting considered here. While the same general approach may extend to non-q-ary lattices such as NTRU-type lattices, this would require separate calibration and validation, since the relevant basis profile and reduction behavior may differ substantially. A systematic study of this setting is left for future work.

Which rank reduction condition is used later. Throughout the remainder of this paper, we explicitly distinguish between the roles of the two rank reduction conditions introduced in Section [3.](#page-7-0)

The practical Approx-SVP rank reduction condition (Condition [1\)](#page-9-3) is used to interpret reduction behavior through the geometric lens of lattice sieving, as predicted by the standard spherical-shell model underlying modern sieve analyses. It captures feasibility at the level of single-vector geometric bounds, without explicitly accounting for basis-quality-dependent amplification, as validated in Section [5.2.](#page-16-1)

The compact Approx-SVP rank reduction condition (Condition [2\)](#page-11-1) is adopted as the operative feasibility criterion for theoretical modeling, security estimation, and optimization procedures in the remainder of this work, including the experimental analysis in Section [5.2](#page-16-1) and [5.3,](#page-18-0) as well as the MSIS security evaluation in Section [6.](#page-19-0) This choice reflects its conservative nature and its derivation from basis-quality-dependent sieving guarantees.

## <span id="page-12-0"></span>4 Adaptive Lattice Reduction Strategies in High Dimensions

In this section, we present adaptive lattice reduction strategies aimed at improving the stability and robustness of lattice reduction in high dimensions. Unlike classical BKZ variants that rely on simulator-driven parameter choices and fixed reduction schedules, the strategies introduced here adapt their behavior dynamically based on the evolving quality of the lattice basis.

In particular, we introduce APBKZ as a general-purpose adaptive reduction strategy, and a head-focused variant, HeadAPBKZ, which restricts the reduction scope once a compact rank reduction condition has been satisfied. Together, these strategies form a flexible reduction framework suitable for high-dimensional lattice reduction and subsequent security evaluation.

#### 4.1 APBKZ: Adaptive Pump BKZ for Stable Lattice Reduction

The PnjBKZ algorithm is among the most efficient lattice reduction methods in high dimensions. Its performance is governed by three key parameters: the blocksize β, the dimension-for-free parameter f, and the jump parameter j. At a structural level, these parameters control successive invocations of the core 

{13}------------------------------------------------

reduction primitive in the G6K framework, namely the Pumpκ,f,β procedure [\[2\]](#page-26-1), which applies lattice sieving to the local block [κ : κ+β−1] and incorporates the dimension-for-free technique by skipping f dimensions to reduce computational cost. In practice, β and j are typically selected using a BKZ simulator based on the Gaussian Heuristic and predicted Gram-Schmidt profiles, while f is chosen according to D4f estimates that aim to recover vectors close to gh(L).

However, the effectiveness of PnjBKZ in high dimensions critically depends on simulator-driven parameter choices and static reduction schedules, both of which may become unreliable when the actual basis quality deviates from Gaussian Heuristic predictions. In particular, inaccurate blocksize selection, overly optimistic dimension-for-free estimates, and aggressive jump strategies often lead to unsaturated reduction, especially in early blocks of high-dimensional q-ary lattices.

Compared to classical PnjBKZ, APBKZ differs in three fundamental aspects. First, it eliminates simulator-driven blocksize prediction and instead derives local blocksize directly from the evolving Gram–Schmidt profile of the basis. Second, it replaces static dimension-for-free estimates with basis-quality-dependent rules that adapt to the current reduction state, thereby avoiding reliance on idealized HKZ-reduced assumptions. Third, it enforces full coverage of early blocks during each reduction tour, preventing unsaturated reduction caused by aggressive jump scheduling.

These design choices reflect the central philosophy of APBKZ: lattice reduction in high dimensions should be guided by the actual quality of the current basis, rather than by fixed schedules or simulator-based predictions. In particular, APBKZ does not depend on any rank reduction condition. This is why it is introduced and evaluated separately from HeadAPBKZ, which is activated only after the compact condition becomes relevant.

APBKZ is governed by three adaptive design rules, which jointly determine its reduction behavior.

Rule 1: Adaptive blocksize selection. For a given index i, APBKZ selects the local blocksize β<sup>i</sup> as the smallest value such that gh(Lπ[i:i+βi−1]) < ∥b ∗ i ∥. This condition ensures that the current block is sufficiently reduced to allow further improvement, while avoiding unnecessarily large blocksizes that incur excessive computational cost.

Rule 2: Adaptive dimension-for-free estimation. Given the selected blocksize β<sup>i</sup> , the dimension-for-free parameter f<sup>i</sup> is chosen as the largest value satisfying

$$\sqrt{\frac{4}{3}} \cdot \operatorname{gh}(\mathcal{L}_{\pi[i+f_i:i+\beta_i-1]}) \geq \|\mathbf{b}_i^*\|.$$

Unlike classical D4f rules that target vectors close to gh(L), this criterion only requires outperforming the current Gram–Schmidt norm. As a result, multiple vectors may qualify in practice, which significantly mitigates unsaturated reduction. It is worth noting that, in principle, one could attempt to determine the 

{14}------------------------------------------------

D4f parameter using traditional estimates that assume the existence of vectors close to gh(L). However, such estimates implicitly rely on the lattice basis being close to HKZ-reduced, an assumption that is rarely met in high-dimensional practical reductions and would require substantially higher computational cost to enforce. In contrast, the adaptive rule adopted here is explicitly aligned with the actual quality of the current basis and provides a lightweight alternative that avoids the overhead of pursuing idealized reduction states.

Rule 3: Scope control via a runtime upper bound. Although the D4f value f<sup>i</sup> is selected adaptively and may vary across blocks, it is always bounded by the local blocksize. APBKZ therefore maintains a runtime upper bound fmax = max<sup>i</sup> f<sup>i</sup> over a reduction tour and restricts subsequent reduction to indices i ≤ n−fmax− 50. This constraint is empirically motivated to ensure robust saturation behavior. Restricting the pump scope to indices i ≤ n − fmax − 50 guarantees that the underlying lattice reduction subroutines always operate on projected sublattices of dimension at least 50. In this regime, the behavior of lattice reduction aligns well with GSA, whereas in lower dimensions, boundary effects often render GSAbased predictions unreliable [\[11\]](#page-27-8). Nearby scope bounds are also tested. Replacing the present rule by i ≤ n−β+fmax led to visibly lower saturation and less stable reduction in our parameter range, whereas relaxing the bound produced no clear benefit. The choice n−fmax−50 is therefore retained as a conservative empirical compromise between stability and efficiency.

Algorithm [1](#page-15-1) summarizes the APBKZ algorithm and explicitly implements the above adaptive rules. Notably, APBKZ does not rely on any rank reduction condition and can be applied as a general-purpose adaptive reduction strategy for Approx-SVP.

### <span id="page-14-0"></span>4.2 HeadAPBKZ: Head-Focused Reduction under Rank Reduction Condition

Once the compact Approx-SVP rank reduction condition (Condition [2\)](#page-11-1) is satisfied for a prefix of length k, further reduction of the lattice tail has negligible impact on feasibility. This observation is consistent with prior empirical findings [\[29\]](#page-28-6), which indicate that during the final stages of BKZ-style reduction, applying SVP subroutines to the first few indices is typically sufficient.

Our rank reduction framework formalizes this phenomenon by providing an explicit criterion on the required prefix length. Under this condition, it suffices for the lattice basis to satisfy the Geometric Series Assumption within the rank-k sublattice L[1:k] . Restricting reduction to this prefix does not affect correctness, as sufficiently short vectors found in early blocks propagate through subsequent size reduction and basis updates.

HeadAPBKZ exploits this property as a restricted execution mode of AP-BKZ. It inherits the same adaptive blocksize and dimension-for-free selection rules, but limits the reduction scope to indices

$$i \le \min(k, \ n - f_{\max} - 50),$$

### 6.2 Refined Security Estimation Framework and an Analytic Approximation

The latter does not replace the numerical estimation, but shows that the original objective can be approximated accurately from a continuous BKZ-shape model, an analytic rank approximation, and direct analytic approximations of the two D4f parameters.

Lattice model and Gaussian heuristic. As in the baseline estimator, solving the target  $MSIS_{k,\ell,\zeta'}$  instance is reduced to Approx-SVP on the associated q-ary lattice

$$\mathcal{L}' = \mathcal{L} \left( \begin{bmatrix} q \mathbf{I} \ M(\mathbf{A}) \\ \mathbf{0} \ -\mathbf{I} \end{bmatrix} \right),$$

with h = nk and  $w = nk + n\ell$ , where  $M(\mathbf{A})$  is the standard integer embedding of the public MSIS matrix  $\mathbf{A} \in \mathbb{R}_q^{k \times \ell}$ . Under the Gaussian Heuristic,  $gh(\mathcal{L}') \approx$ 

$$\sqrt{w/(2\pi e)} q^{h/w}$$

Continuous BKZ-shape approximation. To approximate the algorithm **BKZShapeSim** $(q, h, w, \beta)$ (Algorithm 2) without an explicit loop, we use a closed-form profile with the same slope, total log-volume, and uniform correction. Set  $s_{\beta} := 2 \ln \delta_{\beta}$ , glv :=  $h \ln q$ , and

$$B_{\beta} := \left| \frac{\sqrt{1 + 8 \operatorname{glv}/s_{\beta}} - 1}{2} \right|, \qquad \Delta_{\beta} := \frac{s_{\beta}B_{\beta}(B_{\beta} + 1)/2 - \operatorname{glv}}{B_{\beta}},$$

where  $B_{\beta}$  is the discrete solution of  $s_{\beta}B_{\beta}(B_{\beta}+1)/2 \approx \text{glv}$ . We then approximate the profile by

$$L_t^{(\beta)} \approx \begin{cases} (B_{\beta} - t + 1)s_{\beta} - \Delta_{\beta}, & 1 \le t \le B_{\beta}, \\ 0, & t > B_{\beta}, \end{cases}$$

so that  $i_{\beta} = 0$ ,  $j_{\beta} = B_{\beta}$ ,  $\ell_{\beta} := B_{\beta}s_{\beta} - \Delta_{\beta}$ , and  $\alpha_{\beta} := e^{\ell_{\beta}}/\operatorname{gh}(\mathcal{L}')$ .

Analytic rank from the compact condition. For a preprocessing blocksize  $\beta$ , the compact Approx-SVP rank reduction condition (Condition 2) is

$$\alpha_{\beta} \ge \xi_{\beta} \sqrt{\frac{r}{w}} \, \delta_{\beta}^{w-r+1}, \qquad \xi_{\beta} := (8 \ln 10)^{1/\beta},$$

where r denotes the candidate prefix rank. Replacing the inequality with equality gives

$$F_{\beta}(r) := \ln \alpha_{\beta} - \ln \xi_{\beta} - \frac{1}{2} \ln \frac{r}{w} - (w - r + 1) \ln \delta_{\beta} = 0.$$

Dropping the logarithmic correction term first yields

$$r_{\beta}^{(0)} := w + 1 - \frac{\ln \alpha_{\beta} - \ln \xi_{\beta}}{\ln \delta_{\beta}}.$$

Substituting this into F<sup>β</sup> gives

$$F_{\beta}(r_{\beta}^{(0)}) = -\frac{1}{2} \ln \frac{r_{\beta}^{(0)}}{w}.$$

Under typical parameters, r (0) <sup>β</sup> < w, hence Fβ(r (0) β ) > 0. Moreover,

$$F'_{\beta}(r) = -\frac{1}{2r} + \ln \delta_{\beta}, \qquad F''_{\beta}(r) = \frac{1}{2r^2} > 0.$$

Thus F<sup>β</sup> is convex and locally increasing near the continuous root. Applying one Newton step gives

<span id="page-22-1"></span>
$$\widetilde{r}_{\beta} = r_{\beta}^{(0)} - \frac{F_{\beta}(r_{\beta}^{(0)})}{F_{\beta}'(r_{\beta}^{(0)})} \approx w + 1 - \frac{\ln \alpha_{\beta} - \ln \xi_{\beta} - \frac{1}{2} \ln(r_{\beta}^{(0)}/w)}{\ln \delta_{\beta}}, \quad (14)$$

and we set <sup>r</sup><sup>β</sup> := ⌊reβ⌉.

Single-vector success probability. We adopt the same head–middle model as in the baseline estimator MSISCost(i, j, ζ′ , ℓ<sup>i</sup> , q, β) (Algorithm [3\)](#page-21-0) from Section [6.1,](#page-19-2) following [\[4\]](#page-26-5). Let σ<sup>β</sup> := e <sup>ℓ</sup><sup>β</sup> / p B<sup>β</sup> + 1. Then the middle coordinates are modeled by a Gaussian law with standard deviation σβ, giving

$$p_{\mathrm{mid}}(\beta) = \Pr(|X| \le \zeta') = \mathrm{erf}\left(\frac{\zeta'}{\sqrt{2}\sigma_{\beta}}\right), \qquad X \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_{\beta}^2),$$

while the head coordinates are modeled uniformly modulo q, so phead = 2ζ ′/q. Hence

$$p_{\text{single}}(\beta) = p_{\text{mid}}(\beta)^{j_{\beta} - i_{\beta} + 1} p_{\text{head}}^{i_{\beta}}.$$

Under the present continuous approximation, i<sup>β</sup> = 0 and j<sup>β</sup> = Bβ, so this simplifies to

<span id="page-22-0"></span>
$$p_{\beta} := p_{\text{single}}(\beta) = p_{\text{mid}}(\beta)^{B_{\beta}+1} = \left(\text{erf}\left(\frac{\zeta'}{\sqrt{2}\sigma_{\beta}}\right)\right)^{B_{\beta}+1}.$$
 (15)

Direct analytic D4f parameters. We now derive an analytic approximation for the optimal D4f parameter at a fixed stage dimension κ ∈ {β, rβ}, where β is the preprocessing blocksize and r<sup>β</sup> is the analytic prefix rank obtained above. For each such κ, define

<span id="page-23-0"></span>
$$\delta_{\kappa} := \mathsf{RHF}(\kappa), \qquad A_{\kappa} := \left(\sqrt{\frac{4}{3}}\right)^{\kappa}, \qquad a_{\kappa} := \kappa \frac{w}{w-1} \ln \delta_{\kappa}, \qquad b := 0.292 \ln 2.$$

$$\tag{16}$$

We use the refined model of usable short vectors under dimension-for-free from [\[26\]](#page-28-12):

$$N_{\kappa}(f) \approx \left(\sqrt{\frac{4}{3}} \, \delta_{\kappa}^{-fw/(w-1)}\right)^{\kappa} = A_{\kappa} e^{-a_{\kappa} f},$$

and model the stage success probability, stage cost, and local expected cost by

$$P_{\kappa}(f) = 1 - (1 - p_{\beta})^{N_{\kappa}(f)}, \qquad T_{\kappa}(f) = 2^{0.292(\kappa - f)}, \qquad C_{\kappa}(f) := T_{\kappa}(f)/P_{\kappa}(f).$$

Since ln(·) is monotone, we minimize ln Cκ(f) with respect to f instead of Cκ(f). For small pβ, we use the first-order expansion ln(1 − pβ) ≈ −pβ, together with the Poisson approximation

$$P_{\kappa}(f) = 1 - (1 - p_{\beta})^{N_{\kappa}(f)} \approx 1 - e^{-N_{\kappa}(f)p_{\beta}}.$$

Introducing uκ(f) := Nκ(f) p<sup>β</sup> = Aκpβe −aκf , this yields the following continuous approximation to ln Cκ(f)

$$\ln C_{\kappa}(f) \approx b(\kappa - f) - \ln(1 - e^{-u_{\kappa}(f)}).$$

Differentiating with respect to f gives the stationarity condition

$$b = a_{\kappa} \frac{u_{\kappa}(f)}{e^{u_{\kappa}(f)} - 1}.$$

Setting u := uκ(f) and c<sup>κ</sup> := b/aκ, the stationarity condition becomes

$$c_{\kappa} = \frac{u}{e^u - 1},$$

whose unique positive solution, denoted by u ∗ κ , is[1](#page-0-0)

$$u_{\kappa}^* = -W_{-1}(-c_{\kappa}e^{-c_{\kappa}}) - c_{\kappa}.$$

Since uκ(f) = Aκpβe −aκf , solving for f gives

<span id="page-23-1"></span>
$$f_{\kappa}^* = \frac{\ln(A_{\kappa}p_{\beta}) - \ln u_{\kappa}^*}{a_{\kappa}},\tag{17}$$

and we set f ana κ := max{0, ⌊f ∗ κ ⌉}. For convenience, write f<sup>β</sup> := f ana β and f<sup>r</sup><sup>β</sup> := f ana r<sup>β</sup> .

One-dimensional analytic approximation. Substituting the analytic rank and analytic D4f parameters into the two-stage model gives

$$T_{\beta}^{\text{ana}} = 2^{0.292(\beta - f_{\beta})}, \qquad T_{r_{\beta}}^{\text{ana}} = 2^{0.292(r_{\beta} - f_{r_{\beta}})},$$

$$P_{\beta}^{\text{ana}} = 1 - (1 - p_{\beta})^{A_{\beta}e^{-a_{\beta}f_{\beta}}}, \qquad P_{r_{\beta}}^{\text{ana}} = 1 - (1 - p_{\beta})^{A_{r_{\beta}}e^{-a_{r_{\beta}}f_{r_{\beta}}}},$$

and hence

$$P_{\mathrm{tot}}^{\mathrm{ana}}(\beta) = P_{\beta}^{\mathrm{ana}} + (1 - P_{\beta}^{\mathrm{ana}})P_{r_{\beta}}^{\mathrm{ana}}.$$

Here p<sup>β</sup> is given by [\(15\)](#page-22-0), while A<sup>κ</sup> and a<sup>κ</sup> are defined in [\(16\)](#page-23-0) for κ ∈ {β, rβ}. Therefore

$$\widehat{\lambda}_{\text{MSIS}}^{\text{ana}}(q, h, w, \zeta') = \min_{\beta \ge 50} \log_2 \left( \frac{2^{0.292(\beta - f_{\beta})} + 2^{0.292(r_{\beta} - f_{r_{\beta}})}}{P_{\beta}^{\text{ana}} + (1 - P_{\beta}^{\text{ana}}) P_{r_{\beta}}^{\text{ana}}} \right).$$
(18)

The original four-parameter optimization is over (β, r, fβ, fr). Using the analytic approximations above, the prefix rank r and the two D4f parameters f<sup>β</sup> and f<sup>r</sup> are expressed as functions of β. As a result, the problem is approximated by a one-dimensional search over β, with the corresponding analytic choices

$$r = r_{\beta} := \lfloor \widetilde{r}_{\beta} \rfloor, \qquad \widetilde{r}_{\beta} \text{ is given by (14)},$$

$$f_{\kappa} := \max\{0, \lfloor f_{\kappa}^* \rceil\}, \qquad f_{\kappa}^* \text{ is given by (17)}, \qquad \kappa \in \{\beta, r_{\beta}\},$$

so that, in particular, the final-stage parameter is f<sup>r</sup> = fr<sup>β</sup> .

Numerical refined estimation. The numerical refined estimates are obtained by direct discrete optimization of the original refined MSIS cost model over the parameter tuple (β, r, fβ, fr), namely by searching over the admissible values of the preprocessing blocksize, the prefix rank, and the two D4f parameters, and selecting the combination that minimizes the estimated attack cost. By contrast, [\(18\)](#page-24-0) is the one-dimensional analytic approximation obtained after substituting the analytic rank and analytic D4f parameters into the two-stage model.

Relationship with the numerical estimation. The analytic approximation is derived from the continuous BKZ-shape approximation, the analytic rank approximation, and the direct analytic D4f parameters developed above. For Dilithium-II, Dilithium-III, and Dilithium-V, it yields 119.48, 172.84, and 248.89 bits, respectively, compared with the numerical refined estimates 118.31, 171.75, and 247.78 bits, giving discrepancies of about 1.17, 1.09, and 1.11 bits. This shows that the analytic approximation closely tracks the numerical estimation.

Resulting estimates. Table [6](#page-25-0) reports the optimized parameters and resulting security levels for the Dilithium parameter sets under the refined MSIS estimation, together with the Core-SVP baseline. Compared with the Core-SVP baseline, the refined estimation yields a reduction of approximately 3.65–6.09 bits, reflecting the tighter feasibility constraints introduced by the compact rank reduction condition and the refined probability model.

## 7 Conclusion

In this work, we revisited Approx-SVP rank reduction from a feasibility-driven perspective and identified fundamental limitations in existing conditions when applied to cryptographic-sized lattices. By focusing on the existence of a single sufficiently short vector, we derived refined and compact rank reduction conditions that better reflect the requirements of modern D4f-enabled attacks.

We further proposed APBKZ and HeadAPBKZ, two robust lattice reduction variants whose purpose is to ensure that the basis profiles assumed in theoretical analyses can be reliably realized in high dimensions. Experimental results confirm that these strategies significantly improve stability and efficiency without relying on simulator-driven parameter selection.

By integrating refined rank reduction theory with robust reduction behavior, we developed a concrete security estimation framework for MSIS and applied it to Dilithium. Our results suggest that conservative Core-SVP estimates may overstate concrete security. Specifically, accounting for compact rank reduction behavior leads to a decrease of 3.65–6.09 bits in the estimated security level when D4f techniques are considered. We further instantiated an analytic approximation whose estimates stay within about 1.1 bits of the numerical estimation, indicating that the closed-form approximation remains close to the numerical baseline.

{26}------------------------------------------------

Future work. An interesting direction for future research is to develop a rigorous theoretical understanding of practical heuristics used in lattice reduction algorithms, such as re-randomization. While such techniques improve stability in practice, providing formal guarantees remains an open problem and is beyond the scope of this work.
