### A Note on HCTR<sup>++</sup>

# Mustafa Khairallah m.khairallah@ntu.edu.sg

### February 2026

A recent Accordion mode has been proposed by Öztürk et al.: HCTR<sup>++</sup> construction proposed in [OKY26, Cryptology ePrint Archive, Paper 2026/383]. I identify a fundamental correctness flaw in the design. Specifically, I demonstrate that the decryption algorithm (Algorithm 2) does not correctly invert the encryption algorithm (Algorithm 1), rendering the scheme undecryptable as specified.

The authors have acknowledged the use of AI to refine the conclusion section of their paper. I have discovered this vulnerability completely independently of any AI tools. However, as an exercise, I have provided the algorithm to both ChatGPT and Claude (free versions) in retrospect, to see if they can identify the flaw, and I report my comments/observations. I wish to emphasis that the authors have made no claims or acknowledgment of using AI tools beyond drafting and refining the introduction and conclusion sections, and I make no such claims either. The purpose of this note is point out the vulnerability (mistake) in the design, and to look into how free AI models approach finding it.

I would like to also point out that the authors have since updated their design, and this note only refers to the original version. I have not studied the updated design and make no claims about it.

Any comments made in this note are my own and do not reflect on the opinions of any affiliations or funding agencies.

By definition, H is  $\epsilon$ -AXU, so  $r_e$  is somewhat random (in an informal sense), and its output cannot be predicted without the corresponding inputs. ### **1.2 Comments from ChatGPT and Claude**

Given AI usage has been acknowledged in some capacity<sup>1</sup> , and as an exercise, I tested if famous GPTs can be trusted to review such cryptography papers, especially ones with serious flaws like the one presented here. I had varying success. Both Claude and ChatGPT (free versions) were *eventually* able to find the flaw. However, their approach was quite different. ChatGPT was more trusting of the authors assumptions, even insisting the scheme was invertible/correct. It was only able to find the flaw only after I explicitly hinted to an issue in computing . Afterwards, it was able to articulate the flaw clearly. Claude on the other hand was more skeptical of the paper, identifying several proof gaps immediately, and just by asking in the construction is invertible, it found the flaw. ### **1.3 Can this be fixed?**

Occasionally, there are vulnerabilities/flaws in newly proposed designs that boil down to simple typos, minor mistakes in domain separation, or small mistakes that can be fixed easily. In this instance, I believe the flaw is more structural. The authors attempt to increase the IV size during the stream encryption step by making sure that any internal collision requires a collision on 2 bits that are somewhat random. But in doing so, the designers created the circular dependency, as bits of these 2 bits have no way of being generated except from the plaintext, or by breaking the unpredictability of either a secure tweakable block cipher or a secure -XOR-universal hash function. Thus, I believe the original version of HCTR++ is fundamentally flawed and should not be used. It is evident that the updated design is quite different. One way to mitigate this issue is to instead expand the output of the tweakable block cipher, but there are already existing constructions for that. In fact, I argue that the HCTR++ attempt is more closely aligned with the PIV paradigm [ST13], rather than the HCTR2 paradigm [CHB21].

I would like to also briefly point out one major gap in the security proof. The authors reduces the security to the PRF security of , then argue than any almost-XOR-universal hash function is PRF-secure up to an advantage bounded by ( 2 ). Of course, PRFs and XOR-universal hash functions are completely different primitives, a PRF is -almost-XOR-universal but not vice versa.

### **1.4 Conclusion**

This note should serve as a cautionary tale, that relying on AI models does not substitute human scientific analysis when it comes to the provable security of symmetric key schemes. These AI models (especially free versions) are still quite far from building useful symmetric key modes of operation without close human supervision.

I have contacted the authors to inquire about the incorrectness flaw, and they have acknowledged the flaw and updated their design. This note is only concerned with the original version.
