# Generalization of semi-regular sequences: Maximal Gr¨obner basis degree, variants of genericness, and related conjectures<sup>∗</sup>

Momonari Kudo† Kazuhiro Yokoyama‡ May 8, 2025

#### **Abstract**

Nowadays, the notion of semi-regular sequences, originally proposed by Fr¨oberg, becomes very important not only in Mathematics, but also in Information Science, in particular Cryptology. For example, it is highly expected that randomly generated polynomials form a semi-regular sequence, and based on this observation, secure cryptosystems based on polynomial systems can be devised. In this paper, we deal with a semi-regular sequence and its variant, named a generalized cryptographic semi-regular sequence, and give precise analysis on the complexity of computing a Gr¨obner basis of the ideal generated by such a sequence with help of several regularities of the ideal related to Lazard's bound on maximal Gr¨obner basis degree and other bounds. We also study the genericness of the property that a sequence is semi-regular, and its variants related to Fr¨oberg's conjecture. Moreover, we discuss on the genericness of another important property that the initial ideal is weakly reverse lexicographic, related to Moreno-Soc´ıas' conjecture, and show some criteria to examine whether both Fr¨oberg's conjecture and Moreno-Soc´ıas' one hold at the same time.

In order to estimate the complexity, it suffices to provide an upper-bound on the so-called *solving degree*: Roughly speaking, the solving degree is the highest degree of polynomials involved in Gr¨obner basis computation, see [[36](#page-38-1), Section 2.2] for rigorous definitions. If the given ideal is homogeneous, the solving degree can be equal to the maximal degree of the reduced Gr¨obner basis with respect to a monomial ordering that one considers, and its upper-bounds have been wellstudied. In the case of inhomogeneous ideals, the solving degree can be *greater than* the maximal degree of the reduced Gr¨obner basis, whence estimating it *directly* is difficult in general, see [[11\]](#page-37-6) and [[36](#page-38-1)] for details. On the other hand, it is well-known (cf. [\[34](#page-38-2), Prop. 4.3.18]) that a Gr¨obner basis of an inhomogeneous ideal with respect to a graded monomial ordering is easily obtained from a Gr¨obner basis of the ideal of *homogenized* generators. Therefore, the homogeneous case is essential, and we focus on that case. In the following, let *K* be a field, and let *R* = *K*[*x*1*, . . . , xn*] be the polynomial ring of *n* variables over *K*. The term "degree" means total degree, unless otherwise noted. Let *R<sup>d</sup>* be its homogeneous part of degree *d* of *R* (i.e., the *K*-linear space spanned by the monomials in *R* of degree *d*) for each non-negative integer *d*. For an ideal (possibly inhomogeneous) *I* of *R*, we denote by max*.*GB*.*deg*≺*(*I*) the maximal degree of the elements of the reduced Gr¨obner basis of *I* with respect to a monomial ordering *≺* on the set *T* of monomials in *R*, following [\[10](#page-37-7), Definition 7] (it is also denoted by deg(*I, ≺*) in the literature of commutative algebra, see e.g., [[31,](#page-38-3) Definition 4]). If *I* is generated by *F ⊂ R*, we may write max*.*GB*.*deg*≺*(*F*) in place of it.

#### Lazard's bound and Hashemi-Seiler's inequality

For a homogeneous ideal  $I \subset R = K[x_1, \ldots, x_n]$  with a generating set F = $\{f_1,\ldots,f_m\}\subset R$ , it is well-known (see e.g., [3, Proposition 1], [45, Equation (24)]) that a Gröbner basis of I with respect to a graded monomial ordering up to degree d can be computed in  $O(md\binom{n+d-1}{d})^{\omega}$ ), where  $2 \leq \omega < 3$  is the exponent of matrix multiplication. (We can also obtain another upper-bound  $O(m\binom{n+d}{d}^{\omega})$  by the proof of [44, Theorem 1.72].) From this fact, it follows in the homogeneous case that the complexity of computing the reduced Gröbner basis of I is upper-bounded by  $O(mD\binom{n+D-1}{D}^{\omega})$  (or  $O(m\binom{n+D}{D}^{\omega})$ ) for any integer D with  $\max.GB.\deg_{\prec}(I) \leq D$ . Hence, finding a tighter bound D of  $\max.GB.\deg_{\prec}(I)$  is important for estimating shapely the complexity of Gröbner basis computation in the homogeneous case. The case on which we focus is the case where the Krull dimension of R/I, denoted by Krull.dim(R/I), is equal to or less than one; see e.g., [31, Section 1] for a summary of existing results when Krull.dim $(R/I) \geq 2$ . In the case where Krull.dim $(R/I) \leq 1$ , Lazard's bound (called also Macaulay's bound) is well-known. Specifically, if K is large enough, then there exists a (suitable) linear coordinate change  $\sigma$  such that  $R/(I^{\sigma} + \langle x_n \rangle)$  is Artinian (equivalently I is in quasi stable position, see Lemma 2.1.8 below). From this, we assume without loss of generality that  $R/(I+\langle x_n\rangle)$  is Artinian. In this case, putting  $d_j=\deg(f_j)$ for  $1 \leq j \leq m$  and supposing that  $d_1 \geq d_2 \geq \cdots \geq d_m$  (descending order), it follows that

$$\max.\text{GB.deg}_{\prec}(I) \le \sum_{j=1}^{\min\{m,n\}} (d_j - 1) + 1$$
 (1.1)

for the graded reverse lexicographical ordering  $\prec$  with  $x_n \prec \cdots \prec x_1$ , see [39, Théorème 3.3] and [38, Theorem 2]. Recently, Hashemi-Seiler [31] re-proved Lazard's bound by using the inequality

<span id="page-2-0"></span>
$$\max.GB.\deg_{\prec}(I) \le \max\{\text{hilb}(I + \langle x_n \rangle), \text{hilb}(I)\}, \tag{1.2}$$

where hilb(J) denotes the Hilbert regularity of a homogeneous ideal J of R. Note that hilb( $I + \langle x_n \rangle$ ) is equal to the degree of regularity  $d_{\text{reg}}(I + \langle x_n \rangle)$  since  $R/(I+\langle x_n \rangle)$  is Artinian, and hilb(I) is equal to the generalized degree of regularity  $\widetilde{d}_{\text{reg}}(I)$ . For the definition of  $d_{\text{reg}}$  and  $\widetilde{d}_{\text{reg}}$ , see Remark 2.1.2 below.

Remark 1. Note that the condition Krull.dim $(R/I) \leq 1$  does not necessarily imply  $d_{\text{reg}}(I+\langle x_n\rangle) = \text{hilb}(I+\langle x_n\rangle) < \infty$ , without considering any linear coordinate change  $\sigma$ . For example, putting  $F := \{f_1 := x_1x_2 - 1, f_2 := x_2 - 1\} \subset K[x_1, x_2]$  (inhomogeneous), it is obvious that Krull.dim(R[y]/I) = 1 for  $I = \langle F^h \rangle$  but  $d_{\text{reg}}(I+\langle y\rangle) = d_{\text{reg}}(\langle F^{\text{top}}\rangle) = \infty$ , where  $F^h$  is the homogenization of F by an extra variable y, and where  $F^{\text{top}}$  is the homogeneous part of highest degree, see Remark 3 below. In this case,  $F^h = \{x_1x_2 - y^2, x_2 - y\}$  and  $F^{\text{top}} = \{x_1x_2, x_2\}$ .

On the other hand, if K is a finite field of order q and if we would compute a zero of  $\langle F \rangle$  over K, we often add the set of so-called field equations  $x_i^q - x_i$  to an inhomogeneous F, say  $F_1 := F \cup \{x_i^q - x_i : 1 \le i \le n\}$ , so that we always have  $d_{\text{reg}}(\langle F_1^h \rangle + \langle y \rangle) = d_{\text{reg}}(\langle F_1^{\text{top}} \rangle) < \infty$ .

#### Our results and conjectures

In this paper, we focus on several cases that are expected to be generic in some sense, and obtain upper-bounds on max.GB.deg $_{\prec}(I)$  and some results related to Fröberg's conjecture [23] and Moreno-Socías' one [41], in those cases. For example, we will assume that  $R/(I+\langle x_n\rangle)$  has the Hilbert series associated to generic forms, as conjectured by Fröberg [23] (see Conjecture 2.3.7 below). Note that a homogeneous polynomial sequence is called *cryptographic semi-regular* (cf. [8]) if its associated coordinate ring has the Hilbert series conjectured by Fröberg, see Definition 2.2.3 and Proposition 2.2.4 below for details. In the literature, a cryptographic semi-regular sequence plays a very important role in analyzing Gröbner basis computation, both in theory and in practical applications to cryptography, see e.g., Bardet et al's works [1], [2], [3], [4], Gorla et al.'s works [8], [10], [11], and our ones [35], [36]. In our earlier work [36], when  $m \ge n$ , we improved Lazard's bound on max.GB.deg<sub> $\prec$ </sub>(F) to  $\sum_{j=1}^{n} (d_j - 1) + 1$  with  $d_1 \leq \cdots \leq d_m$  (ascending order), supposing that  $(f_1|_{x_n=0},\ldots,f_i|_{x_n=0})$  is cryptographic semi-regular on  $K[x_1,\ldots,x_{n-1}]$  for each  $1\leq i\leq m$ . In [36], we also defined a generalized cryptographic semi-regular sequence (see Definition 2.2.8 below for the definition and Remark 2.2.9 for a construction), as an extension of cryptographic semi-regular sequence to the case of Krull dimension one.

As the first result of this paper, we prove Theorem 1.1, which provides an upper-bound on  $\max.\mathrm{GB.deg}_{\prec}(I)$  for a generalized cryptographic semi-regular sequence and a complexity bound on computing a Gröbner basis of I.

<span id="page-3-0"></span>**Theorem 1.1.** Let  $R = K[x_1, ..., x_n]$  be the polynomial ring of n variables over a field K (in any characteristic), and  $\prec$  the graded reverse lexicographical ordering with  $x_n \prec \cdots \prec x_1$ . Let I be a homogeneous ideal of R generated by homogeneous polynomials  $f_1, ..., f_m$  in  $R \smallsetminus K$  such that  $Krull.dim(R/I) \leq 1$ . Assume that  $(f_1|_{x_n=0}, ..., f_m|_{x_n=0})$  is cryptographic semi-regular on  $K[x_1, ..., x_{n-1}]$  (equivalently  $(f_1, ..., f_m, x_n)$  is cryptographic semi-regular), and that  $(f_1, ..., f_m)$  is generalized cryptographic semi-regular, Then the following (1) and (2) hold:

(1) We have

<span id="page-3-1"></span>
$$\max.GB.\deg_{\prec}(I) \le \max\{d_{\text{reg}}(I + \langle x_n \rangle), \widetilde{d}_{\text{reg}}(I)\} \le D^{(n,m)}, \tag{1.3}$$

{4}------------------------------------------------

where

<span id="page-4-0"></span>
$$D^{(n,m)} := \begin{cases} \deg\left(\left[\frac{\prod_{i=1}^{m}(1-z^{d_i})}{(1-z)^n}\right]\right) + 1 & (m \ge n), \\ \sum_{i=1}^{n-1}(d_i-1) + 1 & (m = n-1). \end{cases}$$
(1.4)

(2) The complexity of computing a Gröbner basis of I with respect to  $\prec$  is upperbounded by

<span id="page-4-1"></span>
$$O\left(m\binom{n+D^{(n,m)}-1}{D^{(n,m)}}^{\omega}\right) \tag{1.5}$$

arithmetic operations in K, where  $2 \le \omega < 3$  is the exponent of matrix multiplication.

The bound  $D^{(n,m)}$  in (1.3) is optimal for a homogeneous ideal I satisfying the assumptions of Theorem 1.1, see Example 3.1.4 below. The proof of (2) in Theorem 1.1 is based on a well-known fact that the a Gröbner basis of I is obtained by computing the reduced row echelon form of the degree-D Macaulay matrix for any D with  $D \ge \max.\text{GB.deg}_{\prec}(I)$ , see Section 3.1 for details.

Our assumption that a homogeneous polynomial sequence is generalized cryptographic semi-regular could be useful to analyze the security of multivariate cryptosystems and so on (cf. [36, Section 4.3]). The bound  $D^{(n,m)}$  given in (1.4) coincides with Lazard's bound when  $m \in \{n-1,n\}$ , while it can be sharper than Lazard's one when m > n. We also note that, the complexity bound (1.5) is slightly better than the usual bound  $O(mD\binom{n+D-1}{D})^{\omega}$  (see e.g., [3, Proposition 1], [45, Equation (24)]) and than  $O(m\binom{n+D}{D})^{\omega}$  (which comes from the proof of [44, Theorem 1.72]) for an arbitrary D with  $D \ge \max.\text{GB.deg}_{\prec}(F)$ .

As the second result, we shall prove that Hashemi-Seiler's inequality (1.2) is as possible as tight under some assumptions, one of which is that an initial ideal is weakly reverse lexicographic. Here, a weakly reverse lexicographic ideal is a monomial ideal J such that if  $x^{\alpha}$  is one of the minimal generators of J then every monomial of the same degree which preceds  $x^{\alpha}$  must belong to J as well. Moreno-Socías [41] conjectured that, for the graded reverse lexicographical ordering, the initial ideal of a generic ideal is a weakly reverse lexicographic.

<span id="page-4-2"></span>**Theorem 1.2.** Let  $R = K[x_1, ..., x_n]$  be the polynomial ring of n variables over a field K (in any characteristic), and let  $\prec$  be the graded reverse lexicographical ordering  $\prec$  on R with  $x_n \prec \cdots \prec x_1$ . Let I be a proper homogeneous ideal of R with Krull.dim $(R/I) \leq 1$  and Krull.dim $(R/(I + \langle x_n \rangle)) = 0$ . If  $(f_1|_{x_n=0}, ..., f_m|_{x_n=0})$  is cryptographic semi-regular on  $K[x_1, ..., x_{n-1}]$  (equivalently  $(f_1, ..., f_m, x_n)$  is cryptographic semi-regular) and if the initial ideal in  $(I) = \langle LM_{\prec}(I) \rangle_R$  is weakly reverse lexicographic, then the inequality (1.2) becomes an equality.

# 2.1 Hilbert regularity

Let I be a non-zero homogeneous ideal of R. Let  $\operatorname{HP}_{R/I} \in \mathbb{Q}[z]$  denote the Hilbert polynomial of R/I, namely there exists a non-negative integer  $d_0$  such that  $\operatorname{HF}_{R/I}(d) = \operatorname{HP}_{R/I}(d)$  for any d with  $d \geq d_0$ , where HF is the Hilbert function.

**Definition 2.1.1** (Hilbert regularity (or index of regularity [14, Chapter IX, Section 3])). The smallest  $d_0$  such that  $\operatorname{HF}_{R/I}(d) = \operatorname{HP}_{R/I}(d)$  for any d with  $d \geq d_0$  is called the *Hilbert regularity* of I, denoted by  $\operatorname{hilb}(I)$ , or is called the *index of regularity* of I, denoted by  $i_{\text{reg}}(I)$ .

Throughout the rest of this subsection, we set r := Krull.dim(R/I).

= min{ $d_0$  : dim<sub>K</sub>( $R/I$ ) <sub>$d$</sub>  = dim<sub>K</sub>( $R/I$ ) <sub>$d_0$</sub>  for any  $d$  with  $d \ge d_0$ }.

The middle and the right hand sides are called the *generalized degree of regularity* of *<sup>I</sup>* for *<sup>r</sup> <sup>≤</sup>* 1, denoted by *<sup>d</sup>*ereg(*I*) (cf. [[36,](#page-38-1) Sect. 2.3]): When *<sup>r</sup>* = 0, these are equal to the *degree of regularity d*reg(*I*) := min*{d* : *R<sup>d</sup>* = *Id}*. Namely, we have

<span id="page-8-1"></span>
$$hilb(I) = \begin{cases} \widetilde{d}_{reg}(I) & (r=1), \\ \widetilde{d}_{reg}(I) & = d_{reg}(I) & (r=0). \end{cases}$$
 (2.1.1)

See [[8](#page-37-8)] for bounds on *d*reg(*I*).

Although we mainly treat the case where *r ≤* 1 in this paper, we distinguish hil(*I*) and *<sup>d</sup>*ereg(*I*) for the compatibility with the condition that a sequence of homogeneous polynomials in *R* is regular (cf. Lemma [2.2.12](#page-14-1) below).

**Remark 2.1.3.** Note also that the Hilbert series HS*R/I* is written as HS*R/I* (*z*) = *hR/I* (*z*)*/*(1 *− z*) *r* for some polynomial *hR/I* (*z*) *∈* Z[*z*], which is called the *hpolynomial* of *R/I*. Then, we have hilb(*I*) = deg *hR/I − r* + 1, see [\[7,](#page-37-10) Prop. 4.1.12].

$$hilb(I) \le reg(I) = reg(R/I) + 1,$$

where 'reg' means *Castelnuovo-Mumford regularity*, see [[17,](#page-37-11) §20.5] for the definition. The equality holds if *R/I* is Artinian, i.e., the Krull dimension of *R/I* is zero, see e.g., [\[18](#page-37-12), Corollary 4.4].

**Definition 2.1.4** (cf. [[31,](#page-38-3) Section 3])**.** We say that *I* is in *Noether position* with respect to the variables *x*1*, . . . , xn−r*, where *r* = Krull*.*dim(*R/I*), if the injective ring extension *K*[*xn−r*+1*, . . . , xn*] *→ R/I* is integral.

Although it may be an easy consequence of the theory of hyperplane sections, there exists a hyperplane defined over K that does not pass through any of given finite points in a projective space over  $\overline{K}$  if K is large enough. Here, we prove a somehow detailed argument:

<span id="page-10-1"></span>**Lemma 2.1.10.** Let  $p_1, \ldots, p_N$  be mutually distinct points in  $\mathbb{P}^{n-1}(\overline{K})$ . If the cardinality of K is greater than N, then there exists a linear form  $\ell \in R$  that does not vanish at any of  $p_1, \ldots, p_N$ .

*Proof.* For each point  $\mathbf{p}_k = (p_{1,k} : \cdots : p_{n,k}) \in \mathbb{P}^{n-1}(\overline{K})$ , we denote by  $S_k$  the set of all linear forms that vanish at  $\mathbf{p}_k$ , say

$$S_k = \{y_1x_1 + \dots + y_nx_n \mid y_i \in K \text{ and } y_1p_{1,k} + \dots + y_np_{n,k} = 0\},\$$

which is isomorphic, as K-linear spaces, to the following K-linear space of  $K^n$ :

$$S'_k = \{(y_1, \dots, y_n) \in K^n \mid p_{1,k}y_1 + \dots + p_{n,k}y_n = 0\}.$$

The dimension of  $S'_k$  as a K-linear space is at most n-1. Indeed, for an arbitrary non-zero entry  $p_{i,k}$  of  $p_k$ , the vector with 1 in the i-th entry and 0's elsewhere does not belong to  $S'_k$ , so that  $S'_k$  is a proper subspace of  $K^n$ .

When K is an infinite field, it follows from the irreducibility of an affine space over K that  $\bigcup_{k=1}^{N} S'_k$  cannot cover  $K^n$ . When K is a finite field of order q, we have

$$\#\left(\bigcup_{k=1}^{N} S_k'\right) \le \sum_{k=1}^{N} \#S_k' \le N \cdot q^{n-1} < q^n,$$

whence a desired linear form over K exists.

<span id="page-10-2"></span>**Lemma 2.1.11.** With notation as above, assume that  $\operatorname{Krull.dim}(R/I) \leq 1$ . If the cardinality of K is greater than  $\#V_{\overline{K}}(I)$ , then there exists a linear form  $\ell \in R$  such that  $\operatorname{Krull.dim}(R/(I+\langle \ell \rangle)) = 0$ . Moreover, there also exists a suitable linear coordinate change  $\sigma$  sending  $\ell$  to  $x_n$  such that  $\operatorname{Krull.dim}(R/(I^{\sigma} + \langle x_n \rangle)) = 0$ .

{11}------------------------------------------------

*Proof.* The first part follows from Lemma [2.1.10.](#page-10-1) There exists a *k ∈ {*1*, . . . , n}* such that the *xk*-coefficient of *ℓ* is not zero. We may also assume that the *xk*coefficient is 1, without loss of generality. Let *σ*<sup>1</sup> be the linear transformation of variables *x*1*, . . . , x<sup>n</sup>* defined by a permutation exchanging *x<sup>k</sup>* and *xn*, and write the image of *ℓ* by *σ*<sup>1</sup> as *ℓ <sup>σ</sup>*<sup>1</sup> = *a*0*x*<sup>0</sup> + *a*1*x*<sup>1</sup> + *· · ·* + *anx<sup>n</sup>* for *a<sup>i</sup> ∈ K* with *a<sup>n</sup>* = 1. Then, let *σ*<sup>2</sup> be a linear transformation of variables *x*1*, . . . , x<sup>n</sup>* defined by

$$h^{\sigma_2} := h(x_1, \dots, x_{n-1}, x_n - a_1 x_1 - \dots - a_{n-1} x_{n-1})$$

for *h ∈ R*. Then, the invertible transformation *σ* := *σ*<sup>2</sup> *◦σ*<sup>1</sup> of variables *x*1*, . . . , x<sup>n</sup>* sends *ℓ* to *xn*, so that Krull*.*dim(*R/*(*I <sup>σ</sup>* + *hxni*)) = 0.

#### **2.2 Generalized semi-regular sequences**

In this subsection, we recall the definition of cryptographic semi-regular sequences and that of generalized ones. Throughout this subsection, let *f*1*, . . . , f<sup>m</sup> ∈ R* be *homogeneous* polynomials of *positive* degrees *d*1*, . . . , dm*, and put *I* = *hf*1*, . . . , fmi*.

<span id="page-11-0"></span>**Theorem 2.2.1** (cf. [[15,](#page-37-13) Theorem 1])**.** *For each d with d ≥* max*{d<sup>i</sup>* : 1 *≤ i ≤ m}, the following are equivalent:*

- *(1) For each i with* 1 *≤ i ≤ m, if a homogeneous polynomial g ∈ R satisfies gf<sup>i</sup> ∈ hf*1*, . . . , fi−*1*i<sup>R</sup> and* deg(*gfi*) *< d, then we have g ∈ hf*1*, . . . , fi−*1*iR, namely, the multiplication-by-f<sup>i</sup> map* (*R/hf*1*, . . . , fi−*1*i*)*t−d<sup>i</sup> −→* (*R/hf*1*, . . . , fi−*1*i*)*<sup>t</sup> is injective for any t with d<sup>i</sup> ≤ t < d.*
- *(2) We have*

<span id="page-11-1"></span>
$$HS_{R/I}(z) \equiv \frac{\prod_{j=1}^{m} (1 - z^{d_j})}{(1 - z)^n} \pmod{z^d}, \tag{2.2.1}$$

*where* HS *denotes the Hilbert series.*

*(3) We have H*1(*K•*(*f*1*, . . . , fm*))*≤d−*<sup>1</sup> = 0*, where K•*(*f*1*, . . . , fm*) *denotes the Koszul complex on the sequence* (*f*1*, . . . , fm*)*.*

**Definition 2.2.2** ([[2](#page-36-2), Definition 3]; see also [\[15](#page-37-13), Definition 1])**.** For each *d* with *d ≥* max*{d<sup>i</sup>* : 1 *≤ i ≤ m}*, a sequence (*f*1*, . . . , fm*) of homogeneous polynomials in *R* ∖ *K* is said to be *d*-regular if the equivalent conditions in Theorem [2.2.1](#page-11-0) hold. 

{12}------------------------------------------------

Here, we define a cryptographic semi-regular sequence:

<span id="page-12-0"></span>**Definition 2.2.3** ([2, Definition 5], [4, Definition 5]; see also [15, Section 2]). A sequence  $(f_1, \ldots, f_m)$  of homogeneous polynomials in  $R \setminus K$  is said to be cryptographic semi-regular if it is  $d_{reg}(I)$ -regular.

The Hilbert series of R/I for a cryptographic semi-regular sequence  $(f_1, \ldots, f_m)$  is easily computed, by the following Proposition 2.2.4:

<span id="page-12-1"></span>**Proposition 2.2.4** ([15, Proposition 1 (d)]; see also [4, Proposition 6]). With the same notation as in Theorem 2.2.1, a sequence  $(f_1, \ldots, f_m)$  of homogeneous polynomials in  $R \setminus K$  is cryptographic semi-regular if and only if

<span id="page-12-2"></span>
$$HS_{R/\langle f_1,...,f_m\rangle}(z) = \left[\frac{\prod_{j=1}^m (1 - z^{d_j})}{(1 - z)^n}\right],$$
(2.2.2)

where  $[\cdot]$  means truncating a formal power series over  $\mathbb{Z}$  after the last consecutive positive coefficient. (Note that, in this case, we have  $d_{\text{reg}}(I) = \deg(HS_{R/I}(z)) + 1.$ )

The above 'standard' form (2.2.2) of Hilbert series was introduced by Fröberg in his celebrated paper [23]. We will recall his famous conjecture (Conjecture 2.3.7 below) in the next subsection.

**Remark 2.2.5.** In [25, Definition 1.3], Fröberg-Hollman already defined a *sufficiently generic ideal* as one generated by homogeneous polynomials  $f_1, \ldots, f_m \in R$  satisfying (2.2.2). Namely, a cryptographic semi-regular sequence is nothing but a sequence of homogeneous polynomials generating a sufficiently generic ideal.

Here, we recall the following lemma by Fröberg:

<span id="page-12-3"></span>**Lemma 2.2.6** ([23, b) on page 121]). With notation as above, we have

$$HS_{R/I}(z) \ge^{lex} \left[ \frac{\prod_{j=1}^{m} (1 - z^{d_j})}{(1 - z)^n} \right]$$

in the lexicographic sense, i.e., the first coefficient that differs, if any, is greater on the left hand side, see [23, page 118].

**Remark 2.2.7.** For any non-negative integer d, it follows from Lemma 2.2.6 that

$$\left(\operatorname{HS}_{R/I}(z) \bmod z^d\right) \ge^{\operatorname{lex}} \left(\frac{\prod_{j=1}^m (1-z^{d_j})}{(1-z)^n} \bmod z^d\right).$$

To deal with the case where R/I has Krull dimension one, we extend the notion of cryptographic semi-regular as follows:

{13}------------------------------------------------

<span id="page-13-0"></span>**Definition 2.2.8** ([36, Definition 2.3.3]). A sequence  $(f_1, \ldots, f_m)$  of homogeneous polynomials in  $R \setminus K$  is said to be *generalized cryptographic semi-regular* if it is  $\widetilde{d}_{reg}(I)$ -regular.

<span id="page-13-1"></span>Remark 2.2.9 (cf. [36, Section 4.1]). A generalized cryptographic semi-regular (but neither regular nor semi-regular) sequence is easily generated. For example, choosing coefficients of  $g_1, \ldots, g_m$  with  $m \ge n$  randomly at random from a finite field  $K = \mathbb{F}_q$  and choosing an arbitrary point  $\mathbf{p} = (p_1, \ldots, p_{n-1}, p_n) \in \mathbb{A}^n(K)$  with  $p_n = 1$ , then it is expected that  $(f_1, \ldots, f_m)$  with  $f_j := g_j(x_1, \ldots, x_n) - g_j(p_1, \ldots, p_n)x_n^{d_j}$  is generalized cryptographic semi-regular (but neither regular nor semi-regular). Without loss of generality, we may take  $\mathbf{o} = (0 : \cdots : 0 : 1)$  as  $\mathbf{p}$ , see Section 4 below for details.

<span id="page-13-3"></span>**Example 2.2.10.** Let  $K = \mathbb{F}_{31}$ , n = 3, and  $R = K[x_1, x_2, x_3]$ . Here we consider the case where  $(d_1, d_2, d_3) = (3, 2, 2)$  with m = 3, and let

$$f_1 = 27x_1^3 + 5x_1^2x_2 + 6x_1x_2^2 + 20x_2^3 + 29x_1x_2x_3 + 25x_2^2x_3 + 3x_1x_3^2 + 26x_2x_3^2,$$

$$f_2 = 2x_1^3 + 29x_1^2x_2 + 25x_1x_2^2 + 3x_2^3 + 25x_1^2x_3 + 20x_1x_2x_3 + 25x_2^2x_3 + 19x_1x_3^2 + 24x_3x_3^2,$$

$$f_3 = 12x_1^2 + 12x_1x_2 + 23x_2^2 + x_1x_3 + 10x_2x_3.$$

Putting  $I = \langle f_1, f_2, f_3 \rangle$ , one can verify that

$$HS_{R/I}(z) = 1 + 3z + 5z^2 + 5z^3 + 3z^4 + z^5 + z^6 + \cdots$$

with Krull.dim(R/I) = 1, D := hilb(I) = 5, and dim $_K(R/I)_D = 1$ , so that  $(f_1, f_2, f_3)$  is D-regular (in fact D + 1-regular). Namely  $(f_1, f_2, f_3)$  is generalized cryptographic semi-regular, but is neither regular nor cryptographic semi-regular.

<span id="page-13-4"></span>**Lemma 2.2.11.** If the sequence  $\mathbf{F} = (f_1, \dots, f_m)$  is generalized cryptographic semi-regular, then we have

<span id="page-13-2"></span>
$$\widetilde{d}_{\text{reg}}(I) \le \deg\left(\left[\frac{\prod_{j=1}^{m} (1 - z^{d_j})}{(1 - z)^n}\right]\right) + 1.$$
 (2.2.3)

*Proof.* By our assumption that  $\mathbf{F}$  is generalized cryptographic semi-regular, the equality (2.2.1) holds for  $d = \widetilde{d}_{reg}(I)$ . For any d' with  $d' < \widetilde{d}_{reg}(I)$ , we have  $\operatorname{HF}_{R/I}(d') > 0$ , whence the  $z^{d'}$ -coefficient of  $(\prod_{j=1}^m (1-z^{d_j}))/(1-z)^n$  is also positive. Thus, the inequality (2.2.3) holds.

For a sequence  $\mathbf{F} = (f_1, \dots, f_m)$  of homogeneous polynomials in  $R \setminus K$ , we consider the following four conditions:

- (A)  $\mathbf{F}$  is regular, equivalently  $HS_{R/I}(z) = (\prod_{j=1}^m (1-z^{d_j}))/(1-z)^n$ .
- **(B)**  $\boldsymbol{F}$  is cryptographic semi-regular (i.e.,  $d_{\text{reg}}(I)$ -regular).

{14}------------------------------------------------

- (C) F is generalized cryptographic semi-regular (i.e.,  $\widetilde{d}_{reg}(I)$ -regular).
- (D) F is hilb(I)-regular.

<span id="page-14-1"></span>**Lemma 2.2.12.** For conditions (A), (B), (C), and (D) as defined above, there is an implication:

$$(A) \Rightarrow (B) \Rightarrow (C) \Rightarrow (D), \ but \ (D) \stackrel{r \ge 2}{\not \Rightarrow} (C) \stackrel{r=1}{\not \Rightarrow} (B) \stackrel{m > n}{\not \Rightarrow} (A).$$

Namely, (A) and (B) are equivalent for  $m \le n$ , (B) and (C) are equivalent for  $r \ne 1$ , (C) and (D) are equivalent for  $r \le 1$ , and (A), (B), (C), and (D) are all equivalent for m = n and r = 0.

*Proof.* It is clear that  $(A) \Rightarrow (B) \Rightarrow (C) \Rightarrow (D)$ . When  $r \leq 1$ , it follows from (2.1.1) that  $(D) \Rightarrow (C)$  holds. Also when  $r \neq 1$ , we have  $d_{\text{reg}}(I) = \widetilde{d}_{\text{reg}}(I)$ , whence  $(C) \Rightarrow (B)$  holds. For  $m \leq n$ , Proposition 2.2.4 implies  $(B) \Rightarrow (C)$ .

On the other hand, it is clear that (B) does not imply (A) if m > n. The sequence  $(f_1, f_2, f_3)$  provided in Example 2.2.10 gives a counterexample for the implication (C)  $\Rightarrow$  (B) when r = 1. As for (D)  $\Rightarrow$  (C), here is a counterexample with r = 2: Let  $(g_1, g_2) := (x^3, xy) \in \mathbb{Q}[x, y, z]^2$  and put  $I := \langle g_1, g_2 \rangle$ . Then one can verify that  $HS_{R/I}(z) = 1 + 3z + 5z^2 + 6z^3 + 7z^4 + \cdots$  with Krull.dim(R/I) = 2 and D := hilb(I) = 2, whence  $(g_1, g_2)$  is D-regular but not regular.

#### <span id="page-14-0"></span>2.3 Genericness and Fröberg's conjecture

Given m positive integers  $d_1, \ldots, d_m$ , let  $V_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)} := R_{d_1} \times \cdots \times R_{d_m}$ , which is topologically identified with the affine space  $\mathbb{A}^{N_1}(K) \times \cdots \times \mathbb{A}^{N_m}(K) = \mathbb{A}^N(K)$  of dimension  $N := \sum_{j=1}^m N_j$  with  $N_j := \binom{n+d_j-1}{d_j}$ . Here, we identify a form in  $R_{d_j}$  with a point in  $\mathbb{A}^{N_j}(K)$  by taking the coefficients. We may write  $K^N$  simply for  $\mathbb{A}^N(K)$ . We also correspond a point  $\mathbf{F} = (f_1, \ldots, f_m) \in V_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}$  to an ideal  $\langle \mathbf{F} \rangle_R := \langle f_1, \ldots, f_m \rangle_R$ , where the subscript R may be omitted if the meaning is clear from the context.

Following Fröberg's definition (cf. [23], [24], [27]), we say that a form of  $R_d$  is generic if all monomials of degree d occur with nonzero coefficient and if all the coefficients are algebraically independent over the prime field of K. We call  $\mathbf{F} = (f_1, \ldots, f_m) \in V_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}$  a generic sequence if all  $f_i$ 's are generic forms with coefficients that are mutually algebraic independent over the prime field. If K does not contain N elements that are algebraically independent over the prime field of K (for example K is an algebraic extension of the prime field), we may replace K by its arbitrary extension field L containing such N elements. When we consider a property of Hilbert series, this makes a sense since every Hilbert series is not changed by extending the field of coefficients.

On the other hand, the genericness of a property for  $\mathbf{F} \in V_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}$  is defined as follows:

{15}------------------------------------------------

<span id="page-15-0"></span>**Definition 2.3.1** (cf. [[43,](#page-39-6) Section 1])**.** For given *n*, *m*, and (*d*1*, . . . , dm*), a property *P* for *F* = (*f*1*, . . . , fm*) *∈ Vn,m,*(*d*1*,...,dm*) is said to be *generic* if it holds on a non-empty Zariski open set in *Vn,m,*(*d*1*,...,dm*) , namely there exists a non-empty Zariski open set *U* in *Vn,m,*(*d*1*,...,dm*) such that

$$U \subset \{ \mathbf{F} = (f_1, \dots, f_m) \in V_{n, m, (d_1, \dots, d_m)} : \mathbf{F} \text{ satisfies } \mathcal{P} \}.$$

Here, we recall Fr¨oberg's important lemma for the minimality of the Hilbert series on generic sequences. Note that the characteristic of *K* is assumed to be 0 in the original paper [[23\]](#page-38-4), but it can be easily proved that the assertion of the lemma holds for any characteristic. We here state the lemma in a slightly modified (but equivalent) form for the convenience of our use.

<span id="page-15-1"></span>**Lemma 2.3.2** ([\[23,](#page-38-4) Lemma 1])**.** *With notation as above, let* Ω *be an extension field of K, and let C* := *{cj,t* : 1 *≤ j ≤ m, t ∈ Td<sup>j</sup> } be a set of elements in* Ω *that are algebraically independent over the prime field K*<sup>0</sup> *of K. <span id="page-18-1"></span>**Lemma 3.1.1.** With notation as above, if m = n - 1 and if the sequence  $(f_1, \ldots, f_m, x_n)$  is cryptographic semi-regular (equivalently, it is regular since m + 1 = n), then we have  $\widetilde{d}_{reg}(I) = d_{reg}(I + \langle x_n \rangle_R) - 1$ , and hence

$$\max.\text{GB.deg}_{\prec}(I) \leq \max\{d_{\text{reg}}(I + \langle x_n \rangle), \widetilde{d}_{\text{reg}}(I)\} = d_{\text{reg}}(I + \langle x_n \rangle) = \sum_{j=1}^{n-1} (d_j - 1) + 1$$

for the graded reverse lexicographical ordering  $\prec$  with  $x_n \prec \cdots \prec x_1$ . Moreover,  $(f_1, \ldots, f_m)$  is generalized cryptographic semi-regular (equivalently, it is regular since m < n).

*Proof.* Since  $(f_1, \ldots, f_m, x_n)$  is regular,  $(f_1, \ldots, f_m)$  is also regular, so that

$$\operatorname{HS}_{R/I}(z) = \operatorname{HS}_{R/(I+\langle x_n \rangle)}(z) \cdot \frac{1}{1-z} = \operatorname{HS}_{R/(I+\langle x_n \rangle)}(z) \cdot (1+z+z^2+\cdots)$$

with

$$\operatorname{HS}_{R/(I+\langle x_n\rangle)}(z) = \frac{\prod_{j=1}^{n-1} (1-z^{d_j}) \cdot (1-z)}{(1-z)^n} = \frac{\prod_{j=1}^{n-1} (1-z^{d_j})}{(1-z)^{n-1}} = \prod_{j=1}^{n-1} \left(\sum_{k=0}^{d_j-1} z^k\right),$$

which is a polynomial. It is clear that  $\widetilde{d}_{reg}(I) = d_{reg}(I + \langle x_n \rangle_R) - 1$ , as desired.  $\square$

$$M_{\leq D}(F) = \begin{pmatrix} M_D(F) & & \\ & \ddots & \\ & & M_{d_0}(F) \end{pmatrix}$$

with  $d_0 := \min\{\deg(f_j) : 1 \leq j \leq m\}$ , where each  $M_d(F)$  is a homogeneous Macaulay matrix defined as follows: For each d with  $d \geq d_0$ , the degree-d homogeneous Macaulay matrix  $M_d(F)$  of F has columns indexed by the monomials of  $R_d$  sorted, from left to right, according to the chosen order  $\prec$ . The rows of  $M_d(F)$  are indexed by the polynomials  $m_{i,j}f_j$ , where  $m_{i,j} \in R$  is a monomial such that  $\deg(m_{i,j}f_j) = d$ . - (4) Go back to (1).

whence max.GB.deg $_{\prec}(I) = 3$ . Namely, we have

$$3 = \max.\text{GB.deg}_{\prec}(I) = \max\{d_{\text{reg}}(I + \langle x_n \rangle), \widetilde{d}_{\text{reg}}(I)\} = \widetilde{d}_{\text{reg}}(I) = D^{(n,m)}.$$

(In this case, we can replace  $D^{(n,m)}$  by  $D^{(n,m)}-1$  in (1.5)).

#### 3.2 Proof of Theorem 1.2

As in the previous subsection, let  $\prec$  be the graded reverse lexicographical ordering on  $R = K[x_1, \ldots, x_n]$  with  $x_n \prec \cdots \prec x_1$ . We also denote the restriction of  $\prec$ on  $K[x_1, \ldots, x_{n-1}]$  by the same notation  $\prec$ . In the following, we shall prove Theorem 1.2, namely the inequality (1.2) becomes an equality if the initial ideal  $\operatorname{in}_{\prec}(I) = \langle \operatorname{LM}_{\prec}(I) \rangle_R$  is weakly reverse lexicographic. Here, a weakly reverse lexicographic ideal is a monomial ideal J such that if  $x^{\alpha}$  is one of the minimal generators of J then every monomial of the same degree which preceeds  $x^{\alpha}$  must belong to J as well (see also e.g., [43, Section 4] for the definition).

As in [30] and [31] (see also [35] and [36]), we consider the elimination ideal

$$I' := (I + \langle x_n \rangle_R) \cap K[x_1, \dots, x_{n-1}] = \langle f_1 |_{x_n = 0}, \dots, f_m |_{x_n = 0} \rangle_{K[x_1, \dots, x_{n-1}]}.$$

Since  $R/(I + \langle x_n \rangle)$  and  $K[x_1, \ldots, x_{n-1}]/I'$  are isomorphic as graded rings, they have the same Hilbert series, and  $d_{\text{reg}}(I') = d_{\text{reg}}(I + \langle x_n \rangle_R)$ . Put  $D := d_{\text{reg}}(I') = d_{\text{reg}}(I + \langle x_n \rangle_R)$ . By comparing Hilbert series, it can be also easily checked that, the sequence  $(f_1, \ldots, f_m, x_n) \in R^{m+1}$  is cryptographic semi-regular if and only if  $(f_1|_{x_n=0}, \ldots, f_m|_{x_n=0}) \in K[x_1, \ldots, x_{n-1}]^m$  is cryptographic semi-regular, equivalently these sequences are both D-regular.

Let G (resp. G') be the reduced Gröbner basis of I (resp. I') with respect to  $\prec$ . Note that, since  $I + \langle x_n \rangle_R = I'R + \langle x_n \rangle_R$ , the reduced Gröbner basis of  $I + \langle x_n \rangle_R$  is given by  $G' \cup \{x_n\}$ , whence max.GB.deg $_{\prec}(I') = \max$ .GB.deg $_{\prec}(I + \langle x_n \rangle_R)$ .

To prove Theorem 1.2, we start with extending some results in [35] to our case.

<span id="page-23-1"></span>**Lemma 3.2.1** (cf. [35, Corollary 2]). With notation as above, we assume that  $(f_1, \ldots, f_m, x_n) \in R^{m+1}$  is cryptographic semi-regular (i.e.,  $(f_1|_{x_n=0}, \ldots, f_m|_{x_n=0}) \in K[x_1, \ldots, x_{n-1}]^m$  is cryptographic semi-regular), namely both the sequences are D-regular with  $D := d_{reg}(I') = d_{reg}(I + \langle x_n \rangle_R)$ . Then, we have

<span id="page-23-0"></span>
$$(\langle LM_{\prec}(I)\rangle_R)_d = (\langle LM_{\prec}(I')\rangle_R)_d \tag{3.2.1}$$

for any d with d < D.

Proof. We first prove  $(\langle LM_{\prec}(I)\rangle_R)_d \supset (\langle LM_{\prec}(I')\rangle_R)_d$  for any non-negative integer d. For this, it suffices to show that  $LM_{\prec}(f) \in LM_{\prec}(I)$  for any  $f \in I'_d \setminus \{0\}$ . We may assume that f is homogeneous, and then it is written as  $f = g + x_n h$  for some  $g \in I_d$  and  $h \in R_{d-1}$ . Here, g is not divisible by  $x_n$ , since otherwise  $f \notin K[x_1, \ldots, x_{n-1}]$ , a contradiction. Therefore, we have  $LM_{\prec}(f) = LM_{\prec}(g)$  with  $g \in I$ , as desired.

Since  $(f_1, \ldots, f_m, x_n)$  is *D*-regular, it follows from [15, Proposition 2 a)] (cf. [35, Corollary 1]), its sub-sequence  $(f_1, \ldots, f_m)$  is also *D*-regular. Hence, we have

$$HS_{R/I}(z) \equiv \frac{\prod_{j=1}^{m} (1 - z^{d_j})}{(1 - z)^n} \equiv \frac{\prod_{j=1}^{m} (1 - z^{d_j}) \cdot (1 - z)}{(1 - z)^n} \cdot \frac{1}{1 - z} 
\equiv HS_{R/(I + \langle x_n \rangle)}(z) \cdot \frac{1}{1 - z} \equiv HS_{R/(I + \langle x_n \rangle)}(z) \cdot (1 + z + z^2 + \cdots) 
\equiv HS_{K[x_1, \dots, x_{n-1}]/I'}(z) \cdot (1 + z + z^2 + \cdots) \pmod{z^D}.$$

Thus, for any d with  $d < D = d_{\text{reg}}(I')$ , one has  $\text{HF}_{R/I}(d) = \sum_{i=0}^{d} \text{HF}_{K[x_1, \dots, x_{n-1}]/I'}(i)$ , so that

<span id="page-24-0"></span>
$$\dim_K R_d - \dim_K I_d = \sum_{i=0}^d (\dim_K K[x_1, \dots, x_{n-1}]_i - \dim_K I_i'). \tag{3.2.2}$$

Since  $(JR)_d = x_n^d J_0 \oplus x_n^{d-1} J_1 \oplus \cdots \oplus x_n J_{d-1} \oplus J_d$  for any of J = I' and  $J = K[x_1, \ldots, x_{n-1}]$  (note that this holds also for  $d \geq D$ ), the right hand side of (3.2.2) is equal to  $\dim_K R_d - \dim_K (I'R)_d$ , so that  $\dim_K (I'R)_d = \dim_K I_d$ . Here, it follows from [29, Theorem 5.2.6] that  $\dim_K (\langle LM_{\prec}(I)\rangle_R)_d = \dim_K (\langle LM_{\prec}(I'R)\rangle_R)_d$ . Since  $\langle LM_{\prec}(I'R)\rangle_R = \langle LM_{\prec}(I')\rangle_R$ , we finally obtain (3.2.1).

**Lemma 3.2.2** (cf. [35, Lemma 2]). Under the same setting as in Lemma 3.2.1, for each degree d < D, we have

$$LM_{\prec}(G)_d = LM_{\prec}(G')_d. \tag{3.2.3}$$

*Proof.* This can be proved just by translating the proof of [35, Lemma 2]: Replace R', R,  $\langle \mathbf{F}^h \rangle_{R'}$ ,  $\langle \mathbf{F}^{\text{top}} \rangle_R$ ,  $G_{\text{hom}}$ , and  $G_{\text{top}}$  in the proof by R,  $K[x_1, \ldots, x_{n-1}]$ , I, I', G, and G' in our case, respectively.

<span id="page-24-2"></span>**Lemma 3.2.3.** Under the same setting as in Lemma 3.2.1, we also suppose that  $R/(I + \langle x_n \rangle)$  is Artinian (equivalently  $K[x_1, \ldots, x_{n-1}]/I'$  is Artinian), namely  $D < \infty$ . Furthermore, we assume that  $\langle LM_{\prec}(I) \rangle_R$  is weakly reverse lexicographic. Then we have max.GB.deg $_{\prec}(I) \geq D$  and max.GB.deg $_{\prec}(I') = D$ .

Proof. Put  $d := \max.\text{GB.deg}_{\prec}(I)$ . Assume for a contradiction that d < D. Since  $\text{Krull.dim}(R/(I + \langle x_n \rangle)) = 0$ , it follows from Lemma 2.1.5 that there is an element g in G with  $\text{LM}_{\prec}(g) = x_{n-1}^{d'}$  for some  $d' \leq d$ . Then, any monomial  $t \in R_{d'}$  with  $t \succeq x_{n-1}^{d'}$  (namely t is any monomial in  $K[x_1, \ldots, x_{n-1}]_{d'}$ ) belongs to the initial ideal  $\text{in}_{\prec}(I) = \langle \text{LM}_{\prec}(I) \rangle_R = \langle \text{LM}_{\prec}(G) \rangle_R$  by the weakly reverse lexicographicness. Here, by d < D together with (3.2.3), we have  $\langle \text{LM}_{\prec}(G) \rangle_R = \langle \text{LM}_{\prec}(G') \leq_d \rangle_R$ . This implies that t also belongs to  $\langle \text{LM}_{\prec}(G') \rangle_{K[x_1,\ldots,x_{n-1}]}$ , and hence  $K[x_1,\ldots,x_{n-1}]_{d'}/I'_{d'} = 0$ . Thus, we have  $d \geq d' \geq d_{\text{reg}}(I')$ , a contradiction.

By the same argument as above, we can show that max.GB.deg $_{\prec}(I') = d_{\text{reg}}(I')$  as follows. Note that max.GB.deg $_{\prec}(I') \leq d_{\text{reg}}(I')$  follows from a general fact written in the second paragraph of Remark 3.2.4 below. Thus, we suppose to the contrary that  $d := \max.\text{GB.deg}_{\prec}(I') < D := d_{\text{reg}}(I')$ . Then, it follows from (3.2.3) that  $\text{LM}_{\prec}(G') = \text{LM}_{\prec}(G) \leq d$ , and the initial ideal in  $_{\prec}(\langle G' \rangle) = \langle \text{LM}_{\prec}(G') \rangle_{K[x_1,\dots,x_{n-1}]}$  has the weak reverse lexicographicness up to d. Since there is an element g in G' with  $\text{LM}_{\prec}(g) = x_{n-1}^{d'}$  for some  $d' \leq d$ , any monomial in  $K[x_1,\dots,x_{n-1}]_{d'}$  belongs to  $\langle \text{LM}_{\prec}(G') \rangle_{K[x_1,\dots,x_{n-1}]}$  and so  $K[x_1,\dots,x_{n-1}]_{d'}/I'_{d'} = 0$ , which implies  $d \geq d' \geq D$ , a contradiction.

<span id="page-25-0"></span>**Remark 3.2.4.** In Lemma 3.2.3, when  $R/(I + \langle x_n \rangle)$  is Artinian (or equivalently  $K[x_1, \ldots, x_{n-1}]/I'$  is Artinian), we can easily prove max.GB.deg $_{\prec}(I') = D$  if  $\operatorname{in}_{\prec}(I') = \langle \operatorname{LM}_{\prec}(I') \rangle_{K[x_1, \ldots, x_{n-1}]}$  is a weakly reverse lexicographic ideal, assuming neither that  $(f_1, \ldots, f_m, x_n)$  is cryptographic semi-regular nor that  $\operatorname{in}_{\prec}(I) = \langle \operatorname{LM}_{\prec}(I) \rangle_R$  is weakly reverse lexicographic.

In general, for any homogeneous ideal  $J \subset R$  with Krull.dim(R/J) = 0, we have max.GB.deg $_{\prec}(J) \leq \operatorname{reg}(J) = d_{\operatorname{reg}}(J)$  by [5, Corollary 2.5] (see also [30, Proposition 4.15]), and it is straightforward that the equality holds if the initial ideal  $\langle \operatorname{LM}_{\prec}(J) \rangle_R$  of J is a weakly reverse lexicographic.

Proof of Theorem 1.2: Put  $D := d_{\text{reg}}(I + \langle x_n \rangle_R) = d_{\text{reg}}(I')$  and  $D' := \widetilde{d}_{\text{reg}}(I)$ . From our assumption that  $R/(I + \langle x_n \rangle)$  is Artinian, we have the inequality (1.2), i.e., max.GB.deg $(I) \leq \max\{D, D'\}$ . The case where Krull.dim(R/I) = 0 is clear from Remark 3.2.4, so we assume that Krull.dim(R/I) = 1. Note that, for the reduced Gröbner basis G of I, the elements of  $LM_{\prec}(G)$  are the minimal generators of the monomial ideal  $\langle LM_{\prec}(I) \rangle_R = \langle LM_{\prec}(G) \rangle_R$ .

First we consider the case where  $D' \geq D$ , so that  $\max\{D, D'\} = D'$ . Put  $d := \max.\text{GB.deg}_{\prec}(I) \leq D'$ . Then, it follows from Macaulay's basis theorem (cf. [33, Theorem 1.5.7]) that  $R_d/I_d$  has a K-linear basis of the form

$$\{t \in R_d : t \text{ is a monomial and } t \notin \langle LM_{\prec}(I) \rangle_R = \langle LM_{\prec}(G) \rangle_R \},$$

which is called the *standard monomial basis*. We set  $r := \dim_K R_d/I_d$ , and write this standard monomial basis as  $\{t_1, \ldots, t_r\}$  with  $t_1 \succ \cdots \succ t_r$ . In the following, we prove that  $\{t_1x_n^s, \ldots, t_rx_n^s\}$  is a K-linear basis of  $R_{d+s}/I_{d+s}$  for any s with  $s \ge 1$ , from which we have  $\dim_K R_d/I_d = \dim_K R_{d+s}/I_{d+s}$  for any positive integer s, so that  $d \ge D'$  and therefore d = D'.

By Lemma 3.2.3, we have  $d \geq D$ , and thus it follows from Remark 2.1.12 that the multiplication-by- $x_n^s$  map from  $R_d/I_d$  to  $R_{d+s}/I_{d+s}$  is surjective (since  $R/(I+\langle x_n\rangle)$  is Artinian). Therefore the set  $B_s:=\{t_1x_n^s,\ldots,t_rx_n^s\}$  generates  $R_{d+s}/I_{d+s}$  over K. Suppose to the contrary that  $B_s$  is not a basis. In this case, there exists an i such that  $t_ix_n^s$  is divisible by  $LM_{\prec}(g)$  for some  $g \in G$ . Indeed, since the elements of  $B_s$  are linearly dependent over K, we obtain f:=

 $a_1t_1x_n^s + \cdots + a_rt_rx_n^s \in I_{d+s}$  for some  $(a_1, \ldots, a_r) \in K^r \setminus \{(0, \ldots, 0)\}$ . Thus, for the least i with  $a_i \neq 0$ , the leading monomial of f is  $t_ix_n^s$ , which is divisible by  $LM_{\prec}(g)$  for some  $g \in G$ . Putting  $u_i = GCD(t_i, LM_{\prec}(g))$  and  $s_i = t_i/u_i$ , we have  $u_is_ix_n^s = t_ix_n^s$ . (We note that  $\deg s_i = d - \deg u_i \geq \deg LM_{\prec}(g) - \deg u_i = s'$ .) Note that  $s' \geq 1$  since otherwise  $t_i$  is divisible by  $LM_{\prec}(g)$ .

Take an arbitrary monomial  $s'_i$  such that  $\deg s'_i = s'$  and  $s'_i$  divides  $s_i$ . Finally, consider the remaining case, i.e., D > D', so that  $\max\{D, D'\} = D$ . In this case, it follows from Lemma 3.2.3 that  $d \ge D$ , whence d = D.

# <span id="page-26-0"></span>4 Results on genericness with conjectures

Let n be a positive integer, and let  $R = K[x_1, \ldots, x_n]$  be the polynomial ring of n variables over a field K. We may assume that K is infinite. Let  $m, d_1, \ldots, d_m$  be positive integers. As in Section 2.3, let  $V_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)} = R_{d_1} \times \cdots \times R_{d_m}$ , and naturally endow it with the Zariski topology. Recall Definition 2.3.1 for the definition of the genericness of a property for  $\mathbf{F} = (f_1,\ldots,f_m) \in V_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}$ . For  $\mathbf{F} = (f_1,\ldots,f_m) \in V_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}$ , we denote by  $\langle \mathbf{F} \rangle_R$  (or by  $\langle \mathbf{F} \rangle$  simply) the ideal generated by  $f_1,\ldots,f_m$ .

# <span id="page-26-1"></span>4.1 Restricted genericness and our conjectures

For  $n, m, (d_1, \ldots, d_m)$ , and a non-negative integer k, we set

```
\Lambda_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}^{\dim=k} := \{ \boldsymbol{F} \in V_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)} : \operatorname{Krull.dim}(R/\langle \boldsymbol{F} \rangle) = k \}, 

\Lambda_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}^{\dim\geq k} := \{ \boldsymbol{F} \in V_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)} : \operatorname{Krull.dim}(R/\langle \boldsymbol{F} \rangle) \geq k \}, 

\Lambda_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}^{\operatorname{csr}} := \{ \boldsymbol{F} \in V_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)} : \boldsymbol{F} \text{ is cryptographic semi-regular} \}, 

\Lambda_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}^{\operatorname{gcsr}} := \{ \boldsymbol{F} \in V_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)} : \boldsymbol{F} \text{ is generalized cryptographic semi-regular} \}.
```

It follows from Lemma 2.2.12 that  $\Lambda_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}^{\mathrm{gcsr}} \cap \Lambda_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}^{\dim=0} \subset \Lambda_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}^{\mathrm{csr}}$  and the equality holds if and only if  $m \geq n$ .

Here, as a variant of Conjecture 2.3.7, we start with raising the following conjecture (cf. [36, Conjecture 4.3.4] as its strict version):

{27}------------------------------------------------

<span id="page-27-0"></span>**Conjecture 4.1.1.** Assume that K is an infinite field. For any n, m, and  $(d_1, \ldots, d_m)$ , the property that  $\mathbf{F} = (f_1, \ldots, f_m) \in V_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}$  is generalized cryptographic semi-regular is generic.

We see that Conjectures 2.3.7 and 4.1.1 are equivalent to each other. For this, we first prove the following lemma:

<span id="page-27-1"></span>**Lemma 4.1.2.** For any n, m, and  $(d_1, \ldots, d_m)$  with  $m \geq n$ , the property that  $R/\langle f_1, \ldots, f_m \rangle$  is Artinian is generic.

*Proof.* The case m=n is well-known to hold: More strongly,  $\Lambda_{n,n,(d_1,\ldots,d_n)}^{\dim=0}$  is a non-empty Zariski-open set of  $V_{n,n,(d_1,\ldots,d_n)}$ .

Assume m > n, fix n, m, and  $(d_1, \ldots, d_m)$ . Then, the preimage of  $\Lambda_{n,n,(d_1,\ldots,d_n)}^{\dim=0}$  by a natural projection sending an element of  $V_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}$  to the first n-entries is included in  $\Lambda_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}^{\dim=0}$ . Since  $\pi$  is continuous, the preimage is Zariski-open in  $V_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}$ , and it is clearly non-empty, whence the assertion holds.  $\square$ 

**Proposition 4.1.3.** Assume that K is an infinite field. For  $m \ge n$ , Conjecture 4.1.1 is equivalent to Fröberg's conjecture (Conjecture 2.3.7).

Proof. Assume that there exists a non-empty Zariski-open set  $U_1 \subset V_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}$  with  $U_1 \subset \Lambda_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}^{\operatorname{gcsr}}$ . By Lemma 4.1.2, there exists a non-empty Zariski-open set  $U_2 \subset V_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}$  such that  $U_2 \subset \Lambda_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}^{\dim=0}$ . Since both  $U_1$  and  $U_2$  are dense, we have  $\emptyset \neq U_1 \cap U_2 \subset \Lambda_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}^{\operatorname{gcsr}} \cap \Lambda_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}^{\dim=0} \subset \Lambda_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}^{\operatorname{csr}}$ , whence Fröberg's conjecture holds for  $(n,m,d_1,\ldots,d_m)$ .

The converse is obvious by  $\Lambda_{n,m,(d_1,...,d_m)}^{\operatorname{csr}} \subset \Lambda_{n,m,(d_1,...,d_m)}^{\operatorname{gcsr}}$  (cf. Lemma 2.2.12).

Despite of the equivalence between two conjectures, those cannot guarantee that  $\Lambda_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}^{\mathrm{gcsr}}\cap\Lambda_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}^{\dim\geq 1}$  includes a non-empty open set in the subspace  $\Lambda_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}^{\dim\geq 1}$  equipped with the induced topology. To discuss this, we define the restricted genericness as follows:

<span id="page-27-2"></span>**Definition 4.1.4** (Restricted genericness). Let V' be a non-empty subset of  $V_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}$ . For given n,m, and  $(d_1,\ldots,d_m)$ , a property  $\mathcal{P}$  for  $\mathbf{F}\in V_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}$  is said to be *generic on* V' if it holds on a non-empty open set of V' with respect to the induced topology, namely there exists a non-empty Zariski open set U in  $V_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}$  such that  $\emptyset \neq U \cap V' \subset \{\mathbf{F} \in V_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)} : \mathbf{F} \text{ satisfies } \mathcal{P}\}.$ 

Namely, it is important whether the property that  $\mathbf{F} \in V_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}$  is generalized cryptographic semi-regular is generic on  $\Lambda_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}^{\dim\geq 1}$  or not. We will also discuss the genericness on

<span id="page-27-3"></span>
$$Z_{\boldsymbol{p}} := \{ \boldsymbol{H} \in V_{n,m,(d_1,\dots,d_m)} : \boldsymbol{p} \in V_{\overline{K}}(\boldsymbol{H}) \}$$

$$(4.1.1)$$

for each point  $\boldsymbol{p} \in \mathbb{P}^{n-1}(K)$ .

{28}------------------------------------------------

Remark 4.1.5. If K is an infinite field and if the property that  $\mathbf{F} \in V_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}$  is generalized cryptographic semi-regular is generic on  $\Lambda_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}^{\dim \geq 1}$ , then 'almost' all sequences  $\mathbf{F} \in V_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}$  such that Krull.dim $(R/\langle \mathbf{F} \rangle) \geq 1$  are expected to be generalized cryptographic semi-regular.

As the first discussion about the restricted genericness of the generalized cryptographic semi-regularity on  $\Lambda_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}^{\dim\geq 1}$ , we consider the following sub-case, where Krull.dim $(R/\langle \mathbf{F} \rangle) = 1$  and dim $_K R/\langle \mathbf{F} \rangle_D = 1$  for  $D = \widetilde{d}_{\text{reg}}(\langle \mathbf{F} \rangle) < \infty$ . In this case,  $\langle \mathbf{F} \rangle$  has a unique projective zero  $\mathbf{p}$  in  $\mathbb{P}^{n-1}(K)$ . First we show that it suffices to consider the case of the point  $\mathbf{o} := (0 : \cdots : 0 : 1)$  as  $\mathbf{p}$ , by the following lemma:

<span id="page-28-0"></span>**Lemma 4.1.6.** If the property that  $\mathbf{F} \in V_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}$  is generalized cryptographic semi-regular is generic on  $Z_{\mathbf{o}}$ , then it is also generic on  $Z_{\mathbf{p}}$  for any  $\mathbf{p} \in \mathbb{P}^{n-1}(K)$ .

*Proof.* For each  $\mathbf{p} = (p_1 : \ldots : p_n) \in \mathbb{P}^{n-1}(K)$ , let  $\phi_{\mathbf{p}}$  be an invertible projective linear transformation sending  $\mathbf{p}$  to  $\mathbf{o}$ . $$(x_1:\cdots:x_n)\to (x_1-p_1x_n:\cdots:x_{n-1}-p_1x_{n-1}:x_n).$$

$$\Phi_{\mathbf{p}}: V_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)} \to V_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)} ; (f_1,\ldots,f_m) \mapsto (\phi_{\mathbf{p}}^*(f_1),\ldots,\phi_{\mathbf{p}}^*(f_m)).$$

It is straightforward that this is an invertible linear (and hence homeomorphic) map on  $V_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}$ . From our assumption, we have a non-empty Zariski-open set  $U_{o}$  of  $V_{n,m,(d_{1},...,d_{m})}$  intersecting with  $Z_{o}$  such that  $U_{o} \cap Z_{o} \subset \Lambda_{n,m,(d_{1},...,d_{m})}^{\operatorname{gcsr}} \cap Z_{o}$ . Thus, letting  $U_{\boldsymbol{p}} = \Phi_{\boldsymbol{p}}(U_{o})$ , we have  $U_{\boldsymbol{p}} \cap Z_{\boldsymbol{p}} \subset \Lambda_{n,m,(d_{1},...,d_{m})}^{\operatorname{gcsr}} \cap Z_{\boldsymbol{p}}$ .

# 4.2 Genericness on cryptographic semi-regularity and weakly reverse lexicographicness

As additional notations, we simply write LM(\*) and in(\*) for LM $_{\prec}$ (\*) and in $_{\prec}$ (\*), respectively, and we define the following:

For each element  $\mathbf{a} \in \mathcal{A}_{(d_1,\dots,d_m)} := \mathbb{A}^N(K) = \prod_{j=1}^m \mathbb{A}^{N_j}(K)$  with  $N_j = \binom{n+d_j-1}{d_j}$ , we define a map  $\pi_{\mathbf{a}}$  from  $K[\mathcal{C}][X]$  to K[X] by substituting each  $c_{j,t}$  with  $\mathbf{a}_{j,t}$ . $$M := \begin{pmatrix} a_{1,t_1} & \dots & a_{1,t_v} & a_{1,t_{v+1}} & \dots & a_{1,t_w} \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots \\ a_{v,t_1} & \dots & a_{v,t_v} & a_{v,t_{v+1}} & \dots & a_{v,t_w} \end{pmatrix}$$

Then, we apply  $\pi_{\boldsymbol{a}}$  naturally to M by  $\pi_{\boldsymbol{a}}(M)_{i,t} = \pi_{\boldsymbol{a}}(M_{i,t})$  for each (i,t)-entry. As  $\pi_{\boldsymbol{a}}(\bar{\ell}_i)$  is a non-zero constant multiple of  $h_i$ , we have

$$\pi_{\mathbf{a}}(M) = \begin{pmatrix} b_{1,t_1} & 0 & \dots & 0 & b_{1,t_{v+1}} & \dots & b_{1,t_w} \\ 0 & b_{2,t_2} & \dots & 0 & b_{2,t_{v+1}} & \dots & b_{2,t_w} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \dots & b_{v,t_v} & b_{v,t_{v+1}} & \dots & b_{v,t_w} \end{pmatrix},$$

where  $b_{i,t_j} = \pi_{\boldsymbol{a}}(a_{i,t_j})$  for each i and j, and  $b_{i,t_i} \neq 0$  for  $1 \leq i \leq v$ . Let  $M_0$  be the square matrix consisting of the first v left columns of M and  $\overline{M_0}$  the adjugate matrix of  $M_0$ . Namely we have

$$M_0 := \begin{pmatrix} a_{1,t_1} & \dots & a_{1,t_v} \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ a_{v,t_1} & \dots & a_{v,t_v} \end{pmatrix},$$

and  $\overline{M_0}$  is a matrix over  $K[\mathcal{C}]$ . Then,  $\pi_{\boldsymbol{a}}(M_0)$  is a diagonal matrix with non-zero diagonal entries  $b_{1,t_1},\ldots,b_{v,t_v}$  and  $\det(\pi_{\boldsymbol{a}}(M_0))=\pi_{\boldsymbol{a}}(\det(M_0))\neq 0$ . Hence we

{34}------------------------------------------------

have  $\det(M_0) \neq 0$  as an element in  $K[\mathcal{C}]$ . Since  $\overline{M_0}M_0 = \det(M_0)E_v$ , where  $E_v$  denotes the identity matrix of degree v, we have

$$\overline{M_0}M = (\det(M_0)E_v, \overline{M_0}V_{v+1}, \dots, \overline{M_0}V_w),$$

where  $V_j$  denotes the j-th column of M for each j.

For each i, let  $\bar{h}'_i$  be a polynomial in  $K[\mathcal{C}][X]$  converted from the i-th low of  $\overline{M_0}M_0$ . Then, since each  $\bar{\ell}_i$  belongs to  $\langle \boldsymbol{G} \rangle \cap K[\mathcal{C}][X]$  and the left-multiplication of  $\overline{M}_0$  induces low-operations on M over  $K[\mathcal{C}]$ , each  $\bar{h}'_i$  belongs to  $\langle \boldsymbol{G} \rangle \cap K[\mathcal{C}][X]$ . Moreover, we have  $t_i = \mathrm{LM}(\bar{h}'_i) \in \mathrm{in}(\langle \boldsymbol{G} \rangle)$  and  $\mathrm{LC}(\bar{h}'_i) = \det(M_0)$ . Since  $t_i$  cannot be divided by any monomial in  $\mathrm{LM}(\mathrm{GB}(\boldsymbol{F})_{< d}) = \mathrm{LM}(\mathrm{GB}(\boldsymbol{G}))_{< d}$ , the monomials  $t_1, \ldots, t_v$  should belong to the minimal generating set  $\mathrm{LM}(\mathrm{GB}(\boldsymbol{G}))$  of  $\mathrm{in}(\langle \boldsymbol{G} \rangle)$ . Comparing the number  $v = \#\mathrm{LM}(\boldsymbol{G}_d)$ , we have  $\{t_1, \ldots, t_v\} = \mathrm{LM}(\boldsymbol{G}_d)$ .

Now let  $\hat{h}_i = \frac{h'_i}{\operatorname{LC}(\bar{h}'_i)} = \frac{h'_i}{\det(M_0)}$  for each i. Then, we have  $\operatorname{LC}(\hat{h}_i) = 1$  for each i. Since  $\operatorname{GB}(\boldsymbol{G})_{< d} \cup \{\hat{h}_1, \dots, \hat{h}_v\}$  forms a set of inter-reduced elements,  $\{\hat{h}_1, \dots, \hat{h}_v\}$  coincides with  $\operatorname{GB}(\boldsymbol{G})_d$ , that is,  $\bar{h}_i = \hat{h}_i$  for each i.

Finally we show  $\pi_{\boldsymbol{a}}(\bar{h}_i) = h_i$  for each i. Since  $\pi_{\boldsymbol{a}}(\det(M_0)) \neq 0$ , the image  $\pi_{\boldsymbol{a}}(\bar{h}_i)$  is defined for each i. Moreover,  $\mathrm{LM}(\pi_{\boldsymbol{a}}(\bar{h}_i)) = t_i = \mathrm{LM}(h_i)$ ,  $\mathrm{LC}(\pi_{\boldsymbol{a}}(\bar{h}_i)) = 1$ , and  $\bar{h}_i$  is reduced with respect to  $\mathrm{GB}(\boldsymbol{G})_{< d}$  and  $t_j$  for  $j \neq i$ . This implies  $\pi_{\boldsymbol{a}}(\bar{h}_i) = h_i$  for each i by the uniqueness of the reduced Gröbner basis with respect to a fixed monomial ordering.

Finally we consider a generalized cryptographic semi-regular sequence belonging to  $Z_{o}$ , where  $Z_{o}$  is defined in Section 4.1 (see (4.1.1)). In this case, the set  $\mathcal{C}$  of parameters is  $\{c_{j,t}:1\leq j\leq m,\ t\in\mathcal{T}_{d_{j}}\setminus\{x_{n}^{d_{j}}\}\}$ . Moreover, we set  $\mathcal{B}_{d}:=\{(a_{t})_{t\in\mathcal{T}_{d}\setminus\{x_{n}^{d}\}}:a_{t}\in K\}$  for each positive integer d, and set  $\mathcal{B}_{(d_{1},\ldots,d_{m})}:=\prod_{j=1}^{m}\mathcal{B}_{d_{j}}$ . Then, for each element a in  $\mathcal{B}_{(d_{1},\ldots,d_{m})}$ , we define a map  $\pi_{a}$  from  $K[\mathcal{C}][X]$  to K[X] by substituting each  $c_{j,t}$  with  $a_{j,t}$ . The map  $\pi_{a}$  is generalized to a map from  $K[\mathcal{C}][X]^{m}$  to  $K[X]^{m}$ , for which we use the same symbol. Let G be a generic sequence for  $Z_{o}$  defined in Definition 4.1.7. Under this setting, we can apply the same proof in Theorem 4.2.1 to obtain the following results corresponding to Theorem 4.2.1 and Corollary 4.2.2.

<span id="page-34-0"></span>**Proposition 4.2.3.** Let G be a generic sequence for  $Z_o$ . If there exists a generalized cryptographic semi-regular sequence F in  $Z_o$ , that is,  $F = \pi_a(G)$  for some element  $a \in \mathcal{B}_{(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}$ , such that  $\dim_K(K[X]/\langle F \rangle)_D = 1$  for  $D = \widetilde{d}_{reg}(\langle F \rangle) < \infty$  and  $\operatorname{in}(\langle F \rangle)$  is weakly reverse lexicographic, then  $\operatorname{GB}(F) = \pi_a(\operatorname{GB}(G))$  and  $\operatorname{LM}(\operatorname{GB}(F)) = \operatorname{LM}(\operatorname{GB}(G))$ . In this case, G is generalized cryptographic semi-regular with  $\dim_L(L[X]/\langle G \rangle)_D = 1$  for  $D = \widetilde{d}_{reg}(\langle G \rangle) < \infty$  and  $\operatorname{in}(\langle G \rangle)$  is weakly reverse lexicographic. Moreover, in this case, there exists a non-empty Zariski-open set U of  $Z_o$  with respect to the induced topology such that H is generalized cryptographic semi-regular and  $\operatorname{in}(\langle H \rangle)$  is weakly reverse lexicographic for any H in U.

{35}------------------------------------------------

*Proof.* By Proposition 4.1.9, we have

$$\operatorname{HS}_{K[X]/\langle \mathbf{F} \rangle}(z) \equiv \operatorname{HS}_{L[X]/\langle \mathbf{G} \rangle}(z) \equiv \frac{\prod_{j=1}^{m} (1 - z^{d_j})}{(1 - z)^n} \pmod{z^D},$$

where  $D = \widetilde{d}_{reg}(\langle \boldsymbol{F} \rangle) = \widetilde{d}_{reg}(\langle \boldsymbol{G} \rangle)$  and  $\dim_L(L[X]/\langle \boldsymbol{G} \rangle)_D = 1$ . In this case, as  $\boldsymbol{F}$  and  $\boldsymbol{G}$  have  $(0:\dots:0:1)$  as their projective zero,  $x_n^D$  cannot belong neither to  $\langle \boldsymbol{F} \rangle_D$  nor to  $\langle \boldsymbol{G} \rangle_D$ . This implies that  $(K[X]/\langle \boldsymbol{F} \rangle)_D$  and  $(L[X]/\langle \boldsymbol{G} \rangle)_D$  have a common linear basis  $\{x_n^D\}$ , which also implies that max.GB.deg $(\langle \boldsymbol{F} \rangle) := \max\{\deg(h): h \in \mathrm{GB}(\boldsymbol{F})\}$  and max.GB.deg $(\langle \boldsymbol{G} \rangle)$  are both smaller than D. Thus, all arguments in the proof of Theorem 4.2.1 can be applied to the case of Proposition 4.2.3, from which we obtain  $\mathrm{GB}(\boldsymbol{F}) = \pi_{\boldsymbol{a}}(\mathrm{GB}(\boldsymbol{G}))$  and  $\mathrm{LM}(\mathrm{GB}(\boldsymbol{F})) = \mathrm{LM}(\mathrm{GB}(\boldsymbol{G}))$ .

Then, by applying Theorem 2.3.4 to G, there exists a non-empty Zariski-open set  $U_{\mathcal{B}}$  of  $\mathcal{B}_{(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}$  such that  $\mathrm{LM}(\mathrm{GB}(F))=\mathrm{LM}(\mathrm{GB}(\pi_b(G)))$  for any b in  $U_B$ , that is,  $\mathrm{in}(\langle F \rangle)=\mathrm{in}(\langle \pi_b(G) \rangle)$ . This implies that the subset U of  $Z_o$  corresponding to  $U_{\mathcal{B}}$  is also a non-empty Zariski-open set such that H is generalized cryptographic semi-regular and  $\mathrm{in}(\langle H \rangle)$  is weakly reverse lexicographic for any  $H \in U_{\mathcal{B}}$ .

<span id="page-35-0"></span>**Proposition 4.2.4.** Suppose that the conditions of Proposition 4.2.3 are satisfied. Then, for any  $\mathbf{p} \in \mathbb{P}^{n-1}(K)$ , there exists a non-empty Zariski-open set  $U_{\mathbf{p}}$  of  $Z_{\mathbf{p}}$  with respect to the induced topology such that  $\mathbf{H}$  is generalized cryptographic semi-regular and  $\operatorname{in}(\langle \mathbf{H} \rangle)$  is weakly reverse lexicographic for any  $\mathbf{H}$  in  $U_{\mathbf{p}}$ .

Proof. We show the assertion by using the arguments in the proof of Lemma 4.1.6 Let  $\mathbf{p} := (p_1 : \ldots : p_n)$  be a point in  $\mathbb{P}^{n-1}(K)$ . Without loss of generality, we may assume that  $p_n \neq 0$  and moreover  $p_n = 1$ , and consider the following maps, where  $\mathcal{V} := V_{n,m,(d_1,\ldots,d_m)}$ :

$$\phi_{\mathbf{p}}: (a_1: \dots : a_{n-1}: a_n) \mapsto (a_1 - p_1 a_n: \dots : a_{n-1} - p_{n-1} a_n: a_n),$$

$$\phi_{\mathbf{p}}^*: K[X] \ni h(x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n) \mapsto h(x_1 - p_1 x_n, \dots, x_{n-1} - p_{n-1} x_n, x_n) \in K[X],$$

$$\Phi_{\mathbf{p}}: \mathcal{V} \in (h_1, \dots, h_m) \mapsto (\phi_{\mathbf{p}}^*(h_1), \dots, \phi_{\mathbf{p}}^*(h_m)) \in \mathcal{V}.$$

Then  $\phi_{p}^{*}$  is a ring homomorphism and  $\Phi_{p}$  is an invertible linear map on  $\mathcal{V}$  and its restriction on  $Z_{o}$  induces a bijection between  $Z_{o}$  and  $Z_{p}$ . We also let  $\prec$  be the graded reverse lexicographical ordering with  $x_{1} \succ x_{2} \succ \cdots \succ x_{n}$ .

Now we suppose that the conditions of Proposition 4.2.3 hold. Then the generic sequence G for  $Z_o$  is generalized cryptographic semi-regular sequence with  $\dim_L(L[X]/\langle G \rangle)_D = 1$  for  $D = \widetilde{d}_{reg}(\langle G \rangle) < \infty$  and  $\operatorname{in}(\langle G \rangle)$  is weakly reverse lexicographic, and there is a non-empty Zariski-open set  $U_o$  in  $Z_o$  such that, for any H in  $U_o$ , H is generalized cryptographic semi-regular and  $\operatorname{in}(\langle H \rangle)$  is weakly reverse lexicographic.

{36}------------------------------------------------

Meanwhile, since  $\phi_{\boldsymbol{p}}^*$  does not change LM for any polynomial in L[X] and  $\operatorname{in}(\langle \boldsymbol{F} \rangle) = \operatorname{in}(\langle \Phi_{\boldsymbol{p}}(\boldsymbol{F}) \rangle)$  for any  $\boldsymbol{F}$  in  $U_{\boldsymbol{o}}$ , it can be shown easily that, for any  $\boldsymbol{H}$  in  $U_{\boldsymbol{o}}$ ,  $\Phi_{\boldsymbol{p}}(\boldsymbol{H})$  is generalized cryptographic semi-regular and  $\operatorname{in}(\langle \Phi_{\boldsymbol{p}}(\boldsymbol{H}) \rangle)$  is weakly reverse lexicographic. Let  $U_{\boldsymbol{p}} = \Phi_{\boldsymbol{p}}(U_{\boldsymbol{o}})$ . Then,  $U_{\boldsymbol{p}}$  is also a non-empty Zariski-open set of  $\Phi_{\boldsymbol{p}}(Z_{\boldsymbol{o}}) = Z_{\boldsymbol{p}}$  (see the proof of Lemma 4.1.6). Thus,  $U_{\boldsymbol{p}}$  satisfies the assertion of Proposition 4.2.4.

Remark 4.2.5. It is clear that the converse of Proposition 4.2.4 also holds, that is, if there is a generalized cryptographic semi-regular sequence  $\boldsymbol{H}$  in  $Z_{\boldsymbol{p}}$  such that  $\dim_K(K[X]/\langle \boldsymbol{H} \rangle)_D = 1$  for  $D = \widetilde{d}_{\text{reg}}(\langle \boldsymbol{H} \rangle) < \infty$  and  $\operatorname{in}(\langle \boldsymbol{H} \rangle)$  is weakly reverse lexicographic, then the sequence  $\boldsymbol{F} = (\Phi_{\boldsymbol{p}})^{-1}(\boldsymbol{H})$  belongs to  $Z_{\boldsymbol{o}}$  and it satisfies the condition of Proposition 4.2.3.
